# Chapter 17

She woke at 0430.

For a moment she didn't know where she was, which was unusual. She always knew where she was. Then she did know, and she lay still in the dark of his room and listened to the base and to his breathing and to the quality of the morning, which was the cool quality of early September, dew on the windows, a stillness before anything required action.

She could leave. She'd thought about this, in the way she'd thought about all of it. She could get up now, before the base woke, and be in her own room before the corridor was occupied and the morning was simply the morning and last night was something that had happened and that she knew how to manage.

She lay there.

She thought about managed. She thought about what she'd said last night, more managed, cleaner, and his response, and the ceiling, and his hand.

She got up.

She was quiet about it, finding her clothes in the dark with the efficiency of someone who'd dressed in the dark before, though not in this particular dark, not in this room. She was halfway dressed when she heard him.

"You don't have to go."

Not accusatory. Not even requesting. Just a statement, offered and complete.

She looked at him. He was half-awake, watching her from the pillow with the particular stillness of someone who has already made a decision about how they're going to receive the answer.

She finished dressing.

She thought about what to say. She could say: this was a mistake, I need to get back to who I am, the season ends in six weeks and you leave and this was not something I should have let happen. All of it would be true. All of it would be the architecture speaking, the load-bearing wall doing what it was built to do.

"I'll see you at briefing," she said.

He held her eyes for a moment. "All right," he said.

She left.

—

The briefing was at 0700 and she was early for it and had the weather data up on the board before anyone else arrived. Kowalski came in at 0650 and poured coffee and did not look at her face, which meant he'd already read it and made a decision about what he'd seen and was keeping that decision to himself, which was more consideration than she'd expected from Kowalski and which she registered as a small gift.

Reid came in at 0658.

He got coffee. He sat down in the chair he always used, third from the left, notebook on the table. He looked at the weather data on the board. He did not look at her in any way that was different from the way he looked at her at every other briefing, which was attentively and without either performing attention or hiding it.

She ran the briefing. The weather, the index numbers, the two fires still burning to the northeast with ground crews holding them contained, the readiness protocols for the coming week. She covered everything that needed covering. She answered Vasquez's question about the mutual aid rotation. She assigned the afternoon equipment inspection.

She was good at this. She was very good at this.

She had a harder time than usual looking at the board instead of the room.

After the briefing she stayed to update the maps and when the room emptied Reid stayed too. He did this sometimes, staying to look at the regional map, and she'd never asked him to leave.

He was looking at the map when he said: "I'm not going to make this difficult for you."

She kept her eyes on the numbers she was writing.

"I know what you're doing," he said. "And I know why. And I'm not going to push against it."

She set down the marker.

"I'm telling you I know," he said. "That's all. You don't have to manage me like I don't understand the situation."

She turned to look at him. He was at the map, not facing her, the same angle as the briefing room when she'd told him about her father.

"I'm not trying to manage you," she said.

"I know that too." He turned. "I'm saying I know what last night was and I know what this morning was and I'm still here. Not asking for anything different."

She looked at him. The room was quiet, the rest of the crew dispersed to the day's work, just the two of them and the maps and the September morning light coming through the east window.

"The season ends in six weeks," she said.

"Yes."

"You leave when it ends."

"That was the plan," he said.

She held his eyes.

"Reid," she said. "I don't know what I'm doing."

He looked at her for a long moment. Not with the kind of patience that was waiting for her to arrive at an answer. More like the patience of someone who understood that some things took time and had decided to be present for the time they took.

"That's honest," he said.

"It's the most I have right now."

He nodded once. He picked up his notebook. He went to the door and stopped.

"I'll be at the equipment bay if you need me for anything today," he said. Like it was any other morning.

She looked at the maps. Then she picked up the marker and went back to the numbers.

—

Torres cornered her at lunch.

Not aggressively. Torres's version of cornering was sitting next to someone and being quiet until the moment felt like the right moment, which was more effective than aggression and which Remy had observed Torres deploy with Kowalski twice in the last month alone.

She ate her food.

Torres ate her food.

The mess was loud around them, the crew at the peak of their end-of-deployment appetite, plates full and conversation going.

"You don't have to tell me anything," Torres said eventually.

"Good."

"I'm just. I want you to know that whatever it is, the crew is behind you. In the sense of." She paused. "He's a good person. That's all I'm saying."

Remy looked at her food.

"He is," she said. Quietly.

Torres nodded. She ate the rest of her food in a comfortable silence that felt, Remy thought, like the best version of what friendship could offer, which was presence without requirement.

She thought about Reid saying I'm not going to make this difficult for you.

She thought about six weeks.

She ate her food and went back to work and told herself she was fine.

She was mostly fine.

The parts that weren't fine, she kept to the list.

—

She stayed late in the operations room that night, past when she had any operational reason to. She was reviewing the deployment reports from the major fire, the full accounting, four hundred pages of documentation that would go to the regional office and become part of the permanent record.

Her name appeared on those pages frequently. The calls she'd made, the positions she'd assigned, the decisions that had been the right decisions and had produced the right outcomes. The record was clean. It said exactly what had happened and the professional judgment had been sound throughout.

The record did not say: and during all of this she was also tracking a specific person's location with a precision she didn't apply to equipment.

She thought about that. Not with guilt, exactly. More with the particular clarity that came at the end of a long thing. She'd managed the season. She'd kept the crew safe. She'd done the job.

She'd also let something in that she hadn't let in before, and she'd done that while doing the job, and both things were true at the same time, and she was still figuring out what to do with the both of them.

She finished the reports. She turned off the light.

In the corridor she stood for a moment and listened to the base. Quiet. The season's fires were behind them. The crew was sleeping or gone. Down the hall, the light under Reid's door.

She walked past it.

She was good at walking past things. It was one of her best skills.

She lay in her bunk and looked at the ceiling and thought about the parking lot that morning. The space between them that was exactly the right size. His hands in his pockets.

She thought about what came next. Six days left in the season.

She thought about enough.
