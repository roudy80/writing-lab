# Chapter 18

She didn't know what he knew until she understood that he knew it.

The tell was small. She nearly missed it.

They were three days out from the major fire, the base back in its operational rhythm, and she was doing a walk-through of the equipment cache with Vasquez when Reid appeared in the doorway with the camera and asked if he could do some close-up work on the reserve chutes. She nodded without stopping the walk-through. He came in and set up in the corner.

She was going through the cache inventory log with Vasquez when she heard the change in the room. Not a sound. An absence of sound, the specific quality of someone who has just read something and stopped moving.

She looked up.

Reid was at the far end of the cache, the camera lowered. He was looking at the reserve chute rack. Not at the chutes. At the deployment log clipped to the rack frame, the sheet that listed every time a reserve had been pulled for inspection and the reason for the pull.

She'd written those logs. The notation system was hers. The entry she'd added three years ago that said post-incident inspection, D. Kocher deployment, weather event, see supplemental was hers.

She watched him read it. She watched him understand what he was reading.

He didn't look up. He set the camera down on the equipment table carefully, the lens cap already on it, and stood there for a moment with his hands on the table and his back to her.

Then he picked up the camera and left.

She looked back at the inventory log. She kept her voice even.

"The third row needs a full tension check," she said to Vasquez. "Let's do it today."

—

She thought about it that evening. She thought about it with the precision she brought to things that needed to be thought through carefully, laying out the variables and working through the logic.

He knew about the Ochocos incident. The public record said there was a weather event. The incident report would say there was a crew in a hazardous position and a re-routing and a crew member down. What the incident report didn't say, because she'd never told anyone, was the four seconds.

He'd pieced it together through the log. Through a notation she'd made with no audience in mind, post-incident inspection, the kind of notation that meant something to someone who knew what they were looking at.

He knew there was more to the story. He didn't know what the more was.

She lay on her bunk and stared at the ceiling.

He was going to think about it. He was going to do what he did, which was go quiet and patient and process the information and then decide what to do with it. He'd found her father the same way, through public record, and he'd waited a week before bringing it up and when he'd brought it up he'd said it carefully, not as a weapon.

She thought about whether she was going to tell him. About the four seconds. About standing in a burning drainage in the Ochocos with a crew that needed her and being somewhere else for four seconds that had cost Danny a ravine and broken leg and three months of recovery.

She'd carried this for three years. She'd never told anyone because there was nothing useful in the telling. The call she'd made was the right call. Danny was alive and back and not blaming her. The outcome was the outcome.

She'd carried it because the carrying was accurate. Because a person who understood their own history carried the parts of it that were true.

She turned over in the bunk.

She thought about him at the equipment table with his back to her. The care of how he'd set the camera down. The way he'd left.

He was going to sit with it. He was going to process it the way he processed everything, carefully, with the full weight of his attention, and then he was going to do something with it.

She thought about what she wanted that something to be.

She didn't sleep for a long time.

—

He was at the briefing board at 0540 the next morning.

She came through on her way to PT and stopped when she saw him, not because it was unusual for him to be there early but because of what he was looking at. He was looking at the regional fire map, the one she updated every morning, the one that had the red circle around the northeastern drainage from six weeks ago. The circle was still there. The fire that had run from it was now contained and marked, a hatched zone in the fire history overlay.

He turned when he heard her.

She looked at him.

"You found the log," she said.

"Yes."

"Three years ago," she said. "Ochocos. There was a weather event on a jump and a crew member went down."

He held her eyes. "Yes."

"The incident report says what it says." She kept her voice level. "There's more to it."

He didn't move. Didn't reach for the notebook.

"Not now," she said. "I'm not ready to tell you now." She looked at the map and then back at him. "But I wanted you to know that I know you found it. And I'm not going to pretend I don't know."

He was quiet for a moment.

"All right," he said.

"All right," she said.

She went to PT.

She ran harder than usual, long enough that Kowalski, who was running alongside her, looked at her twice and didn't say anything, which was its own kind of statement. She ran until the thing that needed running out was mostly run out and then she walked the last quarter mile and cooled down and went to the briefing.

Reid was at his usual seat. Third from the left. Notebook on the table.

He looked at her the same way he always looked at her. She looked at him the same way she always looked at him.

The briefing was at 0700. She ran it.

The base did what it did.

—

She went back to the cache that evening alone, after the crew had dispersed, and found the log herself. Read the notation she'd written three years ago. Post-incident inspection, D. Kocher deployment, weather event, see supplemental.

She'd written it because she was thorough and because the equipment warranted inspection after any incident involving a crew member down. She'd written it in the clinical shorthand she used for everything official because official language was a container that held things at a distance.

She'd never thought about who might read it. She'd written it for the record, which was for institutional use, not for a journalist who knew how to read notation the way she read terrain.

She stood in the cache with the log in her hands.

She thought about the twelve days ahead of her and what they would be like with this between them, known but not spoken, her having told him she wasn't ready and him having heard it and gone quiet in the way that meant he'd heard it completely.

She thought about the fact that the twelve days were going to end regardless.

She put the log back on the rack and went to do the equipment inventory she'd originally come in here for. The counts were right. They were always right.

She left the cache and stood in the corridor for a moment. The base was quiet. Down the hall, through the wall, she could hear the very faint sound of Reid's monitor, footage being reviewed.

He was going to be patient. He would wait as long as she needed.

She'd known that for months.

She went to her room and sat on the edge of the bunk and thought about what it meant to know that someone would wait and to keep making them wait anyway.

She thought about the kind of courage that moved toward things.

She thought about twelve days.
