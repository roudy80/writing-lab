# Chapter 20

She told him the next morning.

Not what she'd decided, not a resolution, nothing clean. Just: she'd been thinking about what he'd said and she needed more time and she thought he should know she'd been thinking about it.

He said okay.

She went to run the morning briefing.

The week that followed was the strangest week of the season. The fires to the northeast were winding down, the October weather bringing moisture the landscape had been waiting for since June, the Haines Index dropping for the first time in two months. The crew was good, running the last deployments with the focused efficiency of people who could feel the finish line. She ran three briefings, two deployments, one training session. Everything operational. Everything on the metrics she tracked.

Underneath it, something she couldn't track.

She was aware of him differently now. Not the controlled awareness she'd developed over the summer, the deliberate attention she managed and redirected. Something less managed, looser in the joints, the way a structure felt when you'd removed something load-bearing and the walls were redistributing the weight but hadn't found their new positions yet.

She'd given him something in that room. Not willingly, not cleanly. He'd taken it apart in a way she hadn't been able to defend and she'd told him to leave and he'd left and she'd woken up the next morning and the thing was still out in the open where he'd left it.

She kept working.

She kept not touching what he'd said about her father.

—

The thing she kept turning over was this: she'd gone into the work to understand what her father had chosen. And she'd found out. Twelve seasons of jumping into fires and she'd found out so thoroughly that the choice had become automatic, had become who she was, had become the work itself and the crew and the base and the particular gold of Oregon light in the evenings.

She understood what he'd chosen. She'd been living inside it.

And she'd designed her life so that no one would have to grieve her the way her mother had grieved her father. That was the other thing. The thing that lived on the same foundation as the first thing.

She'd done both of those at once and called it settled.

Reid had pointed at the part that wasn't settled. The part that was still in a drainage in the Ochocos for four seconds. The part that was still a nine-year-old in a doorway.

He'd said: what are you going to do about the fact that your father is still in you.

She didn't have an answer yet. But she was working on one for the first time in three years instead of running the calculation that always came out the same.

She thought about that. She thought about it and then went back to work.

—

She ended it on a Tuesday.

Not because anything had changed. Not because she'd arrived at an answer. She ended it because October was five days away and Reid's embed ended with the season and she could feel the wall coming back up with the same automated efficiency it had always operated at, and she could feel herself letting it, and somewhere underneath that she was aware that the letting was a choice she was making and that she was making it anyway.

She found him in the evening after the briefing, at the east fence line where she sometimes went, which she'd known he knew about since he'd come to the fence line himself back in July. She'd thought about that when she decided to go there. She'd gone anyway.

He was there before her. Which was also familiar.

"Reid," she said.

He turned.

She looked at him in the evening light. Five months of him at this base, in her space, in her mornings and briefings and deployments and one night that she'd been trying not to think about clearly because thinking about it clearly told her things she couldn't afford to know right now.

"The season ends Friday," she said.

"Yes."

"Your embed ends with it."

"Yes."

She looked at the tree line. "I think we should close this out before then. While we still have the operational structure. While it's easier."

He was quiet for a moment.

"Easier for you," he said.

"Yes."

"Not a question," he said. "I know."

She looked at him. He was looking at her with that level attention and there was something in it she'd never seen before in five months of reading his expressions, which was a careful and specific sadness he wasn't trying to hide or manage.

She hadn't expected the not-hiding.

"You're doing it," he said.

"Yes."

"You know you're doing it."

"Yes." She held his eyes. "I know exactly what I'm doing. I'm choosing it anyway."

He looked at her for a long moment.

"All right," he said. Not capitulation. Not agreement. Just receipt.

"Reid." She stopped.

He waited.

She didn't have what she'd been about to say. She'd had something and it had dissolved when she'd looked at him not hiding the sadness.

"The documentary," she said finally. "It matters. What you've been making here. I want you to know that."

He held her eyes. "I know," he said. "Thank you."

They stood there for a moment in the cooling evening. The pines were moving in a light wind. Somewhere to the east, on the fires that were winding down, the smoke was going horizontal in the last of the day.

"Go get some sleep," she said. The same words as the kitchen in June and she heard them as she said them and she thought he heard them too because something moved in his expression.

She turned and went back inside.

She walked down the corridor to her room and went in and sat on the edge of the bunk and looked at the wall.

She'd ended it cleanly. She'd been in control of every sentence. She'd said exactly what she'd planned to say and she'd looked at him not hiding the sadness and she'd done it anyway and now it was done and the season was ending and in five days he'd be gone and the architecture would hold and she'd be all right.

She sat on the bunk.

She was all right.

She sat there for a long time not moving and told herself she was all right.

—

She lay on the bunk in the dark and ran through the logic.

October was coming. He was leaving. She'd been clear-eyed about both of those things when she'd stood at the fence line. The architecture had required it and she'd let the architecture do its work.

I know exactly what I'm doing. I'm choosing it anyway.

Both of those things had been true when she said them.

She turned over.

Outside, somewhere to the northeast, the fires that were winding down were still going, slowly, in the October wet. She lay in the dark and thought about the question Reid had asked her in the planning room: what are you going to do about the fact that your father is still in you.

She didn't have the answer. But she was aware of the question in a way she hadn't been before this season. Buried questions stayed the same shape. Aware questions changed.

She went to sleep before she could finish arguing with herself.
