# Chapter 21

Reid pulled back.

Not dramatically. He was not a dramatic person. He was at briefings and at PT and in the equipment bay and in the mess at the times he was always in those places, and he was professionally present in all of them, and he was personally gone in the specific way of someone who has understood an instruction and is following it carefully.

She'd asked for this. She'd gotten what she'd asked for.

She ran the base through the last week of the season with her full attention. Two more deployments, both clean, the fire weather finally breaking toward what it had promised since the June rain. The Haines Index in range she hadn't seen since spring. The crew in the good mood of people who had worked hard for five months and could see the end and were allowing themselves to anticipate the off-season.

She ran debriefs. She reviewed deployment reports. She went over equipment inventory with Vasquez and identified what needed to go out for service before spring. She did all of it.

In the briefings Reid sat in his usual chair and took his notes and watched the weather data and occasionally, on channel two during the last deployment, said something short and accurate that she used. She nodded at the radio when he did it. He didn't respond to the nod.

Danny said something on Wednesday.

She was at the vehicle bay checking out the last of the seasonal equipment when Danny came to stand next to her. He didn't say anything immediately, which was his way of saying: I have something and I'm deciding.

She kept working.

"He asked me about you again," Danny said. "Reid. Yesterday."

She kept her hands on the equipment. "What did he ask."

"Nothing specific. More like he was." Danny paused. "He was asking about the version of you I know. Not the lead jumper. The other version."

She looked at the equipment.

"What did you tell him?"

"That the other version is the same person," Danny said. "That you act like they're separate but they're not." He paused. "That anyone who'd been paying attention for five months would already know that."

She was quiet.

"He looked like someone who knows something and is deciding what to do with it," Danny said. "I've seen that look on you about a hundred times and I recognized it on him."

She received the description without responding to it.

"You could just tell me to mind my business," Danny said, without heat. "I'd understand."

"Mind your business, Danny."

"Sure." He didn't move. "He's a good person, Remy."

"I know."

"Okay," Danny said. He went back to his work.

She looked at the equipment bay. Through the open door the October sky was the sharp cold blue of the season's last chapter, the kind of sky that came after fire season, when the smoke was gone and the air was clean again and everything had its edges back.

She went back to work.

—

The last deployment of the season was Friday. A small fire, a hold-over from the northeast cluster, gone to the ground in the overnight dew and needing a mop-up crew to finish it. She ran it with four people and had it done in three hours and was back at base by noon.

The crew celebrated in the way they always celebrated, quietly, over a meal that Torres had organized. There was food and noise and some good-natured accounting of the season's worst moments, which was how the crew processed what they'd been through. She sat at the head of the table and listened and laughed when it was true and ate the food Torres had made.

Reid was at the far end of the table. He ate and listened and when something was funny he smiled without performing it and when the crew started telling the story of the wind reversal on the major fire he was quiet in the way of someone who had been there and didn't need to narrate it.

She looked at him once across the length of the table.

He was looking at her.

They both looked away.

She gave a short end-of-season address, nothing formal, just the words she always found for this moment: that she was proud of the work, that the season had been serious and they'd met it seriously, that she was glad they'd all come home. She meant all of it. She said it and meant it and sat back down and the meal continued.

After, she did the last equipment lockdown with Vasquez and the final season paperwork with Okafor and by early evening the base was winding down into its off-season configuration. The crew trickled out in ones and twos. Torres hugged her, which Torres did at the end of every season, and Kowalski shook her hand with unusual gravity, and Danny held her eyes for a moment before he left and she held his back.

She was the last one in the operations room when the base went quiet.

She sat at the desk for a moment in the silence of a base that was done with its season. Outside, through the window, the October evening was coming in, the last of the light going out of the sky in that particular way that said not just tonight but the season, the whole long burning summer, put to bed.

She thought about what she was going to do.

She thought about the tree line going dark and her father in a drainage and Reid saying what are you going to do about the fact that your father is still in you and the answer she'd been working toward for two weeks and hadn't found yet.

She sat there until the room was fully dark.

Then she went to find Danny.

—

She found Danny at the equipment cage, finishing the off-season lockdown inventory, the kind of work that could be done in the dark and didn't require conversation.

She stood next to him and picked up the next item on the list and they worked for a few minutes in the way they worked together, without needing to arrange it.

"I need to tell you something," she said.

He kept working.

"The Ochocos jump," she said. "Three years ago."

He was still.

"I froze," she said. "For four seconds. Before the call. I was somewhere else and I came back and I made the call but the call was four seconds stale and that's why you were on the wrong position when the run started."

She kept her hands on the equipment.

"I've been carrying that since it happened and I haven't told anyone and I don't think I was going to tell anyone until this season." She paused. "I'm telling you now because you deserve to know and because I think I should have told you three years ago."

Silence.

Danny put down the item in his hands. He turned to look at her.

She let him look.

He studied her face for a long moment with the directness he always had, the plain unhurried regard of someone who saw things and reported them accurately. She held it.

"You made the right call," he said.

"Yes."

"The outcome was the outcome."

"Yes."

He was quiet for a moment. "What were you in those four seconds?"

She looked at him. "My father's fire," she said. "I was nine years old in a doorway."

Danny nodded slowly. Not a nod of agreement. A nod of recognition, like a piece of information settling into a place that had been waiting for it.

"I always thought there was something," he said. "After. I could never find it in the record."

"There's nothing in the record."

"No." He picked up the next item. "You've been carrying it alone."

"Yes."

"That's," he said, and paused. "That's a hard three years, Remy."

She looked at the inventory sheet. "You went down a ravine."

"I went down a ravine," he said. "And you got the crew out and I came back the next season and we've done good work together." He turned to look at her again. "You don't owe me three years of carrying something alone."

She was quiet.

"Thank you for telling me," Danny said.

She nodded once. She couldn't say anything for a moment.

They went back to the inventory.

They finished it in the dark and closed the cage and walked back across the lot in the October cold and when they reached the junction where the paths split toward their respective rooms Danny stopped.

"He's a good person," Danny said. "In case you needed someone to tell you that one more time."

"I know," she said.

"Then stop making him work so hard for it," Danny said. Without heat.

He went to his room.

She stood in the cold for a moment and looked up at the sky, which was the thick black of a moonless October night, the stars dense and cold and clear in the way they got at the end of fire season when the smoke was finally gone.

She thought about Monday.

She went inside.
