# Chapter 22

She drove to Bend on Saturday.

Her mother met her at the door, which meant she'd been watching for the truck, which she did when she knew Remy was coming and sometimes when she didn't. She was small and gray now, her mother, and her hands when she hugged Remy were the hands of someone who gardened seriously, capable and slightly rough, and she smelled like the particular combination of coffee and the soap she'd always used that Remy associated with every kitchen she'd ever had.

"You look tired," her mother said.

"It was a long season."

"Come in."

The house was the house it had always been, modest and warm, full of the things her mother had accumulated over thirty years of living in it. Books organized in a way that had its own logic. The garden tools in the entryway because the garden was adjacent to everything. The photograph of her father on the sideboard in the living room, the one that had been there since she was nine years old, the one she'd been able to look at for approximately the last six of the thirty years since it was taken.

She looked at it now. He was in his jump gear, not fully suited, standing on a tarmac she didn't recognize with two other jumpers she'd never met. He was laughing at something. His hands were on his helmet, not putting it on, just resting there.

His hands.

She looked at them for a moment and felt something that wasn't exactly grief and wasn't exactly recognition but lived between them. They were her hands too. She had never noticed that before. She stood there long enough that her mother appeared in the doorway of the kitchen with the coffee already made, watching her.

Her mother made coffee and they sat at the kitchen table and her mother talked about the garden, the late season tomatoes, the deer that had gotten into the beans again, the neighbor who had planted a windbreak that was going to change the light on the south side. Remy listened and drank the coffee and was in the kitchen of the house she'd grown up in and let the season come down off her slowly the way adrenaline came down, gradually, in stages.

Then her mother said: "Tell me about the journalist."

She looked at her cup.

"Danny called me," her mother said. Not apologetic. Just explaining the available information.

"Of course he did."

"He was concerned. In the Danny way."

She put her cup down. The kitchen was the same kitchen it had always been, the same window over the sink looking out at the garden, the same marks on the door frame where her mother had tracked her height until she left for college. The same quality of light in the afternoon, coming through the old glass and spreading flat across the table.

"His name is Reid," she said. "He spent the season documenting the crew for a documentary. He leaves Monday."

"And?"

She looked at the table. "He asked me something I couldn't answer. About my father. About whether I've." She stopped.

Her mother waited.

"I built my life around not being the person someone else had to grieve," Remy said. "You know that. You've known it since I was young enough that I didn't know I was doing it."

"Yes," her mother said quietly.

"And he looked at that and said it's a choice. Not a fact about me. A choice I keep making." She paused. "He wasn't wrong."

Her mother looked at her for a long moment. She had her father's patience, her mother, the particular stillness of someone who would wait for the actual thing.

"I'm not a cautionary tale," her mother said.

Remy looked up.

"I know you've been carrying that," her mother said. "That you watched me after your father died and decided what you saw was what loving someone cost." She folded her hands on the table. "And I understand why you saw it that way. You were nine. What you saw was real."

Remy was quiet.

"But I want you to hear me say something," her mother said. "I would do it again. Every day of it, including the days after. Not because losing him was worth it. But because what we had before was worth it. And I had thirty-two years of being whole before I had to learn to be different." She paused. "You saw the after. You built your whole life around the after."

Outside, the garden was in its October state, the summer's growth going to seed, still beautiful in the way things were beautiful when they'd done what they were supposed to do.

"He's leaving Monday," Remy said.

"I know."

"I ended it before it was really anything. To keep it manageable."

Her mother looked at her with an expression Remy couldn't quite name. Not pity. Not disappointment. Something more like recognition.

"Your father would have told you something," her mother said. "He wouldn't have said it well because he was not always articulate about this kind of thing. But he would have said: the risk isn't the same as the loss. The risk is just the risk."

Remy sat with that.

She looked at the window and the garden and then at the sideboard in the other room where she could see the edge of the photograph from here, her father's shoulder, the tarmac.

She thought about his hands on the helmet. Not putting it on. Just resting there.

She thought about four seconds in a drainage. About a nine-year-old in a doorway. About three years of carrying something that was true and that she hadn't known how to put down because putting it down felt like it would require her to look at something else, something underneath the four seconds, which was the grief she'd never let herself have because she'd been too busy building a wall that kept the grief inside and everyone else outside and calling it protection.

She looked at her own hands on the table.

She thought about what she was going to do.

She drove back to Redmond in the early evening, the October light dying fast in the way it did now, the days short again. She drove with the window down and the cold air coming in and thought about Monday.

She thought about what her mother had said.

She thought about what Reid had said.

She thought about the wall. It was still there. She could feel it the way you felt something structural, not as a presence but as the shape of what it held in place. Fifteen years of architecture did not dissolve in one afternoon in her mother's kitchen.

But her mother had said: the risk isn't the same as the loss. And she had looked at her father's hands and recognized them.

She drove most of the way back without thinking about anything coherent. The cold air came through the window and the dark came down fast the way it did in October and somewhere past the Deschutes River she let herself think about what she actually wanted.

It took the rest of the drive.

She got back to the base and went inside and lay in her bunk and looked at the ceiling.

Monday was thirty-six hours away.

She was a person who made decisions. She'd always been a person who made decisions. The decision had to be made with information, not with fear, and tonight she had the information.

She let herself think about what she actually wanted.

That alone took a long time.
