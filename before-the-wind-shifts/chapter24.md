# Chapter 24

Everyone was safe by midday.

The mop-up took until three in the afternoon, the final accounting of a fire that had made its last argument and been answered. She ran the debrief at the LZ, standing in the October sun with the crew around her, the smoke column behind them thinning as the line held. She said what needed saying. The crew said what they said. It was over.

She loaded the crew for extraction and did the after-action documentation on the flight back and by the time they landed she had completed everything the job required.

At the base she supervised the gear breakdown, completed the seasonal paperwork with Okafor, and was thorough about both. She checked in with each crew member. Petrov, who was quiet in the particular way of someone who'd been close to something and was still processing it, she pulled aside.

"You did right when I called it," she said. "You moved."

He looked at her. "I didn't understand what was happening until I was already running."

"That's what the training is for," she said. "You moved when I said move. That's the whole thing."

He nodded. She let him go.

The crew dispersed. It was Sunday evening. The season was finished.

She was coming back from the vehicle bay when she saw Reid loading equipment cases into his rental truck. Not all of it, not the full breakdown, just the camera cases and the hard drives. The first load.

She stopped at the edge of the lot.

He hadn't seen her yet. He was lifting a case into the truck bed, careful with it the way he was careful with all his equipment, and he was alone and his back was to her and she stood there for a moment and just looked at him. The particular set of his shoulders. The way he moved. Five months of watching him and she knew this person's physical presence better than she knew most people's faces.

He turned and saw her.

They looked at each other across the lot.

She walked over.

He didn't say anything, waiting with his usual patience.

"I need to tell you something," she said. "About today."

"All right."

"I didn't freeze." She held his eyes. "On the Petrov call. I read it and I moved and I was completely present. No four seconds." She paused. "I wanted you to know that."

He looked at her quietly. "I watched," he said. "I know."

"I'm not telling you for confirmation." She looked at the equipment case. "I'm telling you because you asked the right question and I've been working on the answer and that's part of the answer."

He was still.

"The other part," she said, and stopped. She looked at the lot, at the base behind him, at the October sky going into its evening colors. She thought about her mother's kitchen and the photograph on the sideboard and her father's hands on the helmet. She thought about a wall built by a nine-year-old and maintained for fifteen years and what it meant to choose the wall versus what it meant to simply be the wall.

"The other part I'm still working on," she said. "But I wanted you to know there's an other part."

He looked at her for a long moment. The parking lot was quiet, just the two of them and the truck and the equipment cases.

"There's something I need to tell you too," he said.

"Okay."

He opened the passenger door of the truck and reached inside and came out with a hard drive, one of the small ones from the camera system. He held it out to her.

She looked at it.

"The cache log footage," he said. "The day I found the notation. This is the only copy. I never backed it up to the main archive." He paused. "I want you to have it."

She looked at the hard drive.

"That's not nothing," she said. "Professionally."

"No," he said. "It's not."

She looked at him. She thought about the footage of her hands on the map, the footage he'd flagged and never used without her permission. She thought about him saying whatever you want me to do with it.

She took the hard drive. It was lighter than it looked.

"You understand," she said, "that this doesn't change anything about the documentary. You can still document the incident. The public record. The outcome."

"I know."

"I'm not asking you to hide anything."

"I know." He held her eyes. "I'm giving you the four seconds. The rest was always going to be yours to decide."

She stood there in the lot with the hard drive in her hand and the sun going down behind the western ridge and thought about what it meant that she was standing here and that this was what he'd chosen to give her.

She thought about everything he could have done with what he'd found and everything he'd chosen not to do and the camera in the shale and Danny's leg and a crew that was intact and a season that had been what it had been and a man who was leaving tomorrow.

"Reid," she said.

He waited.

"You're leaving tomorrow."

"Yes."

"Where."

"Portland, Monday night. Then back to New York by Thursday. The edit is going to take months." He paused. "I have an apartment there."

She looked at the hard drive in her hand. "I ended it," she said. "Last week."

"I know."

"I was scared," she said. "Not of you. Of." She paused. "Of being right about how much it mattered."

He was very still.

"I've been thinking about what you said," she said. "About my father. About the choice I've been making." She looked at him. "You were right. It's a choice. I'm allowed to make a different one."

He held her eyes and didn't say anything, which was the right thing, because she wasn't done.

"I don't know what different looks like," she said. "I'm not offering you something specific. I'm telling you I'm working on it and that you should know that before you leave."

He was quiet for a long moment.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

"Yes."

"In the planning room," he said. "When I asked you the question about your father being in you. You told me to leave."

"Yes."

"But you were thinking about it the whole time."

"Yes."

He looked at her steadily. "You're telling me now," he said. "Before I leave. Not after."

She looked at him. "Yes."

"That's different," he said. "From your usual process."

She felt the edge of something that was almost a smile. "Yes," she said. "It is."

He looked at her with the full weight of his attention, the attention she'd spent five months learning to hold without flinching, and she held it now and didn't redirect it or file it or look away.

"Remy," he said, and stopped.

"Don't decide anything tonight," she said. "That's not what I'm asking. I'm just. I'm telling you where I am. Because you asked good questions and they mattered and you deserve to know they mattered."

He was quiet.

"Okay," he said.

She handed the hard drive back to him.

He looked at it.

"Keep it," she said. "I don't need the footage. I know what happened." She held his eyes. "Use it or don't. I trust your judgment."

He took the hard drive. Something moved in his expression and settled.

She left him in the parking lot and went back inside.

She stood in the empty briefing room for a moment in the dark, not turning the light on, the familiar shapes of the maps and the boards and the chairs. The room had been hers for six seasons. She knew every surface.

She'd said a true thing to a person who'd been careful with true things the entire time he'd known her.

She didn't know what happened next. That was all right.

She turned the light on and sat down at the table and started writing up the final after-action report for the season.

Outside, the October night was coming in.
