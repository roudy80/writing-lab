# Chapter 25

She went to him at ten o'clock.

Not because she'd decided, lying in her bunk in the small hours, that this was the logical conclusion of the available evidence. She'd been doing that her whole life. Deciding things through logic, through calculation, through the careful sequential process of someone who trusted evidence over impulse.

She went because she wanted to and she'd finally let herself know that she wanted to.

The light was on under his door. She knocked.

He opened it.

He looked at her without surprise, which told her he hadn't slept either. He stepped back from the door.

She came in.

His room was mostly packed. The hard cases were stacked near the door, the equipment broken down, the monitor gone into its case. The folding table was still there and the chair and his bag on the bed. The room looked like what it was, a space being prepared for departure, the residue of five months compressed into what fit in a truck.

She stood in the middle of it.

"I'm going to say one thing," she said. "And then you say what you want to say."

He waited.

"The season I got here," she said. "My second season. Before I knew the crew, before I knew this base or these drainages or any of it. I was in a jump over a terrain I hadn't worked before and I read the wind wrong and I came down on the wrong side of a ridge and I had to walk two miles back to the LZ in the dark with a sprained ankle and a pack that weighed forty pounds."

He was watching her.

"I was furious at myself the entire way," she said. "Because I'd read it wrong and I knew I'd read it wrong and I was paying for it in the dark. And somewhere in the second mile I stopped being furious and I was just." She paused. "I was just walking. The terrain was the terrain and the ankle hurt and I was going to get home and I was going to be more careful about that ridge configuration next time." She looked at him. "And I thought: this is the job. You read it wrong sometimes. You pay for it honestly. You get home and you try again."

She stopped.

"I've been reading myself wrong for a long time," she said. "About what I need and what I can manage and what the actual cost is of the choices I've been making." She looked at him steadily. "I'm not promising anything specific. I don't know yet what different looks like in practice. I know it involves Portland and New York and conversations I don't know how to have yet." She paused. "But I'm not going to keep walking in the wrong direction because I decided at nine years old what direction was safe."

The room was quiet.

He crossed the distance between them and his hands came up to her face, both of them, and he looked at her for a moment from close range with that level undefended attention.

"I've been waiting five months for you to get here," he said.

"I know," she said. "I'm sorry it took this long."

"Don't apologize," he said. "This is exactly when you got here."

She looked at him. She thought about what it was to be seen accurately. Not the version she'd designed to be seen. Seen.

"Maren," he said.

The name went through her like a current. Her real name, her whole name, in his voice, and she understood that he'd been saving it the way she'd known it was being saved, and the sound of it was the thing she hadn't known she was waiting for.

She kissed him.

And it was not the same as the first time, which had been decision, evidence reviewed and acted on. This was something older and simpler. This was just her and this was just him and the packed room and the October dark outside and the end of the season and the beginning of whatever this was.

He held her like someone who had been patient for a long time and was now allowed to stop being patient, and she let herself be held the way she never let herself be held, without managing it, without keeping one part of herself outside of it and watching.

She was completely there.

All of her.

Later they sat on the edge of his bed, her head against his shoulder, both of them looking at the stacked cases and the packed room.

"Portland Monday night," she said.

"Yes."

"When does the edit start?"

"I have three months of footage. It'll take most of the winter." He paused. "I'll be in New York for it."

She thought about New York. She'd been twice, both times for training conferences, and she'd found it loud and fast and entirely unlike anywhere she'd ever felt comfortable. She thought about this with interest rather than resistance.

"I have four months off-season," she said. "Starting now."

He was quiet for a moment. "What are you saying."

"I'm saying I have four months." She looked at the window, the dark outside. "I'm not saying move to New York. I'm saying I have four months and some of it I usually spend doing nothing very productive and New York is a place where things happen."

She felt him exhale.

"Remy," he said.

"Maren," she said. "When it's just us."

He was quiet for a moment. She felt his hand tighten slightly around hers.

"Maren," he said, and the name sounded different when he said it with intention, when she'd asked him to, when it was a door she'd opened rather than one he'd unlocked. "I would very much like to show you New York."

She thought about the wall and the architecture and the nine-year-old's decision and all the careful load-bearing construction of fifteen years.

She thought about what it meant to choose rather than to simply be.

She thought about her father's hands resting on the helmet, not putting it on, just there.

"Okay," she said.

She meant it completely.

---

He was awake before her in the morning, which did not surprise her.

She could tell by his breathing, already different from the breathing of sleep. She lay still for a moment and looked at the ceiling of his room with the equipment cases stacked near the door and the October dark at the window and the quality of a morning with something in it.

"You're awake," she said.

"Yes."

She turned to look at him. He was looking at the ceiling too, and when she turned he turned, and they looked at each other in the gray pre-light of early October at a base in central Oregon that was neither of their homes and that had been, for five months, the whole world.

"Are you all right?" he said.

She thought about the question. She was aware of the wall, the architecture, the fifteen years of carefully maintained structure. Still there. Not gone. She hadn't expected gone. She'd expected what she had, which was the same wall and the same architecture and a door she'd found that she hadn't known was there.

She could choose the door.

"Yes," she said. "Are you?"

"Yes," he said. And then: "I'm aware that you're going to go back to your room before the base wakes up."

"Yes."

"And that you're going to run the morning briefing in the way you always run the morning briefing."

"Yes."

"And that tomorrow I'm going to load a truck and drive to Portland."

"Yes."

He was quiet. She was quiet.

"I want to ask you something," he said. "And you don't have to answer it now."

"Okay."

"Is this the part where we figure out what this is?" he said. "Or is this still the part where you don't know yet?"

She thought about it honestly. She thought about New York and November and addresses she'd have to look up. She thought about a documentary that would take all winter and a season that was over and the particular way her mother had said I would do it again.

"I think," she said carefully, "that this is the part where I know what I want and I'm learning to stay in the room with knowing it."

He was quiet for a moment.

"That's enough," he said. "That's more than enough."

She looked at him.

"I've been patient for five months," he said. "I can be patient for however long this takes."

She thought about patience. She thought about what it meant that he'd been patient without making her responsible for the patience, without using it as a thing she owed him. Just patient the way he'd been everything, present and unhurried and not requiring the situation to be different than it was.

"I'm going to try not to make you be patient forever," she said.

"I know," he said.

She got up.

She dressed in the dark the way she'd done before, quiet and efficient, and at the door she turned back and looked at him.

He was still looking at her.

"I'll see you at briefing," she said.

This time it was different.

She walked down the corridor to her room in the pre-dawn quiet and stood in the middle of it for a moment before she needed to start getting ready for the day. The room was hers, organized the way she organized things, everything in its place. She looked at it.

She thought about what it was to know what you wanted.

She went to the window and stood there and watched the October sky begin its slow work of becoming morning, the black going first to deep blue and then to the particular gray that came just before the light. The eastern ridge was invisible and then a shape and then a line and then itself.

She watched it happen.

Ready.

That.

And something else now too, something that didn't have her usual name for it. Something that had arrived in a season and couldn't be unlearned.

She watched the ridge line find itself in the morning light.

Then she went to start the day.
