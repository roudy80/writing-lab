# Chapter 26

He left on a Monday.

The truck was loaded by eight in the morning, the equipment cases in the bed, his bag in the passenger seat, the things that had constituted five months of his life at this base broken back down to the elements they'd started as. She stood in the parking lot and watched him do the last of it.

The morning was clear and cold, the first real cold of October, the kind that suggested winter had made up its mind. Her breath was visible. The eastern ridge line was sharp against a sky that was the particular blue of high-pressure systems, clean and borderless.

He closed the tailgate.

He looked at her across the lot.

She crossed it.

They stood close enough that she could see the details she'd been memorizing for five months without knowing she was memorizing them. The line of his jaw. The particular way his expression settled when he was fully present in a moment. The steadiness she'd spent the whole summer reading and had finally, at the end, learned to simply be in rather than analyze.

"I'll call when I'm in Portland," he said.

"All right."

"The edit takes as long as it takes. But I'll know the shape of it by December."

"Okay."

He looked at her. "You could just come in November."

She considered this. She thought about November in New York, which she imagined was cold and loud and nothing like central Oregon, and which she was apparently willing to find out about.

"Send me an address," she said.

Something in his face settled into a warmth she recognized. He'd always had it, that warmth, from the beginning, but she'd been spending the summer trying not to look directly at it.

She could look at it now.

"There's something I need to ask you," he said.

"All right."

"Danny," he said. "The three years you've been carrying the four seconds. I think you should tell him."

She was quiet.

"Not for the documentary," he said. "Nothing to do with me or the film. Just." He paused. "He's been back two seasons. He came back. I think he knows there's something you've been carrying and I think he's been waiting for you to put it down."

She thought about Danny in the equipment bay running his thumb along a connector the way she'd taught him. Danny saying that's the problem in the voice of someone who had been waiting a long time to say it. Danny watching her at the end-of-season meal with a look she'd filed under things she'd work out later.

"I know," she said.

"Just so you know I think that."

"I know." She looked at him. "I was going to."

He nodded once.

She reached into her jacket pocket. She'd been carrying the thing since Sunday morning when she'd found it in the gear room during the last equipment lockdown, a carabiner she'd pulled for replacement and then held in her hand for a moment longer than she needed to before putting it in her pocket.

She held it out to him.

He looked at it. A simple carabiner, standard issue, the gate polished smooth from years of use, the metal of it the particular gray of equipment that has been handled and trusted and maintained.

"Twice is how you check," she said. "Three times is how you know."

He looked up from the carabiner to her face.

She held his eyes. "You know," she said.

He took it from her hand. He looked at it for a moment and then he put it in his jacket pocket and when he looked back at her his expression was the thing she had no category for and had stopped needing one.

He pulled her in.

She let herself be held in the parking lot in the October cold with her face against the front of his jacket and the smell of him around her and the carabiner in his pocket between them. She let it be fully what it was. Not managed, not observed from one remove, not analyzed for what it signified.

She felt the specific weight of a decision made in full knowledge.

He pressed his mouth to her hair and said something quiet she didn't catch and didn't ask him to repeat because the meaning was clear without the words.

She pulled back and looked at him.

"Drive safe," she said.

"I will."

She stepped back. He walked around to the driver's side and got in and she stood in the lot and watched the truck move toward the gate.

At the gate he stopped and looked back.

She raised a hand.

He drove through the gate and down the county road and she watched the truck until the distance took it, until it was a shape and then a color and then the road empty and the dust of it settling.

She stood there for a moment.

The October morning was cold and clean and very still. The base was behind her, empty now of the season, the crew gone, the fires out, the particular silence of a place between what it was and what it would become again in spring. The eastern ridge line caught the early light, the pines dark against it, the sky above them the kind of blue that looked like it would last forever even though she knew better.

She thought about her father's hands.

She thought about a nine-year-old who had made a decision in a doorway and a woman who had carried it for fifteen years and a season in which someone patient and careful had stood on the corridor side of a line and waited without requiring her to cross it.

She thought about November.

She turned and went back inside.

The base had work to do before spring. Equipment to process, reports to file, the off-season maintenance that kept the place ready for the season it didn't know yet it was going to have. She had four months and a phone that would ring tonight from Portland and an address she'd be given at some point that was probably on a street she'd have to look up.

She made coffee in the mess kitchen, her coffee from her pot, and stood at the window that looked out at the training slope.

The slope was empty. The grass was gold and dry, the same color it had been all summer, and in a week or two the first rains would come and it would green up and by spring it would be ready again. The cycle was the cycle. It had always been the cycle.

She drank her coffee and looked at the slope and thought about everything that was true.

She was Remy. She was Maren. The work was hers. The choice was hers. And one person who had been careful with true things knew her whole name and was driving toward Portland with a carabiner in his pocket.

The morning light came through the window and spread flat across the table.

She finished her coffee. She rinsed the mug and set it on the rack and went to start the work.

Ready.

Still that.

But different now in the way that mattered.
