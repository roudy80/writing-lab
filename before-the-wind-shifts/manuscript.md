# Before the Wind Shifts

BEFORE THE WIND SHIFTS

A Novel

Chapter 1

The jump went textbook until the wind didn't.

Remy read it coming. Three seconds, maybe four. The smoke column tilted and the grass in the clearing below went from bending south to standing straight up, that half-second of stillness before everything reorganized itself. She felt it in her chest first. Not fear. Something older than fear, a pressure change she'd learned to trust before she'd learned to name it. She keyed her radio.

"Shifting. Move east. Now."

No one questioned her. That was the thing about a crew that had jumped together long enough. They learned to move on tone, not explanation. By the time the wind hit hard out of the northwest, they were already in the black, standing on ground that had already burned, nothing left to feed a fire. Safe.

She pulled her gloves off and looked at her hands. Steady. They were always steady.

Below, in the clearing, the grass flattened and came back up. The fire made its run to where they'd been standing forty seconds ago and found nothing but churned earth and boot prints.

"Good call, Remy." Vasquez, pulling his face shield up. Already moving to the next task.

She didn't answer. The call had been obvious to anyone paying attention, and she'd learned a long time ago that responding to compliments on things that were simply your job created a conversational debt she didn't want to carry.

Danny was coming up the slope from the eastern anchor, pack straps cinched tight, moving well. His left knee had been the question mark coming into the season. She'd watched him all spring without making it obvious she was watching, the way she tracked all of them. The left knee, the way Petrov held his left shoulder slightly high when he was fatigued, the particular tells that told her what the official reports didn't. She knew her crew the way she knew terrain. Not through documents but through attention. He caught her eye and nodded once.

She nodded back.

The debrief happened at the edge of the LZ, everyone still in gear, sweat drying in the afternoon air. She ran through it the way she always did. Not praise, not criticism, just the facts of what happened, what worked, what she'd adjust. Fourteen minutes from the jump to containment on the north flank, twenty-two minutes total before they handed it off to the ground crew. She identified two sequencing decisions she wanted to talk through before the next deployment. The crew listened. When she was done, they moved without being dismissed, breaking into the work of packing out with the efficient quiet of people who'd done this enough that the process was automatic.

She watched them for a moment before she started breaking down her own gear. This was the part of the job most people on the outside didn't understand. Not the fire. Not the jump. The work that came before and after, the ten hours of maintenance and preparation and debrief for every one hour of active suppression. The fire was the occasion. The job was everything else.

"Heard we're getting a journalist."

Kowalski, talking to no one in particular while coiling line. He had a gift for dropping information into the middle of a conversation that hadn't started yet.

Remy kept her hands on her gear. "Heard that too."

"Documentary. Whole season."

"I know what it is, Kowalski."

"Just saying. Could be interesting."

She looked at him. That was enough. He went back to his line.

It wasn't interesting. She knew exactly what it was. Someone from outside spending a season trying to turn what they did into a story other people could feel something about. She'd read the paperwork. Long-form documentary project, a journalist named Reid Matthews with two previous embeds, one with a Coast Guard unit in Alaska, one with a wildland engine crew in New Mexico. Vetted. Cleared. Approved by people above her pay grade.

She'd looked up his work. The Alaska piece was good. He understood how to stay out of the way without disappearing from the story. The New Mexico piece was better. He'd spent four months with an engine crew and the final cut ran ninety minutes and the thing that made it worth watching wasn't the fire footage. It was the people. He'd gotten them to be real on camera, which was harder than it sounded and which meant he was either patient or perceptive or both.

Neither quality made her want him here. But she knew how to manage it.

Fine. She'd managed worse.

---

The base at Redmond ran on rhythm. Mornings were PT, equipment checks, weather briefings. Afternoons were training or standby depending on the fire weather index. Evenings were whatever the day left you with, which in May was usually paperwork and the Oregon light that came in through the bay doors around four o'clock, gold and low, making everything look slightly distant. Like something you were remembering instead of seeing.

She liked evenings. The crew dispersed to their routines. She could work through the next day's logistics without anyone needing anything from her. The base had a particular quality at that hour, the urgency of the day gone out of it, just the hum of equipment and the sound of the wind in the pines that ran along the eastern fence line.

She was going through gear inspection in bay three when she heard the truck.

Different engine note than the crew vehicles. She didn't look up from the carabiner she was checking. Running her thumb along the gate, feeling for the stress fracture she suspected. Twice to be sure. She set it in the discard pile and reached for the next one.

Boots on concrete. Careful gait, not tentative. Someone paying attention to where they were walking.

"Maren Reyes?"

She looked up.

He was standing at the bay entrance. First thing she registered wasn't him. It was the camera, a professional rig slung over one shoulder, the kind of setup that took real knowledge to use. The lens cover was on. The camera was hanging loose at his side, not raised. He'd come into her workspace the way you came into anyone's workspace. Gear stowed, nothing pointed.

She set down the carabiner.

"Remy," she said.

He absorbed this without apology. "Reid Matthews. I think your base commander is expecting me."

"He is." She reached for the next carabiner. "You're early."

"Flight got in ahead of schedule." A pause. "I can come back."

She looked at him more carefully then. He was taller than she'd expected, quieter in his bearing than his résumé had suggested. The camera hung from one hand naturally, not like equipment he was managing but like something he forgot he was carrying. She'd looked at his photo online, had formed some image of how the presence would translate in person. The person was different. Not more or less than she'd expected. Just different in ways she'd need to recalibrate for.

Most people, in his position, would have pushed. Found a way to make themselves present, to establish that they'd arrived, that the timeline could begin. He'd offered to disappear instead. She didn't know yet whether that was instinct or something he'd learned made people trust him.

Either way it was the right answer.

"Captain Okafor's office is second door off the main corridor." She picked up the carabiner. "I'll find you when I'm done."

He nodded once and left without lingering. She heard his footsteps fade without a single hesitation. He already knew the layout. She went back to the carabiner.

Clean metal. No fracture. She moved it to the keep pile.

She worked through the rest of the rack methodically. Found two more compromised gates, one with a crack she could feel before she could see it, one with a locking mechanism that was stiff in a way that would become a problem under cold conditions. She tagged them both and updated the discard log and started on the next row.

Outside, the light was going. She could tell by the way the shadows in the bay lengthened and then disappeared as the angle shifted, the gold going out of everything and leaving the flat gray of early evening.

---

She heard later, from Vasquez, that Reid Matthews had spent forty minutes with Okafor going through protocols they'd already agreed to. Where he could go, where he couldn't, the chain of command during deployments, what required additional consent. No pushback on any of it.

"He asked good questions," Vasquez said. The tone of someone filing a report.

Remy was reading the weather briefing. "Did he."

"The kind where you can tell he already knows half the answer."

She turned a page and didn't say anything.

Outside, the last of the evening was going gold above the ridgeline. Down the hall, a radio played low enough that she could hear the shape of music without the notes. The base settled into its nighttime sounds, the particular sequence she knew so well she stopped hearing it consciously: the gear room refrigeration cycling on, the last vehicle in the lot cutting its engine, the wind finding the loose panel on the north side of the equipment bay and lifting it once, twice, then letting it rest.

The numbers in the briefing suggested a dry June. The Haines Index was trending. She turned a page and made a note in the margin.

The ridge to the east was catching the last of the light and holding it.

She reached the last page and set the briefing down. Looked at the tree line going dark at the edge of the property. Then she got up to start the next thing.

By the time it was full dark she had finished three more items on the list she kept in her head and had added two more. Somewhere in the base, in whatever bunk room they'd given him, a journalist was either sleeping or not sleeping. She didn't think about which.

She thought about the dry June. The ridge lines that would matter when it stopped raining. The weather the season had promised and would eventually deliver.

She thought about work. That was the thing about work. It was always there, always enough, and it never asked anything back.

Chapter 2

He showed up the next morning at 0545.

Not because anyone told him to. PT started at six, and she found him already in the equipment bay when she came through on her way to the track. Sitting on a crate with a small notebook, not the camera, writing something in the low light with the focused quiet of a man who'd made a habit of being where things happened before things happened.

She stopped in the doorway.

He looked up. Didn't scramble to explain himself. Just looked up the way you look up when someone comes into a room you're already comfortable in.

"Morning," he said.

"You're not cleared for unsupervised access to the bay."

"I know." He held up the notebook. "I'm in the corridor. Technically."

She looked at the floor. The line between the bay and the corridor was a painted stripe, yellow, worn thin in the middle from years of boots. He was on the corridor side by about eight inches.

She looked back at him.

"Okafor's protocol sheet says equipment areas require escort," she said.

"It does." He didn't move. "I'm not in the equipment area."

She could have argued the eight inches. She didn't, because he was right and they both knew it, and arguing a technicality she'd lose was a worse position than acknowledging the boundary was working as designed.

"PT in fifteen minutes," she said. "You can observe. Stay out of the drill patterns."

"Understood."

She went to get changed.

---

He ran.

She hadn't expected that. She'd expected him to stand on the edge of the track with the notebook or the camera, documenting. Instead he ran with the crew. Not at the front and not at the back, settling into the middle distance at a pace that was sustainable without being impressive. No performance. No attempt to keep up with the faster runners. Just present, at a pace he could hold.

He wasn't slow. She clocked that. He had the long-distance build of someone who ran regularly, and his form was economical in the way that came from years of practice rather than recent effort. He just wasn't making anything of it.

Vasquez noticed. She could tell by the way Vasquez didn't say anything about it.

The drills afterward were rope work and pack carries. Reid stepped back from those, found a wall to lean against, got the notebook out. She caught him writing twice and both times he was looking at the crew, not at her. On the third pass she looked for him and he was watching Petrov work through the pack carry sequence, and the angle of his attention was specific in the way she recognized. He was watching what Petrov was doing wrong. She'd been watching the same thing for three weeks.

She went back to the drill and didn't think about what it meant that they were looking at the same thing.

After cleanup he found her at the briefing board in the main corridor, where she was marking the updated fire weather index. The Haines Index had bumped overnight, the kind of change that didn't mean anything yet but that she tracked consistently, so that when it did mean something she'd have the context.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

She kept marking numbers. "You can ask."

"The Haines Index. You updated it before the morning briefing."

"I did."

"The briefing covers it at seven."

"I know."

A beat.

"Do you always get here before the briefing covers things?" he asked.

She set down the marker. Turned to look at him properly for the first time that morning. He was holding the notebook loose at his side, not open. Not writing.

"I get here before the briefing covers things," she said, "because by the time something makes it onto a briefing it's already happened. I'd rather know while I can still do something about it."

He nodded slowly. Filing it without writing it down.

"Same reason you were in the corridor at five-thirty," she said.

Something shifted in his expression. Not caught. More like recognized.

"Same reason," he agreed.

She picked up the marker and went back to the board. He stayed a moment, then moved off down the corridor. She heard his footsteps stop. He'd doubled back to the briefing room, where the day's weather maps were already tacked up.

She went back to her numbers and didn't look after him.

---

The crew had opinions. Kowalski thought the documentary was a bad idea but was entertained enough to suspend judgment, which for Kowalski was essentially a standing ovation. Torres thought Reid was attractive, which she announced to no one specific and everyone in earshot, twice. Vasquez withheld comment, which from Vasquez was a favorable review. Danny had talked to him at breakfast for twenty minutes and come back looking like he'd found something interesting. She didn't ask what.

She made a note of all of it without making it obvious she was making a note of it. The crew's relationship to Reid would matter when things got difficult, and things always got difficult. A crew that resented an outside presence got subtle about it in ways that created operational complications. A crew that was too accommodating got careless about the boundaries that kept everyone safe. She needed to watch how it settled.

So far it was settling well. She didn't entirely trust that.

That evening she was in the planning room going through deployment maps when Reid knocked on the open door.

"Ten minutes?" he said. "I want to walk through how you'd like me to handle jump notifications. Not what the protocol says. What you actually want."

She looked at him.

"When a call comes in," she said, "you have ninety seconds to get your gear and be at the truck. Not ninety seconds after you finish what you're doing. Ninety seconds from when I say go."

"Understood."

"If you're not at the truck, we don't wait."

"Understood."

"If I tell you to hold position during a deployment, you hold it. I don't want to be thinking about where you are when I'm thinking about where the fire is."

"That's fair."

She studied him. He took it the way she'd given it. Straight, no hedging, no attempt to find the room in the terms. He was just accepting them.

"Questions?" she said.

"One." He leaned against the door frame, not coming in. "If I'm holding position and I see something you might want to know. What's the protocol?"

She thought about it. It was a good question. A journalist who'd spent four months with an engine crew had picked up enough to know that information flow on a fire was not linear.

"Radio me," she said. "Channel two. Keep it short. Don't editorialize."

"Copy." He straightened. "Anything else?"

"Not tonight."

He nodded and left. She watched through the open door as he paused at the bulletin board in the corridor. The weather printout she'd pinned that afternoon. He stood there about ten seconds. Then he went.

She went back to the deployment maps.

Outside, the May dark had come in cool and sharp, still cold at night this time of year, the pines moving in the wind she could hear through the single window. She worked until eleven. Turned the light off and stood in the dark for a moment before leaving. Just to let her eyes adjust. The maps were shapes now. Topography without detail.

She thought about what he'd said. Not what the protocol says. What you actually want.

She tried to remember the last time someone had asked her that.

She left the room and went down the hall and didn't find an answer.

---

She found him at the equipment inventory the next afternoon, not writing, not filming. Just working. Torres had been pulling secondary harnesses for a re-inspection and somehow Reid had ended up on the other end of the process, handing equipment down from the upper rack while Torres called the tag numbers. He wasn't doing it to be helpful in a visible way. He was doing it because it was the next logical step in a process that needed doing and he was there.

Torres didn't thank him. She just kept calling numbers.

Remy watched from the doorway for a moment before moving on. There was something she needed to understand about him and she hadn't found it yet. She understood his professional instincts. She understood his patience. She didn't yet understand what he was actually after, what the real story was that he'd come here to tell, because nobody came to a smokejumper base for a full season on a documentary project without a specific thing they were trying to find.

His previous embeds had been about institutions under pressure. The Coast Guard unit had been navigating a budget crisis. The engine crew in New Mexico had been dealing with the aftermath of a fatality. He didn't make films about competence. He made films about people navigating something difficult.

She wondered what he thought she was navigating.

She moved on before he could look up and catch her in the doorway.

Chapter 3

He was good at disappearing.

That was the thing she hadn't expected. She'd assumed a documentarian would be present the way journalists were present. Forward, visible, always angling for the next angle. Instead Reid Matthews moved through the base the way weather moved through a valley: you'd look up and he'd be there, and you'd look again and he'd be somewhere else, and the space he'd occupied wouldn't feel emptied because he hadn't pushed into it to begin with.

It should have made her comfortable. It didn't.

The first week settled into patterns. He was at PT before six, running at the same sustainable pace, notebook afterward. He ate in the mess with the crew and was quiet at meals in a way that let conversation happen around him without shaping it. She caught him talking to Torres once about Oregon fire history, something Torres had opinions about, and he was asking questions the way someone asks questions when they already know enough to ask the right ones. Torres talked for twenty minutes. Reid wrote half a page.

She adjusted her read of him. Not upward, not downward. Sideways. He was more careful than she'd assumed and less performative, which meant she needed different variables to track. The variable she'd been running, how much management will this require, kept returning a lower number than she expected, and a lower number was not necessarily good news. It just meant she didn't know yet what the actual variable was.

He caught her watching him on the third day. She was in the back of the bay and he was in the corridor with his notebook and she'd been watching him for about forty seconds before he looked up. Not guilty. Just looked up, met her eyes, held it a beat, went back to writing.

She looked back at her equipment.

On the fifth day she caught him watching her.

She was running Petrov through anchor protocol on the south training slope. Petrov had solid instincts but rushed the setup, always convinced he was taking too long when he was taking exactly long enough. She'd been working on it for two weeks. The problem wasn't technique, it was trust: Petrov didn't yet trust that being thorough was faster than being fast, that a well-placed anchor at four minutes was worth more than a questionable one at two. She was walking him through it the third time, her voice flat and patient, when she felt the particular quality of attention from a direction she wasn't facing.

She didn't turn. She kept her eyes on Petrov and the anchor point and finished the sequence.

When she turned around Reid was fifty yards up the slope, notebook at his side, not writing. He didn't look away when she turned. He met her eyes the same way he had in the bay. Steady, not aggressive, like being seen was what was happening and there was no reason to make it a thing.

She turned back to Petrov. "Again," she said. "From the top."

Petrov ran it again. This time he slowed down in the middle section, where he always rushed, and she could see him fighting the impulse to speed up, working against his own instinct. He finished four seconds slower than his fastest time and the anchor was clean.

"Better," she said.

She didn't look back at the slope.

---

That evening she was at the sink in the small kitchen off the bay, washing grease off her hands after the cache inventory, when she heard him come in and stop.

"I've been watching you with Petrov," he said.

She kept washing. "I know."

"The anchor sequence. Three times, every time."

"Twice is how you check. Three times is how you know."

She heard him lean against the counter. The old floor registering the shift in weight.

"Does he know yet?" Reid asked. "That three times is the point?"

She dried her hands and turned around. He was leaning against the counter with the notebook closed, watching her with that level attention she was starting to recognize as just the way his face worked.

"He'll know when he knows," she said. "Explaining it before that doesn't help."

"You're waiting for him to feel the difference."

"I'm waiting for him to trust the difference." She hung the towel back on its hook. "Those aren't the same thing."

Reid opened the notebook and wrote something. She watched him without trying to read it.

"What are you writing?" She hadn't planned to ask.

He looked up. "That there's a distinction you make between trust and knowledge. And that you teach to the trust, not the knowledge."

She looked at him for a moment.

"That's accurate," she said, and left.

---

She thought about it that night. Not about him. About the distinction. She'd been running that sequence three times since her third season without naming why. Twice was information. Three times was the thing that got written into muscle, into reflex, into the place where a person's hands knew what to do before their brain had caught up. The kind of knowing that didn't require decision-making under pressure because it had already been decided, over and over, until it was simply what the body did.

She'd known it without ever putting words to it.

He'd named it in four words.

She didn't know what to do with that. She put it away and went to sleep.

---

The second week, he got the camera out.

Equipment first, not people. The way line was coiled, the geometry of a drop zone before a training jump, the specific choreography of gear staging before a deployment. He moved through the space with it the way he moved with the notebook. Patient, not asking for anything, not positioning anyone.

The crew adapted faster than she expected. Torres started talking directly into the camera whenever she had a strong opinion, which was frequently and which Torres apparently considered a service she was providing. Kowalski ignored it with the studied ignorance of someone who had decided to be natural and was working at it. Danny moved around it the way you moved around someone at a party you were comfortable not talking to. Easy, no avoidance and no performance.

She watched the crew adjust to him the way she watched for everything. Looking for friction, for resentment, for the subtle territorial behaviors that emerged when an outside presence stayed too long. She didn't find much. He was careful about it, she could see that. He never asked to be included. He was just present and useful when he could be useful and out of the way when he needed to be out of the way.

She gave it four days before someone tested him. It was Kowalski.

She was across the bay and couldn't hear it. She saw Kowalski say something to the camera, something designed to provoke a reaction. Reid lowered the camera and looked at Kowalski for a moment. Said something back. Kowalski blinked. Said something shorter. Reid nodded and raised the camera and kept shooting.

Whatever he'd said, it worked. Kowalski looked settled afterward. Like something had been agreed to.

She wanted to know what Reid had said.

She didn't ask.

---

Later that week, alone in the briefing room after the crew had cleared out, she found a note on the table. Reid's handwriting, addressed to Okafor. A request to expand his access to the south training area during off-hours. Standard channels, formal request, nothing pushed. She could see from the language that he'd looked up the exact phrasing from the protocol agreement before writing it.

She set it back where she'd found it.

He was doing everything right.

That was the problem. She knew how to manage people who pushed too hard or not enough. People who operated exactly at the boundary of what they should, consistently, without friction. She didn't have a system for that. It left her nothing to correct and no clear reason to keep her guard elevated.

Her guard stayed elevated anyway.

Outside, the training season was getting into its rhythm, days longer now, the light staying past nine, the pines casting long shadows across the training slope in the early evenings. The fire weather was building the way it built every year. A number here, a number there, the Haines Index tracking upward in increments. And then one day you'd look at it and it wouldn't be something to monitor anymore. It would be something to act on.

She was ready for that. She knew how to do that.

The other thing she was less certain about.

She thought about the note to Okafor that evening. The way he'd gone through proper channels for something small, something he could have just done and apologized for later if anyone noticed. Most people in his position would have pushed the informal route. He'd used the formal one.

It was methodical. Careful in the way that suggested he'd learned to be careful somewhere along the way, not born to it. People born to carefulness had a different quality. They were precise because precision was natural to them. He was precise in the way of someone who understood the cost of imprecision and had decided to pay the upfront price instead.

She wondered what he'd broken before he'd learned to be this careful. She didn't ask. She went to bed.

But she lay there for a while in the dark, listening to the pines, thinking about what it meant that she was asking the question at all.

Chapter 4

She found out about the footage on a Tuesday.

Vasquez mentioned it in passing, the way Vasquez mentioned most things that mattered. Reid had been filming during the evening training session, the one she'd led on reading fire behavior from terrain, using the topographic maps and the dry-erase boards and the method she'd developed over years of trying to get people to see landscape as a system rather than a surface. She hadn't known Reid was there. She'd checked the schedule and he'd been logged for off-site B-roll, the ridge footage he'd requested access for.

"He came back early," Vasquez said.

She went to find him.

He was in the small room off the corridor they'd set up for his equipment. A folding table, two hard cases, the monitor he used for reviewing footage. The door was open. He looked up when she appeared in it.

"You filmed the training session," she said.

"I did." No hesitation. "I should have checked back in when I came back early. That's on me."

"I need to see what you have."

He turned the monitor toward her without asking why. She pulled up the chair he'd pushed back and sat down. He queued the footage.

It was good. That was the first thing she registered and didn't want to register. He had the room from a back angle, her at the front with the maps, the crew arranged in the loose semi-circle they formed when she was teaching. The light was low and he'd handled it well, the image clean. She could hear her own voice in the audio, clear, explaining how a north-facing slope held fuel differently than a south-facing one, how a drainage was a road for fire, how the landscape was always telling you something if you'd learned to read it.

She knew what she'd said. She'd said it a dozen times.

She watched herself say it and understood exactly why he'd stayed to film it.

"This is the part," she said.

"I know."

The part where she used her hands. She'd never been aware of it before, but watching herself now she could see it. The way her hands moved over the topographic map while she talked. Not pointing but tracing, following the contour lines the way water would follow them, the way fire would follow them, her fingers finding the ridges and the drainages without looking down. The crew was watching her hands as much as her face. She hadn't known that either.

She stopped the footage.

"I didn't consent to this," she said.

"You didn't. The standing agreement covers group training contexts. This qualifies."

She looked at him. "I know what the agreement covers."

"Then you know this is inside it."

"Technically."

He held her eyes. "We can discuss the footage. We can discuss what I do with it. But I'm not going to pretend I was outside the protocol when I wasn't."

She stood up. The chair scraped. She went to the window and stood there for a moment, looking at the training slope in the evening light.

He was right. She'd read the protocol agreement four times before she signed it, because she read everything four times, and group training contexts were covered, and this was a group training context. He hadn't violated the line. He'd stood on the corridor side of it and filmed something she hadn't known she was giving him.

She turned around.

"That footage," she said. "The part with the hands."

"Yes."

"I need you to understand something about that." He was watching her the way he always watched her. Steady, patient, not filling the silence. "What those people are learning in that room isn't technique. It's a way of seeing. If that footage gets cut into a two-minute segment with a score underneath it, it becomes a different thing. It becomes watchable. That's not what it is."

"What is it?"

"It's slow. It takes years. And it only works if the person in that room decides they want to see the way the landscape sees itself. You can't watch someone else learn it and get it. You have to be in the room." She stopped. "If you put that footage out there, you're not documenting it. You're replacing it with something that looks like it."

Reid was quiet for long enough that she thought he might argue. Then: "I understand the distinction."

"Do you."

"Yes." He turned back to the monitor. "I'll flag the sequence. Nothing with the hands goes out without your specific sign-off. Not covered by the standing agreement. That's my call."

She looked at him. He'd just voluntarily contracted himself past the protocol. She hadn't asked him to.

"That's not necessary," she said.

"I know. I'm doing it anyway."

She stayed in the doorway for a moment. The equipment cases behind him, the monitor with her own image paused on it, his notebook on the corner of the table. The window going gold above the tree line.

"Why?" she said.

He looked at her steadily. "Because you're right about what it is. And because I'm here to document the real thing, not a version of it that fits a segment length."

She didn't have an answer for that. Or she had an answer but it was one she didn't want to give in this room, which was that she'd expected him to be less careful than this and she didn't know what to do with careful.

"The audio," she said instead. "On that footage. What were you thinking about using it for?"

He leaned back in the chair. Looked at the ceiling for a moment, then back at her. "Honestly? I'm not sure yet. I came up here thinking I knew the shape of this story. I'm less sure about that now."

She didn't ask what he meant. She wasn't sure she wanted to know.

"Flag the sequence," she said. "Like you said."

"Already done."

She left. In the corridor she stopped and stood still for a moment. Not thinking, just standing, the particular stillness she used when something needed to be set down somewhere before she could move forward. The base ran its evening sounds around her. Somewhere a door. Voices in the mess. The refrigeration unit in the gear room cycling on.

She'd come in there ready to be in the right. He'd been in the right instead and then voluntarily stepped past it.

She thought about the footage. She thought about her hands moving over the map and the crew watching and herself not knowing any of it was visible. She'd assumed she knew what she showed people and what she didn't. She was good at knowing that. She'd been practicing for a long time.

She filed the information and kept walking. But it sat differently than other information sat. It had weight she hadn't assigned it.

She went back to work.

---

She found herself thinking about the footage two days later in a context that had nothing to do with Reid.

She was running the terrain briefing for an incoming mutual aid crew, a six-person team from a base in northern California who'd be supplementing for a week during a staffing gap. She was at the maps again, her hands moving the way they apparently moved, tracing the drainages and ridgelines, and somewhere in the middle of it she became conscious of her hands in a way she hadn't been before. Not self-conscious, not performing. Just aware.

She kept going. But afterward, walking back across the base in the late afternoon heat, she thought about what it meant to be seen doing something you'd never known you were doing.

She'd spent a long time constructing what she showed people. What she let through and what she held back, what was legible and what stayed interior. She was good at it. She'd thought she was good at it.

Reid's footage had shown her something doing something she hadn't authorized. Not maliciously. Not in violation of protocol. Just truthfully.

She didn't know yet whether that was a problem or a different kind of thing entirely.

Chapter 5

The call came at 0320.

She was already half-awake. She always was when the fire weather index was elevated, some older part of her nervous system tracking the conditions even in sleep. The pager went and she was dressed in ninety seconds and in the truck bay in four minutes and Reid was at the truck in five.

She clocked it without comment. He'd made the window.

The fire was thirty miles east, a lightning strike from the dry cell that had moved through late afternoon, caught in a drainage and climbing. The initial report put it at forty acres and moving, which meant by the time they got there it would be more. The flight took twenty-two minutes. She spent it going over the terrain map she'd pulled on the tablet. The drainage running northeast, the ridge line that would define the eastern anchor, the fuel load in the area. Bone dry. Nothing had rained in seventeen days and the ground had that particular exhausted quality she recognized from past seasons, the kind of dryness that wasn't just absence of moisture but a positive condition, a readiness.

Reid was in the jump seat behind her, not filming. The camera was stowed in its case.

She noticed.

The crew ran through their pre-jump checks in the focused quiet that settled over them once a call came in. Torres checking her reserve. Kowalski running through the radio sequence. Danny with his eyes half-closed, the particular stillness he entered before a jump that she'd come to recognize as his version of preparation. She'd asked him about it once, years ago. He'd said he was going through the landing. Every step of it, from exit to ground. By the time he jumped, he'd already landed a hundred times.

She'd started doing the same thing after that, though she'd never told him.

"Matthews," she said, without turning.

"Here."

"You hold at the LZ until I radio position. Then you come to me."

"Copy."

"Not to the fire. To me."

"Copy."

She turned back to the map.

The jump itself was clean. The wind was out of the southwest at eight miles per hour, steady, manageable. She watched each member of her crew exit and track and hit the LZ and move to their staging positions and then she was out herself, the familiar shock up through her legs, collapsing the chute and already scanning. The meadow grass was knee-high and dry. The smoke column to the east was thicker than the report had suggested. She revised her estimate upward.

She radioed position and had the crew moving within four minutes.

---

She was aware of Reid behind her for the first hour. Not distractingly. He was good at this, she was starting to understand. Present without requiring management. He moved when the crew moved, held when they held, stayed exactly where she'd put him. The camera was up now, pointed at the work rather than at her, and she let it recede from her attention because the fire required her attention and the fire was what mattered.

The fire had gotten into the timber above the drainage, which complicated the east anchor. She could see from the torching patterns that it had been in the canopy for at least forty minutes, longer than the initial report had accounted for. She adjusted the line and moved Torres up to support Kowalski's position and made the call to let the lower portion run to the black rather than fight it uphill.

The right call. But the kind that required certainty because it meant standing and watching fire burn through country you were choosing not to protect. She'd made this call before and she'd make it again and it was still the kind of call that required you to be sure, because the crew was watching and the crew needed to move without hesitation and hesitation from her would travel through the line like a signal through wire.

She was sure. The crew moved.

Around 0600, with the eastern anchor holding and the north line progressing, she had a moment to stop. Not rest. There was no rest on a line. But she could stand and read the terrain and assess. The sun was coming up behind the smoke column, which meant the wind would shift when the day heated up, and she needed to know what that meant for the east anchor before it happened rather than while it was happening.

She was working through it when she became aware that Reid was standing about ten feet behind her.

Not filming. The camera was at his side, pointing at the ground.

He was watching the fire the way she watched it. Not passively but actively, reading it. His eyes were moving across the slope the way she'd taught Petrov to move his eyes. Taking in the whole picture before the details, looking for the patterns rather than the events.

"Wind's going to shift," she said. Not to him specifically. But not away from him either.

"What time?"

"Hour and a half. Maybe two."

He looked at the ridge line to the east. "That changes the anchor."

She looked at him.

"Torres is at the right position if the wind holds," he said. "If it shifts northeast that's a problem."

She turned back to the fire. He'd looked at the map on the flight. She'd watched him look at it. She hadn't registered that he'd been reading it, not just seeing it.

"Yes," she said.

She radioed Torres. Made the adjustment early, while they had time. Torres moved without question. An hour and forty minutes later the wind shifted northeast and the adjustment held and the anchor held and the north line held and by nine-thirty they were calling it contained.

---

She was back at the LZ going over the after-action with the crew when she caught the look Vasquez gave her. Small, the particular look Vasquez used when he'd noticed something worth noting. She followed his eyes.

Reid was forty feet away, sitting on his pack at the edge of the LZ, reviewing footage on the camera's small screen. He looked like every other member of the crew at this point in a long morning. Tired, functional, settled into the quiet that comes after sustained effort. Like he'd been here before. Not this fire, not this crew. But this kind of work. The flatness of a body that had been running hard and was now allowed to simply exist.

She looked away.

On the flight back she went over the maps again. The crew was quiet around her. Kowalski was asleep before they'd climbed to altitude, which was a talent she'd always envied. Danny was looking out the window at the smoke column they were leaving behind, which she knew he did on every extraction and had never asked him about.

Reid was writing in the notebook.

She didn't look at what he was writing. She went back to the maps and ran through the sequence of calls she'd made and found nothing she'd change. The adjustment to Torres had been the right one and she'd made it at the right time and the rest had held. Clean.

But somewhere between the eastern ridge and the base she adjusted something. A calibration she'd made before the season started, based on what she'd assumed he was and how much attention she'd need to allocate to him. She moved it without naming the movement.

He was less of a variable than she'd thought.

She didn't know yet what that made him. She put it on the list of things she'd work out later and looked back at the terrain map and watched the mountains flatten into high desert as they flew west toward home.

The debrief that evening was longer than usual, the mutual aid coordinator on the line for forty minutes going through the containment timeline. She gave her report and answered questions and when it was done she sat in the empty briefing room for a few minutes before going to get food.

She thought about Reid saying That changes the anchor. She thought about the fact that he'd been right, and that he'd said it as a statement rather than a question, meaning he'd done the calculation already and arrived at certainty rather than suspicion. Four months with an engine crew in New Mexico. He'd been paying attention the whole time.

She thought about the camera being down when things moved fast.

She'd told herself it was operationally sensible, that he'd put it away because he was in the way. But she'd been watching him for weeks now and she didn't think he did things for one reason. He'd put the camera down and he'd read the terrain and he'd said the right thing and none of those were separate.

She got up and went to get food and didn't think about what it meant that she kept returning to a single person's choices as objects of analysis.

She was thorough with her crew. That was all it was.

Chapter 6

She knew he'd found Danny because Danny told her.

Not immediately. It was two days after, Danny mentioning it the way he mentioned most things, in passing, without apparent weight. They were doing a systems check on the reserve chutes and he said, almost to the equipment, "Talked to your journalist the other day."

"He's not my journalist."

Danny had the particular smile he used for things he found gently funny. "He asked good questions."

"That's what I keep hearing."

"About you, mostly."

She kept her hands on the chute. "And what did you tell him?"

"The truth." Danny handed her the next connector. "That you're the best lead jumper I've ever worked with. That you see things on a fire three moves ahead of everyone else. That if I'm in a bad situation I want you making the calls." He paused. "That you don't let people get close enough to return the favor."

She looked up at him.

Danny wasn't looking at her. He was examining the connector with the careful attention he gave everything that kept people alive, running his thumb along the edge the way she'd taught him, back when she'd been teaching him.

"He asked about the jump," Danny said. "Last year."

Everything in her went quiet. Not outwardly. Her hands kept moving, the system check continued, she was aware of all of it continuing. But underneath it.

"What did you tell him?"

"That there was a run nobody predicted. That you called it and got us out." He set the connector down. "That I caught a ravine on the way. That I don't blame you for anything."

She was quiet.

"That's all?" she said.

"That's all." He picked up the next connector. "It's all true."

She knew it was all true. What Danny didn't know, what no one knew, was the four seconds. The part that came before the call. The part where she'd been somewhere else, somewhere that had nothing to do with that fire or that drainage or the crew she was responsible for. The part that had cost Danny the ravine.

She'd been over it a thousand times. She'd run the calculation every way she knew how. The call she made was the right call. The call was correct. The four seconds were in the call and the call was still right and Danny was alive and not blaming her and she was here, and none of that helped. That calculation had never helped.

She reached for the next connector.

"He's going to find out more," Danny said. "If he's good. And he seems good."

"I know."

"Just so you're ready."

She looked at him then. Her oldest crew member, the one who'd been on more jumps with her than anyone still at this base, the one who'd gone down a ravine on her four-second delay and come back the next season and never once made her pay for it. She looked at him and he looked back with the particular directness he had. Danny saw things plainly and reported them plainly and didn't dress them up or soften them or make them about himself.

"I'm always ready," she said.

"Remy." He said it gently, the way you say something you've been waiting a long time to say. "That's the problem."

She didn't have an answer for that. She turned back to the chute inventory and finished the check and when she tallied the count it was right.

---

She thought about what Danny had said to Reid. Not the content. The words were straightforward, nothing she hadn't known about herself. She thought about the fact of it. Reid had gone looking for her history and found Danny and Danny had told him the truth about her, which was the best possible version of what could have happened and also the version that left her with nowhere to stand.

She couldn't resent Reid for doing his job. She couldn't resent Danny for being Danny. She couldn't even resent the truth, which was just the truth and didn't have intentions.

What she could do was be aware of it. There was now a version of her in Reid's notebook that had come from someone who knew her, not from what she'd shown him. She thought about what it changed. It changed the angle of his attention. She'd noticed it two days after the Danny conversation, in a look he gave her across the briefing room. Not different exactly, but wider. Like a photograph that had been cropped and then uncropped. Like he'd been seeing a portion and now he had more edges.

She preferred the cropped version. It was the one she'd designed.

---

That evening after the crew had dispersed she found herself in the equipment room alone, running her hands over the secondary harness rack with no operational reason to be doing so. Just touching things. A habit she'd had since before she could name it. When her head was full she went to the equipment, to the physical fact of gear that was maintained and ready, that did what it was designed to do.

The door to the corridor was open and she could hear the base's evening sounds. Somewhere at the far end Reid was in his room, probably writing. She'd mapped his patterns without deciding to, the same way she mapped everything.

She thought about Danny saying That you don't let people get close enough to return the favor. She thought about him saying That's the problem.

She'd been thinking about it since that afternoon and what she'd arrived at was that Danny was wrong. Not about the fact of it. The fact was accurate. But wrong about it being a problem. It wasn't a problem. It was the design. The design had been built for reasons that were still true. The reasons didn't stop being true because someone stated the design out loud.

She knew what happened when you let people become necessary. She'd watched it hollow her mother out from the inside, that particular grief, the kind that doesn't announce itself but restructures everything quietly until you reach for something that used to be there and find the shape of it and nothing else. Her mother had loved her father completely and her father had gone into a fire and not come back and her mother had spent the years since then at half-volume, the lights still on but something essential rerouted.

Remy had been nine years old and she'd understood it even then, the way children understand things they can't name yet. She'd made a decision without words, standing in the doorway of her mother's bedroom three weeks after the funeral, watching her mother sleep in the middle of the afternoon. Not a dramatic decision. Not the kind you announce. Just a quiet internal rearrangement, a load-bearing wall going up.

She didn't let people become necessary. She was good at her job. She was present for her crew and she managed them well and she cared about their safety in the way she cared about outcomes, with her full attention and complete professionalism.

She didn't let anyone past the wall.

Danny thought this was a problem. Danny was wrong.

She took her hands off the harness rack and went to wash them at the bay sink. The water came out cold, the way it always did this time of evening, and she let it run until it warmed. Outside the window the Oregon night was coming in, the last light going out of the sky in strips.

She heard Reid's door down the hall, the particular sound of it opening. Footsteps. He was heading to the mess for the late coffee he'd started making a habit of, she knew that pattern too, he went around nine-thirty when the mess was empty and came back with a mug and wrote for another hour before lights out.

His footsteps paused.

She was still at the sink, water running, and she understood that he'd paused because the light was on in the bay and the door was open and he could see her in there. She didn't turn around.

After a moment his footsteps continued toward the mess.

She turned the water off and dried her hands and stood for a moment in the quiet of the bay, the equipment arranged around her in the careful order she maintained, everything in its place, everything ready.

The design worked. It had always worked.

She turned off the light and went to bed.

Chapter 7

It rained on a Thursday.

Not fire weather rain, not the kind that mattered operationally. Just a front moving through from the coast, a low-pressure system that brought two days of steady gray and grounded everything. No jumps. No training on the outdoor slopes. The base went into its rain-day rhythm, equipment maintenance and indoor briefings and the particular restlessness of people built for movement who were temporarily required to stay still.

She used the time to go through the jump records for the season, documentation she kept that went beyond what the official logs required. Notes on each crew member's deployment performance, things she tracked in her own shorthand. Adjustments she wanted to make to certain drills. She was three hours into it when she ran out of coffee.

The mess kitchen was empty at that hour, past nine, the dinner crew long gone. She made a full pot because it was a full-pot kind of evening and while it brewed she stood at the window and watched the rain move across the training slope in sheets, the grass bending flat and coming back.

She heard him come in behind her.

She didn't turn around.

Reid poured himself a mug from the pot she'd just made and leaned against the counter, the old floor registering his weight, and they stood in the kitchen with the rain on the window and didn't say anything for a while.

It wasn't uncomfortable. That was the thing she noticed. She was standing in a small kitchen with a man she'd known for six weeks and the silence between them wasn't the kind that needed filling.

That was new information.

"You're doing the season records," he said. Not a question.

"Running behind on them."

"Can I ask what you track?"

She considered whether to answer. The notebook he'd brought with him was resting on the counter by his mug. He hadn't opened it.

"Performance variables," she said. "Things the official record doesn't capture."

"Like what?"

"How a person moves when they're tired. Whether they get slower or whether they get sloppy. Those are different problems. How they communicate under pressure. Whether they go quiet or go loud." She paused. "Who I can put next to who."

He was quiet for a moment. "You're managing people like terrain."

She looked at him.

"Not dismissively," he said. "The way you read terrain. You're looking for what's load-bearing and what's going to shift under pressure."

She turned back to the window. The rain was heavier now, the tree line at the edge of the property disappearing and reappearing in the gray.

"In this job," she said, "the terrain and the people are the same problem."

"What's the terrain doing right now?"

"Saturating. Ground that was at risk yesterday isn't at risk right now." She watched a gust move through the trees. "By Saturday the sun comes back and everything that absorbed this water puts it right back into the air. We'll be back to critical conditions within forty-eight hours."

"So the rain is temporary."

"The rain is always temporary."

He picked up his mug. She could feel him looking at her the way he looked at things he was trying to understand.

"Why smokejumping?" he said.

She had an answer for this. A good one, true enough to hold up, not so true it cost her anything.

"It's the most effective way to reach fires in terrain ground crews can't access in the initial attack window. Early suppression prevents escalation. The work matters and the margin for error is real, which keeps it honest."

He didn't write anything down.

"That's the answer you give," he said.

She looked at him.

"It's true," he said. "But it's the answer you've given enough times that it comes out clean." He set his mug down. "I'm not asking what smokejumping is. I'm asking why you."

The rain ran down the window. Outside the slope was invisible, just gray.

She knew why. She'd always known why. She'd stood in her mother's doorway at nine years old and made a decision and the decision had carried her through a degree in fire science and a training program and a dozen seasons. She knew why the way you know things you've never put into words, because putting them into words makes them available to other people.

She should give him the clean answer again. She was good at redirecting.

"My father was a jumper," she said. "He died on a jump when I was nine."

Reid didn't move. Didn't reach for the notebook.

"I didn't go into this to follow him. That's not what it is. I went into it because after he died I needed to understand what it was that had been worth it to him. Worth the risk, worth the absence, worth leaving a family that needed him to go do a job most people will never see." She stopped. "I needed to know what he chose."

"And?"

"I found out." She looked at the rain. "I understand what he chose."

"Does that help?"

The question landed somewhere she hadn't been expecting. She'd answered questions about her father before. The questions were always versions of the same question. Was it hard. Did you always want to do this. Do you feel connected to him out there.

Nobody had ever asked if understanding helped.

"No," she said. "Not the way you'd want it to."

Reid was quiet. Outside the rain made its sound against the window, steady and without urgency, the kind of rain that wasn't going anywhere soon.

"My father's a surgeon," he said after a while. "Cardiothoracic. Sixty-two years old and still operating, still the best in his department by most accounts. I grew up watching him be extraordinary at something and I went into journalism to be in rooms where things were actually happening. Real stakes, not abstract ones." He paused. "I've been doing this eleven years and there's still a part of me that thinks I'm watching from the window instead of being inside."

She turned to look at him. He was looking at his coffee.

"That's why you put the camera down," she said. "On the first deployment. When the crew had to move fast."

He looked up.

"You could have kept filming. You didn't."

"I was in the way."

"You've managed not to be in the way when things were moving fast before. You made a choice."

He didn't answer that. He looked back out at the rain.

She let it sit. She'd said a true thing and he'd heard it and that was enough.

"I should get back to the records," she said.

"I know."

She picked up her mug and turned to leave. She was through the kitchen door and into the corridor before he spoke.

"Remy."

She stopped. Didn't turn around.

"Thank you," he said. "For the real answer."

She stood in the corridor for a moment. The base was quiet around her, rain on the roof, the distant hum of the refrigeration unit, someone's radio too far away to make out.

She didn't turn around because she didn't know what her face was doing and she preferred not to find out.

"Get some sleep," she said, and walked back to the records.

She sat down at the table and found her place in the documentation and worked until midnight. Thorough and efficient and not thinking about the kitchen.

She thought about it later, in the dark of her room with the rain still going on the roof. She ran through what she'd said. Her father. The choosing. The part about understanding not helping the way you'd want it to.

She'd given him something real and it had felt less like falling than she'd expected.

She filed it the way she filed things she couldn't yet use and closed her eyes and listened to the rain and eventually slept.

It was still raining in the morning. But Saturday, she knew, the sun would come back.

The rain was always temporary.

Chapter 8

July came in hot and stayed that way.

The Haines Index climbed through the first week and by the second it had crossed into the range where she stopped tracking it as a number and started reading it as a condition. The air had a quality she recognized from bad seasons, a particular dryness that wasn't just low humidity but something more total, like the landscape was holding its breath. The creek that ran along the eastern fence line dropped six inches in eight days. The grass on the training slope went from green to gold to the color of old paper.

She briefed the crew on elevated readiness protocols. Extended PT, reduced recovery windows between deployments, the specific adjustments she made when the season was moving from active to serious. They received it without complaint. They'd all seen serious before.

She started noticing Reid's rhythms more precisely.

Not intentionally. She tracked everyone's patterns because patterns were information and information was what kept people from getting hurt. But she became aware, somewhere in the second week of July, that she was tracking his more closely than she tracked the others. That she knew without looking up what time he'd be at the briefing board in the morning, what time he'd break for the evening, when the light would be on under his door and when it would go out.

She noted it and adjusted her attention elsewhere.

It didn't entirely work.

---

He'd started doing a thing at meals.

He would sit down with his food and the notebook and for the first ten minutes he wrote. Not looking up, not participating in the crew conversation, just writing. Then he'd close the notebook and put it away and eat the rest of the meal and be present in the conversation in whatever way was natural. The division was clean and consistent and it made the notebook feel like a private thing rather than a professional tool when he was sitting across the table from you.

She watched the crew respond to it. They'd stopped noticing the notebook the way they'd stopped noticing the camera, not because it wasn't there but because it had become predictable. Predictable things didn't require management.

Torres had started saving him a seat. Not always, not every meal, but often enough that it was a pattern. She noticed this because she noticed patterns.

"He told me something yesterday," Torres said, appearing at Remy's shoulder during equipment staging one afternoon.

Remy kept her hands on the carabiner rack. "What's that."

"About why he came here specifically. Not just smokejumpers in general. Here."

She didn't ask. Torres would tell her regardless.

"He said he wanted to find out what it looked like when someone was completely in the right place." Torres picked up a carabiner and turned it over in her hands. "Like, the perfect alignment of a person and a job. He said most people he films, there's always a gap. What they're doing and who they are. But sometimes you find someone where there's no gap. And that's what he's trying to document."

Remy looked at the rack.

"He say who he had in mind?" she said.

Torres gave her the look that question deserved and walked off.

Remy went back to the rack. She checked three more carabiners. Tagged one. Moved the rest.

She thought about no gap. She thought about being completely in the right place. She thought about whether the thing he was documenting was what he thought it was, or whether the camera and the notebook and the careful patient attention had simply caught the outside of something and was calling it the inside.

She kept moving.

---

He found her on the east side of the base that evening, past the fence line where the pines ran along the drainage. She went there sometimes in the late evenings, when the air had cooled and the light was going out of the sky in long horizontal lines. It was outside the base perimeter technically, but it was also thirty feet from the fence and she'd been going there since her second season without anyone asking why.

She heard him coming. His footfall on dry ground.

He stopped at the fence line. Not coming through, just standing at it.

"Torres told you," she said.

"Yes."

She was looking at the tree line. The pines were dark against the last of the light, the sky behind them going from orange to a deep clear blue that would be black in another ten minutes.

"It's a tidy story," she said. "Person and job. No gap."

"You think I'm wrong."

"I think it's the part you can see." She watched the light change. "Gaps aren't always visible from the outside."

He was quiet for a moment. "What's the gap?"

She considered not answering. She was good at not answering. She had a dozen ways to redirect that didn't feel like deflection, that felt like the conversation simply moving in a different direction, and most people never noticed the difference.

"My father died doing this," she said. "And I still do it. You can call that alignment if you want." She paused. "You can also call it something else."

"What would you call it?"

She turned and looked at him. He was on the other side of the fence, hands in his jacket pockets, watching her with that steady unhurried attention. The last light was hitting the left side of his face and leaving the right in shadow.

"I'd call it more complicated than a documentary chapter," she said.

Something moved in his expression. Not offense. Something closer to recognition.

"Fair," he said. "I'll keep looking."

He turned and walked back toward the base. She watched him go, the crunch of his footsteps on the dry ground fading, and then stood there a while longer in the cooling dark.

He was going to keep looking. She believed him. She believed it the way she believed things she'd verified through observation rather than things she'd simply been told. He was methodical and patient and he'd been at this long enough to know the difference between the story he'd come to tell and the story that was actually there.

She thought about what he'd find when he kept looking.

She turned back to the tree line. A nighthawk cut across the last strip of sky above the pines, fast and precise, and was gone. The temperature had dropped six degrees since she'd come out here and her jacket was doing the work it was designed to do.

She stayed another few minutes. Not because she needed to. Just because the dark was restoring something she hadn't known was depleted, a quality of quiet the base didn't have when the crew was in it.

Then she went back inside and got ready for tomorrow.

---

The slow accumulation, that was the only name she had for it. The way something could be happening without announcing itself, without an event you could point to and say: there, that's when it started. She'd watched fires build this way. A week of low humidity and rising temps, a week of fuel curing in the heat, a week of winds from the wrong direction, and then one dry lightning cell and the whole prepared landscape ignited and you had to respond as though the fire had appeared suddenly when in fact it had been arriving for weeks.

She knew how to read arriving fire. She was less practiced at reading the other kind of accumulation.

She lay in her bunk that night and listened to the July heat settling over the base and thought about Torres saying no gap and Reid saying I'll keep looking and the nighthawk crossing the dark strip of sky.

She thought about the fence line. That she'd been on one side of it and he'd stayed on the other, and that the not-crossing had been a kind of courtesy she hadn't asked for and hadn't known she wanted.

Outside, the night made its sounds. The pines in a light wind. An animal moving somewhere in the drainage.

She closed her eyes and told herself she was going to sleep and eventually she did.

She woke at 0430 two days later to a pager that wasn't going off and lay there for a moment trying to understand what had pulled her out of sleep. The room was dark. The base was quiet. No pager, no wind, nothing she could identify.

She got up anyway, the way she sometimes did when something she couldn't name told her to. Pulled on clothes and went to the briefing room and checked the weather data that had come in overnight.

The Haines Index had jumped three points.

She stood there in the dark briefing room with the data on the screen and thought about the drainage to the east, about the fuel load she'd been watching, about the wind pattern the models had been showing for three days that nobody else had mentioned in a briefing yet because it hadn't risen to the threshold that triggered a briefing.

She started drafting the morning's elevated readiness update. It was 0445. The briefing wasn't until 0700.

At 0500, she heard footsteps in the corridor. Unhurried. Familiar.

She didn't look up from the screen.

Reid appeared in the doorway with two mugs and set one on the corner of the table without asking whether she wanted it. Then he leaned against the door frame and looked at the data on the screen.

He didn't say anything. He drank his coffee.

She picked up the mug and went back to the update.

Outside, the first gray light was starting to come into the window. Somewhere to the east, in the dry forests she'd been watching for two weeks, the landscape was quietly completing its preparation.

They worked in silence until the rest of the base woke up.

Chapter 9

The crew noticed before she did.

She became aware of this on a Wednesday in mid-July, when she walked into the mess for lunch and the conversation didn't stop but reorganized itself the way conversations do when the thing they were just discussing is suddenly in the room. She'd been in enough rooms to know the sound of that reorganization. She got her food and sat down and didn't acknowledge it.

Torres was watching her over the rim of a coffee mug with an expression Remy had catalogued weeks ago as I have information and I am deciding whether to deploy it.

She ate her lunch.

"You were up early this morning," Torres said.

"I'm always up early."

"Reid was up early too."

"Reid is always up early."

"You were up early at the same time," Torres said. "In the briefing room."

"We were both looking at weather data."

"That's what I told Kowalski," Torres said, in the tone of someone who had told Kowalski something else entirely.

Remy put her fork down. She looked at Torres. Then she looked at Kowalski, who was suddenly very interested in his food. Then she looked at the rest of the table, which had arranged itself into a collective portrait of people who were not paying attention.

"The Haines Index jumped overnight," she said. "I went to check it. Reid was awake for the same reason people who have been embedded with a fire crew for two months are awake when the Haines Index jumps. It was a coincidence."

"Sure," Torres said.

Remy picked up her fork.

"He brought you coffee though," Torres said.

She ate her lunch without further comment. Across the mess, Reid was sitting at a table with Danny and two of the seasonal crew, writing in the notebook with the particular focused stillness that meant he wasn't aware of or wasn't acknowledging the conversation happening on the other side of the room.

She watched him for approximately three seconds before looking back at her food.

She was being efficient. She was tracking the room.

---

She had it out with herself that evening in the equipment bay, which was where she went when she needed to think without anyone asking her questions. The physical work of the inventory gave her hands something to do and left her mind available, which was the arrangement she preferred for thinking through things she didn't want to think through.

The crew had noticed something. She needed to know what there was to notice.

She ran through it methodically. What she'd done, what she'd allowed, what she'd failed to manage appropriately. She was fair about it because fairness was the only useful standard. Unfairness would just let her off the hook and the hook was useful.

She'd allowed the fence line conversation. She'd stayed in the kitchen longer than necessary. She'd accepted coffee at 0500 without asking why it was being offered. She'd been tracking his patterns with more precision than the others and she'd noticed that and adjusted and then gradually stopped adjusting.

None of those were large things. But large things were made of small things and she knew how to read accumulation.

She was also aware, being fair about it, that she'd been here before. Not exactly here. But in the vicinity. She knew what it felt like to manage an attraction to someone professionally, to keep it in its correct category through consistent deliberate effort. She'd done it before and she knew how to do it.

The difference, being completely fair about it, was that before she'd been managing an attraction and this felt like something that was also managing her, working on her from the outside in alongside whatever she was doing from the inside out, and the two processes were not moving at the same rate.

She put a carabiner in the discard pile. Reached for the next one.

She could handle it. She'd been handling things her whole career, the entire point of the architecture she'd built was that she could handle things. One journalist, one season, one specific and understandable response to extended proximity and shared experience and an attention she hadn't prepared for because she hadn't known the kind existed.

She could handle it.

She reached for the next carabiner and ran her thumb along the gate and felt nothing wrong with it and moved it to the keep pile.

---

Reid, for his part, gave her nothing to object to.

He was exactly what he'd been from the beginning. Present without being intrusive. At the boundary without pushing it. Asking questions she sometimes answered and sometimes didn't and accepting both outcomes with equal ease.

She would have found it easier if he'd given her something to push against. An overreach, an assumption, some evidence of the thing the crew clearly thought was happening. She had nothing to work with. He brought coffee at 0500 and said nothing about it and went back to his room when the briefing was done and left her with no version of events she could object to except the one that was happening entirely inside her own head.

She told herself this was manageable. She told herself this in the equipment bay and then again while she was reviewing the next day's weather data and again while she was falling asleep.

She mostly believed it.

---

The deployment came two days later, a hot afternoon fire in a drainage she'd been watching. She had them in the air inside eight minutes from the call. On the ground in four minutes from exit. The fire was exactly where she'd expected it to be, behaving exactly as the fuel load and terrain had suggested it would, which should have been satisfying and was mostly just clarifying.

She worked the fire. The crew worked the fire. Reid was where she'd put him and the camera was where she needed it to be and everything moved with the efficiency of a crew that had been doing this together long enough that the communication was mostly redundant.

At one point, working the south flank with Kowalski while Torres anchored the east, she heard Reid's voice on the radio. Channel two. Short. No editorializing.

"Head fire's spotting over the ridge. Wind's pushing east."

She was already turning. She'd seen the spot smoke in her peripheral vision a beat before his voice came through. She keyed the radio and confirmed and made the call and moved the crew and ten minutes later they were safe and the situation was managed.

She didn't think about it during the work. She thought about it on the flight back.

He'd been watching the right thing. He'd used the channel she'd told him to use. He'd said exactly what she needed to know and nothing else.

She looked at him across the aircraft. He was writing in the notebook, head down, not looking at her.

He'd been ready for that moment. She didn't know how long he'd been ready for it. She didn't know how to factor that in.

She went back to the terrain maps and let the question sit where questions sat when she didn't have an answer yet.

---

Three days after the deployment she was coming back from a solo run along the eastern fence line, the early morning still cool enough to run hard, and she passed Reid on his way out for his own run. They were going in opposite directions and it was the kind of passing that required nothing, a nod, a word, keep moving.

He slowed for a moment. Not stopping. Just reducing speed.

"Haines is up another point," he said.

"I know," she said.

They kept moving in their respective directions.

She ran another half mile before she let herself think about the fact that he'd checked the Haines Index before his morning run and that he'd known she would want to know and that he'd told her in the two seconds available without making a thing of it.

She ran another quarter mile.

She thought about what Torres had said. No gap. Person and job.

She thought about what she'd told him at the fence. More complicated than a documentary chapter.

She picked up her pace and ran until her lungs required her to stop thinking about anything except the breathing.

Chapter 10

The moment of softness came on a Thursday morning, without warning, the way the moments that cost something always did.

She was running a gear inspection in bay two, alone, the crew on a scheduled rest day after three deployments in six days. The base was quiet in the way it got on rest days, not empty but unhurried, people moving slowly through the spaces with the quality of bodies that had been operating at a deficit and were now, cautiously, allowing themselves to stop.

She wasn't resting. She did the gear inspection because it needed doing and because she trusted her own hands on equipment more than she trusted inactivity.

Reid came through the bay around nine carrying a length of rope he'd pulled from the training cache, which she'd given him access to for the B-roll footage he'd been shooting of the equipment itself. Close work, texture and detail. He'd mentioned wanting the interior shots, the weave of the rope under a macro lens.

He set up in the corner of the bay where the light came in clean from the east-facing window. She was aware of him setting up the way she was aware of everything in a space she was responsible for, a peripheral attention that processed and filed without demanding focus.

She kept working.

He was quiet for a long time. The shutter of the camera occasionally. The sound of him repositioning, setting the rope down, picking it up again. She'd gotten used to those sounds the way she'd gotten used to the base's other ambient sounds, the refrigeration unit, the flag line in the wind, Kowalski's particular way of walking.

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

She kept her hands on the carabiner she was checking. "You can ask."

"Do you ever get tired of being the person who sees things first?"

She looked up.

He was still looking at the rope through the lens, adjusting the focus, not looking at her.

"I mean it genuinely," he said. "Not as a compliment. More like. Is it tiring to always be running the calculation when other people aren't running it yet."

She set the carabiner down.

She thought about the honest answer. The honest answer was that she didn't experience it as tiring because she didn't experience it as a choice, the way she imagined people who were right-handed didn't experience reaching for things with their right hand as tiring. It was just what she did. It was how she was organized.

But there was a different honest answer underneath that one.

"Sometimes," she said. "When the thing I'm seeing is something I'd rather not see yet."

He looked up from the camera then. Looked at her across the bay with an expression she didn't have a clean category for. Not pity. Not the soft look people got when they thought they were offering comfort. Something more level than that. More like the look of someone who recognized something.

"Yeah," he said.

That was all. He went back to the rope.

She picked up the carabiner and ran her thumb along the gate.

The moment lasted maybe four seconds. She could feel that it was the kind of four seconds that didn't announce itself but that she'd be able to locate later, if she tried. A before and an after to it, unremarkable on the surface.

She finished the carabiner and reached for the next one.

---

Later, alone in the small office off the main corridor where she did her paperwork, she put it away. She was methodical about this, the same process she used for other things that needed containing. Identify the thing, assign it a category, close the drawer.

The thing was that he'd asked the right question. Not a question anyone had asked her before, not exactly. People asked whether the job was stressful, whether she found the responsibility difficult, whether she ever thought about slowing down. Those questions were about the work.

His question had been about her. The texture of being her.

She'd answered it honestly without planning to.

She went back to the paperwork. Outside, the July heat was pressing against the windows, the kind of heat that made the air above the pavement shimmer and the distant ridge lines look soft at the edges.

She thought about the way he'd said yeah. Not as agreement. As recognition. The difference between someone saying I understand and someone saying I know what that is.

She thought about it for about thirty seconds. Then she stopped.

---

That evening, alone in her room after the briefing, her mind let go of the immediate work and went somewhere quieter. It was there before she could redirect it.

Not the question. Not the answer. His hands on the rope in the morning light, moving over it the way you touched something you found genuinely interesting. Not clinical. Something between professional and personal. The way they'd looked when he'd finally set the rope down and the light had caught the line of them.

She felt the wanting arrive before she could do anything about it. Not the vague generalized kind. Something rooted in those particular details. His hands. His voice saying yeah like he'd been waiting for someone to ask the right question and had stopped expecting it. The four seconds in the bay when neither of them had said anything more and it had been enough.

The wanting was not the problem. The problem was the tenderness underneath it, specific and quiet, reaching her before the wanting did.

She turned over in her bunk and pressed her face into the pillow and thought about tomorrow's weather data and the drainage to the northeast and the crew rotation she needed to finalize.

The thought didn't clear. It subsided. There was a difference.

She lay in the dark and waited for it to pass the way rain passed, leaving the landscape underneath not wetter so much as more prepared, ready for the right conditions to arrive.

She told herself this was manageable.

It was manageable. The thought had passed.

What it had left in the landscape was her own problem.

---

She didn't tell him any of this. She got up the next morning and went to PT and ran and briefed the crew and checked the weather data and found him in the equipment bay at some point in the afternoon and said something operational and he answered something operational and they moved past each other without incident.

She was good at this.

She was very good at this.

---

Two days later she was watching Petrov run the anchor sequence on the south slope, alone, and she saw something shift in the way he moved through the middle section. He'd slowed down without thinking about slowing down. His hands were sure without checking whether they were sure.

He finished and looked up at her. Not asking for confirmation. Just finishing and looking up.

"There," she said.

He nodded once. He knew.

She stood there for a moment after he'd walked back down the slope, looking at the anchor he'd set. Clean, load-bearing, right. The product of six weeks of three repetitions. Trust, not knowledge.

She thought about four seconds. The ones she'd taken in a drainage on a fire that wasn't this one, looking at something that wasn't there. The ones that still lived in the architecture of her body, recorded somewhere in the same muscle memory she was trying to build in Petrov.

The thing about trust was that it was specific. You could trust your hands on an anchor sequence and not trust your hands in a fire that reminded you of another fire. The training worked right up to the edge of what it had been trained for.

She walked back down the slope and went to do the afternoon briefing.

Chapter 11

She was in the main briefing room when Reid found her father.

Not in the room. In the room with her, the day after. She could see it in the question he asked.

She was going over deployment reports from the prior week, three fires in eight days as the season built toward what was now clearly going to be a significant August. Reid had come in to look at the terrain maps, which he did regularly now with a fluency that no longer surprised her. He stood at the east wall where the large-format regional map was pinned, looking at the drainages she'd marked, and then he looked at the quadrant she'd circled two weeks ago in red marker with a note that said watch.

"This one," he said. Not a question.

"Yes."

"What does your gut say."

"My gut says the same thing the data says." She didn't look up from the report. "That it's a question of when, not whether."

He was quiet for a moment, studying the map. Then: "I've been reading about the base. The history."

She kept writing.

"There's a record," he said. "From nineteen ninety-four. A fatality on a jump in the Ochoco Mountains. Line-of-duty death."

Her pen kept moving. The report continued.

"The name in the record is Thomas Reyes," Reid said. Carefully. Not as a weapon. But not as a throwaway either.

She set the pen down. She'd known this was coming. She'd known it since Danny had told her Reid was looking, since she'd understood what kind of journalist he was. She'd had time to decide how she was going to handle it and what she'd decided was that she would handle it the same way she handled everything: directly and without excess.

"My father," she said.

"I know. I'm sorry."

She looked at him then. He was still at the map, not facing her, which she appreciated without knowing how to say so. It was the right angle for this conversation.

"You're not going to ask me about it?" she said.

"I'm going to ask you if you want to talk about it," he said. "That's different."

She looked back at the report. The numbers in the columns. The containment times, the crew assignments, the fuel types, the weather conditions. All of it reducible to data, to pattern, to the kind of information you could act on.

Her father was not reducible to data.

"He jumped into a drainage in the Ochocos in August of ninety-four," she said. "A wind event that the weather service didn't predict. Happened fast." She paused. "He was good. Everyone who worked with him said he was good. It wasn't a failure of skill."

"Just the weather."

"Just the weather." She picked up the pen again. "He was thirty-one years old."

Reid didn't say anything for a moment. She appreciated that too.

"How old were you?"

"Nine."

The briefing room was quiet. Outside, she could hear the crew doing equipment staging, the metallic sounds of gear being moved and sorted, voices at a working volume.

"My mother never recovered," she said. She hadn't planned to say that. She'd planned to give him the contained version, the version she gave the people above her pay grade who occasionally needed context for why one of their crew leads had grown up in a smokejumper's household. The version that was true and unrevealing. "She's functional. She's fine. But there's a version of her that stopped being available after he died and I don't think it ever came back."

"And you became a smokejumper anyway."

"I became a smokejumper specifically," she said. "It wasn't in spite of it. It was because of it. That distinction matters."

She heard him move. He'd turned from the map and was sitting down at the table, not close, two chairs away. She could see him in her peripheral vision.

"You said something in the kitchen," he said. "That you needed to know what he chose."

"Yes."

"And you found out."

"Yes."

"But it doesn't help."

She looked at him. "I told you that already."

"I know." He had the notebook but it was closed on the table in front of him. "I'm trying to understand what it doesn't help with. What the gap is."

She was quiet for a long time. Long enough that she thought she might simply let the silence sit until it moved the conversation somewhere else. She'd done that before. She was good at it.

"Understanding why someone chose something doesn't tell you whether they were right," she said finally. "My father chose to jump into fires. I understand why he chose it. I chose it too." She looked at the deployment report in front of her. "But I watched my mother spend thirty years with the consequence of that choice. And I can't know if he'd have made it the same way if he'd known what the consequence would be."

The room was quiet.

"So you made a different choice," Reid said.

She looked at him.

"You understand what he chose," Reid said, "and you chose the work but not the rest of it. The part where someone else carries the consequence."

She didn't answer. Not because she was deflecting. Because he was right and she didn't have anywhere to go with that right now that wouldn't cost her more than she was prepared to spend in this room.

He seemed to understand this. He opened the notebook. Not to write. Just to have something to do with his hands that wasn't looking at her.

She picked up the pen and went back to the report.

They stayed in the room another twenty minutes. She worked. He read something he'd written earlier. The crew sounds came through the wall.

When she was done she gathered the reports and stood.

"For the documentary," she said.

He looked up.

"My father. You can use the public record. The facts of it." She met his eyes. "The rest is off record."

"Understood." He held her eyes for a moment. "I would have asked before I used anything."

"I know," she said. "I'm telling you anyway."

She left the room and walked down the corridor to the equipment bay and stood there for a moment in the smell of gear and oil and the particular dry heat of a July afternoon, her hands at her sides, not doing anything.

She picked up the next thing on the list.

She was good at that. It was one of the things she was best at.

She didn't examine what it said about her that it had gotten easier to say her father's name to Reid than to most people she'd known for years. She put it on the list of things she'd work out later.

The list was getting long.

---

That night she called her mother.

She did this once a week, Sunday evenings usually, a rhythm she'd maintained for years. Her mother lived in Bend, forty minutes south, and they saw each other in the off-season and talked on the phone during it and the conversations were warm and careful and covered the things that could be covered. Her mother asked about the crew. She asked about the garden. They talked about weather and food and the neighbors her mother had opinions about.

They did not talk about her father. They hadn't in years. It wasn't a rule anyone had articulated. It was just the shape of what was survivable for both of them.

Tonight she called on a Thursday. Her mother answered on the second ring.

"You're not calling on Sunday," her mother said. Not accusatory. Noticing.

"I know."

"Is everything all right?"

"Yes," she said. "I just wanted to call."

Her mother was quiet for a moment, processing this the way she processed things, carefully and without rushing. She'd been a teacher before her father died, middle school science, and she still had the quality of someone who waited for the actual question to emerge before answering the question she'd been asked.

"Something happened at work," her mother said.

"Nothing bad." She was sitting on the edge of her bunk, looking at the window. Full dark outside. "We have a journalist embedded for the season. He's been doing research. Background on the crew."

"And he found your father."

"Public record," she said. "He found the public record."

Her mother made a sound that wasn't quite a word. The sound of someone setting something down carefully.

"Did you talk to him about it?"

"Some."

"Was that all right?"

She thought about the briefing room. Reid at the map with his back to her, which had been the right angle. The notebook closed on the table. The way he'd said I'm sorry and moved on without making her manage his having said it.

"Yes," she said. "Actually yes."

Her mother was quiet again. Then: "You know, your father would have liked being documented. He always thought what they did wasn't visible enough. That people didn't understand what it took."

She hadn't known that. Or she'd known it somewhere, some old peripheral knowledge, and had stopped being able to access it.

"He would have been terrible at it," her mother said, and laughed softly. "He would have talked too much and shown off and driven the documentary person absolutely crazy."

She laughed too. The sound of it surprised her a little.

"Probably," she said.

They talked for another twenty minutes. The garden, a neighbor, a book her mother was reading. The ordinary things. And underneath them, carried by neither of them directly, the warmth of a conversation that had touched something real and survived it.

She hung up and sat for a moment in the dark room.

She thought about her father talking too much and showing off. She thought about him at thirty-one, in love with the work the way she was in love with the work, not yet knowing that one of his jumps would be the last. She thought about her mother laughing.

The grief she usually kept at a specific distance had moved tonight, shifted its weight, and she'd felt it but it hadn't been unbearable. It had been more like the ache of a muscle that hadn't been used in a long time, being used.

She didn't know what to do with that either.

She added it to the list and went to sleep.

Chapter 12

The weather delay came on a Sunday night.

They'd been staged for a deployment since late afternoon, a fire forty miles northeast that had gone from monitored to active in three hours, the kind of escalation that happened when the fuel was ready and the wind was ready and everything the season had been building toward finally arrived at its occasion. She'd had the crew prepped and waiting by five. By seven the air support coordinator had grounded them. A convective cell had moved in over the jump zone and the turbulence was in a range that made the jump unacceptable. They'd wait it out.

The crew dispersed to the readiness room, the particular restless patience of people who'd prepared to act and been told to wait. She stayed in the operations room monitoring the weather data. At some point she became aware that Reid had pulled a chair to the corner of the room and was writing, the notebook on his knee, not a camera in sight.

By ten o'clock the crew was asleep in shifts. She was still at the monitor. The convective cell was moving but slowly.

At ten-thirty Reid closed the notebook.

"Tell me something about your first season," he said.

She looked at him. "Why."

"Because we have three hours and I'd rather know something true about you than watch weather data that isn't going to change for ninety minutes."

She looked back at the monitor. The cell was a yellow mass on the radar, slow-moving, patient.

"I was twenty-four," she said. "First season out of training. I was the youngest person on my crew by four years." She paused. "I was scared the entire time and I didn't tell anyone."

"Scared of the fire?"

"Scared of making a wrong call in front of experienced people. The fire I could read. The people were harder." She watched the cell on the radar. "I spent six months being very precise and very quiet and hoping nobody asked me a question I didn't have the answer to."

"Did they?"

"Constantly." She allowed a small amount of something that wasn't quite a smile. "I was right about half the time. Which turned out to be enough."

He was quiet for a moment. "Is that when you learned to track people the way you do?"

"What way."

"The way where you know things about them before they know you're paying attention."

She considered how to answer that. The honest answer was that she'd been tracking people her whole life, starting in the years after her father died when her mother's emotional state had become a variable she needed to read accurately to know how to be in a given moment. She'd developed it for survival before she'd developed it for leadership, and it had turned out the skills were identical.

She didn't say that.

"First season," she said. "I had a crew leader who made a call I thought was wrong. I didn't say anything. The call worked out, but I spent three weeks running the scenario in my head trying to figure out whether I'd been wrong or lucky." She paused. "After that I started paying closer attention. Not to second-guess. To understand."

"Did you figure it out? That scenario."

"Eventually. He'd seen something in the fuel pattern that I hadn't seen yet. I found the same pattern on a different fire three months later and recognized it." She looked at the radar. "That's the thing about experience. It's not faster processing. It's more things to match against."

He wrote something in the notebook. She watched him without remarking on it.

"Your turn," she said.

He looked up.

"First assignment," she said. "Something you were scared of."

He thought about it. She liked that he thought about it, that he didn't have an answer queued.

"City council meetings," he said.

She looked at him.

"My first assignment out of journalism school was a mid-sized paper in Ohio. I covered city council for fourteen months. I was terrified of getting a fact wrong in print. Of misquoting someone, getting a number wrong, having to run a correction." He looked at the notebook. "I quadruple-checked everything. Called back every source. My editor thought I was the most thorough reporter she'd ever had. I was actually just scared."

"What changed?"

"I got a fact wrong," he said. "A budget figure. Got it wrong by forty thousand dollars. Had to run the correction." He paused. "It was fine. Nobody died. The paper printed the correction, I called the councilman to apologize, he said he'd seen worse, and life continued."

"And then you weren't scared anymore."

"And then I was scared of different things." He turned a page in the notebook. "The list just changes. It doesn't get shorter."

She thought about that. She thought about her own list, which had changed shape over twelve seasons but never actually reduced. You got competent at the current fears and new ones moved in to replace them.

"What are you scared of now?" she said.

He looked at her for a moment. Outside, the wind had picked up, she could hear it finding the loose panel on the north face of the building, the familiar lift and settle.

"That I'll make something that's almost true," he said. "That I'll get the facts right and the record right and miss the actual thing." He paused. "I've done it before. Done it well enough that nobody noticed but me. I'd rather fail completely and visibly than succeed at the wrong thing."

She was quiet. The radar cell moved another increment to the northeast.

"Is that what you think might happen here?" she said.

"It's what I'm working against," he said. "The thing I'm most afraid of with this one specifically is that I'll make something that looks like you and isn't you. Because you're."

He stopped.

She waited.

"You're harder to see accurately than anyone I've filmed," he said, carefully. "Not because you're hiding. Because the visible parts are so complete that they read as the whole picture."

She held his eyes. The room was quiet, just the wind and the radar signal and the hum of the equipment.

"And you think there's a whole picture," she said.

"I know there's a whole picture," he said. "I've been watching you for three months."

She looked back at the radar. The cell had shifted. She leaned forward and checked the updated projection. The jump zone would be clear in an hour and twenty minutes.

"We'll be in the air by midnight," she said.

He looked at the screen. "What do you need from me?"

"Wake Vasquez. He'll wake the others." She stood and reached for her radio. "And get your gear on."

He was at the door before she'd finished the sentence.

She stood for a moment after he left, looking at the radar. The cell was moving northeast now, the jump zone opening in its wake. She could see the fire on the thermal imaging, a cluster of heat signatures that had spread in the last two hours.

She thought about you're harder to see accurately than anyone I've filmed.

She thought about I know there's a whole picture.

She picked up her radio and got to work.

---

The jump was clean. The fire was what the thermals had said it was, a drainage fire that had backed into the timber and needed a line cut above it before it reached the upper slope where the fuel load was catastrophic. She put the crew on it and the crew worked it and by four in the morning they had the line and by six they had containment and by eight they were on the ground waiting for extraction.

Reid sat near her at the edge of the LZ. Not next to her. Near her. The exhausted proximity of people who had been working alongside each other through the night and were now allowed to simply be in the same space.

He didn't write. He had the notebook but he didn't open it. He looked at the smoke column in the early morning light and she looked at it too and they sat there in the quiet with the quality of something that had been said and received and was now just present between them.

She thought about I know there's a whole picture.

She thought about the fact that three months ago that sentence would have been threatening and now it was something else. Not not-threatening. But the threat was different. Not that he'd expose something she'd hidden. More that he'd see something she hadn't looked at in a long time and she'd have to see it too.

The extraction helicopter came in low from the west. She stood and the crew stood and they loaded out.

On the flight back she slept for the first time in the air, something she never did, her head against the bulkhead and her body making the decision before her mind could prevent it. She woke twenty minutes out from base and looked out the window at the Oregon landscape coming back into recognizable shapes, the high desert giving way to the foothills, the foothills to the pines.

She didn't look at whether Reid had noticed she'd slept. She looked at the landscape.

But she knew, without looking, that the answer was yes.

Chapter 13

The harness needed two people.

It was a secondary rig, modified for a non-jumper, and the fitting process required someone to hold the back panel while the other worked the front buckles. Standard procedure when you had a civilian in the field who might need emergency egress equipment. She'd fit it on him before the first deployment and it had been operational and impersonal and she hadn't thought about it after.

This time Vasquez was on a call with the air coordinator and Torres was working the cache inventory and Kowalski was somewhere and she was running behind and Reid was standing in the equipment bay with the harness and a deadline and no one else available.

"Turn around," she said.

He turned.

She reached around him and pulled the back panel into alignment, feeling for the clips through the material, working by touch the way you worked in low light or with gloves. She'd fit hundreds of harnesses. The process was automatic.

Except that she was aware, in a way she hadn't been the first time, of the proximity. The specific geometry of fitting a harness on another person required you to be close. She was behind him and then beside him checking the shoulder straps and then in front of him working the chest buckle, close enough to smell the particular combination of soap and wood smoke and something that was just him, close enough to see the line of his jaw and the way he was looking at the wall slightly past her head, not looking at her.

Not looking at her carefully. The care in it visible.

She worked the chest buckle. The clip was stiff from the cold morning and she had to work it twice before it seated. She was aware of the pressure of her hands against the front of the rig, against the material there, and she was aware that he'd taken a breath that he was now holding, and she was aware that neither of them was talking, which was not unusual during a fitting but felt unusual now.

She got the buckle seated.

She should step back. She'd done the buckle. She should step back and run the load check and they'd be done.

She ran her hand from the chest buckle up to the left shoulder strap, checking the tension, which was the next step and was required and was not a reason to not step back.

The strap was fine.

She moved to the right shoulder strap. Fine.

She was still not stepping back.

"Remy," he said.

Her name in his voice. The particular register of it, quiet and even, a statement rather than a question.

She stepped back.

"Load check," she said. She ran the sequence. She checked every point of attachment with a thoroughness that was professional and complete and that took longer than it needed to. When she was done she stepped back again, a full step this time, and looked at the rig and not at him.

"Good," she said. "You're fitted."

"Thank you."

She turned and picked up her own pack from the bench and put it on. Her hands were steady. She was aware of this with a kind of gratitude that said more about the moment than she wanted it to.

They went to the truck. She drove. He was in the passenger seat with the camera on his lap and the seatbelt on and his eyes out the window, and they drove the first ten minutes without speaking.

"Haines is holding," she said eventually.

"I know," he said.

They drove.

---

The fire that day was a containment support job, supplementing an engine crew that had a line established but needed air resources for the northeast corner where the terrain made ground access slow. Straightforward work, the kind she could run mostly on reflex, which was useful because some part of her attention was running a different process in the background and she needed the work to be manageable on reflex.

The process in the background was: what had just happened in the equipment bay.

The answer was: nothing. She'd fit a harness. He'd said her name. She'd stepped back. That was the full accounting. Nothing had happened.

The process kept running anyway.

She put the crew on the northeast corner and worked alongside Kowalski on the lower anchor while Vasquez and Torres pushed the line. It was hot work, mid-August heat in a drainage with no wind, the kind of afternoon that ate through your water faster than you tracked. She kept an eye on everyone's consumption without making it obvious.

Reid was on the ridge above the anchor, filming, camera up and steady. She could see him from where she was working. He wasn't looking at her.

She looked at her work and not at him.

At the midday break she drank water and ate and thought about harness fittings, which were impersonal and procedural and which she'd done hundreds of times. She thought about the chest buckle that had been stiff. She thought about him saying her name and what he'd meant by it.

He'd meant: I know what's happening right now. That was all. He'd said her name the way you said someone's name to bring a moment to a close without naming the moment. Carefully. So that neither of them had to account for it.

She was grateful for that too, which told her something she was not ready to look at directly.

She put her water bottle away and went back to work.

---

On the flight home she sat across from him instead of beside him, the way the aircraft seating fell. He was looking at footage on the camera screen, headphones on, and she was doing the after-action documentation on the tablet, and they were in the same aircraft and not in each other's space and it was fine.

It was fine.

She finished the documentation and looked out the window. The sun was going down behind them as they flew west, the landscape below going from late afternoon gold to shadow in the way it did this time of year, fast and absolute.

She thought about the way the buckle had felt when it finally seated. The small physical certainty of a thing going where it was designed to go.

She thought about stepping back.

She thought about the fact that she'd had to tell herself to step back, and that it had not been immediate, and that the not-immediate was different from anything she'd managed before in twelve seasons and several situations that had required management.

The aircraft banked and she looked at the mountains below. They were beautiful in the way things were beautiful when you were tired enough to stop filtering. She let herself see them for a moment.

She closed her eyes and thought about tomorrow's briefing and what the Haines Index was going to say.

Behind her eyelids the buckle seated. His name in her mouth, not said. The three seconds before she'd stepped back. The specific way her hands had known where to go.

She did not open her eyes for a long time.

When she did, the mountains were still there.

She could handle it. She told herself this.

But the list of things she was telling herself was getting longer than the list of things she simply knew, and she was aware of the distinction, and it was the kind of distinction she'd built her whole system on.

Chapter 14

The call came on a Tuesday and by Thursday it was the biggest fire of the season.

She'd known it was coming. The Haines Index had been there for two weeks. The drainage she'd circled in red on the regional map in late July was the one that went first, a lightning strike at 0200 that the initial aerial assessment put at eighty acres and climbing. By the time she had the crew in the air it was two hundred. By the time they were on the ground it was six hundred and the air coordinator was pulling in resources from two other regions.

This was not a one-crew fire. She knew that immediately, the way you knew things that were visible from the air. The smoke column was massive, the kind that generated its own weather, convective currents that bent the surrounding air and made the behavior unpredictable in ways that changed faster than you could model. She'd worked fires like this before. They had a personality. They did not behave.

She briefed the crew in the air, over the noise of the aircraft, and their faces had the particular quality she recognized from the serious deployments: present, focused, the usual individual textures all reduced to the same thing, which was readiness. Even Kowalski, who had an opinion about everything, was just looking at the maps.

Reid was in the rear jump seat. She'd told him twice he didn't have to come on this one, that he could stay with the air coordinator and get footage from there, that this was going to be extended and difficult and the field conditions would not be comfortable.

He'd said he was coming.

She'd told him the hold position rule applied more strictly on a fire like this, that when she said hold she meant it absolutely, that if anything happened that put the crew in a position where they needed to move fast there would not be time to locate him and he needed to be where she'd put him.

He'd said he understood.

She'd looked at him for a moment. Then she'd turned back to the maps.

---

The drop zone was a ridge to the west of the fire's head, the last defensible position before a fuel-heavy slope that would go if the fire made a run north. She put the crew down in two groups, a minute apart, and had them moving to assigned positions before the second group had fully staged. The pace was different from smaller deployments. Everything was faster and the stakes of speed were higher and she was aware of both simultaneously.

She put Reid on the ridge at the drop zone with a clear sight line to the operation below and told him: here.

He didn't argue.

She went to work.

The fire's behavior in the first six hours was aggressive but readable. She made four line decisions, each one the product of terrain analysis and fuel assessment and wind observation and the particular intuition that was just pattern recognition from twelve seasons compressed into a read that felt instant but wasn't. The crew moved when she told them to move and held when she told them to hold and nobody got hurt.

At hour seven the wind shifted.

Not the predictable afternoon shift, a quarter turn, manageable. A full reversal, out of the northwest at thirty miles per hour, the kind of event that turned fire decisions into fire problems within minutes. She keyed her radio before the shift hit the crew positions.

"All units, wind reversal incoming. North anchor hold position. South group pull to the black, now."

She was moving while she talked, heading toward Vasquez's position on the south flank, because Vasquez was farthest from the black and had the most ground to cover and she needed eyes on him.

The wind hit. The fire responded the way fire responded to a reversal, like a thing that had been held back and was now given permission. It surged north, torching through the canopy at the western edge of their line, the sound of it different from steady burning, a deeper register, more continuous.

She found Vasquez's crew and moved them and they moved clean and she counted heads and everyone was there and she was already on the radio to the north anchor checking position when she became aware of something she shouldn't have needed to be aware of.

Where Reid was.

She'd put him on the ridge at the drop zone. The drop zone was now in the path of where the fire was going.

She keyed her radio on the second channel.

"Matthews. Status."

Static. Then: "Ridge position. Fire's moving north fast."

"Get off the ridge. Move east along the fence line, away from the drainage. Do it now."

"Copy."

She turned back to Vasquez. Head count. All present. She radioed the north anchor. Holding.

She made herself not think about Reid while she worked the next twenty minutes, which were the most critical twenty minutes of the day and required everything she had. She worked them. The line held where the line needed to hold and she let go where she needed to let go and by the end of the twenty minutes the fire had made its run and was in terrain she'd chosen for it to be in and the crew was intact and she could breathe.

She keyed the radio.

"Matthews."

"East side of the ridge. Clear."

She let out a breath that she hadn't let out yet.

"Stay there," she said. "I'm coming to you."

She found him twenty minutes later, sitting on a rock at the eastern end of the ridge with the camera on his knee. He was looking at the smoke column. The camera was not raised.

She stopped ten feet from him.

He looked up. He looked, she thought, like someone who had been in close proximity to a large and indifferent thing and had understood its indifference clearly for the first time. Not shaken. More calibrated. Like the measurement he'd been using had just been corrected.

"You're all right," she said. It came out less flat than she intended.

"Yes." He paused. "Are you?"

"I'm doing a head count." She looked at him. "You're the last one."

He looked at her steadily. "I'm all right," he said. "Thank you for the radio call."

She nodded once. She needed to get back to the crew.

She turned to go and he said, "Remy."

She stopped.

"That was," he said, and paused, and seemed to decide against whatever he'd been going to say. "I understand the job better now than I did this morning."

She stood there for a moment. The smoke column behind him was massive and shifting, the sky above it a particular orange-gray she'd seen on the worst days of bad seasons. Down below, her crew was pulling line and checking positions and doing the ongoing work of a fire that wasn't finished yet.

"Come on," she said. "I need you where I can see you."

He stood and followed her back down the ridge.

She did not think about the four seconds it had taken her to make the radio call. The four seconds she'd spent being aware of where he was instead of being fully present in the position of her crew.

She did not think about it. She put it away and worked the rest of the day and worked it well and everyone went home.

She thought about it that night. In the dark of her bunk with the sounds of the base settling around her, she thought about four seconds. About the particular texture of those four seconds. About what it meant that she'd spent them the way she had.

She thought about a drainage in the Ochocos. A different four seconds. A different cost.

She lay there for a long time before she slept.

Chapter 15

The fire ran for four more days.

She worked it the way she worked everything, methodically, with full attention, making the calls as they came and not spending more than necessary on the ones already made. The crew rotated through rest cycles. Mutual aid resources came in from three regions. By day two the incident command structure had expanded to thirty people and she was coordinating with two other crew leads, which required a different kind of management than running her own crew, more translation, more interpretation, more time spent making sure the picture she had was the picture they had.

She didn't sleep more than four hours at a stretch. She ate when the schedule allowed it. She checked the Haines Index every two hours even when she already knew what it said.

Reid was in the field for all of it.

Not always with her crew, on the larger deployment. Sometimes with the engine crews working the south flank, sometimes at the air operations point where the logistics coordinator was managing the retardant drops. He moved through the expanded incident command the same way he moved through everything, patient, present, not requiring management. She'd hear from Vasquez or Torres where he'd been spotted and she'd make a note and move on.

On the third day he came back to her crew's sector.

She was working through a ridge assessment with Kowalski when she became aware of him behind and to her right, camera up. She kept working. The ridge assessment was technical and required Kowalski's full attention and her own and she couldn't manage additional variables.

But she was aware of him. Specifically aware, in the way she'd been aware of him since August, the particular signal of his presence that had become part of how she read the space around her.

She finished the ridge assessment. Kowalski went to implement it. She turned.

Reid was filming the smoke column to the north, the camera tracking slowly across the column's base where it was doing something she'd been watching for twenty minutes. The column was leaning. When they leaned they were talking to you and the message was always the same: the pressure system above had changed and whatever the fire was about to do, it would do it faster than the forecast suggested.

She was on the radio before the sentence was finished in her head.

The column leaned another degree. She watched it while she talked.

Then she heard the sound. Not the fire, not the crew, something underneath both of them. A low sustained roar from the north drainage that meant the column wasn't just leaning. The fire had made its decision.

"All units, north drainage is running. Clear the south slope, move to anchor positions, do it now."

The crew moved. She moved with them, not away from the drainage but laterally, to the position she'd preselected two days ago for exactly this scenario, the saddle in the ridge that would let them watch the run without being in it.

She counted heads as they moved. Vasquez, Torres, Petrov, the two seasonal crew, Kowalski, Danny.

Danny was moving but he was moving stiff, his left leg doing the thing it sometimes did when he'd been on his feet too long on broken ground. She'd been watching it for days. He was making the distance but not with margin.

She came up beside him without breaking stride.

"I've got it," he said.

"I know." She matched his pace, not faster, just alongside. "How's the knee."

"Fine."

"Danny."

"It's fine, Remy."

She looked at the terrain ahead of them. The saddle was two hundred meters. The fire's roar was getting louder, which meant the drainage below was feeding it, which meant they had more time than it sounded like but not unlimited time.

"Set your pace," she said. "I'm with you."

He didn't argue. He found his pace and she matched it and they made the saddle with two minutes to spare and watched from the ridge as the fire came up out of the drainage below in a column of flame and heat that bent the air above them and made the light go orange and strange.

It was enormous. That was the thing about the big ones. Even after twelve seasons they were enormous.

She felt the heat on her face. She kept the count in her head, all seven of her crew at the anchor, and she didn't need to count again but she did anyway.

All present.

She let the breath go.

---

It was Danny who told her about Reid.

They were at the anchor, watching the fire come down, and Danny was next to her and he said, quietly, without preamble: "Your journalist put the camera down."

She looked at him.

"About fifteen minutes ago," Danny said. "When Petrov was moving to position. He went sideways on that loose shale above the saddle and Reid was behind him and when Petrov went down Reid caught him. Dropped the camera to do it."

She looked out at the fire. The column was still running, still massive, but the direction was now taking it east of them and the danger window was narrowing.

"Petrov's all right?" she said.

"Petrov's fine. Bruised. Would have been worse without the catch." Danny paused. "Reid went back for the camera after. It was still running."

She was quiet.

She thought about the moment on the ridge two days ago when Reid had said I understand the job better now than I did this morning. She thought about him filming from the beginning of the season, always with the camera, the professional distance of the lens between him and the world he was documenting.

She thought about the camera on the ground in the shale.

"He didn't even think about it," Danny said. Not a question.

"No," she said. "He didn't."

Danny looked at the fire for a moment. Then he looked at her with the direct unhurried way he had. "You've been watching him for months," Danny said. "I've been watching you watching him."

She didn't answer.

"That's all I'm saying," Danny said.

He moved away to check on Petrov.

She stood at the saddle and watched the fire run east and thought about a camera on the ground in the shale and a man who had put it there without thinking.

She didn't go find Reid that evening. She did the after-action and the crew briefing and the coordination with the incident command and the equipment check and all of it in sequence and correctly and when it was done she went to the tent that was serving as her quarters for the duration and lay on the cot and looked at the canvas ceiling.

She thought about what Danny had said. She thought about it the way she thought about things that were true and that she couldn't do anything immediate with. She held it.

She turned over. Outside, the fire was still burning. She could smell it even in here, the smoke that had been in her clothes and her hair for four days, and she lay in the tent listening to it and thought about a man who had put a camera down without thinking about it.

She slept eventually. The thought was still there in the morning.

Chapter 16

They got back to base on a Thursday afternoon, five days after the call had come in.

The crew unloaded in the silence of people who had been working at the edge of their capacity for an extended period and were now, suddenly, not required to. Not depleted, exactly. More like a wire that had been under tension for days and was now slack, still functional but needing a moment before it remembered its own shape.

She supervised the gear breakdown and the equipment return and the preliminary after-action documentation. She debriefed with Okafor for forty minutes. She checked in with each crew member individually, the quick assessment she made after every extended deployment, looking for the signs of things that needed attention. Everyone was tired. Kowalski had a blister that needed looking at. Danny's knee had stiffened on the drive back and she told him to ice it and come see her tomorrow and he said he would, which meant he would.

She did all of it and then she showered and changed and stood in the middle of her room for a moment with nowhere to be for the first time in five days.

Outside, the late afternoon light was the gold of early September, the days already shorter than July, the angle of it lower and more patient. Through her window she could see the eastern tree line and above it a sky so clean and blue it looked like the fire season hadn't happened, like the smoke of the last two weeks had been a thing she'd imagined.

She went to find Reid.

---

He was in his room at the end of the hall, the door half-open, sitting at the folding table with the monitor going and the footage from the deployment spread across a timeline on the screen. He looked up when she appeared in the doorway.

He looked like she felt. Tired in the way of the past five days, not sharp-edged tired but the deeper kind, the kind that lived in the body rather than the mind. There were two mugs on the table. One empty. One still steaming.

He pushed the full one toward the edge of the table nearest her without saying anything.

She came in and picked it up and sat down in the other chair, the one she'd pulled up weeks ago to watch the footage of her hands. She wrapped both hands around the mug and looked at the timeline on the monitor.

The footage was extraordinary. She could see that without effort. The smoke column on day one, its color and motion, something the camera had caught that the eye registered differently in real time. The crew working the south flank in the flat light of a smoke-filtered afternoon. Danny and her, side by side, moving toward the saddle at matched pace.

She hadn't known he'd been filming that.

"The camera kept running," she said.

"Yes."

"When you dropped it."

He looked at the monitor. "Yes. I watched it later. Seven seconds of shale and then sky."

She looked at the cup in her hands. The coffee was good, the real kind, not the pot in the mess. He'd made it in the small French press she'd seen on his equipment table once and not remarked on.

"Petrov told me," she said. "On the drive back."

"It wasn't a decision," Reid said. "I want to be clear about that. It wasn't me deciding the camera could wait. It was just. He was going down and the camera stopped mattering."

She looked at him.

"I know that's not a remarkable thing," he said. "People catch people. It's not."

"It is," she said. "Here. In the field. With the camera." She held his eyes. "You've been filming for eleven years. That camera is how you see."

He was quiet.

"I know what it cost," she said.

He looked at the monitor. On the timeline, the footage from the drop continued. The saddle. The fire coming up from the drainage, the column of it, the heat distortion in the air above. Her face turned toward the fire, jaw set, reading it. She hadn't known he'd gotten that either.

"What I know about you," he said, not looking at her. "What I've been documenting for five months. The way you move through this. The way you read things. The anchor sequence, three times." He paused. "Your father. The way you said you understood what he chose."

She was very still.

"I've been making a documentary about a smokejumper crew," he said. "And somewhere in the last five months it stopped being that."

She looked at him.

He looked back. The room was quiet, the base settling into its evening sounds around them, and outside the September light was going gold and low and patient.

"I know," she said. Quietly. Simply.

He leaned forward and set his own mug down on the table and she put hers down too, unhurried, and when she looked up he was still looking at her with that level undefended attention that she'd been trying to read correctly for five months and had finally, tonight, read correctly.

She leaned across the table and kissed him.

Not tentative. She was not a tentative person and this was not a moment for tentativeness. It was a decision she'd arrived at through five months of evidence and she made it the way she made all her decisions, completely, and his hand came up and found the side of her face and stayed there with a warmth that went through her like something structural giving way.

She pulled back enough to look at him.

His expression was something she didn't have a category for and didn't try to make one. She just let it be what it was.

"I've been making a significant effort not to do that," she said.

"I know," he said. "I noticed."

"That's embarrassing."

"No." His thumb moved slightly against her jaw. "No, it's not."

She looked at him for another moment. She was aware of her own pulse in a way she was not normally aware of it, not alarm, not fear, just the body registering something real.

"Reid," she said.

"Yes."

"I need you to understand something before this goes further."

"All right."

"I don't do this." She held his eyes. "Not because I can't. Because I've made choices about my life that this complicates. And I need you to know that going in."

He looked at her steadily. "What choices."

"I don't let people in far enough to matter at a level I can't manage." She said it plainly, the way she said things that were simply true. "I've been very good at it for a long time. And you're the first person in a long time who's made that feel like something I'm doing rather than something I simply am."

He was quiet for a moment. His hand was still at her jaw.

"I'm not asking you to stop being who you are," he said.

"I know."

"I'm not asking for anything you haven't already given."

She looked at him. She thought about the kitchen in the rain and the fence line and the briefing room and his name said quietly in the equipment bay and the footage of her hands moving over a map and a camera on the ground in the shale.

"You've been asking for things the whole time," she said. "You've just been asking the right way."

His expression shifted. Not a smile, something quieter.

"Is that a yes?" he said.

"It's a yes," she said. "I'm aware of what I'm doing."

He looked at her for a long moment and then he said, "Come here," and it was the first time he'd asked her for something directly, plainly, without the careful patience that had been his register since May, and something in the directness of it went through her the same way the first kiss had, structural, irrevocable.

She went.

---

She had thought she knew what this would be. She'd had a version of it in the back of her mind since July, some managed anticipation she'd kept at distance, a background sense of what it would require. She had been wrong about almost all of it.

Not wrong about the wanting. She'd known the wanting accurately enough. But she'd imagined she would manage this the way she managed other things that were difficult and real, from a slight remove, some part of her always slightly outside what was happening, observing and categorizing even as the rest of her participated. That was how she operated. She'd thought it would be the same.

It wasn't the same.

He kissed her the way the rest of him worked, unhurried, thorough, like he'd made a decision and intended to honor it completely. His hands moved to her waist and she felt the warmth of them through her shirt and that warmth was specific in a way she hadn't expected, specific to him, to his hands, and she understood that the months of watching those hands had lodged something in her that the reality of them was now paying out.

She pulled back enough to look at him. His expression was still that unreadable thing. She reached up and set her hand against his jaw, the slight roughness of it, feeling him the way she felt equipment she trusted. Learning it.

"I thought about your hands," she said. "In July. I've been thinking about your hands since July."

He looked at her for a moment. Then he said, "Good," and his hands moved with intention and she stopped having thoughts that were separate from what was happening.

He was deliberate about it. Not slow, deliberate, which was different. There was a difference between someone proceeding carefully and someone who knew exactly what they were doing and had decided to do it completely. She had spent five months reading how Reid Matthews approached things that mattered to him. She should not have been surprised. She was surprised anyway.

Her shirt. His. The particular unhurriedness of his hands finding her, not rushed, not tentative, moving with the same precision she'd watched him bring to everything else. She had thought she'd be self-conscious. She wasn't. She was only aware of him, of his mouth at her throat, of the weight of him, of her own hands learning the geography of his shoulders, the line of his spine.

At some point she said his name and he answered her without words and the answer went through her like the first kiss had, like something giving way at a load-bearing point.

She had thought the wanting would be manageable once it had an object. She had been wrong about that too. The wanting had an object and the object was here and present and entirely specific and it was not manageable in any register she possessed. She didn't try to manage it. She let it be what it was, all of it, without keeping any part of herself outside it to watch.

That was the thing she hadn't expected. That there was no outside of it. She was entirely there, in her body, in the room, in the dark behind her eyelids, in the specific heat of his breath against her neck. She was completely present in a way she was only otherwise present in a fire, when the terrain required everything and the self that observed got quiet and the self that acted was all that remained.

This was that. But the opposite of fire. This was the thing she'd been building walls against.

She understood that clearly. Somewhere in the middle of it she understood it clearly and did not stop, and later that would seem like the most important thing: that she had understood it and continued anyway.

He said her name. Not at the end of something, in the middle of it, quiet, like it had surfaced on its own, like something he'd been carrying that had finally found its way out. She heard it the way she'd been hearing her name in his voice for five months, the register of it low and specific and entirely hers, and something in her chest cracked open along a seam she hadn't known was there.

She pressed her face against his neck and held on.

She would carry that for a long time. Not the pleasure of it, though she would carry that too. The specific sound of her name in his voice at that moment, like it was something he needed to say and had finally been allowed to say it.

---

Afterward, in the quiet of his room with the evening dark at the window and the base making its nighttime sounds at a remove, she lay still and looked at the ceiling.

She was aware of his breathing beside her, the warmth of him, the specific weight of the moment that was not manageable in the usual way. She was aware of the fact that she was in a situation she had designed her entire adult life to prevent and that she was not, at this particular moment, looking for the exit.

That was worth noting.

She hadn't known about this part. The part after. The quality of lying in the dark with someone and not needing the silence to be a different thing.

He wasn't asleep. She could tell by his breathing.

"You're thinking," he said.

"I'm always thinking."

"What about."

She considered what to tell him. The honest answer was that she was thinking about the fact that this had changed something and she didn't know yet what it had changed it to, and that not knowing the shape of a thing was one of the conditions she found hardest to operate in.

"I'm thinking that I'm not handling this the way I expected to," she said.

He was quiet for a moment.

"How did you expect to handle it?"

"More managed," she said. "Cleaner."

He turned his head toward her. She could feel the movement.

"Is that bad?" he said.

She thought about it honestly. She thought about the architecture she'd built and the wall she'd maintained and the care with which she'd arranged her life around a center that didn't require another person to hold its shape.

She thought about looking at the ceiling and not looking for the exit.

"I don't know yet," she said. Which was more honest than she'd intended and also exactly true.

He didn't push. He turned back to the ceiling.

After a moment his hand found hers in the dark, not urgently, just settling itself there the way things settled when they found their place.

She didn't move her hand away.

She looked at the ceiling. The base made its sounds. Outside the September night was cool and complete and the season was still running, still building toward whatever it was going to be, the ticking clock she'd been hearing since May getting incrementally louder.

She didn't think about that yet.

She let herself be in the room and in the quiet and in the warmth of his hand against hers, and she didn't examine it or file it or try to know yet what it meant.

She let it be what it was.

For now.

Chapter 17

She woke at 0430.

For a moment she didn't know where she was, which was unusual. She always knew where she was. Chapter 19

He brought it up on a Friday evening, twelve days after she'd told him she wasn't ready.

She was in the planning room, the last of the crew gone, the base settling into its night. She'd been at the deployment maps for an hour, running the late season scenarios, the fires that were still active to the northeast, the weather patterns that were going to determine the next two weeks. October was eight days away. The season was in its final chapter and she could feel it, the quality of an ending in the weather and the light and the way the crew talked about what they were doing after.

He knocked on the open door.

She looked up.

He came in and sat down in the chair across the table. Not the one near the door. The one that said I'm staying.

"Tell me," he said.

She looked at him. She thought about saying not yet, which was still true, which would still work. She thought about the twelve days she'd spent with the knowledge that he knew, and what it had been like to carry that alongside everything else she was carrying.

She thought about I'm not going to make this difficult for you. About the way he'd held her hand in the dark and not asked anything of it.

"The Ochocos fire," she said. "Three years ago. We were in the drainage working a line above the fire's head. The fire made a run nobody predicted. I was calling position for the crew."

She looked at the maps.

"For four seconds I wasn't there."

Reid was still.

"I was somewhere else. My father's fire, or the image I have of it from the news reports and what people told me afterward. I was nine years old standing in a doorway reading the news on my mother's face." She paused. "Four seconds. Then I came back and I called it and got the crew to the black."

Silence.

"Danny was already moving on the last position I'd called. Four seconds stale. He went down a ravine. Broken leg, smoke inhalation." She looked at her hands on the table. "He was airlifted. He survived. He came back this season. He doesn't blame me."

"I don't blame myself for the call. The call was right. I made it as soon as I came back." She looked up. "I blame myself for the four seconds. For never having dealt with my father's death in a way that meant I could stay present in a drainage that looked like his."

Reid said nothing. He was looking at her with the full weight of his attention, the thing she'd spent five months learning to read, and what she read in it now was not pity and not judgment and not the particular soft quality of someone who was about to try to fix something.

He was just there. Receiving it.

"I never told anyone," she said. "Because the call was right and Danny survived and there was nothing operational to report. The four seconds didn't change the outcome."

"But they're still in you," he said.

"Yes."

"Because you should have been present and you weren't."

"Because I've spent fifteen years telling myself that I do this cleanly. That I don't carry things into the field that belong somewhere else." She heard her own voice and noted the quality of it, the particular flatness that meant she was close to something. "And on that jump I found out I do."

He was quiet for a long time. Outside the base was its nighttime self, the pines in a light wind, the refrigeration unit, the sounds she'd learned to not hear.

"Does Danny know?" Reid said.

"About the four seconds? No."

"Would it help him to know?"

She looked at him. The question surprised her. Not because it was wrong, but because it was a different question than the one she'd been living with.

"I don't know," she said honestly.

"Because it might hurt him," Reid said. "Knowing that you froze. Or it might help him. Knowing that you've been carrying it."

She hadn't let herself get that far in the analysis. She'd stopped at the outcome: the call was right, Danny was alive, nothing operational to report. She'd kept the four seconds in a category by themselves and hadn't followed the logic further.

"I don't know," she said again.

Reid leaned forward, elbows on the table. He looked at her steadily.

"I have the cache log footage," he said. "From the day I found the notation. I didn't plan to film it. The camera was running and I picked it up and put the cap on it."

She was still.

"I haven't watched it," he said. "I don't know what I did in that moment. I'd rather you know that I don't know."

She looked at him. She thought about the day she'd watched herself on his monitor, hands on the map, teaching without knowing she was teaching. She thought about what it was to be seen doing something you hadn't chosen to reveal.

"Why are you telling me that?" she said.

"Because you've been carrying the four seconds alone for three years," he said. "And I found out through a notation you didn't write for me, in a log you didn't expect anyone to read that way. And I think the least I can do is be honest about what I have."

The room was very quiet.

"What are you going to do with it?" she said. "The documentary."

He held her eyes. "Whatever you want me to do with it."

She stared at him. She'd expected many things in this conversation. She hadn't expected that.

"That's not how journalism works," she said.

"No," he said. "It's not."

She understood what he was telling her. She understood the cost of it.

She stood up from the table, and the chair went back hard on the floor, and she turned to the window and stood there with her back to him and her hands at her sides and looked at the dark tree line and felt something working through her that she didn't have a name for. Not anger. Not grief. Something that was moving through the architecture the way something structural moved when pressure was applied from a direction it hadn't been built for.

"Three years," she said, to the window.

"Yes."

"I've been carrying it for three years and it hasn't done one useful thing. I've been over it a thousand times. I know the call was right. I know Danny's alive. I know the four seconds were inside the time buffer. I know all of it and it doesn't help."

"No," he said. "It doesn't."

She turned around. "Don't agree with me just to agree with me."

"I'm not." He was still at the table, still steady, not moving toward her or away. "I'm agreeing because what you've been doing with it doesn't help and you know that. You've been running the calculation over and over and the calculation always comes out the same because you're asking it the wrong question."

She looked at him.

"You're asking whether the call was right," he said. "It was. You've established that ten thousand times. The call was right."

"Then what's the question."

"The question," he said, "is what you're going to do about the fact that your father is still in you. That he's still in the field with you. That you went into this work to understand what he chose and you found out and you're still in the drainage with him." He paused. "That's not a calculation problem. That's a different kind of problem."

She stood there.

The room was very quiet.

She'd expected the confrontation to be about the four seconds, about Danny, about the incident report and the professional judgment and whether she'd done the right thing. She'd had all of that prepared. She'd had the defense of it prepared.

He'd walked right past the defense.

"Reid," she said.

"Yes."

"I need you to leave."

He stood. He picked up his notebook. He walked to the door and stopped.

"I'm not going anywhere," he said. "I mean tonight. I'll be in my room."

"That's not what I meant."

A pause. "I know," he said.

He left.

She thought about a nine-year-old in a doorway reading the news on her mother's face.

She thought about a drainage in the Ochocos.

She thought about a man who'd said the question is what you're going to do about the fact that your father is still in you and then stood up and left when she'd asked him to, which was also its own kind of answer.

She turned off the light and went down the hall and did not knock on his door.

She went to her room and lay down in the dark and thought about what he'd said until she found the edge of sleep.

She crossed it angry. She woke up knowing something she had not let herself know.

Chapter 20

Chapter 21

Chapter 22

His hands.

"I know."

She thought about four seconds in a drainage. About a nine-year-old in a doorway. About three years of carrying something that was true and that she hadn't known how to put down because putting it down felt like it would require her to look at something else, something underneath the four seconds, which was the grief she'd never let herself have because she'd been too busy building a wall that kept the grief inside and everyone else outside and calling it protection.

She looked at her own hands on the table.

She thought about what she was going to do.

She thought about what her mother had said.

She thought about what Reid had said.

She thought about the wall. It was still there. She could feel it the way you felt something structural, not as a presence but as the shape of what it held in place. Fifteen years of architecture did not dissolve in one afternoon in her mother's kitchen.

It took the rest of the drive.

She got back to the base and went inside and lay in her bunk and looked at the ceiling.

Monday was thirty-six hours away.

She was a person who made decisions. She'd always been a person who made decisions. The decision had to be made with information, not with fear, and tonight she had the information.

She let herself think about what she actually wanted.

That alone took a long time.

Chapter 23

The call came Sunday morning at 0800.

A hold-over from the northeastern cluster had moved overnight, pushed by an early October wind event into unburned fuel, and the air coordinator needed her crew for a final mop-up that had become something more serious. Twenty acres, moving, the last active fire of the season making its argument.

She had the crew in the air by 0845.

Reid was at the truck when they staged. She'd seen him coming across the lot from the operations building with his pack and camera and the look of someone who'd heard the call and made a decision. She'd told him months ago: ninety seconds from when she said go. He'd made the window.

She did not tell him he didn't have to come. She'd already made that mistake once.

The wind event had turned the fire's head northwest, which put it running toward a steep slope that would give it speed and toward a ridgeline that would give it nothing to stop on. She needed to cut it off before the ridge.

She briefed the crew in the air. They listened with the end-of-season focus of people who knew this was the last one and wanted to do it right.

She briefed Reid on channel two.

"This one's moving faster than the last three," she said. "I need you to hold at the LZ until I radio position and then come directly to me. Directly to me. If the wind changes you'll hear it. Do not move toward the fire without my position."

"Copy," he said.

She looked at him across the aircraft. He was looking back.

"Thank you for coming," she said. She hadn't planned to say it.

He held her eyes. "Yes," he said.

They flew.

---

The drop zone was a cleared ridge above the fire's northwest flank, the only viable LZ within range. She got the crew down in two groups and had them moving to the anchor positions she'd planned on the flight. The fire was louder than the report had suggested, which meant the wind was feeding it more than the overnight imagery had accounted for. She adjusted the line position and moved Torres up and put Vasquez and Danny on the south anchor.

She radioed Reid the position and he came to her and she put him thirty meters upslope with a clear line of retreat and told him to stay there.

She went to work.

The fire was aggressive but directional and she had the terrain for it, the ridgeline above working in her favor for once, the fire running toward stone rather than fuel at the north end.

Then the wind shifted.

Not a reversal. A thirty-degree rotation, but thirty degrees was enough. It pushed the fire's eastern flank into country she hadn't needed to defend and suddenly she was managing two fronts instead of one and moving personnel and it was the kind of cascade that was manageable if you caught it early and catastrophic if you didn't.

She was catching it.

She'd made the first call and was on the radio making the second when she heard it.

The sound was wrong.

It took her half a second to locate it and when she located it her body was already moving. Petrov's anchor on the upper slope, something in the fire's behavior above him that she'd been tracking in her peripheral vision and that had just crossed a threshold she'd learned to recognize before she could name it. The terrain was channeling heat from the eastern flank into his position. It wouldn't be obvious to him yet. It would be obvious in about ninety seconds and ninety seconds was not enough time.

She was on the radio.

"Petrov, abandon anchor, move directly north along the ridge, now, don't stop."

She could see his position from where she was. She saw him look up, look around, start moving.

He was moving too slowly. He didn't understand yet.

She was running.

The distance was two hundred meters up a slope that was loose shale and dry grass and she was covering it with the total focused effort of a body that had been trained for exactly this for twelve seasons. She wasn't thinking. She was moving.

She reached him and took his arm and they were moving together, north along the ridge, and she could feel the heat on the left side of her face now and she didn't look at it. She kept moving.

They cleared the position with forty seconds to spare.

She stopped on the north end of the ridge and looked back. Where Petrov's anchor had been, the fire was now doing exactly what she'd predicted. It was a clean read. It was a textbook read. She'd made it and moved in time and Petrov was standing next to her slightly wide-eyed but intact.

She counted her crew on the radio. All present.

She let the breath out.

And then, without deciding to, she looked for Reid.

He was where she'd put him, thirty meters upslope, standing at the edge of his hold position. She could see him clearly across the slope. He was looking at her. He'd watched the whole thing, she understood, from the moment she'd started running. He'd watched her run two hundred meters up a shale slope and pull her jumper out of a bad position and he hadn't moved from where she'd put him.

He'd trusted her call.

She looked at him across the smoke and the distance and she felt something go through her that wasn't the adrenaline, wasn't the relief, wasn't the cold clarity of a clean decision executed. It was simpler than all of those things. It was the recognition of a specific person in a specific place at a moment when recognition was the only response her body had left.

She hadn't made the calculation to save Petrov because Reid was watching. She'd made it because it was the right call and it was her job and her crew was her crew.

But she knew, standing there with the smoke blowing past and Petrov breathing hard beside her, that she hadn't frozen.

Not for a second. Not for a fraction of a second. She'd been completely present.

She thought about that.

She thought about a drainage in the Ochocos and a nine-year-old in a doorway and three years of a calculation that always came out the same.

She thought about what had changed.

Nothing had changed. Reid was leaving tomorrow. She'd ended it. The season was over.

Everything had changed.

She keyed the radio and got back to work.

Chapter 24

Everyone was safe by midday.

Chapter 25

All of her.
