A Word Before You Begin
 
Some gates you cross change everything. Others you barely notice until you look back and realize how far you've traveled. This book is about both kinds.
 
It's written for the quiet people carrying weight they can't name, building things others can't see, standing guard at gates most people don't know exist. It's for fathers trying to raise boys into men, for veterans learning to live with what they've seen, for anyone who's discovered that the deepest strength often speaks in whispers.
 
These aren't just my stories. They're maps through territory we all must cross. Territory where hands learn to be gentle after they've been hard, where minds learn stillness in the middle of storms, where hearts learn to hope when hope feels like risk.
 
You don't need to have worn a uniform to understand what it means to stand watch. You don't need to be a father to know what it feels like to guard something precious. You don't need to be a man to recognize quiet strength when you see it.
 
What you need is the willingness to walk beside someone who's been learning, slowly and often badly, how to build foundations that hold.
At the entrance to our property stands a red gate. We chose red so it would stand out against all the black gates that line these county roads, so people would know where to turn when they were looking for us. Over the years, that gate has become something more than a boundary marker. It's where I wait when my family comes home. It's where I learned what it means to be faithful in the small things.

Walk with me. The red gate is just ahead.




Prologue

My barrel was almost touching the window when I posted up. A round in the chamber, finger on the trigger, slack out. I could see her clearly through the glass panes, a girl pressed against her chest, trying to use her as a shield.

I had a clean shot.

The afternoon sun slanted through the window, casting everything in sharp relief. Shadows had crisp edges, and the light made even the smallest details stand out. The air was just the right temperature, not too hot, not too cold, perfectly clear, where sounds carried and the world felt vivid.  I could hear a bird that had been startled from the tree branches above, its wings beating as it flew off. A car stopped at the stop sign across from the house, blinker on to turn the opposite direction. Both neighbors on either side had no cars in their driveways, no kid toys in their yards.

I wasn’t tunnel-visioned. Time didn’t speed up; I was comfortable, calm, thinking outside the box about crossfire and backdrop, not only for me but for my partner. I considered possible shots from inside the house and other responding units that might arrive. I knew bullets could deflect through window panes, especially the lighter rounds we carried, but I was confident in the distance. As I got closer, I adjusted my aim, accounting for the way my shots would hit slightly below my red dot at very close range.

That kind of spatial awareness doesn't come from being the cool guy or naturally gifted. It only comes with training and experience.

My partner was at the front door, yelling, trying to break it down. The lock rattled against the frame with each impact, metal on metal in a rhythm that matched my heartbeat. But that red dot stayed on her the entire way as she moved through the living room, my finger still ready. The glass reflected nothing back at me, I could see straight through to the woman whose pain had finally won, using an innocent child as her final shield against a world that had become too heavy to carry.

I was ready to punch through the window pane to make the shot even cleaner if I needed to, or throw myself through the bottom part of the window where it was weakest. The calculations ran automatically: angle of entry, glass fragments, minimal risk to the child, maximum certainty of stopping the threat.

When I had that shot, it contained only calculation. With zero emotion behind it. All training. Moments like this leave zero room for emotion. But I also had more conviction because of my previous experience. If someone threatens a kid, the calculation becomes simple. That weight isn't going to be on my mind. I can pull the trigger guilt-free if it means getting that kid out of there safe. Not everyone has this same level of conviction, my previous experiences had trained emotion out of  me. 

My hands were steady. Not just mechanically steady, but morally steady. These same hands that could change a diaper or tie a shoe or ruffle hair with impossible gentleness could also deliver precise violent results when innocence was at stake. The paradox didn't trouble me anymore. It had taken years to understand, but now I knew: sometimes the most loving thing you can do is be willing to destroy what threatens those who can't protect themselves.

The woman's eyes were hollow, empty, but there was pain and maybe anger mixed in. Like looking into a well and seeing the darkness that has taken hold if. She wasn't evil. She was broken by something that wasn't her fault, someone whose darkness had finally overwhelmed her capacity to fight it. But broken doesn't mean harmless. And that little girl's life hung in the balance of decisions measured in milliseconds.

Then she set the child down. Walked to the front door. My red dot stayed on her the entire way, finger still ready. The lock clicked. The door opened. My partner rushed in, secured the situation, got the kid out safe.

It was over in seconds. No shots fired. No violence. But I had been ready. Completely ready.

The child was fine. Safe. Returned to her father while her mother got the help she needed. Another call, another day, another small victory in the long war between protection and chaos.

But that readiness, the cold precision, the moral clarity, the hands that could transform from gentleness to controlled violence in a heartbeat, that didn't come from nowhere. Those steady hands were forged through years of learning when to hold on and when to let go, when to build and when to tear down, when to trust and when to act.

That's not where this story begins. It begins with a quiet kid who preferred to keep his head down.

Introduction: Looking Up
The red gate stands between our place and the world. Right now it's catching the last of the evening light, the paint we chose to make it stand out now mellowed by weather and time into something deeper, more honest. My hands rest on the top rail, feeling the spots worn smooth by this same ritual, me, waiting here while the Texas sky turns colors that don't have names.
The chain in my hand has weight to it. Substantial. The kind of weight that says this is real, this matters, this is worth protecting. I wrap it once around my palm, feeling the links press into skin that's learned the difference between holding on and letting go.
Down the county road, I can see headlights. Too far yet to know if it's them, but my body knows the rhythm of waiting. How many times have I stood here? After grocery runs, after her furniture business calls, after the boys' adventures with their mother that always run longer than planned because she says yes to one more stop, one more moment of making ordinary Tuesday into something they'll remember.
The chatter of gravel under my boots as I shift my weight. Same sound it made the day we first walked this property, when it was just empty land and impossible dreams. We couldn't even find it on the map, but something about the roll of the hills, the scattered oaks, the way you could see for miles, we knew. Called the realtor from the car. "Offer asking. We want it."
That was after everything had fallen apart and been rebuilt. After the military said my body was done. After we lived in an RV eating mac and cheese we couldn't afford. After I learned that sometimes the strongest thing you can do is call your parents and say, "I can't do this alone."
When did you first realize that strength could be quiet?
For me, it was watching my father's hands transform. Hands that could wrestle with rusted bolts and stubborn machinery became gentle when he sat beside a dying pilot's bed. Just the presence of another veteran who understood the weight of flying nuclear missions, of carrying that responsibility until it settled in your bones. My father's hands would rest on that pilot's arm, and there was love in it. Not the word, men like them didn't say the word much. But the thing itself, unmistakable.
The headlights are closer now. It's them, I know the way the Expedition takes that last turn, maybe a little faster than necessary because the boys are arguing about something and my wife just wants to get them home.
But I'm not in a hurry. This moment, standing here between day and night, between our land and everyone else's, this is where I've learned to be still. Not the empty stillness of absence, but the kind that runs deep, like those underground rivers that carve caverns no one sees.
I didn't know I was looking for this when I was younger. Back then, I kept my head down. Stayed in my lane. Worked hard. Didn't make noise. And for a long time, I thought that was strength, the ability to carry weight without anyone noticing.
Maybe it served me for a while. Maybe it was survival. But watching my boys grow, feeling the weight of what I want to leave them, I've realized something: putting your head down isn't always noble.
Sometimes it's just easy.
The gate between silence and speech is one we all guard. But I've spent enough nights on my porch, crying quietly about men who didn't come home from helicopters I was supposed to be on, to know that holding everything in isn't strength. It's slow erosion. Like rust on metal you thought was protected.
So this is me looking up.
The Expedition slows and turns into our drive. Through the windshield, I can see them. My wife behind the wheel, beautiful in that exhausted way mothers get at the end of long days. The boys already moving in their seats, ready to be home. She rolls down the window as they pull up close.
"Dad! Guess what happened at-" "We saw a hawk!" "Mom let us-"
Their voices overlap, each story the most important thing in the world. I swing the gate closed behind them, the chain settling into place with its familiar rattle. They continue up the drive while I secure the latch.
This is what I want them to know, that I'll always be here at the gate. Not just physically waiting, but actively watching. That when life gets hard, when they feel underwater, when things don't go their way and they need a place or an ear, I'm not sitting idly by. I'm looking for them.
But more than that, I want them to have something they can hold when I'm not here to wait at gates. Something that says: This is what I learned. This is what held me up when everything was falling down. This is how you build a foundation that won't crack when the storms come.
I think about what I wish someone had told me when I was a teenager. What I'll want my sons to know when I'm gone. The lessons that only come through living them, like what I learned from a kid trying to buy a burrito with a fake credit card. Or how waiting at a red gate became my picture of faithful love. Or why my hands had to learn violence before they could understand gentleness.
This isn't a loud book. There won't be any shouting from stages or ten-step programs to follow. I don't have all the answers. I still mess up more than I'd like. But I've seen enough, failed enough, prayed enough, and watched enough to know this:
The sun's power is in its presence, not its noise.
You don't need to yell to be heard.
You don't need to fight to be strong.
And you don't need to have everything figured out to take the next faithful step.
If you're tired, that's honest. If you're doubting, that's human. If you're searching, you're not alone.
This is a book about the gates we cross and the hands that guide us through them. About building foundations that hold when the storms come. About learning when to keep your head down and when to look up. About the quiet strength that doesn't announce itself but shows up when it matters most.
I walk back to the truck, gravel shifting beneath my boots, and follow their path up the drive toward home. The gate stands secure behind me, ready for tomorrow's ritual, tomorrow's return, tomorrow's simple act of being exactly where love requires you to be.
Tonight, there will be chaos. Dinner and homework and bedtime battles. Small hands that need washing, small hearts that need settling. My wife and I will pass each other in the hallway, maybe touch hands briefly, that secret language of parents who are building something together even when they're too tired to talk about it.
And later, when the house is finally quiet, I'll stand on the porch and look at the stars. Same stars I watched in Iraq, but different now. Not because they've changed, but because I have. Because I've learned that the deepest water is the stillest. That the strongest hands are often the gentlest. That waiting at a gate for someone you love is its own kind of prayer.
This book is me looking up from all that weight I've been carrying quietly. It's me finally saying: I was here. This is what I learned. Walk with me.
The red gate will still be there tomorrow. And so will I.
But tonight, I have boys to tuck in and a wife to sit beside and a foundation to keep building, one quiet choice at a time.

Chapter 1: The Foundation Holds

Some foundations are built with concrete and steel. Others are built with hands that know when to be strong and when to be gentle. The strongest foundations, I’ve learned, are built with both, steady enough to hold when the storms come, flexible enough to bend without breaking when the pressure mounts.

I’m not writing this to preach at you.

I’m writing because I know what it feels like to sit in silence and wonder if anyone else understands what it means to carry weight quietly, to build something worth protecting, to stand guard at gates that others can’t even see.

I don’t want to be the voice shouting from the stage.

I want to be the one walking beside you on the way home.

That’s what this book is. A walk. Through memories, moments, mistakes, and through the peace I found buried beneath them all. Through the gates that shaped me and the hands that taught me how to hold steady when everything else shook loose.

The lessons of a lifetime don’t always come from grand moments. Often, they come from quiet days, steady hands, and the small choices we make before anyone is watching. Growing up, I learned early that being a good person wasn’t about boasting or being loud. It was about showing up, consistently, humbly, and with purpose. My father didn’t say much, but his actions spoke volumes. He worked hard, cared deeply, and carried himself with quiet dignity. I remember every single service project, every act of care for a neighbor, every widow he helped.

But it was watching his hands that taught me the most about what strength really means.

One man we visited regularly was bedridden. He was a former B-52 pilot who’d flown Operation Chrome Dome missions, circling the Arctic loaded with nuclear bombs, knowing that if he ever got the order to fly into the Soviet Union, he wouldn’t be coming back. Mission after mission of hoping “it’s not this time.” The weight of that responsibility had settled into his bones over the years, and now age had settled there too.

The gate to his house was nothing special. It was a simple screen door that led into a world where valor lived quietly in a medical bed. As a kid, crossing that threshold meant stepping into a place that smelled different than our house. Not stinky, but that distinct scent you get walking into a nursing home. The mixture of medicine, old fabric, and the particular heaviness of time moving slowly. I probably noticed the smell too much, dwelling on it, but I never said anything because my father never once looked like it affected him.

The contrast inside was sharp and stayed with me. The pilot was in a medical bed with an oxygen tank, positioned in their main living room. They had it dimmed so the light wasn’t too harsh on his eyes, but they lived out in the country, so the windows framed beautiful views. Rolling fields, oak trees reaching toward big Texas sky. Outside was bright, open, full of strength and possibility. Inside was dimmer, quieter, where this man whose body was failing him carried stories of flying nuclear missions into Arctic darkness.

My dad would always sit right next to the pilot’s bedside, scooting the chair up close, often leaning over and talking loud enough so he could hear, but with a certain softness. His voice got louder but softer, if that makes sense. My father’s body language was soft, attentive, deliberate.

And then I’d watch the transformation happen.

These were hands that could take a beating working on vehicles, hands that could fix anything from opening a stubborn jar to wrestling with rusted bolts. Hands that knew the right tool for every job and wouldn’t quit until the work was done correctly. But the moment he sat beside that pilot’s bed, those same hands became something else entirely.

They became gentle.

My father’s hands would sometimes rest on the pilot’s arm, and there was a love in it you could feel across the room. The hardness was replaced with a softness you couldn’t necessarily see, but you could sense it in the air. It was like watching steel become silk. The same strength, but applied with tenderness that honored what this man had carried, what he had sacrificed, what he was still carrying even as his body gave way.

The pilot couldn’t tell many stories. He was pretty weak. But what came out through my dad’s questions was profound. They’d talk like two old buddies about missions and Strategic Air Command, because my father understood the weight of those flights. He’d served in Vietnam and knew that world of carrying responsibility that could break you if you thought about it too long.

We’d stay about forty-five minutes to an hour, and I could see in every moment that my dad wasn’t in a hurry to leave. He was there to visit, to uplift, to be present for a fellow veteran who didn’t get many opportunities to talk about the things that had shaped his life. There was no agenda except presence, no goal except letting this man know he was seen and valued.

I usually positioned myself next to my dad but scooted back a little, watching and listening. That image stuck with me like a photograph I could pull up whenever I needed to remember what strength really looked like. It taught me that strength wasn’t always about toughness or loudness or making sure everyone knew how capable you were. Sometimes it looked like my father’s hands becoming soft. Sometimes it looked like sitting beside someone who’d flown nuclear missions and just listening to their stories with the respect they deserved. Sometimes it looked like showing up week after week, not because you had to, but because someone needed you to be there.

But while my dad had leadership positions and often sat up on the stand at church, it was my mother who showed me the quietest and strongest kind of faith I had seen. Every single Sunday, she was there, with us kids in the pew, holding the gate to our spiritual foundation with a determination that never wavered.

Sunday mornings had a quiet energy in the house. That determined atmosphere of needing to get up and get going, of crossing the threshold from the ordinary week into something sacred. The preparation started Friday nights when I had to clean my room and hang my clothes ready in the closet. Military precision even then, everything in its place for the sacred rhythm ahead.


By the time I was old enough to dress myself, I knew the Sunday morning choreography by heart. Someone would be cooking eggs or pouring cereal while getting ready. My mom’s voice would call out instructions from her bathroom. Not sharp or mean, just “Get your shoes on, we’re going.” She’d yell to be heard over the bustle, but it was firm and loving, the voice of someone guarding something precious.


We usually left early. We came from a military family, and being 15 minutes early meant you were late. Sometimes one of us would throw a tantrum or refuse to go, but there was this quiet gentleness in her voice that said, “We’re going to church.” Her face didn’t look stern by any means, but assertive. Deal with it. You better go do what I said, because we’re leaving.

It was just routine. It was just what we did. But now I understand what she was really doing. She was standing guard at the gate of faith, making sure nothing could keep us from crossing that threshold each week. Like Saturday mornings, if I woke up and heard the lawnmower already going while I was still in bed, I knew I better get out quick and go help. Not saying I was perfect at it. I was a typical kid who would complain and belly-ache. But I knew the rhythm. Sunday mornings, I knew we needed to get up and get going.

When we walked into church, we’d always go to where we were sitting and set our stuff down, then she would go mingle and talk to people. She loved getting to know people. I later learned that church was like church family to her, and she wanted to catch up with friends. I remember complaining after church about how long we had to stay because she wanted to talk to this person or that person. “I just wanna go home. I just wanna go to grandma’s house. Why do we have to stay so long?”

During the service, if we acted up or got too loud, she’d lean over and say, “You wanna go to the bathroom?” And we knew what that meant. We’d get a stern talking to. So we better shape up. But in moments when I wasn’t necessarily acting out, maybe just playing with my toy a little too loud, she would scratch my back.

Her hands, gentle, patient, persistent, would move across my shoulders and I would freeze in whatever position because it felt good, and I didn’t want it to stop. That simple touch got us through a lot of sacrament meetings. Those hands carried love without saying a word, creating a connection that made even a fidgety kid want to be still and present.

I’m sure she had Sundays where she didn’t feel like dragging children to church. But she did it anyway. Every. Single. Week. Some people might think she was being too strict, forcing us into something. But now that I’m a parent, I see it differently. It wasn’t pressure. It was love. She was guarding the gate to something she knew we’d need later, even when we couldn’t see its value yet.

She loves Christ, and she wanted us to know Him. Probably not until later in life when I had kids did I realize it wasn’t just about church attendance, but something deeper. She wanted that message of His love to come across, and she knew that showing up mattered. She knew that crossing that threshold every week, even when we didn’t want to, was building something in us that would hold when everything else shook loose.

Faith, for me, was quiet at first. It was my mother reading scripture at the table. It was prayer before meals, even when things were tight. It wasn’t some grand display. It was daily trust, built one Sunday at a time, one conversation at a time, one gentle hand on the back at a time. The scriptures didn’t promise an easy life, but they taught me how to stand when the wind pushed hardest. I started to understand that strength is tested not when the road is smooth, but when it’s stripped bare.

Later, I found myself in places where that foundation was tested. Overseas. Alone. Tired. In the desert, in armored vehicles, under starlight so sharp it reminded me of home in Central Texas. Some nights we’d park the MATVs, blacked out, engines off, and sit for a moment. I’d scan the area with night vision, clear it, and then, if only for a second, I’d look up. The stars were brilliant, ancient, steady. The air was still. I’d take a deep breath and feel the contrast: how small I was, how steady God was, and how important it was to keep a clear head. Then I’d turn back to the gun and return to watch. That’s the rhythm I learned: stillness and alertness, side by side.

That foundation didn’t make me perfect. It made me ready. Because no matter where you come from or what you believe, life will shake you. It will come for your identity. Your family. Your faith. And when it does, you won’t rise to the occasion. You’ll fall to your level of preparation. You’ll discover what gates you’ve been guarding and what your hands have been trained to do.

I learned this right after my wife and I got married. We’d been apart more than we’d been together. I left for basic training while we were dating, then technical school, seeing each other only occasionally on weekends for months. I proposed at the spot of our first date and first kiss, and we got married the Friday after I graduated tech school, on my dad’s birthday, in that same spot.

That next Monday, we drove all the way across the country to Fairchild Air Force Base in Washington with little to no money, married to someone I’d actually been apart from more than together. When we got there, there was no house ready for us. We had to rent an RV by the night that we couldn’t even afford. The gate to our future seemed locked tight, and we didn’t have the key.

The call to my parents was harder than it should have been. Here I was, married less than a week, and I had to explain that we didn’t even have money for ham sandwiches. That the Air Force husband who was supposed to provide couldn’t even buy groceries. They sent much more than we asked for, and that help carried us until my first paycheck.

The RV was one of those rental units that had seen better days, probably better decades. The kind with thin walls that shook when an eighteen-wheeler passed on the highway, and a door that stuck just enough to make you wonder if you’d get locked out. Inside, everything was designed for efficiency over comfort. The kitchenette was barely wide enough for one person to stand in, with a two-burner stove that took forever to heat anything and a refrigerator that hummed constantly but barely kept things cool.

My wife stood in that narrow space, her hands working over a single pot of boiling water, making mac and cheese from a box that cost ninety-nine cents. She heated up hot dogs in another small pan, the cheap kind that split when they got too hot. We couldn’t afford anything else. Really, we couldn’t even afford that. The foundation I thought I’d built was showing cracks under the pressure of real life.

I sat at the flimsy table, the kind that folds down from the wall and shakes every time you put your elbows on it, and watched her work. Her movements were careful, precise, making the most of what we had. No complaints. No comments about how this wasn’t what she’d signed up for. Just her hands, steady and patient, turning almost nothing into something that would fill our stomachs.


But something beautiful happened in that cramped space that smelled faintly of other people’s travels and disinfectant. We sat across from each other at that little table, our knees almost touching in the narrow aisle, and ate mac and cheese from paper plates. Somehow, she’d taken those cheap hot dogs and made them taste like something special. She sliced them just right, maybe added a dash of something from the tiny spice packets she’d tucked in her purse. The cheese sauce was the powdered kind that never quite mixes right, but her hands had worked magic with it, stirring in a little extra milk, getting every lump out until it was creamy and rich. She has this gift for taking the simplest ingredients and doctoring them up into something that makes you forget what you started with.

Her hands reached across that small table to touch mine between bites. “We’re doing this together,” she said. Not dramatic, not forced. Just a simple statement of fact that carried more weight than any promise we’d made at the altar.

What could have been stressful became formative and special. In that tiny space, with our belongings in garbage bags and our future uncertain, we learned something neither of us could have planned. Our foundation wasn’t just what we could build with our own hands, it included the people who had been building into us all our lives, ready to catch us when we fell.

That’s when I learned that your foundation isn’t just what you build. It’s also who you can call when your plan falls apart. It’s the gate your parents guard until you’re strong enough to guard it yourself. It’s the hands that taught you how to be gentle even when you need to be strong.

For me, the preparation started early. And it wasn’t just mine. It came from people who modeled steady hands, patient correction, and quiet strength. My father showing me that the same hands that could muscle through mechanical problems could become tender when someone needed comfort. My mother demonstrating that guarding what matters most sometimes looks like simple, unglamorous consistency. My wife showing me that the strongest foundations are built by two people who refuse to quit on each other, especially when everything else is uncertain.

That’s what this book is about. Not just the things I’ve lived, but the truths I’ve found useful, again and again. It’s not just for veterans, or Christians, or men. It’s for anyone trying to walk through chaos without becoming chaotic themselves. For anyone trying to hold peace in a loud world, to build something worth protecting, to know when your hands should be strong and when they should be gentle.

Those lessons continue to be refined. My wife has a gift for connecting with people and recognizing when they have a need. Often we’ll visit someone together with our kids, and I’ll find my parenting mind and my “get it done” mind taking over, stressing about the kids being loud or touching something fragile, thinking about bedtime or all the worries waiting at home. How often do we miss the moment because we’re worried about everything else? My wife will remind me that I need to be there, I need to speak up, I need to be attentive.

I have a deep love for serving others, but I’m not perfect at it. Not even as good as my wife at it. But together, I think we make a pretty decent team. Her hands open doors I might not even see. My hands steady things that might otherwise fall. Together, we’re learning to guard the gates that matter most for our family.

Chapter 2: The Quiet in the Storm

The gate between safe and hostile had already been crossed that morning. Not the physical wire, that would come later. But the invisible threshold every airman knows: from routine to real, from training to warfare. I sat in the JTOC, my hands steady on familiar radios, not yet knowing that tonight those same hands would have to choose between panic and peace in the space of a heartbeat.

Some thresholds announce themselves. Others you cross without knowing until you’re already on the other side.

The JTOC was a plywood building with a concrete floor, thick with the dust and tiredness that collected over years of different people, different shifts, different crises. Walking in felt like entering a nerve center, cables snaking across the floor like veins, the constant hum of electronics mixing with occasional radio chatter. The air tasted metallic, like the inside of an old truck, seasoned with burnt coffee grounds and the particular staleness that comes from too many people breathing the same recycled air for too many months.

As you walked in, there was a huge desk made of plywood, tickets on the back wall showing who was out and about off base. The main wall had tons of TV screens and computer screens, an Air Force guy, a couple of Brits, a Marine, all working different alarm systems, cameras, communications equipment. Each screen told part of a larger story we were paid to watch but hoped never to see completed.

It was late at night. Routine, busy, and quiet all at once. Everyone was tired, wanting to get back to their bunks. The familiar sounds of our small war: occasional radio chatter, keyboards clicking as I kept logs, small talk that filled the spaces between nothing and something. Always coffee going, that burnt smell mixing with the scent of electronics and building dust. We’d joke around with the Brits and Scots, though half the time I couldn’t understand their accents at all. They’d have to turn on English subtitles to English movies for the English-speaking American who apparently didn’t know “true English.”

My hands moved across the keyboard with practiced efficiency. These were the same hands that had learned precision from my father, hands that knew the right tool for every job, hands that understood the weight of doing things correctly. Tonight, they would learn that sometimes the most important work isn’t fixing anything at all. Sometimes it’s just choosing stillness when everything else chooses chaos.

Then the doors swung open hard. They had a little rope tied to a water bottle to force them closed, and it smacked back against the wall with a crack that cut through our comfortable routine. Just by the look on his face, his franticness, the slightly elevated voice cutting through our comfortable routine, it stopped the room. Everyone turned to look. The coffee mug in the Brit’s hand paused halfway to his mouth. The Marine’s fingers froze over his keyboard. Everyone realized something wasn’t right.

“They went down,” he said. “They’re all gone.”

The words hung in the stale air like smoke. In that moment, the gate we’d all been guarding, the one between the abstract possibility of loss and its concrete reality, swung wide open. There was no closing it again.

The silence was heavy, sharp. That adrenaline focus that can almost hurt because your body responds and knows your brain has to start working a mile a minute. But my hands didn’t shake. They remained still, as if they understood something my mind was still processing. How do you hold stillness and urgency in the same moment? Like deep water with storms on the surface, both things true at once, neither canceling the other.

I immediately knew it was our guys. After a month in country, they’d approached us and said they needed our MATVs to go along on missions in case they got hit. We’d been going out with them regularly, not daily, but often. I was just a regular guy, not special operations, but they let me tag along and watch the cool guys do cool things. They were humble, willing to help with our comms equipment, good dudes. One of them was experienced, older, seasoned, nothing seemed to phase him, always had the situation under control.

But here’s what I couldn’t tell anyone: I was supposed to be on that helicopter that night.

The gate between life and death isn’t always visible from a distance. Sometimes you only see it in the rearview mirror, realizing how close you came to crossing through.

I had the 4-on-1-off cycle, and on one of those four days, I worked the JTOC. I’d gotten specifically trained for it because I wanted to go above and beyond, wanted to prove I belonged in rooms where decisions mattered. That night was my night to work it. I couldn’t ask someone else to cover because going on the helicopter was kind of an “ask forgiveness, not permission” deal. Not many people even knew I was doing it.

When I realized it was their bird that went down, there was no relief. Just guilt, confusion, anger. The mind plays tricks when it can’t process what the heart already knows. My brain almost shut that off, it felt fake for a long time, like I was watching a movie about someone else. To this day, I still have that problem with trauma. The mind builds its own gates to protect itself, sometimes locking out what it needs to process most.

I knew what would happen next. Anytime there were fatalities, they’d shut off all internet communications to make sure families weren’t notified by accident. The digital gates between theater and home would slam shut, leaving us in the silence of official protocols and unofficial grief.

So before the network went dark, I pulled out my phone. My hands were absolutely steady, not shaky, not numb. I could feel everything, the smooth glass of the screen, the weight of the device, the slight warmth from the battery. These weren’t the hands of someone in shock. They were my father’s hands, steady under pressure, knowing that precision matters most when the stakes are highest. These were hands that had been trained since childhood to do the right thing, the right way, especially when it matters.

I was in the moment, thinking, but it was all systematic. One step after another. My fingers moved across the screen with the same deliberate care my father had taught me for every tool, every task. But this wasn’t about fixing anything mechanical. This was about reaching across an ocean to touch the heart of someone who needed to know I was still breathing.

I typed five words that carried the weight of every promise I’d ever made: “I’m okay. I love you.”

What do you say when you might never get to say anything else?

I hit send. In that simple gesture, my hands crossed another gate, this one between silence and speech, between carrying the weight alone and sharing it with the person who mattered most. Then the network went dark, and we were alone with what we knew.

I still remember the cracked blue school chair I was sitting in. The vinyl had split along the back, and the once shiny legs had faded with time. The smell of the wood on the desk, worn smooth by countless hands before mine. The way everything in the room seemed to slow, as if the air itself had thickened. I sat in that chair with a stillness I didn’t yet know I had, and somehow, by sending that message, by putting first things first, I had the clarity to do my job.

That’s what I mean by a still mind.

It’s not a blank mind. It’s not apathy. It’s not detachment. A still mind is one that knows where to turn when the ground shifts beneath your feet. It’s been trained to choose peace ahead of panic, to anchor itself in a deeper truth so it’s not thrown by every wave that crashes over the bow. The stillness I found wasn’t the absence of storm, it was the deep water beneath it, the part that stays unmoved even when hurricanes rage above.

There’s a strange thing that happens in moments like that, when life flips on its head and everything that seemed urgent a minute ago suddenly disappears. A part of you stops pretending. The surface-level thoughts fall away, and you’re left with what matters. That’s why I sent the text. Not because it was dramatic, but because it was simple and true: I’m okay. I love you. That’s all that mattered when everything else turned to noise.

The day we had the flare emergency was different, but it taught me the same lesson about gates and hands working together.

It started with a helicopter declaring an emergency. A flare had hung up and wasn’t ejecting clean, which meant the pilot was flying with live ordnance misfiring beside the aircraft. I watched it unfold in real time through video feeds, crackling radio traffic, urgent chatter, people looking around the room for answers. The gate between routine and crisis had opened again, but this time we might be able to close it.

Then the C-RAM system picked up an incoming round. A mortar. The defensive gate we’d built around our base was about to be tested. We had to make a split-second call on whether to fire the countermeasure. Static crackled through the radio as another unit reported live ordnance discovered on base. They were setting up a perimeter but needed more personnel and equipment to secure the area. It was shaping up to be one of those nights we’d trained for but never expected to live through. The kind where instructors pile crisis on top of crisis to break you, except this time the alarms were real and there was no reset button. The British lieutenant, who didn’t have clearance for all these decisions, looked straight at me and said, “What do we do?”

So I did what I had to do.

My hands moved across the equipment with practiced precision. I plotted the fastest route for EOD to the helicopter and the new site. I marked out the grid squares. Pulled additional security from patrol routes, redirected them to the ordnance site for perimeter support. Cleared the range and made sure comms were tracking every moving piece. I coordinated to lock down the affected sectors. I told the right people what needed to happen, when it needed to happen, and in what order, and we did it. I was only an A1C, a two-striper, but in that moment, I was the calmest voice in the room.

That’s not because I’m special. It’s because my hands had already learned, through pressure, how to think past the noise. It’s because my father had taught me that when chaos comes, you don’t get excited, you get systematic. You use the right tool for the job, and you do it right the first time, because there might not be a second chance.

Stillness doesn’t mean the world around you is quiet. It means you are. It means your hands know what to do even when your heart is racing. It means you’ve crossed enough gates to recognize the territory on the other side.

The transition from war zone to civilian life is so jarring that you wonder if you’ll ever stop feeling like a stranger in your own country. Iraq had been hot, loud, dusty, and dangerous, but familiar. I’d gotten used to the rhythm of threat and response, the clarity that comes when everyone knows the stakes. There were cards and jokes and routines and friends. We all knew the deal. You didn’t have to explain yourself.

But back home, everything was paved and clean and comfortable, and no one seemed to understand what we’d come from. They weren’t wrong to move on, but I hadn’t. The gate between wartime and peacetime had been crossed, but part of me was still standing at the threshold, looking back.

The first time I remember understanding what it meant to be caught between gates was back home, on my front porch, at night. I had been back from deployment for a little while, but something in me hadn’t come home yet. I turned off the porch light, sat outside in the cool Washington air, and tried to find that calm I used to feel while staring up at stars in Iraq.

The Washington night had its own language, tree frogs creating waves of sound that rose and fell like breathing, the distant hum of traffic on the highway that never really stopped, the particular way suburban quiet isn’t quiet at all but full of air conditioning units humming and TVs murmuring and lives continuing behind lit windows. In Iraq, the darkness had been absolute, stars so bright they threw shadows. Here, streetlights turned everything orange, and you could barely see the stars at all. 

I looked at our home, the grass we cut every Saturday, the classic white picket fence that was supposed to mean we’d made it, and wondered why I felt so far away from it all. The contrast was sharp and disorienting. Outside was the comfortable darkness of Washington night, the kind where the biggest danger was stepping on a dog toy in the yard. Inside was bright, warm, safe, my wife watching TV, everything exactly as I’d pictured it would be. But I felt trapped between the two, belonging fully to neither.

The porch beneath my bare feet was smooth and cool, worn by weather but solid. Nothing like the rough concrete and plywood of the JTOC. The air smelled like cut grass and the neighbor’s barbecue smoke, not dust and diesel and that particular smell of electronics running too hot for too long.

That night, I cried. Quietly. Alone. The kind of tears that come when you finally stop trying to be strong enough to carry everything by yourself.

It wasn’t because of one single memory. It was all of them. The loss of the helicopter crew. The young guys with futures that never came. The chaos we’d navigated and the calm we’d learned to carry. The guy who walked into a bathroom and never came out. How death and tragedy had become so common that we’d learned to function around them. And the bitterness that started to quietly build in my heart because I didn’t know how to talk about any of it.

The hands that had been steady in crisis felt useless in peace. The gate-keeping instincts that had served me well downrange felt out of place in a world where the biggest threat was traffic or a missed deadline. I was carrying tools for a job that no longer existed, and I didn’t know how to put them down.

It took me years to realize that holding everything in wasn’t strength, it was a slow erosion. Bitterness crept in without making a sound, like rust on metal you thought was protected. I would get frustrated at people who didn’t care about the war anymore, or who talked about it like it was ancient history. But at the same time, if someone did bring it up, I’d shut down, thinking, “You don’t know what you’re talking about.” I didn’t want to be mad. But something in me was stuck between gates, unable to fully enter either world.

And then, little by little, I started opening up to my wife.

Not in one big sit-down, not in some dramatic conversation. Just small moments. A few words here. A memory there. And every time, she met me with grace. With tenderness. Her hands, gentle, patient, persistent, began to do something mine couldn’t: they helped me open the gate between isolation and connection.

That’s when I realized: I could trust her not just with my life, but with my heart. She wasn’t going to fix me. She was just going to listen. And that, more than anything, began to change me. She taught me that some gates can only be opened from the inside, but that doesn’t mean you have to open them alone.

Even now, I have to remind myself: I don’t need to control every outcome. I just need to choose stillness again. The lesson applies everywhere, especially in parenting.

We have a baby who likes to eat at night, a four-year-old who was recently hooked up to an EEG machine for four days, couldn’t get wet or move very much because he had to be connected to wires and monitored by cameras, and a six-year-old who’s losing the last bits of toddler in him while I’m trying to take it all in before he moves to the next stage. Three boys full of energy with space to run. It’s amazing and I wouldn’t trade it, but when you’re on multiple days of no sleep, cleaning a house that gets dirty the next moment, caring for the land, and trying to balance setting your kids up for the future without screwing them up, it can become a lot.

The gates of fatherhood are different from the gates of war, but they require the same steady hands, the same choice to respond rather than react.

Especially when one kid is mad about his monster truck, another can’t figure out how to build with magnet tiles, and the baby demands to be fed. With days of that and no break, you fray at the edges. Your temper gets shorter. Little things seem like mountains. The gate between patience and explosion starts to look very thin.

This morning our baby was crying for food when my four-year-old bit his finger while eating breakfast, spilled his orange juice, and my six-year-old screamed because of the noise. It all went to hell in a moment, the kind of domestic chaos that can test you more than incoming mortars because there’s no protocol for it, no clear enemy to identify and neutralize.

I first picked up the one in pain and gave him a hug. My hands, the same ones that had plotted grid squares cleared ranges, and destroyed threats, now held a small boy and kissed his hurt finger. Instead of scolding about the juice, I cleaned it up quietly. Started feeding the baby with one arm while I explained to my six-year-old that we don’t need to freak out and scream when things get hectic.

That may seem like obvious choices when you’re well-rested, but when you’re sleep-deprived and dealing with kids constantly, it can be a tough decision not to blow up. I’m not perfect. But I’ve learned that the same stillness that served me in the JTOC serves me in the kitchen. The same steady hands that functioned under pressure can function under the different pressure of three boys who need their father to be present, not just powerful.

That’s the lesson I’m still learning. When I feel the fray in my nerves and want to raise my voice, that’s the moment God usually reminds me: restraint is stronger than reaction. Sometimes my kids don’t need correction. They just need to be told they’re loved. They need to know I’m here, that I’m not going anywhere, that their father knows how to stand guard at the gates that matter most.

The gate between war and home may be behind me now, but I carry what I learned there into every moment. Not the trauma, that’s mine to bear and process. But the truth: stillness doesn’t mean you’re passive. It means you’re anchored. And it doesn’t come from having all the answers. It comes from knowing where to turn when you don’t.

My hands have learned to do different work now. They change diapers instead of plotting coordinates. They build blanket forts instead of clearing ranges. They pray over small heads instead of managing crises. But they’re still the same hands, steady, purposeful, trained to do the right thing when it matters most.

And every night, when I tuck my boys into bed, I remember that text message: “I’m okay. I love you.” Because that’s still what matters most when everything else falls away. That’s still the message that crosses every gate between fear and peace.

Sometimes I can still feel that cracked vinyl under my palms, still smell that mixture of dust and electronics. That chair held me while I learned what it meant to be still when everything else was falling apart. Now, years later, when chaos comes, when all three boys are melting down at once, when bills pile up, when the weight of being responsible for other lives feels too heavy, I remember: I’ve sat in this chair before. Different room, different crisis, but the same choice: stillness or chaos, deep water or surface storm.

I choose the deep water. Every time, I try to choose the deep water.

Chapter 3: The Still Fight
The training facility stretched out like a small town built for violence. Plywood buildings painted desert tan, windows cut into walls to create sight lines and danger zones, concrete barriers positioned to force decisions under pressure. Everything designed to feel real enough to matter, familiar enough to foreign streets we might walk someday.
The gate between hesitation and action is measured in heartbeats. Sometimes in milliseconds. But that afternoon on the training range, time stretched like warm steel, and I learned that my hands could be patient even when my pulse was not.
We were in a live-fire exercise with simunition. Paint-filled rounds that sting like a wasp through fabric when they find skin. The air tasted of gunpowder and dust, that sharp metallic tang that sits on your tongue after ammunition has been burning downrange for hours. Inside my helmet, the temperature climbed with each breath, sweat gathering where the padding pressed against my forehead. I was pressed against a concrete barrier with a few others, the rough surface gritty against my shoulder, exchanging fire with opposing forces across this simulated urban landscape.
Someone popped out from behind cover across the way, weapon raised. The moment opened up like a photograph developing. I could see the barrel of his rifle, the angle of his stance, the way he moved with purpose. My finger found the trigger. The shot was there. Clean. Easy.
But something in me paused.
My hands stayed steady on my weapon, but they didn't act. Not yet. Everything slowed down, the way it does when your body knows more than your mind about what's happening. The dust motes hanging in the afternoon light. The sound of my own breathing inside my helmet. The weight of the rifle in my hands, familiar as my own heartbeat.
I waited. Watched. I needed to be sure.
Was he aiming at us?
The question formed itself with crystal clarity. Not just pointing his weapon our direction, but targeting us with intent. In that stretched moment, I could see the difference. His stance shifted slightly. His aim adjusted. His finger moved to his trigger.
Yes. He was.
Then my hands did what they'd been trained to do. I raised my weapon and took the shot. Calm, aimed, deliberate. The simunition round found its mark, painting him with evidence of impact. Clean. Quiet. Final.
Afterward, as we cleared weapons and gathered for debrief, someone asked why I didn't shoot sooner. The question came with genuine curiosity, not criticism. In training, speed often wins. React first, survive longest.
I just said, "I had to make sure he was shooting at us."
How long can you afford to wait when every second might be your last?
That's when it clicked. I wasn't wired to panic. I didn't get tunnel vision. I could wait inside the pressure, think, and still act with intention when the moment demanded it. And I liked knowing that about myself. Because it wasn't loud. It wasn't dramatic.
But it was decisive.
The instructor nodded. "Good. That's how you don't shoot the wrong person."
Some gates you cross with violence. Others you guard with restraint. Learning the difference between them might be the most important lesson any warrior learns.
But I want to be clear about something. You might notice that I share stories from training, and maybe that makes it sound like I just watched from the sidelines, like I was preparing but never living the real thing. But I didn't stand on the edge. I've seen real pain. I've seen what war does to the body and the soul. I've seen things that people couldn't understand unless they lived it, moments where my brain told me it must've been a movie because it couldn't make sense of what my eyes saw.
And still, I choose not to write about most of it.
Not because it didn't matter. But because the impact isn't in the explosion. Sometimes, it's in the stillness that follows. And that's the part I'm trying to capture. Not the moment everything broke, but what it took to keep going anyway.
Some of the greatest fights in life leave no headline. No scars that people can see. Sometimes they don't even come with enemies, just quiet, grinding resistance that you feel in your bones. The fight where you sit in a room full of people who don't know what you carry. Where you walk into church or work with a smile that feels like it belongs to someone else. The fight where you keep doing the work, even though nothing about it feels heroic.
That's the still fight. The one most people miss. The still fight is like deep water, all the power moves beneath where no one can see it.
There was a time when I was serving in a role where I didn't have rank, but I had responsibility. The gate between authority and leadership isn't marked by stripes on a sleeve. Sometimes it's marked by what you're willing to carry in silence.
I was watching people outrank me choose the easy route. Slacking off, cutting corners, wasting the time we didn't have. We were part of a mission that demanded more. More focus, more preparation, more resolve. And yet some of them treated it like a vacation, like the work we were doing didn't matter to anyone who wasn't there to see it.
My hands wanted to make fists. My voice wanted to cut through their casual attitude with the sharp edge of accountability. It would've been easy to blow up. To call them out in front of everyone. To let my frustration speak louder than my character. But I didn't. Not because I didn't care, but because I cared enough to do it right.
The strongest fights are the ones that look like nothing's happening. The most important victories are the ones no one sees you win.
I waited. I watched. And when I saw something cross the line, I pulled one of them aside. Privately. Respectfully. But directly. And I said what needed to be said. Not to shame, not to win, but to wake him up.
That was the quiet fight. The kind that doesn't get awards or recognition. The kind where your hands learn restraint instead of retaliation. But that's where integrity is forged. In the space between what you could do and what you should do.
Sometimes I've stayed quiet long enough to realize that the real battle wasn't with anyone else. It was with myself. The pressure to drift. The feeling that no one else was holding the line, why should I? The gate between excellence and mediocrity that everyone else seemed willing to let swing wide open.
And that's when the line comes back to me: The Lord is my strength and my song.
I don't say that out loud. I don't quote it. But I live it. That's the strength I pull from. The music that keeps me moving forward when I don't feel like singing. Because this life isn't just about defending yourself. It's about staying anchored to what matters when everything around you suggests that nothing matters at all.
The training facility was built to look like danger. But the real danger lived in quiet moments, a living room where everyone pretends things are fine, an office where you bite back words that would feel good to say but wrong to live with.
I've had moments in fatherhood that mirrored that fight. Days when I'm too tired, too frustrated, or too overwhelmed. Days when my kids ask for something simple, and I feel the weight of the world behind their question. "Will you play with me?" they ask. But what I hear is, "Are you still here for me?"
The gate between presence and performance is thinner than you think. My hands could grab the nearest task, the urgent email, the project that feels more important than building a block tower. The exhaustion sits heavy in my shoulders from carrying three different schedules, and my body wants to sink into the couch, scroll through my phone until the day feels manageable again. But those moments don't need efficiency. They need faithfulness.
My hands know what they want to do, check my phone, finish the task that feels urgent. But I choose different. I get down on the carpet, feeling the texture of fibers through my jeans, the slight coolness of the floor seeping through fabric, and I build what they ask me to build. My voice answers what they ask me to answer. My attention focuses on what they need it focused on.
The still fight is the choice to show up. Again and again. Not perfectly, but present. The uniform changes, but the fight doesn't.
I never saw the kind of legacy that breaks families in my own home. My parents were amazing. But I saw it in others. I saw how quick a bad legacy could start, how easily the gates we're supposed to guard could swing open to let the wrong things through. And I made up my mind early that wasn't going to start with me. Not in my house. Not with my name.
That meant choosing stillness in ways that weren't natural to me. Learning not to yell when I was angry. Learning to breathe when I wanted to run. Learning to listen when everything in me wanted to fix. I'm still learning that one.
One of the hardest lessons I ever learned is that leadership isn't just about what you say. It's about what you're willing to carry in silence. Not secrecy, silence. The sacred kind. The kind where you bear something because no one else can, and you don't make a scene about it.
There was a moment during a night operation where something went very wrong. I won't share the details. I don't need to. But I'll say this: people broke. Under pressure. Under fear. Under exhaustion. Some of them snapped in visible ways. Others held it in, but you could see it behind their eyes.
And afterward, we sat in the dark and said nothing. The weight of our gear pressed into shoulders aching from hours of tension. The metallic taste of spent adrenaline lingered on my tongue, mixing with the grit of sand that had worked its way between my teeth. The desert darkness was thick and heavy, swallowing sound. No radio chatter filled the silence like it usually did. Just someone's breathing still coming too fast, the distant hum of generators back at base, and my own heartbeat that seemed too loud in my ears. The ground still radiated the day's heat while cool night air settled on my neck, but the exhaustion that sat in my bones was different from just being tired.
It wasn't my job to fix what had happened. But I stayed up that night, keeping watch. Not because I had to, but because someone needed to. The still fight. The gate between what's required and what's needed. My hands holding steady on my weapon, my eyes scanning for threats, my mind processing what we'd seen and done.
That's the thing about the quiet battles. They're usually lonely. But they're never wasted.
Even now, as I write this, I think of the people who are fighting battles no one knows about. The spouse trying to hold their marriage together. The dad who goes to a job he hates just to keep the lights on. The young man trying to stay clean another day. The woman who's still in church even though she cries herself to sleep most nights. The kid who keeps showing up at school even when home is chaos.
They're in the fight.
And the world may never see them as warriors. But I do. I see you.
If I could say anything to someone who's still holding the line in the quiet places, it would be this: You are not weak for being tired. You are not broken for feeling alone. And you are not less of a fighter just because no one claps for you.
The world needs more quiet fighters. People who build with patience. Who protect without applause. Who lead without demanding a following. People whose hands know when to hold steady and when to act, whose presence guards the gates that matter most.
That's the kind of strength I want to live with. Not the loud kind that dominates a room, but the quiet kind that stays long after the crowds are gone. That's the kind of man I want to be. That's the kind of father I want my boys to remember. That even when I was hurting, I stayed. Even when I was afraid, I stood watch.
Even when I was silent, I was still fighting, hands steady on ordinary things while winning wars no one else could see. The same concrete barrier that once pressed rough against my shoulder in training now feels different in memory. Now the barriers I lean against are different, a kitchen counter while kids melt down, a doorframe while I choose patience over reaction. The texture is smoother but the choice is the same: steady pressure, patient waiting, knowing when to act.

Chapter 4: The Right Way 
There's always a right tool for the job. But sometimes the most important tool isn't in your toolbox at all. Sometimes it's knowing which gate you're trying to walk through, and whether your hands are steady enough to open it when it matters most.
The pitcher's mound rises from the diamond like an altar, that sacred fifteen inches of elevation where everything you've practiced either holds or falls apart. The dirt beneath your cleats has been worked smooth by countless feet before yours, packed firm by the weight of hopes and failures, mixed with the dreams of every kid who ever stood there believing this was their moment.
My father taught me that from the time I could hold a wrench. There is always a right tool for the job. He will take the time to find the right tool, no matter how long it takes or how inconvenient it might be.
Which is in complete opposition to how I approach work on the farm. If I'm building a fence or putting in a gate or fixing a tractor, and I don't have the right tool right there at the right moment, I just find what I have and make it work. It doesn't always make the best work, but it gets the job done, and you can move on to the next project.
We still laugh about it now, this contrast between his precision and my makeshift solutions.
But watching him work, I began to understand something deeper. This wasn't just about wrenches and screwdrivers. This was about building a foundation that could hold weight. His hands moved with purpose, with precision that came from years of understanding that shortcuts in the beginning create bigger problems later. Every bolt tightened correctly. Every connection checked twice. Not because he was slow, but because he understood the cost of doing it wrong.
This applied to everything. Learning how to change oil, learning how to change a car tire, all the way down to wiping windows the right way. Because he said, "If we wipe the windshield wrong and your mother is driving the car at night, we need her to be able to see." That was his mindset. The right tool, the right way. Always thinking about who was depending on the work being done correctly.
My father did a lot of electronics work in the Air Force. I have rudimentary knowledge from him and from working construction, but it requires precision. In the military, his workspace was probably a lot more organized than his shop now. These days he has tons and tons of tools, and his shop shows it. Looking from the outside, it can look like a mess. But if you ask him where one specific type of tool is, he will always know where it's at. Everything has its place still. Even if you don't know where it's at, he will know.
The way he explained right way versus wrong way was always focused toward my mother and making sure we were safe. When I was a kid, I could earn money by mowing the yard or washing cars. It started from an early age. I had to do it right.
I still remember one time when I didn't want to wash my mom's car. She was particular about how things were done. When dusting shelves, I had to move every object to dust underneath. When washing the car, I had to dry it right or else it would leave spots. One day when I totally didn't want to wash her car, I washed the entire thing with a rag and Windex. For years, I claimed I washed that car. Not too long ago, I finally admitted to her that yeah, I used Windex. Now that I'm out of her house and can say that.
As a kid, I tried to take shortcuts and got corrected for it. Whether that meant more oversight or being taught how to do a job right, I was corrected. In a gentle way, but corrected.
I learned this lesson early in baseball too. When I was young in the farm league, I played about half a season and then told my father, "You know what? I'm tired. I don't want to be here anymore." But he told me, "Nope. You signed up. You're gonna finish this season." That has stuck with me ever since. When you commit to something, you see it through. You don't quit just because it gets hard or uncomfortable.
But baseball became something more than commitment for me. It became the place where my father's lessons about precision found their truest expression. When everything is working in baseball, you can get into a zone and you don't have to think about it. It just comes natural. But that comes from a lot of practice.
Baseball was my life growing up. It was art because you didn't have to be the tallest, fastest, or strongest. Even a heavy guy can be a great pitcher if his mechanics are right. He can leverage his weight the right way. I loved it because it was a leveling field. Baseball is 99% mental and just a little bit physical. At least for pitching. Maybe that's not true for every position, but for pitching, that rings true.
My father's approach to precision showed up in how I approached pitching. He was a pitcher when he was younger. He loved baseball too. He taught me well, but he didn't coach my little league teams. He wanted me to learn from others. I respected that, especially when I got older, because although he was great at it and a great teacher, he knew he didn't have everything that could be taught to me.
Side note: my dad had a torn rotator cuff, so especially later on in life, he couldn't quite throw as much with me. I could throw hard enough that nobody wanted to help me warm up. My mother would put on some catcher's equipment and go warm me up in the bullpen. Which is awesome for a mother to do.
Good pitching mechanics take a lot of practice. I remember the very first step, where you put your foot for your first step of motion, we would do that 100 times before we would add the second step. We would do that 100 times, then add the third step and do it 100 times. When I brought my leg up in the leg kick, it came very far up. It probably could have touched my nose sometimes. But he would make me stop in that moment and balance. You had to find that perfect balance and just sit there. It was hard. But once you get that balance down, great things can happen.
My father knew, just like with his tools, that there was a right way to do this. Every motion had to be precise. Every step had to be practiced until it became natural. Because when you're on the mound with the game on the line, you don't have time to think about mechanics. They have to be automatic.
The difference between having perfect mechanics and being able to use them became clear when I started playing in two very different worlds.
I didn't click with the high school teammates at all, so I mainly played in a farm league in another town called Rockne. I don't even think anybody in high school ever knew I could pitch. But I could pitch pretty well there. I had a passion for it, could throw very hard, and could pick where I wanted to hit my spots. I was drafted and was the first pick of the first round my last year. That was cool, even though I had ended up dislocating my leg that year and was still recuperating.
It was like the Bad News Bears. It was just a bunch of kids playing baseball, and I loved it. The field was so bad that ground balls could take a hop in the opposite direction in an instant. But that didn't matter because the teammates were my teammates and my friends. I played with them year after year and we got to know each other. Nobody cared if we struggled. We were just there for each other, just playing baseball.
In high school, there were a little more stakes involved. Coaches get paid. If they lose too much, they might get fired. The teammates, I hadn't played with them for years like the others. There were friend groups, and me being the quiet guy, I didn't get into those friend groups. I couldn't quite get into the groove, couldn't get into the zone. And it affected my mechanics and it affected the way I played.
The farm league felt like home. The environment was right. When I walked through that gate, I belonged. In high school, I was technically more skilled, but I was also alone. Disconnected. My hands knew what to do, I had practiced the motions thousands of times, but my mind wasn't right.
The failure story that taught me everything happened in my junior year. It was the bottom of the 9th inning. The score was like 10 to 9, a high-scoring game. We had gone through all our pitchers and were down to seeing which position players could pitch. Because it was a small school, everybody dabbled in every position.
Warming up in the bullpen, I was fine. Better than fine. I was in my element.
The bullpen was tucked away down the left field line, out of sight from most of the crowd, separated from the main action by a chain-link fence. It felt like my own private space, just me and the catcher working through my routine. No coaches breathing down my neck, no teammates watching every throw, no parents in the stands tracking my every move. The leather of my glove carried that particular scent of oil and sweat and countless catches, mixed with the dusty smell of the warning track that surrounded us.
I was having to prove myself, sure, but there wasn't pressure. The coaches were just seeing who could throw. It was between me and another guy, and I knew I had experience, so I was confident.
My arm felt loose, strong. The ball came out of my hand clean, with that satisfying snap you get when everything's working right. I could hear the pop in the catcher's mitt, could see him nod his approval after each pitch. My fastball was hitting his glove right where I wanted it. My leg kick felt natural, balanced. I found that still point my father had taught me, that perfect moment of control before the forward motion.
The mechanics were automatic. Step, balance, drive, release. Step, balance, drive, release. Like a machine that had been perfectly calibrated. My hands knew exactly where to grip the ball, how to let it roll off my fingertips. The rubber under my feet felt solid, familiar. This was what I'd trained for thousands of hours to feel like.
I threw maybe fifteen pitches, mixing fastballs and changeups, hitting spots with the kind of precision that made me remember why I loved this game. The catcher called for inside corner, I painted inside corner. High and tight, right where he set up. Low and away, and the ball dove exactly where it was supposed to.
"Looking good," he said, flipping the ball back to me.
I felt good. Confident. Ready. Like I could throw strikes all day long, like I could get anybody out. This was my territory, my craft, the thing I'd been doing since I could hold a baseball. Out here in the bullpen, away from everything else, I was exactly who I was supposed to be.
The coach walked over and said, "You're up, Weston."
I jogged toward the mound thinking I was about to show everyone what I could do.
But then when I got onto the mound, everything changed.
I can still feel the dirt under my cleats, that particular texture of a pitcher's mound that's been worked over by dozens of feet before mine. The rubber felt foreign under my right foot, even though I'd stood on many mounds before. My hands were sweating inside my glove, and I could taste the salt from my lip where perspiration had started to gather. The afternoon sun that had felt warm in the bullpen now beat down like judgment.
It was daytime, so there were no blinding stadium lights. Just the flat, honest light of a Texas afternoon that showed everything clearly. It was a tiny crowd because it's a small town, maybe thirty people scattered in the bleachers. Parents checking their watches, a few kids playing under the stands. It's not like it was some big moment, some classic bottom-of-the-9th situation where I had to perform for a championship.
But my eyes kept finding the wrong things to focus on. Instead of my catcher's mitt, I locked onto the face of a coach I barely knew, his arms crossed, his expression unreadable. Instead of going through my pre-pitch routine, I became aware of how my uniform felt too big in some places, too tight in others. The bill of my cap seemed to sit wrong, casting a shadow that made everything feel off-kilter.
I could feel my heart beating in my throat. Not the good kind of adrenaline that sharpens everything, but the kind that makes your hands feel disconnected from your brain. I gripped the ball and felt the seams under my fingertips, the same seams I'd traced thousands of times in practice, but now they felt foreign, like I was holding a rock instead of a baseball.
The first batter stepped in. I went into my windup, the motion I'd practiced until it was automatic, until I could do it in my sleep. But halfway through, it was like my body forgot the sequence. My leg kick felt too high, then too low. My arm angle was wrong. The ball left my hand with no conviction behind it, sailing wide of the plate like it had a mind of its own.
Ball one.
I could feel the sweat starting to run down my back, soaking into my jersey. The sun seemed hotter now, bearing down on the back of my neck. I took the ball back from my catcher and tried to reset, tried to find that quiet place in my mind where mechanics lived. But instead, I kept hearing the silence from the dugout. Not encouraging chatter. Just the heavy quiet of teammates who barely knew my name, watching me fail at the thing I was supposed to be good at.
Ball two. Ball three.
My hands were shaking now, just barely, but enough that I could feel it when I gripped the ball. The kind of tremor that makes you second-guess every movement. I stepped off the mound, wiped my hands on my pants, and tried to remember what my father had taught me about balance, about finding that still point where everything worked.
But I wasn't in the farm league anymore. I wasn't with teammates who knew I could pitch, who'd seen me strike out their friends, who'd cheer when I hit my spots. I was alone on a mound, wearing the wrong uniform, trying to prove something to people who had no reason to believe in me.
Ball four. Walk.
The next batter stepped in, and I could feel everything unraveling. My mechanics were there, buried somewhere in muscle memory, but my mind was screaming loud enough that I couldn't hear them. Each pitch felt like I was throwing with someone else's arm, like I was watching myself fail from outside my own body.
Ball one. Ball two. I was walking him too, and I knew it, but I couldn't stop it. My hands felt like they belonged to someone else, someone who had never thrown a baseball before.
Then I heard it. "Time!"
The coach's voice cut through the afternoon air, and I knew he was walking out to the mound. I didn't want to look at him. I kept my eyes fixed toward home plate, willing the umpire to crouch back down, willing the next batter to step in so I could have another chance. I could hear his footsteps getting closer, that distinctive sound of cleats on dirt that every baseball player knows means your day might be over.
Part of me wanted to beg for another chance, wanted to promise I could find the strike zone if he'd just let me try one more batter. But I knew my mind wasn't in it. I knew I was fighting the wrong battle, trying to force something that couldn't be forced. I wanted that rubber to feel like home again, wanted to step back and find my balance the way my father had taught me. But I was trying to pitch my way into belonging, and that's not how belonging works.
When he reached the mound, I finally looked at him. "Weston, I have to," he said, and stuck out his hand.
Walking off the mound, I was angry. I was so mad at myself that I'm sure some tears came. In that moment, I probably learned nothing. It probably destroyed what little confidence I had. I was mad enough that there wasn't space to learn anything.  
I had to suck it up and keep going, coach put me over at shortstop, and I still had to stay in the game. I still had to be there if the ball came my way. Although I'll admit, I prayed with everything I had that no ball came my way because I felt like I couldn't play at all.
But now, especially looking back, I realize pitching is a mind game. Once your mind's defeated, you don't have any hope. Unless you can battle back and win your mind back, it's not gonna work.
When have you had perfect technique but been in the wrong place to use it?
That's when I understood what had happened on that mound. It wasn't the mechanics that failed me. It was the gate I was trying to walk through. I was trying to force belonging instead of earning it, trying to prove I could pitch to people who'd never seen me succeed.
Being the quiet kid in the farm league was fine because people knew me, and I knew them. I would open up a little bit more. But being the quiet kid in a small town, trying to fit into a team that I hadn't played with? That's a recipe for not doing well.
Perfect practice makes perfect, but sometimes imperfect is what's needed. Sometimes the right tool isn't just about skill, it's about being in the right place with the right people who know who you are. I was learning to be like water - finding the shape of where I belonged rather than trying to force my way through solid rock.
I learned something that day that would serve me for the rest of my life, though I didn't understand it then. My father's lessons about precision, about having the right tool for the job, about doing things the right way, those weren't just about mechanics. They were about building a foundation that could hold when everything else started shaking.
The mechanics were there. The preparation was there. I had practiced those motions thousands of times. But what I hadn't prepared for was how much the environment mattered. How much belonging mattered. How much confidence mattered.
In the farm league, I had all of that. I belonged. My teammates knew me. My mind was clear, so my mechanics worked. In high school, I was technically more skilled, but I was also alone. Disconnected. And when your foundation isn't just about technique but about trust and belonging, even perfect mechanics can fall apart.
My father taught me that there's a right tool for every job. What I learned on that mound was that sometimes the right tool isn't just about skill, it's about having your mind right. It's about being in the right place with the right people who know who you are.
Years later, in the military, I would remember this lesson. When you're under pressure, when everything is on the line, preparation matters. But being where you belong matters too. Having people around you who know what you're capable of, even when you forget it yourself.
The precision my father taught me with tools, my mother reinforced with catching gear and endless practice sessions, became the foundation for everything that came after. Not just the technical skills, but the understanding that doing things right matters. That preparation matters. That the people around you matter.
Some foundations are built with concrete and steel. Others are built with repeated motions, practiced until they become automatic. But the strongest foundations are built with both skill and belonging, technique and trust.
Now, standing behind my own boys as they learn to throw, I watch their small hands grip the ball the way mine once did. The same mound rises before them, that sacred fifteen inches where dreams either take flight or crash to earth. But this time, I'm not just teaching them mechanics. I'm teaching them that the right tool for the job includes knowing where you belong, and that sometimes the most important preparation happens not in your arm, but in your heart.
The dirt feels different under their cleats than it did under mine all those years ago. Softer, somehow. More forgiving. Because this time, they're not trying to prove they belong. They already do.

Chapter 5: Quiet Strength

At Buescher State Park, two mighty oak trees stood witness to every version of myself I've ever been. Their massive trunks rose from the Texas earth like ancient pillars, bark rough with decades of weather, branches spreading wide enough to shelter a dozen people. When they ask you to sit down and make a four-year plan, I had no idea where to start. My hands were learning to be steady, even when my mind felt scattered. The gate between boyhood and manhood isn't marked by ceremony. Chapter 6: The Still Mind

When you’re writing a book, of course, you think back to your English teachers. But there’s one gate I walked through that I still remember, even though I didn’t know it was a gate at the time.

A deer stepped into the meadow.

That was my first sentence in Mrs. Edwards’ class. Basic. Forgettable. The kind of sentence you write when you’re not trying, when you think English is just another requirement to check off before you can get back to what matters.

Mrs. Edwards’ classroom felt different from the moment you stepped inside. Not just the arrangement of desks or the posters on the walls, but something in the air itself. Something that said: your thoughts matter here.

The classroom was arranged in a semicircle rather than rows, desks forming a gentle curve that made everyone feel included in whatever conversation might unfold. Natural light streamed through windows on one side, casting everything in warm morning brightness that made even Monday mornings feel like possibilities. The walls were covered with student work, not just store-bought motivational posters, giving the space a lived-in feeling that belonged to us. The air carried that particular school smell, dry erase markers, old books, and something indefinable that spoke of years of thoughts being shaped in this room.

English was my worst subject in high school, but I still vividly remember my first day walking through that door. I still remember my last.

Mrs. Edwards was different. Her classroom felt more collaborative than others. She was there to teach us, but it wasn’t rigid. She had a lesson plan, I’m sure, but it could change based on what came up in class discussion. Her hands moved when she talked, not frantically, but with purpose, like she was shaping ideas in the air, pulling thoughts from nothing and making them real. She was there to teach us English, but she was really there to teach us about life too. Nothing that crossed any boundaries, but she absolutely dove into making us better people. She was a molder of minds, not just a teacher.

You could tell she cared. She listened. She was there for us. I think that came from many years of being a counselor before teaching. You could feel it in the way she positioned herself when a student was talking, leaning in slightly, hands resting still on her desk, eyes focused completely on whoever was speaking. You could see her actually thinking about what you said, not just waiting for her turn to talk. That’s what made her different. She wasn’t there just to collect a paycheck and move on.

There was more opportunity to speak up in her class. We got to share ideas, especially when we dove into different novels and books. You felt safe to share your ideas because she was awesome, but also because you knew if someone attacked you for those views, she would absolutely be there to defend you. Even if she didn’t agree with you, she would be there to defend your right to think it. She shut down any condescension early, her voice getting that quiet firmness that made everyone pay attention.

I’ve learned over the years that Mrs. Edwards and I have very different ideas about how to fix the world’s problems. Back then in high school, I had no idea. I think that’s beautiful as a teacher. That she could teach life lessons without letting her biases interfere. That’s super hard to do, and most don’t put in the effort. But she did it amazingly.

That first day, she had us write one simple sentence. Mine was about a deer. Something basic, forgettable. “A deer stepped into the meadow.”

Then she took us through this step-by-step process of how to expand and really develop a scene with words. How to describe it so someone else could see it in their mind, could step through the same gate you were opening with language.

Her hands moved across the whiteboard as she demonstrated, adding layers like an artist adding brushstrokes to a canvas. The dry erase marker squeaked slightly with each stroke, leaving trails of blue ink that transformed simple words into something more. “What kind of subject do you have?” she asked the class, her voice carrying genuine curiosity, not judgment. “What does your scene look like? What time of day? What season? What sounds are there? How does the air feel?” Each question opened another door, another gate to walk through in whatever story we were each creating.

I watched other students lean forward as their own simple sentences began to bloom. The sound of pencils on paper filled the room, that particular scratching that means thoughts are being born. One girl, who rarely spoke up, raised her hand. “Could my character be scared of something?” Mrs. Edwards’ face lit up like she’d been waiting all day for exactly that question. “Perfect. What might it be scared of? How would that show in its body language?”

The room became electric with possibility. Students who usually slumped in their chairs were sitting up, pencils moving across paper. I could hear the scratching of erasers, the soft sounds of words being born and reborn on white paper as each of us worked on our own unique sentences. Even the dust motes in the morning light seemed to pause, hanging in the air like they were listening too.

My simple sentence about a deer eventually became: “A young doe stepped cautiously from the cool shadows of the oak trees into the sun-drenched meadow, her ears twitching at every sound as the tall grass swayed gently in the warm afternoon breeze, always ready to bolt back to the safety of the woods.”

It was an amazing transformation. Not just of the sentence, but of my mindset about English. Before, it had been just a simple subject to get by in. I’d think, “Why do I need to write a paper I’m never going to use again?” I had never been taught the purpose of writing. But after seeing that moment in my mind that I had created out of nothing, I realized I could actually do this. I had created art, in a sense, through words that could put pictures in someone else’s mind.

My hands, which had always felt clumsy with a pencil, had somehow learned to build something beautiful with words. I had found their right tool for the job.

That didn’t go anywhere for a long time. Writing a book still seemed impossible. But I realized then that I could shape words to make something I wanted, to tell a story that mattered. It gave me confidence I’d carry into college and beyond.

Who saw something in you before you had eyes to see it yourself?

That lesson about opening gates with language stayed with me, even when I didn’t know it. Learning to create doorways for others to step through. Learning that sometimes the most important work happens in stillness, in the quiet space where thoughts form into words.

Mrs. Edwards continued to teach me that lesson in different ways throughout the year. But it wasn’t until the very last day that I understood what she’d really been doing all along.

The last day of the year the room felt different, charged with the weight of endings and beginnings. Sunlight streamed through those same windows, but now it carried the promise of summer, of futures that were rushing toward us whether we felt ready or not. The air itself seemed thicker with emotion, with things unsaid that were about to be said. Even the familiar smell of dry erase markers and old paper seemed tinged with nostalgia.

She went around the room and said something special about each student. I watched her move through our semicircle, her hands quiet now, resting on her desk as she spoke to each of us. No gestures today, just stillness, like she was holding space for what needed to be said. The mood was reflective, happy, but heavy too. As she worked her way around, I was getting a little nervous. I don’t take compliments well. I brush them off because I don’t think super highly of myself.

But it was obvious she had put time into this beforehand. She thought of each student individually and said something very deep, not surface level. Her eyes carried the weight of someone who had really seen us, really known us.

She stopped at one student first, the class clown who could make anyone laugh, even on the worst Mondays. She said, her voice warm with affection, “You have this incredible gift. You can walk into a room full of strangers and within minutes have them all laughing together. That’s not just humor. That’s leadership. You bring people together through joy, and that’s something the world desperately needs.”

The student, for once, was speechless. His usual quick grin softened into something deeper.

A few seats over, she paused at another student, who wrote poetry that made our creative writing sessions feel like sacred space. “You paint with words the way artists paint with brushes. When you write, you don’t just tell us what happened. You make us feel like we lived it ourselves. That’s a rare gift.”

The girl’s eyes filled with tears she tried to blink away.

Then she came to me. I thought it would be more surface level because that’s all I thought I had given off. Just surface.

In the back of my mind, I thought, I’m an introvert. Nobody really knows me. Not out of meanness, just because I’m a quiet guy. I felt invisible wherever I went, and not in a bad way, not in a loner kind of way. I’m just comfortable being quiet, comfortable on my own.

When she got to me, I could almost see a tear in her eye. She said it very evenly, with power behind it. Not loud, but I knew she 100% meant it: “Weston, it’s because of guys like you it makes it a little bit easier to send my daughter out into the world one day.”

Man, I got choked up. I was floored. I was amazed that she had even seen me.

What went through my mind? For a mother to say that meant a lot. Coming from a mother, that meant more than I can describe. She meant that in a crazy, oftentimes evil world where there are guys who want to take advantage or guys who just aren’t great, I gave her hope for her daughter that there are still good guys out there. At least that’s what it meant to me.

It absolutely changed how I thought about being the quiet guy. I realized people were watching. Even if you don’t think you give off anything, you really do. If people pay attention, they’ll see you. That’s the kind of person I wanted to surround myself with. People who actively see you. Not just listen, because I didn’t really talk much, but who listened to your actions.

I took that as a strength from then on. Before, I thought my quietness was shyness, which to an extent it was. I thought it was a weakness because I didn’t put myself out there. But it’s actually a strength. A quiet strength that doesn’t need to announce itself. Hands that do their work without fanfare. A presence that guards important gates without making a show of it.

What Mrs. Edwards said meant even more because it made my quietness feel like a strength. She taught me that still waters run deep, and the deepest waters carve the strongest channels. Not through force or noise, but through patient, persistent presence. It connects to how I understand having a still mind now. A quiet person doesn’t mean they don’t have anything to share. It doesn’t mean they’re weak.

There’s a difference between an empty mind and a still mind. A still mind isn’t inactive. It’s pondering, trying to think things through more deeply, trying to internalize lessons. Because when the storm comes, the deeper water stays calm while the surface gets thrashed about. Down deep, it’s still calm, still thriving. Shallow water gets rippled by everything.

When I’m writing this book now, I’m using those same skills she taught me about creating pictures with words. About opening gates with language that let others step into experiences they’ve never lived. Every time I write anything, her lessons sit in the back of my mind. I don’t want to let her down, because she put so much into teaching me that I was capable of more.

The picture in my mind as I write this book is completing the art. Completing the picture of trials that didn’t just destroy me, but rebuilt me into something stronger. Maybe these words can create that same doorway for you. Maybe they can help strengthen your core so that storms don’t break as much.

Because that’s what I learned about the still mind through everything that came after Mrs. Edwards’ class. The still mind doesn’t mean you never feel chaotic. When the storm comes, of course you feel anxiety. Of course you feel thrashed about. But if your core down deep is still, like those deep waters she taught me to create with words, then your core will stay safe. When life tries to beat at you, the storms will test you. They’ll cause some damage, but your core will remain intact.

When storms are coming, it’s okay to take a break. It’s okay to take a moment to restart and refocus. Even if it’s a very quick moment just to let your mind focus in on one thing. That’s the still mind at work.

I try to see my own boys the way Mrs. Edwards saw me. I want them to understand that it’s okay to be shy, but it’s also okay to have quiet confidence. It’s okay to be the loud, funny one in the room too. It’s okay to have whichever strength you have.

My boys already show different personalities. Our oldest is the creative one who loves music and art. Our second son is the builder, constructing elaborate cities with Play-Doh and magnet tiles. Our youngest is our happy baby, easy to smile, bringing joy wherever he goes.

I see this balance in my marriage too. My wife creates the ideas. I build them. When you have both types, you get beauty that has a purpose.

Mrs. Edwards taught me that words could create art. That a still mind could run deep. That being seen for who you really are, not who you think you should be, changes everything.

The storms were coming. I didn’t know it then, but they were. And when they did come, I would need every bit of that still water, running deep.

That deer I wrote about on my first day? It’s still there, stepping cautiously from shadows into light. Only now I understand, that deer was me. And these words are my meadow, the space I’m creating for others to step into when they’re ready. A place where it’s safe to be quiet, where still waters run deep, where the right words at the right moment can change how someone sees themselves forever.

Just like Mrs. Edwards did for me.

Chapter 7: Into the Shadows
There are gates we cross that change how we see everything that comes after. Some open onto beauty so profound it stops your breath. Others open onto darkness so complete it threatens to swallow everything you thought you knew about what hands are meant to hold, what eyes are meant to see, what foundations are meant to withstand.
Some thresholds announce themselves. Others you cross without knowing until you're already on the other side, and you realize you're standing in territory where different rules apply, where your hands must learn new ways to steady themselves in the presence of what shouldn't exist but does.
There was a moment that burned itself into me, not because of what happened, but because of what I felt beneath my boots when everything changed. Sometimes the most important thresholds are marked by nothing more than pebbles and sand, a single strand of wire, and the sudden understanding that you've crossed into territory where different rules apply.
We were riding out from Al Assad in a MATV, the familiar rumble of armor and engine drowning out everything except the sound of my own breathing inside my gun turret. The vehicle's air conditioning struggled against Iraqi heat, blowing dust that had worked its way into every seal and vent.
Al Assad was supposed to have been a secure base in Iraq. They talked about swimming pools and real facilities in the briefings back home. When I got there, I realized it was much different than it had been before. There weren't very many personnel. As US troop levels drew down, locals took over most of the base. No swimming pool like most had made fun of. No unit buildings, just reclaimed bunkers. Not even a real gym. Just a skeleton of what it used to be, like walking through a house where most of the family had already moved out.
The driver, a guy I'd just met since I was fresh there, slowed the vehicle as we approached what he called "the gate." We'd just finished our daily brief where we got information for the day, who's going where, who's doing what, things to look out for. This was routine. Even though I was new, we did this every day. It wasn't significant until it became significant.
"Hop out and open her up," he said.
As I stepped down from the gunner's position, my boots finding purchase on ground that felt no different from any other patch of Iraqi desert, he added almost as an afterthought: "By the way... we're outside the wire now."
To call it a gate was an overstatement. The threshold between American control and everything else was marked by nothing more than a single strand of concertina wire and a metal bar that could stop a vehicle but couldn't stop a half determined dog. The perimeter from base to not base was generous in name only. A few fence posts held up sections of C wire for unknowns to crawl under, and often we'd find blankets draped over it where someone could simply go over the top. To call it a barrier was to give it more credit than it deserved.
But as I walked around the front of the MATV to lift that simple metal arm, everything shifted.
I looked down at my boot and realized: the dirt it just touched was hostile ground. The only American soil on that road was clinging to the treads of my boots.
Everything out there was sand, most of it like talcum powder that gets into everything. But that particular spot, probably because there was a gate there, had pebbles mixed in with the fine sand. The texture was different, coarser, more substantial somehow. I still remember looking down as I stepped through and took a second to make a mental snapshot. Looking down at my tan boots that matched the tan pebbles and tan sand all around. I can still remember the slightly coarser crunch of those pebbles as I put my full weight onto them, like dry gravel giving way under pressure. The temperature of the ground radiated up through my boot soles, already hot at morning, promising brutal heat by afternoon.
I took a deep breath and thought, "Well, this is it."
And I was wearing a freaking baseball cap.
It was pretty common when we were on base to get a little more comfortable and wear a baseball cap instead of full battle gear. We did a modified force protection condition alpha on base, so we could take our gear off. We always, always had to have a gun, whether pistol or rifle, everybody did. But while we were on the somewhat safe base itself, we could take our gear off. It helped with the heat.
I pulled off my baseball cap and reached for my helmet. The familiar weight settled onto my head, the chin strap clicking into place with that snap sound every service member knows. The padding inside was already damp with old sweat, pressing against my skull in spots worn smooth by daily use. It signaled both a physical and mental shift. Physical because you have to put that thing on, strap it in, and get ready. But it signifies a mental shift too because now you're looking for threats. If you're going to put all this gear on, then there's a threat.
It wasn't some big moment where something clicked into place. It was just like, "Okay, first time I've ever been out where this is real. Get the job done."
And something inside me understood: this is real now. You need to be sharper than ever. And if you screw this up, someone could die.
After I got back in the vehicle, we continued our patrol. Mission as normal. We drove through a landscape that seemed to stretch endlessly in every direction. Desert meeting more desert, broken only by the occasional cluster of old, run down buildings that looked like they'd been baking in the sun for decades. Concrete walls pocked with holes, metal roofing that had seen better years, structures that might have been shops or homes or something else entirely, still occupied and active but serving purposes we couldn't guess from a distance.
The desert itself rolled out for miles in every direction until it eventually met small villages on the horizon, or disappeared into the haze where we knew the city lay. Everything was tan and brown and faded, like the color had been bleached out by years of relentless sun. It was beautiful in its own harsh way, but it was also unmistakably foreign, unmistakably not home.
The patrol lasted a few hours before we came back in. Sometimes we went longer, sometimes shorter. Nothing crazy happened. Nobody shot at us. Just a normal day doing our job.
But that moment, stepping across that line, feeling those pebbles crunch under my boots, putting my helmet on, that stayed with me. Because I realized there's a parallel between stepping outside the physical wire and stepping into spiritual hostile territory.
Thankfully, for the physical wire, you see it coming. You have to physically put on your gear and get in the vehicle and cross. So your mind is ready for it. But stepping into spiritual hostile territory? You never know when it's going to come, and really, it's always coming at you. Through your phone, through the TV, through advertisements on the road, through anything and everything. It's constantly bombarding you, even at your most vulnerable. You really have no time to put on your armor and mentally prepare because it's always coming at you.
The way to get your head right spiritually is like putting on your helmet physically. For me, it's probably taking a breath, trying to center myself. It's not always praying and reading scripture and going to church, though those matter. It's more about getting yourself centered, trying to calm the mind, getting ready for the craziness of the day. Whether that's angry people in traffic, angry kids, cranky animals, whatever it might be. Just trying to center yourself and saying, "Hey, I'm going to learn today."
How do you know when you're crossing from safe ground to hostile ground in life? That's the hard part. You never do. You never know.
That night was cooler than you would imagine Iraq could be. The desert air had that particular bite that comes after the sun disappears, when all the heat that's been building in sand and stone all day suddenly releases into a sky so clear you can see stars that look close enough to touch.
I was heading to the shower facility near our CHUs, combat housing units. You'd have a cluster of them surrounded by big concrete T walls that look like upside down T's. They go up probably 12 to 14 feet, real tall, to block shrapnel or rounds from coming in. But you have gaps where you can come in and out, and outside those gaps they have portable trailers, shipped over in a box. Some have showers, some have bathrooms, some are a mix. This was a mix.
I'm law enforcement, so I'm paranoid by nature. Typically, I took my gun every single time, but this one single time, I said, "You know what? I'm just going to go shower. It's been a long day. I'm dusty, tired, hot, sweaty. I'm just going to go chill."
The bathroom was surprisingly clean for deployment. Not sparkling, but maintained. Someone cared enough to keep it functional. The showers were tiny, like phone booths with spigots, but for us, they felt amazing because we didn't have water all the time to take a shower, especially hot water.
There were a couple of guys when I got in there. Normal conversation, normal sounds of soldiers ending a long day. I fell into the familiar rhythm of cleaning up. The hot water felt incredible against my skin, washing away the day's dust and sweat. I could feel my shoulders relax as the heat worked into muscles that had been tense for hours. Steam filled the small space, fogging up the metal mirror, and I found myself moving through the motions I'd done countless times before.
After the shower, I stood at the small metal sink and worked up a lather with my shaving cream. The routine was automatic, soothing even. Steady strokes with the razor, the familiar scrape against skin, rinsing the blade in warm water. My reflection looked back at me through the slowly clearing steam, and for a moment it felt like I could have been in any bathroom anywhere. The mundane comfort of taking care of yourself at the end of a long day.
Then I brushed my teeth, the minty foam and the mechanical back and forth motion of the brush completing the ritual. My mind was completely at ease, running on autopilot through these simple acts of maintenance that connected me to normal life. This was the part of deployment that felt like home: the quiet, personal moments when you could just be human, taking care of the basic things that keep you grounded.
The two guys had left by then, and I was enjoying the peaceful solitude. I was about to spit and rinse, about to go back to my CHU and sink into bed and watch a movie. The fluorescent lights hummed overhead, that particular buzz that sounds like electricity trying to escape. The air was thick with humidity from the showers, mixing the scent of soap and shampoo with the underlying smell of a building that's seen too many people pass through. My hands worked the toothbrush in slow, methodical strokes, completely relaxed, mind wandering to what movie I might watch before sleep.
When the door opened and this guy came in, I knew why that sixth sense started firing.
It was his eyes.
I didn't know him. I'd never seen him before. He had his M4 slung across his chest, which wasn't unusual. We all carried weapons. But I distinctly remember when he opened the door, I could see his face, his eyes. I can't quite describe them. They were almost hollow and empty, but there was pain and maybe anger mixed in there. Like looking into a well and seeing something dark moving at the bottom. Enough to where immediately that sixth sense kicked in. Something's off. So off that it made me speak.
I said, "Hey, what's up, man?"
My voice echoed slightly in the small space, bouncing off the walls and metal fixtures. It sounded too loud, too forced, like I was trying to convince both of us that everything was normal.
He looked at me, then turned and went to the last bathroom stall. Didn't say a word. Just that look, that moment of eye contact that felt like staring into something that had already given up.
I knew I had to get the heck out of there. My hands moved faster, gathering up my stuff, every instinct screaming that I was in the wrong place at the wrong time. I knew something was weird, something different. So I gathered up my toiletries and went out into the cool desert night air. Went down the wooden steps that creaked under my boots and began the trek back to my CHU, the sand crunching underneath my feet with that same sound I'd heard earlier crossing the wire.
When I heard what sounded like a sharp slamming door.
But it wasn't a door.
I didn't feel the cool air as I ran back. Didn't hear my boots on the wooden steps or the sand giving way beneath my feet. Just the sound of my own heartbeat drowning out everything else.
What do you do with hands that have touched things they can't forget?
The next morning at chow, people were talking about it. That's when it set in that it had been real. My brain had gone into self preservation mode, processing what I'd witnessed like it was a movie I'd watched rather than something I'd lived through. It was a protective mechanism, making the experience feel distant and unreal so I could function. But hearing others talk about it brought it crashing back into reality.
I was the last person to see him alive. The first to know he'd opened the final gate by his own hand, the one you can never cross back through.
OSI, Office of Special Investigations, said I was being investigated for homicide. Not that they suspected me, but it was their way of dotting i's and crossing t's. I don't think anybody ever thought I had anything to do with it, but you have to look at every avenue.
My instincts were very, very heightened after that. One of those situations where the hair stands on the back of your neck and your gut is telling you to get the heck out of there. What bothered me the most was, I didn't have a gun. We didn't have to have it when showering and that was the only time I ever left it behind. That hit me probably weeks or months later, maybe after the deployment when I had time to process it. I realized that if he had wanted me to take part in his pain, just one little move and I would have been gone. There was absolutely zero I could have done.
I often wonder why he didn't. The suicide itself, that's his weight to carry, not mine. I don't know what he was going through, so I let that one go. But how easy it could have been for me to go too?
That question lives in me now. Sits in my chest like a weight I can't put down. One moment of different timing. One second longer in that bathroom. One choice by a man whose pain I couldn't see coming. And I would have been gone. Not because I was a target, not because I was a threat, but because I was just... there. In the wrong place when someone else's darkness finally won.
That's what stuck with me and took a lot of space in my mind. How thin the line really is. How quickly everything you are, everything you're building, everyone who loves you, can disappear because of someone else's broken moment.
There's a difference between being trained for physical threats and being trained for spiritual, emotional darkness. They can intertwine, and they can be completely different.
Your body can be trained to react to certain things in certain ways. Reacting to gunfire, it's easy to train your body to take cover and return fire. Your hands know what to do. Your feet know where to go. But training your spirit and emotional side for what comes after? That's a different kind of preparation entirely. I think that's the biggest distinction. We can train and train and train for when things break down, but it's the quiet stillness afterwards that nobody trains you for.
The movies show the explosions, the gunfire, the adrenaline. They show 105mm rounds going off that shake your bones, drone strikes that light up the sky, jets screaming overhead so loud you feel them in your chest. All that noise, all that chaos, all that clarity of purpose when everything is loud and dangerous and you know exactly what you're supposed to do.
But they never show you what comes after. The ringing silence when the guns stop. The moment when your heart rate starts to come down and your hands stop shaking and you're left alone with what just happened. When the adrenaline drains out and leaves you sitting in the dark, staring at nothing, trying to process what you've seen or done or survived.
That's when your mind really goes to work. That's when the questions start. That's when you're most vulnerable, because there's no protocol for the quiet. No training manual for the moment when everything stops and you're left with yourself and what you carry.
It's probably the hardest transition there is. Much harder than facing the possibility of being shot at, to be honest.
Because a firefight can only last so long. Eventually, one side runs out of ammo, or takes casualties, or retreats to regroup. Someone calls for backup or air support. The enemy breaks contact and disappears. You get extracted or reinforced. There's always an end point, always a resolution, even if it's not the one you wanted. But spiritual, emotional battles? There's no time limit. If you don't face them head on, they can last for a lifetime. There's no helicopter pulling you out of that one. 
Even the deepest waters have a bottom. That's what makes them still. They've touched the depths and found solid ground. They've been to the bottom and learned that even there, there's foundation to stand on.
After this experience, there was another time where I recognized those same hollow eyes in someone else. But having seen that darkness in the bathroom, having felt how quickly someone could disappear, I couldn't walk away. I stayed until I knew he was safe. The bathroom had taught me to see the signs, and more importantly, to act on them.
That's what I mean about pulling people out of darkness. But those extreme moments, they're just the end of a long chain of smaller choices. The more important work happens in the everyday moments, the small acts of pulling people out of small darknesses before they become the deeper kind.
My wife understands this better than anyone. She asks how people are and actually cares about the answer. She'll dive into whatever someone tells her, giving them her full attention. She cares enough to ask the cashier at the register how their day is really going. Maybe that comes naturally to her, or maybe it's because she met me when I was a cashier, and that turned out pretty good. Either way, she sees what I'm still learning: amazing people are everywhere, working in all kinds of places, carrying their own weights, needing someone to notice they're human.
I think it's the small acts, pulling people out of small darknesses, that matter most. Because if you do that, there's less chance of the heavier darkness taking hold.
For physical combat, you run drills. You do building clearing, assess threats, work your timing and shooting skills. Spiritual warfare requires the same kind of preparation, just different tools: prayer, scripture study, finding people to worship with, constantly trying to align yourself with what's true.
As a father, my willingness to step into difficult places has changed completely. I would go pull my boys out of anywhere, no matter what. Whether it's a physical danger or an emotional struggle or a spiritual conversation that makes everyone uncomfortable. I'll go there. It doesn't matter.
Because there are dark places you have to be willing to enter for your family. The obvious ones are physical threats, intruders, violence. But those are rare, and I'm trained for those. The harder ones are emotional struggles, spiritual battles. Places where you can't shut off your feelings and go on automatic. You have to stay present, stay connected, think all the way through it.
When you go outside the wire, you're facing the possibility of warfare. When you go out into the world, you're facing the certainty of spiritual warfare. The question isn't whether the battle is coming. The question is: are you prepared? Have you done your training? Are you willing to step into that difficult place, into that conversation, into that uncomfortable space where someone needs help?
The light pierces darkness, but you have to be willing to carry it in. You have to be willing to step outside whatever wire protects you, put your helmet on, and get your head right, knowing that if you mess this up, someone could die.
Sometimes the most important gates we cross are marked by nothing more than pebbles crunching under our boots. Sometimes the most crucial work our hands do happens in dark places where no one will ever write headlines about our courage.
But that doesn't make it less important. If anything, it makes it more so.
Because the darkness is real. But so is the light. And sometimes, the light needs someone willing to carry it into the shadows.

Chapter 8: When Leadership Gets Real

There’s a moment when leadership stops being theoretical and becomes the weight you carry in your chest, the choice you make when two good people disagree, and your hands are the ones that have to choose a direction even when the path isn’t clear.

I used to think leadership meant clarity. I thought that with enough prayer, planning, and unity, we would all land on the same answer. That if we were in tune with God, there wouldn’t be tension, just agreement. But I’ve learned that the tension is often where real leadership begins. Not the title. Not the spotlight. Not the meetings where everyone nods along. The tension.

Because eventually, you’ll hit a moment where two people, both seeking good and both listening for gentle guidance, walk away with different convictions. One feels a deep urgency to act. The other feels the sacred weight of waiting. And neither of them is wrong. That’s when your hands learn what it means to carry something heavier than tools or weapons. That’s when you discover there’s no training manual for the gate between different kinds of wisdom.

I’ve been both of those people. And I’ve worked with both of them too. I know how it feels to sit with a heart full of concern and feel that the longer we wait, the more someone might slip away. I’ve also felt the opposite: that to move too fast or too boldly might fracture trust or do harm. In those moments, you don’t need a microphone. You need humility. Because leadership is no longer hypothetical. It’s real.

And real leadership doesn’t come with a script.

The first time I learned this lesson, I was terrified. I was a staff sergeant in a room full of colonels, sitting in the base theater for a meeting about a nuclear exercise. It was a huge theater with a big stage, maybe 100-200 people scattered across seats that had seen thousands of briefings. The air conditioning hummed overhead with that particular frequency that makes even routine meetings feel urgent.

We were setting up an exercise for nuclear operations where we had to get a certain number of aircraft off the ground in a certain amount of time. An exercise we did often, but for this one we were mixing things up, using different aircraft and more of them. The commander wanted to put an entry control point right in the middle of our operational area because it would give easier access for his pilots.

I could see the problem immediately. My hands, which had been resting calmly on my notebook, started to tighten as I visualized what would happen. Aircraft nose to nose. Too many things moving in too quick of a time. If something went wrong, we’d have aircraft frozen on the ramp, and the whole exercise would collapse into chaos.

But I was the lowest ranking person in that room. A staff sergeant surrounded by officers whose ribbons told stories of decades of experience and responsibility. The kind of men whose hands had signed orders that moved thousands of people and millions of dollars of equipment. What did I know that they didn’t?

My heart pounded against my ribs like it was trying to escape. The reasonable thing would have been to stay quiet, to trust that someone with more rank and experience would see the problem and speak up. But I was also the subject matter expert for my unit, the one who had years of experience getting this mission done at every level. I’d been in every meeting, every briefing. I knew this exercise forwards and backwards, and I knew the natural reactions when things went sideways.

The gate between safety and disaster isn’t always marked by obvious signs. Sometimes it’s marked by a decision that looks reasonable on a briefing slide but falls apart the moment real people with real equipment try to execute it under pressure.

The ground felt like shifting sand beneath me, unstable and threatening to swallow my courage whole.

I raised my hand.

It felt like interrupting, but I spoke up anyway. “Sir, if you put the ECP there, you’re going to have aircraft nose to nose. Too many things have to move in too quick of a time. If something goes wrong, we’re gonna have aircraft frozen on the ramp.”

The room went quiet. Not the comfortable quiet of people thinking, but the uncomfortable quiet of junior enlisted speaking truth to power in front of an audience. I could feel the weight of all those eyes, all that rank, all that experience focused on me and the words I’d just put into the air.

The pilots complained. They didn’t want to walk further to get to their aircraft. But as they thought through what I’d said, you could see recognition dawn on their faces. They realized I was right, and they’d just have to deal with the extra walking.

That moment taught me something fundamental about leadership: it’s not about rank or title. It’s about being willing to speak up when you see something that matters, even when you’re scared. Even when you’re the lowest ranking person there. Even when staying quiet would be easier and safer and more comfortable for everyone involved.

But speaking up in a military briefing is one kind of leadership. The consequences are tactical, the feedback immediate, the stakes measured in mission success or failure. Spiritual leadership opens gates onto different territory entirely, where what you’re protecting isn’t aircraft or schedules, but souls and relationships and the sacred trust people place in your guidance.

I remember once, early in my experience with church leadership, rushing into a conversation I thought I understood. We were at someone’s home for a teaching appointment, sitting around talking about faith and life and what it means to follow Christ. The late afternoon light slanted through unfamiliar windows, casting everything in that golden glow that makes serious conversations feel both intimate and weighty. She was excited, engaged, asking questions, diving into the scriptures and learning about them. I felt what I believed was gentle guidance. I felt peace. So I asked her to take the next step.

“Would you like to follow Christ? Would you like to make the commitment to be baptized?”

Her face changed immediately. She looked appalled. Offended. It took me back because I wasn’t expecting that reaction at all.

“I can’t believe you asked that,” she said. Her tone was angry, hurt, like I’d crossed a line I didn’t even know existed.

I immediately apologized. “I’m so sorry, I moved too fast. What questions can I help you with instead?” I think she could tell my apology was sincere, and she calmed down quickly. At first she thought I was trying to push her, but once she realized I genuinely wanted to understand, she was open to talking.

My hands, which had been gesturing confidently just moments before, now rested quietly in my lap as I backed up and tried a different approach. “I can sense there’s something you’re wrestling with in the scriptures. Can we explore that together?”

Eventually, through what I learned is called an exploratory interview, finding out where someone is rather than where you think they should be, it came out that she wasn’t even sure if Christ was real. That was her struggle, not baptism, not commitment, but belief itself. That was the real concern and the real thing we needed to address.

It taught me a lesson I’ve carried ever since: don’t rush past the real concern. Find the real concern. Sit there with them. The gate between surface questions and deep struggles isn’t always obvious. When someone brings up what seems like a simple issue, ask “What makes you bring that up?” Because they may be seeing something you never saw.

That practice, clarifying the core concern, has changed everything for me. It makes it possible to lead without control, to stand firm without becoming rigid, to act with conviction while still leaving room for others.

What do you do when your strength becomes your weakness?

But the truth is, I didn’t realize how much weight spiritual leadership could carry until I had to step into a bigger role myself. I thought I was strong. I thought I could handle conflict. But I wasn’t prepared for the emotional toll. The deep sorrow when people I care about don’t align. The exhaustion, physical, mental, and spiritual, that comes from wrestling with a dozen competing options, knowing only one can move forward.

In church leadership, the sacred weight can impact everything. People see the choice you make, but they don’t see the sleepless nights of wrestling with God, the constant prayer, the brain fatigue that feels like searching for a needle in a haystack with a dying flashlight. When your mind is exhausted from working so hard on a problem, it can slow your whole body down, make you feel like a well that’s been drawn from too long.

And usually, the breakthrough doesn’t come until you finally sit down in exhaustion and say, “God, I don’t know what to do. I need You.” Only then does the path begin to clear.

What happens when you’re not sure what the right decision is, but you still have to make a call? You go based off your experience, based off what you think, what your spouse thinks. I often go back to scripture. When I’m not sure, maybe the decision I’m wrestling with isn’t the one I need to make right now. Maybe I need to wait.

It can be scary, especially when you feel responsible for making decisions that affect others. When you’re making spiritual decisions, you want to do right by people. You don’t want to lead people in the wrong direction. There’s no easy way around it. You put the work in, you pray, but at the end of the day, you’re putting something out there and standing behind it. It can be nerve-wracking.

That sacred weight taught me to appreciate the quiet leaders I’ve served with. Men who carried enormous responsibility with grace, whose voices went quiet but strong under pressure. They didn’t fill the room, but people looked to them for guidance. I couldn’t see their sleepless nights or conversations with their spouses until I was carrying similar weight myself. They made masterful leadership look effortless, quietly steering without anyone realizing it. I used to admire them. Now, I revere them.

There are still moments I question myself. Not because I don’t care, but because I do. I’ve had to ask, “What if I’m wrong?” more times than I can count. But I also try to surround myself with voices I trust. My wife is one of those. She’ll listen, challenge, and help me find center again. Not to take away the responsibility, but to remind me I don’t have to carry it alone.

And maybe that’s the real secret of leadership when it gets real.

It’s not about being right. It’s about staying faithful in the process. It’s about walking through tension without becoming bitter, protecting others while being willing to be shaped yourself. It’s about having hands steady enough to make hard decisions but humble enough to change course when you learn something new.

Real leadership begins where certainty ends. In the space between what you know and what you don’t, between the weight you carry in your chest and the faith that God will guide your hands to do what needs doing. It’s about learning to guard the gates that matter most, especially when you can’t see what’s on the other side.

The moment when leadership stops being theoretical and becomes the weight you carry? That’s not when you get the title or the position. That’s when you realize the hands that must choose direction are yours, and the choice matters more than your comfort with making it. That’s when you understand being enough isn’t about having all the answers, it’s about being willing to carry the questions until the answers come.

Leadership, when it gets real, teaches you that enough means showing up. Enough means staying faithful in the process. Enough means your hands remain steady even when your heart is uncertain, because the people depending on you need someone who refuses to quit, not someone who has it all figured out.

Chapter 9: The Days You Arrived

I need to pause here and tell you something. This book started as letters to my boys. So forgive me while I talk to them directly. Feel free to listen in.

I think my experiences are important. My intent sitting down and writing this book, and all the effort put into it, was originally to pass on my life lessons and mistakes to smooth other people’s roads. But out of all those experiences, war and building, growing up and learning and going after my own dreams, the words to you in this chapter are the most important words I’ll ever put on paper.

-----

My oldest, the day you were born changed everything about who I thought I was.

All the time your mother was pregnant, I was fine. It wasn’t real yet to me. She could feel you moving around in there, pressing against her ribs, keeping her awake at night. But to me, you were still just an idea, a future possibility wrapped in ultrasound images that looked more like weather patterns than a person. I wasn’t scared because it hadn’t hit me yet what was actually happening.

The moment it became real, I remember forever. We were in the hospital room that smelled like antiseptic and floor wax, your mother gripping my hand through contractions that came in waves. The air conditioning hummed overhead, mixing with the steady beep of monitors tracking your heartbeat and hers. I was trying to be helpful, offering ice chips and adjusting pillows, when your mother looked at me between contractions and said, “Just tell me something. Anything.”

I panicked. What do you say in a moment like that? So I blurted out the first thing that came to mind: “I love horses.”

It actually made her laugh, even through the pain. We still laugh about that to this day, how in one of the most important moments of our lives, that’s what my brain came up with. But maybe that’s exactly what she needed. Not something profound, just something real and completely random from the man she married.

Then the doctor came in and said, “Alright, it’s time to have a baby.”

The bed transformed like something from a science fiction movie. Metal stirrups swung into place with mechanical clicks. This massive surgical light descended from the ceiling, casting everything in harsh white brightness that made the room feel like an operating theater. When that light hit your mother’s face, when I saw the seriousness in the doctor’s eyes, it all became real in a single instant.

The gate between man and father stood wide open, and I was terrified to cross it.

Terrified for your mother that she would be okay, that you would come out healthy, that nothing would go wrong. Just wondering, how in the world am I going to do this? I don’t know how to do this. Before that moment, I actually hadn’t held that many babies, and I don’t think I’d ever changed a diaper. My hands, which had learned precision under pressure in foreign places, suddenly felt clumsy and uncertain. It was absolutely terrifying.

But here’s the thing about gates and the hands that open them: some thresholds transform you in the crossing.

Before you were born, I was scared to hold a baby. I didn’t want to break them, didn’t trust my hands to be gentle enough. The moment you came out, slippery and perfect and screaming like you were announcing yourself to the world, all that disappeared. There is no explanation for how much you love your little kid, especially the moment they’re born. For me as a father, it’s special when your wife is pregnant, but you don’t get to experience it like mothers do. Mothers have a very deep and special connection that comes from pregnancy because they know the baby long before anybody else does. I really only started knowing you when you were born. But all those emotions flooded into one moment, and I was definitely not scared to hold you anymore.

You came out and didn’t cry. You screamed. Full-blown scream that filled the delivery room and probably echoed down the hospital corridor. The nurses looked at each other and smiled like they’d heard this symphony before. And that’s the way you live life. That’s the way you are. You never really cried as a baby, you just screamed. Even today, you’re either quiet or loud. No middle ground. But I love it because you’re not afraid to express yourself. You always know how you feel because you can always see it on your face. I wouldn’t change that. It might be a bit dramatic when you get stung by a wasp, but who cares?

When they placed you on your mother’s chest, your tiny hands opened and closed like you were trying to grab onto this new world. My hands reached out to touch your head, and I felt the soft spot where your skull hadn’t finished growing yet. So fragile, so perfect, so completely dependent on these same hands that had once gripped weapons in distant places.

We wanted to stay in the hospital as long as we could because we were terrified. The nurses would come check on us every fifteen minutes, their soft-soled shoes squeaking on the linoleum floors. It got old pretty quick when all we wanted was to sleep and figure out this whole baby thing, but they kept coming in with their clipboards and cheerful voices. We were like, “You’re gonna let us take this home and be alone? Take this baby home and be alone with him?” Because I had zero clue what I was doing. I had to have the nurses teach me how to swaddle, how to feed you, their patient hands guiding mine through the motions. When you cried, I didn’t know what you needed or wanted. I had to learn that when babies cry, they’re tired, hungry, or they need a diaper change. Honestly, that’s the three main concerns when they’re newborns. You’d be surprised how long it took me to figure that out. But I did.

My middle son, by the time you came along, we had our oldest for a little while. We had gone through the baby stage, and I realized how much I loved it. The sleepless nights that had once seemed impossible now felt like sacred territory, those quiet 3 AM feedings when the world was still and it was just us. So honestly, I was more excited than anything. We thought we had it figured out. We had mapped out the hospital so many times for the first kid that we were like, “We know what we’re doing. We’re fine.”

We were watching Survivor in the upstairs room, the evening air cool through open windows, when your mother’s water broke. My family was downstairs, so I kind of went into action-scared mode and stomped on the floor to wake them up even though I hadn’t done anything yet. The old floorboards creaked and groaned under my frantic footsteps. I was like, “We gotta go! Let’s go! Let’s go!” And your mother was the calm one, saying, “We’re fine.”

We raced to the hospital through empty midnight streets, streetlights creating pools of orange light on wet asphalt. When we got to the parking garage, we realized our Excursion was about a half inch or less from the top of this parking garage. I literally had to drive with my upper half outside the window, the crisp Washington air hitting my face, so I could watch the top and make sure we didn’t scrape. We had to go so slow, and every time we hit a speed bump, we’d scrape the top anyway. It made your mother laugh so hard that a little water would leak out, and she’d yell, “Quit making me laugh!” which would send her into even more laughter. Here we were, racing to deliver a baby, and we’re trapped in a parking garage moving at five miles an hour with your mom dying of laughter between contractions.

Then we went to cross the walkway where we were supposed to go to labor and delivery, but it was shut down because of construction. Orange cones and yellow tape blocked our path like some cruel joke. So, we had to go down to the main hospital and try to go through one door. It was locked. The glass was thick and institutional, and we stood outside trying to get someone’s attention. We talked to security over the intercom, but they couldn’t unlock it. I was like, “Is this the moment where I need to break down this door? But I can’t do that because I need to get my wife to the hospital without going to jail.”

Finally, some nurse let us in and took us to where we needed to go, her scrubs rustling as she moved quickly down the corridor. We thought we were prepared. We were not. A lot different experience than your brother because you just looked around the room and took everything in, the bright lights, the strange faces, the new sounds, then they put you on your mother’s chest and you snuggled with her like you’d been waiting your whole life for that moment. You may rush into things a bit more and get hurt, but you are not afraid to head into something you want or the direction you think you should go. I hope you keep hold of that.

My hands had learned confidence through practice. But you taught me something different about fatherhood. You showed me that each child brings their own personality, their own way of seeing the world. Watching you observe everything so calmly from day one, I realized I was learning as much from you as you were from me.

We could see your head literally wiggling as you worked your way into the world, this determined little person who apparently had places to go and people to meet. You weren’t waiting for anyone’s timeline but your own.

By the time you came along, we had a longer gap between your brother and you, so I almost had to relearn how tiny babies are and how they support themselves. You forget how their heads are so heavy compared to their necks, how carefully you have to cradle that weight. I once again had to have the nurse show me how to swaddle a baby because I had forgotten, her experienced hands moving the blanket in practiced folds while I watched like I was learning origami. You’d be surprised how quickly those abilities can leave your mind. But muscle memory returned quickly, and I still knew the three needs when babies cry.

You’ve taught me that sometimes the greatest strength is simply being joyful, being the one who brings light to ordinary moments.

With you, I learned that being a father isn’t just about protection or provision. Sometimes it’s about learning from the pure happiness of a child who sees good in everything. You reminded me that joy isn’t something you achieve, it’s something you choose, again and again, even when you’re small and everything is new and sometimes overwhelming.

My hands, which had learned steadiness under pressure in foreign places, now learned a different kind of precision: the gentle touch that soothes a crying baby, the firm grip that keeps a toddler safe, the balance between protection and freedom.

Teaching you how to be strong, how to be kind, how to use your own hands with both power and gentleness. Remember this: the world will try to convince you that gentleness is weakness. It's not. Chapter 10: The Foundation of Prayer
Some gates open onto sacred ground before you realize you’re standing on it. Some lessons your hands learn in darkness become the light you pass to small fingers learning to fold in prayer.
The difference between desperate prayer and deliberate prayer isn’t what I expected. Desperate prayer is raw, vulnerable, completely unguarded, the prayer of a man who has nothing left but God and the foundation his parents built before he knew he’d need it. Your hands shake as they fold. Deliberate prayer is intentional, structured, careful. Not because it’s less sincere, but because now I’m not just using the foundation. I’m building it. Your hands are steady as you guide.
The gate between using what was built for you and building for others, that’s where everything changes.
-----
The night we found the rocket; I almost got my team killed because of my ego.
We were running patrol routes outside Al Assad, following close behind the vehicle in front of us through the darkness. Inside the MATV, the air was thick and stale, mixed with the smell of equipment and sweat. When the vehicle in front of mine drove over something on the road, I saw it clearly through our windshield, a cylindrical object partially buried in sand and debris.
Every instinct screamed this was a threat. The cylindrical shape, the way the metal caught what little light we had - nothing natural about how it lay there. I threw the MATV in reverse, backing us away from the object before keying my radio. ‘Possible UXO at my current grid, requesting EOD assessment.’ But they were too far out, couldn’t get to us that night. We marked the spot and left it alone.
The next night, we learned that some other guys had gone out there. They called EOD and said it was just a PVC pipe, don’t worry about it.
That’s when my ego kicked in. “It wasn’t a PVC pipe,” I told my team. My professional pride couldn’t let it go. Instead of letting it be, we decided to go back out and prove it.
My guys were pissed off too. We’d all seen what it was. But we didn’t even have the forethought to bring proper digging tools. We just grabbed whatever we could find from the MATV. The first thing I could grab was this metal rod, the steel cold and unforgiving in my palm.
There we were, in hostile territory, all but one of us hunched over in full battle rattle, scraping away at dirt and sand and rock with metal rods. Trying to expose the back end of this “PVC pipe.” We worked carefully in the darkness, metal scraping against stone until we could see what I’d known all along.
Fins. This wasn’t PVC. This was a rocket. A big one.
I had to take a leak, so I walked away from the vehicle. When I turned back around, the headlights of two MATVs were casting long shadows over my buddies who were still digging this thing out with metal rods.
I could see the dust rising from their work, small clouds of sand and debris floating in the harsh light. Their breathing was heavy from the effort, visible in small puffs in the cool desert air. Metal scraped against stone in a steady rhythm. One of them paused to wipe sweat from his forehead before going back to scraping.
Then it hit me. One wrong angle. One tap in the wrong spot. One fragment of metal striking the wrong surface, and this whole scene would just… vanish.
Reality hit me like a physical blow. We could all just disappear. Right here, right now, because I’d let my ego override wisdom. We were digging around live ordnance with metal tools.
The hands that had gripped that metal rod now felt foreign to me. Like they belonged to someone reckless. Someone who had almost gotten good men killed for the sake of being right.
-----
Later that night, back in my CHU, the concrete floor cold against my knees, I crossed a different kind of gate. From pride to humility.
The space smelled like dust and stale air, months of temporary residents. A bare bulb cast stark shadows from overhead. I knelt beside my narrow bed, my hands folding together in a different kind of precision.
This wasn’t polished prayer. This was desperate, raw, the kind that comes when you have nothing left but God and the hope someone’s listening.
“God, I’m sorry for letting my ego get in the way. I almost got my team killed because I was worried about being right. Thank you for keeping us safe even though I probably didn’t deserve it. Please forgive me for being an idiot. Please keep watching over my wife while I’m here making terrible decisions.”
The prayer wasn’t polished or perfect. It was the prayer of a man who had just stared into the abyss of his own pride and found it nearly fatal. But it was also falling back on something deeper than training. Parents who taught me that when you hit bottom, you take it to God, realign yourself with what’s true, and learn from your mistakes.
When have you found that rock bottom was actually bedrock?
That’s desperate prayer. Raw, immediate, using the foundation someone else built when you’re too broken or scared to build anything yourself.

Deliberate prayer is completely different, and the difference shows me how far I’ve traveled from that desert floor.
It happened on a Tuesday night when our oldest couldn’t sleep. He’d been calling out every few minutes with another worry, another fear. The house was quiet except for the hum of the air conditioner and distant cattle settling outside, sounds of safety, of a world where the greatest threat was a child’s imagination.
Shay and I sat on either side of his bed, the soft glow of his nightlight casting everything in gentle warmth. His small hands fidgeted with his blanket, worry clear in his eyes.
“Hey buddy,” Shay said, her voice quiet in the peaceful darkness. “Let’s talk to God about this.”
This wasn’t desperate prayer. This was intentional, structured, careful. We were building something, not just using it. My hands, which had learned to fold in desperation in a distant desert, now guided small fingers through the basic movements of prayer.
“We start by talking to God,” I said, helping him position his hands. “Then we tell Him thanks for the good things. Then we ask Him for help with the hard things. Then we say sorry if we messed up. And we end in Christs name.”
Shay’s presence beside me was steady. She’s better at this than I am, more consistent with bedtime prayers. But tonight we were a team, both building the same foundation from different angles.
“Jesus God,” our son began, his voice small but clear. “Thank you for Mom and Dad and my brothers. Thank you for our house and our animals.”
I watched his face as he worked through the structure we’d given him, learning the rhythm of gratitude before petition. He asked for help with his fears, for protection, for good dreams. Simple, honest requests from a heart that hadn’t yet learned to doubt someone was listening.
“In Jesus name, amen.”
When he finished, the worry was gone from his eyes. Not because his circumstances had changed, but because he’d learned to place his concerns somewhere larger than himself. The foundation was setting, one prayer at a time.
That’s deliberate prayer. Not desperate, but intentional. Not using the foundation but building it. Teaching the structure so that when the unstructured moments come, my boys will have something to fall back on.

Desperate prayer was me falling back on what my parents had built into me in moments when I had nothing else left. When everything else failed, I still had that foundation to stand on.
Deliberate prayer is my wife and I building that same foundation for our boys, but from a different position. I’m not praying from rock bottom anymore. I’m praying from a place of wanting to give them tools for their own rock bottom moments.
It was Shay who suggested I write about this. She’s the one who reminds me to pray more often than I’d like to admit. “You should write about prayer,” she said one morning. “People need to know it’s not just something you do in church.”
She was right. Prayer, for me, isn’t confined to specific times or places. It’s a constant conversation, the thread that runs through everything else, the background awareness that I’m not doing any of this alone.
But teaching prayer to children requires more structure than living it as an adult. They need the framework, the security of knowing how to approach something as vast as talking to God. We give them words when they don’t have words, patterns when they don’t know how to start.
The goal isn’t to keep them in that structure forever. The goal is to give them something solid to fall back on when life strips away their certainty.
I hope my boys never find themselves in the kind of desperate circumstances that drive you to your knees on a concrete floor in a foreign country. But hard times will come.
When they do, I want them to have what my parents gave me. Not just the knowledge that prayer works, but the muscle memory of how to do it. The foundation that holds when everything else is shaking.
That’s the difference between desperate prayer and deliberate prayer. Desperate prayer was about survival, falling back on what had already been built. Deliberate prayer is about legacy, building something that will hold when I’m not there to hold it myself.
My hands, which learned to fold in desperation under foreign stars, now guide small fingers through the same motions in the safety of home. The prayer itself is the same. The God who listens is the same. But the purpose has transformed from survival to legacy, from using what was built to building what will last.
The gates between desperation and teaching, between using and building, between surviving and thriving, those are daily choices. Daily opportunities to choose what kind of foundation you’re standing on and what kind you’re building for the people who depend on you.
Whether you’re kneeling on concrete in a combat zone or sitting beside a small bed in the safety of home, prayer is the recognition that we don’t do any of this alone.
That’s the most important lesson I can teach my boys. Not just how to pray, but why to pray. Not just the structure, but the confidence that someone is listening, someone cares, and someone is strong enough to carry whatever weight we bring.
That foundation, built one prayer at a time, that’s what will hold when everything else fails. That’s what my parents gave me. That’s what I’m trying to give my boys.
Some gates open onto sacred ground. And once you’ve stood there, once you’ve learned what it means to fall back on what was built for you, your hands can’t help but build the same solid ground for others.
The metal rod that once threatened to destroy everything I cared about now rests silent and forgotten, while these same hands discover their true purpose, not grasping for validation, but guiding small souls toward the foundation that will hold them long after I’m gone.

Chapter 11: When Training Meets Reality

There’s a gate between knowing what to do and actually doing it. Between understanding procedures in a classroom and applying them when your heart is pounding and everyone is watching. Sometimes you cross that gate with competence. Sometimes you stumble through it and learn to laugh at yourself later.

My hands had learned precision from my father, steadiness from training, confidence from months of technical school. But they hadn’t yet learned how to stay calm when adrenaline hit like a freight train, or how to avoid flipping the wrong switch when you’re trying to look professional in front of half the base.

The Security Forces squadron worked like a well-oiled machine, at least in theory. The base was a wing that governed several squadrons, and we were one piece of that larger operation. Under S-3 operations, you had several flight chiefs, typically tech sergeants who led groups of 25 to 30 defenders. Those defenders were broken down into shifts: Alpha 1 and Alpha 2 for day shift, Charlie 1 and Charlie 2 for nights, and if you were lucky enough to have the manning, Bravo flight for swings.

It was a 12-hour shift that started at 6 AM, but you came in around 4:45 to get armed up, grab bathroom breaks, and stand at parade rest for guard mount. That’s where the new guys stood up and explained something from the regulations they’d been told to study the night before. Where we got our BOLOs, be on the lookout for anybody who wasn’t supposed to be there. Where flight chiefs laid out the day’s assignments and briefed us on anything special happening on base.

Gate duty was typically for new guys like me. You had to ride the gate a lot, put your time in before you could get on patrol. Echo 1 was the lead person; Echo 1 Alpha was your partner. Then you had one to three police patrol units roaming the base, flight line control units watching the aircraft, and Echo 2 manning the commercial gate where trucks got searched coming onto base.

The main gate had what we called the gate shack, this big glass-walled building where you could see out to the main highway and look all the way down the long straight road to wing headquarters. Multiple stations where you could check IDs, though we were usually down to just one during slower periods. One person checking IDs while the other rested inside, watching through those glass walls.

I was wearing full gear that day. Tactical vest loaded with rifle ammo, pistol ammo, radio with mic, handcuffs, random gear like batteries and flashlight and whistle, latex gloves and shooting gloves, ballistic plates front and back. A beret that made everything feel heavier and hotter. I was technically the more experienced of the two of us, though we were both pretty green. I just happened to be the more certified person, which made me Echo 1 that day.

I took pride in that. Even if it was as mundane as checking IDs for 12 hours straight, I was proud to be trusted with securing a base with 4,000 personnel and their families. I loved what I did. We’d had world-class training, several months of tech school where we learned everything from mounted operations to dismounted operations, law enforcement, nuclear security, and personal security details. You learned a little bit about everything, then came to your base and learned the specific job, the regulations and special instructions for each position.

I was trained well. I felt confident. I was ready for anything.

Except I wasn’t.

It was late summer, early fall, somewhere in September or October. Clear day, nice weather, quiet morning after the rush hour traffic had died down. Just me and my partner taking turns checking IDs, scanning them through the system, checking them against the barment roster of people who couldn’t come on base, waving cars through. It gets very monotonous very quickly when you’re doing it for hours on end, standing on your feet with all that gear. Your back starts to ache, your feet get sore, but you stay alert because that’s the job.

Then this red four-door Chevy pickup pulled up. Looked like a mother and her son, both handing me dependent IDs. I scanned them and they both showed green in the system, but I always took pride in actually looking at the details. When I saw her birth date, it showed that she should be about half the age she appeared. ID was for someone around 21, and she appeared to be around 40.

It wasn’t super obvious, and I didn’t want to be rude, so I went back to my partner. “Hey, how old does that lady look to you?”

“Forty-ish,” he said.

“Take a look at this ID. Says 21.”

I could see her moving around in the truck, looking around, getting nervous. Something wasn’t right. Traffic was starting to back up behind her, so I went back out. “Ma’am, I’m going to hold onto these IDs. I need you to pull up and over to the left.” We had little cutouts where vehicles could wait.

She protested. “No, we just need to go get his medication. That’s all.”

“Ma’am, you need to pull over there.”

“Just give me the IDs back. We’ll just leave.”

“No ma’am. You’re going to pull up to that turnout and wait there. I’ll be with you in just a moment.”

She kept protesting, and I could feel my voice getting firmer. “Ma’am, pull up there and pull over now.”

Once they pulled the vehicle to the side. I radioed for my flight chief, kept looking back at them to make sure they stayed put. Checked another ID, sent a vehicle through, looked back. The red truck was gone.

I looked around the gate area, then at the outbound lanes. There she was, going at a high rate of speed around the serpentine barriers, trying to get off base. I ran across the gate area to the outbound lanes, and as the truck came speeding toward me, I put one foot down on the road, put my hand up in a stop motion, and yelled, “Halt! Security Forces!”

I still don’t know what I was thinking.

Thank goodness this woman, who was scared and trying to run from authorities, actually stopped her truck.

I ran up to the window and started telling her to shut the vehicle off. In her panic, she opened up the center console and started digging around. In my brain, every alarm went off. I thought she might be reaching for a weapon.

Even though I had never done it before outside of training, even though it felt like I was breaking some kind of rule, almost before I could recognize the thought, I drew my pistol. The metal scraped against the hard kydex holster with that hollow plastic sound as the gun rubbed its way out, then went quiet as it cleared the tip and I rocked my wrist to bring it up into firing position. It was a sound I had heard hundreds of times on the range and in training. First time in the real world with no instructors to give feedback, no range safety instructors behind me, no speaker system saying to draw. I got my gun up and pointed it at her, I was well within the rules but it still felt foreign. “Get your hands on the steering wheel now! Do it now!”

The world collapsed to a tunnel. I could only see her and her son sitting next to her and her hands going into that console. Nothing else existed. The sounds of the base around us disappeared completely. No traffic, no voices, no radio chatter from my gear. Just my own heartbeat hammering in my ears and the sound of my own voice giving commands. My hands on the gun were steady, but my heart rate was through the roof. That was tunnel vision, complete and total, like looking through a cardboard tube.

Thank goodness her son was more level-headed than she was. He reached across, turned off the truck, pulled the keys out, and tossed them out the window. I reached my hand inside, opened the driver’s door, secured her hands, unbuckled her, and pulled her out of the truck to get her in handcuffs.

It was only then that someone started helping me, and it was my flight chief who had been behind me the whole time with his taser up. I had absolutely no idea he was there.

“Nice job,” he said simply.

After we had the situation under control and shut down the outbound lane, I tried to show my flight chief the ID to explain why I had drawn my gun, what had happened, because I was scared I was going to be in trouble. But all that adrenaline was working its way through my system, and my hands were shaking so badly he couldn’t even see the ID.

Not just my hands. My whole body felt like it was vibrating.

“Hey,” he said, his voice calm and steady. “Go back in the shack. Tell your partner to check IDs. Take a breath.”

He handled the rest of the paperwork and statements. An awesome leader who knew exactly what I needed. It turned out she was trying to use her daughter’s ID to get on base because she’d just been divorced and didn’t have military privileges anymore. A legitimate security threat, a textbook response on my part. But my body had never experienced that level of stress before.

The tunnel vision had lasted until my flight chief stepped into my field of view to help with the arrest. That’s when it was like someone flipped a switch and I could suddenly see everything again. Hear everything. Realize he’d been there the whole time.

The shaking stopped after just a few minutes. I felt normal again pretty quickly, went right back to checking IDs like it was any other day. But I was proud that I’d paid attention to the details, that I’d noticed what others might have missed. The training had worked when it mattered. I’d handled a real threat with real consequences.

A few months later, in a completely different kind of situation with no real danger at all, I learned the difference between performing under genuine pressure and just trying not to look like an idiot in front of half the base.

It was January, lots of snow, ice forming where the plows had come through. Cold enough for snow but not brutal, and it was a clear day. I was posted up at a busy four-way stop right by the base gym, morning time, lots of cars and pedestrians. Perfect duty for staying warm in your heated patrol car.

I was in a Ford sedan, standard police vehicle with all the controls laid out in a specific pattern. To the left of the steering wheel, you had a control stick for the floodlight. To the right, past the gear shift, you had your car radio, then the handheld mic, then your police radio with volume and channel controls. Right below that was the light and siren switch. Off in the left position, first click to the right gave you lights, second click gave you lights and sirens. Then a keypad that controlled your air horn and various light bar settings.

Simple setup. Not confusing at all. Just easy to flip that switch all the way over if you weren’t careful.

I was sitting in the parking lot off to the side of the intersection, content to be warm and not out in the elements, watching people come to the stop sign. I wasn’t a huge stickler. If someone slowed down enough that I could count the spokes on their wheels, I considered that close enough to a stop.

This car came up to the intersection, and the driver didn’t stop soon enough or slow down soon enough. He wasn’t going fast or being reckless, but his brakes started sliding on the ice. He even honked his horn to let everyone know. He should have been going slower, braking sooner, especially with pedestrians around and other cars nearby. But he wasn’t doing anything dangerous.

My genuine intention was to flash my lights, just a quick “hey, be more careful next time” signal. But it’s really easy to flick that switch all the way over. And I accidentally flicked it all the way over.

In the middle of this busy intersection, with multiple cars and tons of pedestrians around, on a cold winter morning where sound travels and everything was peaceful and quiet, a police siren suddenly started wailing. Very loud. The sound bounced off the buildings around the intersection, echoing across the entire area. You could probably hear it three blocks away. Breaking that crisp, peaceful morning with urgent noise that made everyone stop mid-stride and turn to stare.

Every single person in the intersection froze. Pedestrians stopped walking. Drivers stopped their cars. Even people coming out of the gym turned to see what emergency was happening. And they all looked directly at me, sitting in my patrol car with the siren wailing for absolutely no reason.

My immediate reaction was to try to sink down into my seat as low as I could, hoping somehow I could just disappear. Maybe if I slouched far enough, people would think the patrol car was driving itself. But everybody was looking at me, and the driver had made his turn and pulled over to the side of the road, clearly thinking he was in huge trouble. I felt completely committed. I couldn’t just drive away now. So, I put the car in drive and pulled up behind him, my mind racing with what the hell I was supposed to do next.

Now I had to call it in. “Control, Police Two. I have a code 5.” That’s a traffic stop, and the proper radio protocol is to say who you’re talking to, then who you are, then your message. Normally you give a complete description of the vehicle, license plate, number of occupants, reason for stop. You wait for them to run the plate for wants and warrants before you approach. But I did it all wrong. I said I had a code 5, gave the license plate, and immediately got out to approach the vehicle, my heart pounding because I had no idea what I was supposed to do next.

What was I going to write on a ticket? “Failure to control vehicle due to ice”? Did I even have a pen? I was trying to remember what cops did in movies, which wasn’t exactly comprehensive training for conducting traffic stops.

This guy was shaking like a leaf. He had one hand death-gripping the steering wheel and the other hand holding his driver’s license, military ID, registration, insurance card, and what looked like every other piece of identification he owned, all of it trembling as he tried to hand it to me through the window.

“I’m so sorry, officer! I honked my horn as I went through! I didn’t mean to slide like that! Here’s everything! Is there anything else you need?”

I felt terrible. This poor guy was more nervous than I was, and I was the one conducting my first traffic stop with absolutely no plan.

I calmed him down, explained that I saw him go through the stop sign but that these conditions were tricky, just wanted him to maybe brake a little sooner and slowdown in the ice. I checked his documents against the registration, everything was fine, control had already confirmed no wants or warrants. Technically, he hadn’t come to a complete stop, so I was justified in the traffic stop, but I was 100 percent not going to write a ticket for sliding on ice, and I let him know that. This guy was obviously scared and nervous, but I was probably more nervous than he was. I was conducting my first traffic stop completely unprepared, feeling like a complete rookie.

While I was talking to the driver, another patrol unit rolled up. A sergeant, much more experienced than I was. They figured I might need help since they knew I was new. He walked up to the back of the car while I was still talking to the driver.

“I’ll be right back,” I told the guy, then walked back to the sergeant.

“Alright, what are you going to do next?” he asked.

“I’m not going to write this guy a ticket. He went through the stop sign, but he wasn’t being reckless. He wasn’t doing anything wrong.”

“Up to you if you want to write or not. If you don’t, that’s fine. But here’s what you did wrong.” He walked me through several radio procedures I’d messed up, explained how I should have waited for wants and warrants before approaching the vehicle.

“Alright, just let him go if you’re not going to write a ticket.”

I walked back to the driver. “Have a great day, sir. You’re good, just watch for ice.”

The guy was actually thankful, even though he didn’t know I was never going to pull him over in the first place. He’d been more scared than me, and I’d been terrified.

The sergeant walked me through the right way to do a traffic stop, gave me confidence for future encounters. But I felt like an idiot. The biggest rookie in the world, couldn’t even flip a switch right, conducting my very first traffic stop alone in front of tons of people who had stopped their conversations to watch every move I made. I felt so uncomfortable with all those eyes on me.

Later that day, a few of the sergeants asked if I’d ever seen the form ID 10-T. When I said no, they exchanged looks and got way too happy about it. One of them disappeared and came back with a sheet of paper printed with “ID 10-T” at the top, followed by “I, the undersigned, am an Idiot.” They handed it to me with huge grins, barely containing their laughter.

“You need to fill this out and turn it in,” one of them said with a completely straight face.

It took me about three seconds of staring at it before I realized what ID 10-T spelled out. They absolutely lost it.

Honestly, that night I laughed about it. I told my wife the whole story and we both laughed. I was thankful everybody was safe, there was no real threat, and it was a big learning experience.

What I learned was that there’s a huge gap between training and reality, even with world-class training. Training cannot replace experience and actually doing the job. In training, you know what’s going to happen, or at least you know nothing truly crazy will occur. In reality, even when you’re stopping someone for simply rolling through a stop sign, you never really know what’s going to happen. You never know what kind of reaction you’re going to get or what kind of person you’re dealing with.

The gate incident showed me that my training worked when it mattered. I’d handled a real threat correctly, made the right tactical decisions, kept everyone safe. The tunnel vision and shaking hands weren’t failures. They were normal physiological responses to high stress that would improve with experience.

The traffic stop showed me I still had a lot to learn about the routine stuff. That confidence could make you careless with simple procedures. That looking professional meant more than just having the right training. It meant practicing until everything became automatic, until you could flip the right switches and follow the right procedures even when you were nervous.

Both incidents taught me that competence comes through experience, not just knowledge. That your hands learn steadiness through repetition, through making mistakes and getting better. That the gate between training and reality is crossed one situation at a time, one lesson learned through doing rather than just studying.

Years later, when I found myself with complete spatial awareness during a hostage situation, able to calculate crossfire and trajectory while staying calm under pressure, I would remember these early lessons. The journey from shaking hands trying to show an ID to steady hands ready for precise action. From accidentally triggering sirens to moving with quiet confidence through dangerous situations.

The gate between rookie and experienced officer isn’t marked by time or rank. It’s marked by accumulated moments of learning what works and what doesn’t, of understanding the difference between knowing procedures and living them.

If I could tell that rookie version of myself anything, it would be this: Take a breath. The worst thing you can do is panic. Don’t panic and you’re going to be okay. As long as you radio where you’re at so your guys can find you if something happens, the rest will come with experience. Make sure you’re safe, the people around you are safe, and you’re going home to your family that night. Everything else is just learning.

You’re going to make mistakes. You’re going to look stupid in front of people. You’re going to be the one who ruins a peaceful morning with accidental sirens. But you’re also going to handle real threats when they come. You’re going to protect people when it matters, truly learn what it means to put your life on the line for others you don’t even know. And eventually, your hands will learn to be steady not just when your heart is calm, but when everything inside you is racing.

That’s how competence is built. One mistake at a time, one success at a time, until the distance between training and reality gets smaller and smaller, until you can cross that gate without stumbling.

Chapter 12: What I Missed

Sometimes the most important gates are the ones you almost walk past. Sometimes the hands that need steadying are so small you barely notice them reaching out. I want to tell you about a kid and a burrito, and how missing what’s right in front of you can haunt you long after the sirens stop.

It was winter on base, that particular kind of cold that cuts through your uniform and settles in your bones. The kind of day where your breath forms small clouds and the metal on your gear stings when it touches bare skin. We got a call about someone trying to use a fake debit card at the BX. Routine. Ordinary. The kind of call that makes you think about lunch instead of life and death.

When we got there, we found this kid. Maybe eight years old. Trying to buy a burrito with a card that wasn’t his. He had this big, fluffy winter jacket on, the kind that swallows small frames whole, so we couldn’t really see much of anything about him underneath all that padding.

To be honest, it was just weird. I’d been in law enforcement for a few years by then, and I hadn’t seen something like that before. Kids don’t usually commit credit card fraud. So, it was more of a puzzle than anything else. What’s this kid doing here? How’d he get a fake card? Just strange.

My hands were steady as I took notes, going through the motions. Same routine I’d done hundreds of times. But something felt off in a way I couldn’t name yet. The gate between routine and revelation was standing right there, and I was about to walk through it without knowing.

We eventually tracked down his mother and got her to come pick him up. Standard procedure. We weren’t going to arrest an eight-year-old or get him in trouble. He was just a kid who didn’t know any better. This was something for his parents to handle.

When the mother showed up, she immediately apologized. “Oh my goodness, I’m so sorry this happened.” She was obviously concerned, walking fast across the parking lot, her heels clicking against concrete, her eyebrows furrowed. The fluorescent lights from the BX cast everything in that harsh, artificial glow that makes even normal situations feel slightly unreal.

She carried herself right for someone who was worried. Her hands moved quickly, gathering her purse, checking her phone, the kind of nervous energy you’d expect from a parent dealing with an embarrassing situation.

We all figured out pretty quickly what kind of card it was. One of those fake ones you get in the mail advertising credit offers. She said he probably got it from a stack of mail, which made sense at the time. Kids do weird things. They don’t understand the difference between real cards and junk mail.

My flight chief had me take the lead, giving me experience. I asked the standard questions. How old he was, where she’d been, what she was doing when this happened, how long he’d been gone. The questions rolled off my tongue automatically, my pen moving across the notepad in practiced strokes.

But looking back now as a father, something was off about where her concern was focused. She was worried about the situation. That he’d tried to buy food with a fake card, that he’d caused this whole incident. But she wasn’t asking the questions I would be asking now. Not “Is he okay?” or “How long has he been wandering around?” or “What if something had happened to him?”

She was more focused on getting him out of there than understanding what had happened. It wasn’t over the top, but in hindsight, she was rushing to leave. Her hands kept checking her watch, her keys, anything that might signal it was time to go. At the time, I just saw a concerned mother dealing with an embarrassing situation. Now I see someone who wanted this conversation to end.

She should have been distraught. She should have been in tears that her kid had been gone long enough to make it all the way to the BX, trying to buy food because he was hungry. But she wasn’t.

The gate between suspicion and certainty was right there. I just didn’t see it yet.

Of course, now I know they were trying to hide something. So of course she wouldn’t be distraught. Of course she’d want to leave quickly. Of course her questions focused on damage control instead of her child’s wellbeing.

A couple weeks later, we got another call to that same house. It was a domestic call between the husband and wife. Nothing too serious. Probably the neighbors called because of all the yelling that carried through thin base housing walls. So we went in focused on them, looking for the usual signs. Had anybody been hit? Was anything broken in the house?

Everything seemed in place for a typical military family with kids. It wasn’t perfectly clean, but nothing stood out as dirty or unfit. The kind of lived-in mess you’d expect with children. Toys scattered on the carpet, dishes in the sink, laundry folded but not put away. The house smelled like dinner and fabric softener and the particular stuffiness of a home where people actually live.

The couple didn’t seem like your typical domestic call either. You could see they’d had a fight that had escalated, but nothing physical. Nothing crazy had happened. Just two people who’d let stress and frustration boil over into shouting.

And the other kids looked normal. Normal weight, normal outlook, normal concern about what was happening. They hovered in doorways watching us with the wide-eyed curiosity kids get when police officers are in their house.

That’s when I saw the padlock.

It was up high, screwed into the door and door frame with one of those hasp hinges. A regular white door, just a typical laundry room door off the kitchen. And my first thought was practical: this is base housing, and they’re going to have to fix that when they move out, and I knew how much of a pain that was going to be.

Base housing maintenance was notorious for charging for every little thing. Every nail hole, every scuff mark. A hole drilled through a door frame would cost them their security deposit and then some.

But then we opened the door.

The smell hit you first. Before your eyes could adjust to the dimmer light, before you could process what you were seeing, your nose told you something was very wrong. It wasn’t overpowering, but it was unmistakable. The smell of someone who’d been in too small a space for too long without proper facilities. Unwashed skin and stale air and something else underneath that made your stomach turn.

The room was small, smaller than you’d expect for a laundry room. There was a washer and dryer pushed against one wall, but most of the space was taken up by a makeshift bed. Some blankets thrown on the floor, a pillow that had seen better days. Normal lighting filtered in from a small window, so it wasn’t pitch dark inside, but dim enough that your eyes needed a moment to adjust.

There was food storage in there, but nothing that a kid could just go eat. Canned goods on high shelves. Things that required opening or heating. Nothing within reach of small hands.

I didn’t notice any toys.

And there he was. Without that big puffy jacket, you could see how skinny he really was. His clothes hung on his frame like they belonged to a bigger kid. The normal clothes were actually clean, which somehow made everything worse. Someone was maintaining appearances on the outside while letting him waste away on the inside.

I could see he was anxious. Not relieved that adults were there. Not confused about what was happening. Just constantly anxious, like a wild animal that’s learned to expect pain from every human contact. He wasn’t going to dart or run. He was clammed up, looking down, quiet. When my partner tried to talk to him, he didn’t really respond.

He looked like a little animal that was trapped, if that makes sense. Scared, yes, but not like you’d expect a kid to be scared of strangers. It was like he was looking for a place to hide, not from us, but from the world itself. Trying to retreat inside his own mind because his body had nowhere safe to go.

There was something in his eyes that I’ll never forget. This wasn’t fear of getting in trouble. This wasn’t embarrassment or confusion. This was the look of someone who had learned that the world was fundamentally unsafe, that the people who were supposed to protect him were the ones he needed protection from.

That’s when everything hit at once.

The realization crashed over me in waves, each one worse than the last. My mind raced back to him trying to buy food with a fake card. A hungry kid trying to feed himself because no one else would. It went back to the reaction of the mother, how she’d rushed to leave, how her concern had been focused on getting him out of there instead of understanding why he was there in the first place.

The first wave was pure, white-hot rage. A fiery anger that started in my chest and spread through my arms until my hands wanted to make fists, wanted to grab the people who had done this and make them understand what they’d stolen from a child. The kind of anger that makes your vision go red around the edges, that makes every muscle in your body coil tight like a spring ready to snap. I wanted to find them and make them feel every bit of hunger, every moment of fear, every second of abandonment this kid had endured. My jaw clenched so hard I could taste blood from where I’d bitten my tongue.

Then came the second wave, and it nearly buckled my knees. Crushing sorrow that hit like a physical weight dropping on my shoulders. This little boy, this innocent kid who should have been worried about homework and cartoons, had been reduced to trying to buy food with fake plastic because he was starving. The image of him in that tiny room, alone, afraid, forgotten by the very people who were supposed to love him most. It was the kind of grief that makes you want to collapse right there on the floor, curl up and weep for a world where this could happen.

But the third wave was the worst. That sick, nauseating feeling in the pit of my stomach that I had almost walked away. That I had been so close to missing this completely. If there hadn’t been a domestic call, if they hadn’t been fighting that night, if I hadn’t noticed that padlock, this kid would have died in that room, and I would never have known. The thought made me physically ill. My hands were steady on the outside, but inside I was shaking with the terrible knowledge of how thin the line was between saving him and losing him forever.

All three emotions crashed together in the space of maybe ten seconds, and then I had to bury them. Completely. I could soften my face when I looked at the kid, let him see that I cared. But when I turned back to the parents, I had to become business as usual. Professional. Calm. I needed them to keep talking, to give me information, to not realize how much I knew or how much I despised what they’d done.

So, I stuffed that rage down deep where they couldn’t see it. Buried the sorrow behind questions about timelines and procedures. Swallowed the sick feeling and kept my voice level, my questions routine. It wasn’t until we had the evidence we needed, until CPS was called and he was safe, that I could let even a fraction of what I felt show on my face.

Looking back, I think that’s where pressure builds inside of you. You learn to control those reactions, to compartmentalize that kind of emotional intensity, and you get good at it. Maybe too good. Because when you can shove down righteous anger about a starving child, you can shove down anything. And all that pressure has to go somewhere.

My hands stayed steady, but my heart was pounding. Professional training kicked in even as every father instinct I had was screaming. This wasn’t a domestic disturbance anymore. This was a child in immediate danger, and every second we’d spent talking to his parents about their argument was a second he’d continued to suffer.

We called an ambulance. Called CPS. Got him to the hospital. The doctors told us later that he was just days away from dying. His heart was enlarged from malnutrition. His body was shutting down. It was a hard process getting him back to health, but he was saved. And taken out of that family.

When I heard that he was days away from dying, you’d think it would bring more anger. But it was actually just sadness. Deep, bone-aching sadness. Sadness that we had missed it the first time. That he had experienced it for a week or two longer because I hadn’t seen what was right in front of me. Regret that settled in my chest like a weight I’d carry forever.

We didn’t do anything wrong legally. If we had gone into that house the first time without cause, we would have been breaking laws. Maybe that kid wouldn’t have gotten out at all because of fruit of the poisonous tree. We couldn’t have used evidence against them if we’d obtained it illegally.

But you still have that regret. You still think what didn’t I do, or what did I miss, or what more could I have done. My hands had been steady, my questions had been thorough, my procedures had been correct. But somehow I’d missed the most important thing.

I still think about that kid often. Not when I’m disciplining my own boys or when I see them acting up. It’s more random. Quiet moments when I’m washing dishes or driving to town or watching my sons sleep. But it’s there as a background conviction that shaped how I see the world. That I’ll defend kids. I’ll defend the defenseless. Or at least try to.

From that day forward, I vowed that if I had a call with a kid involved, I would try to get in the house. I would make every effort I could without breaking the law to ensure that the kid was taken care of, to make sure that another one didn’t slip through the cracks in my watch.

Because if that kid had died, it would have wrecked me for the rest of my life. And the regret I carry now would be so much bigger if that kid hadn’t made it.

That experience refined me. It didn’t define me, but it refined me. It taught me that sometimes the most important gates are invisible. Sometimes the hands that need steadying are hidden behind locked doors. Sometimes protection means seeing what others want to keep hidden.

There was another call later that showed me just how much it had changed me. We had this address we frequented often because the mother was off her medication and making threats. So much so that the judge gave full custody to the father and she wasn’t even allowed to be alone with her kid.

After several calls to that same house, I can still recall the exact address and the road it’s on, what the home looks like. We got a call one day that she had barricaded herself in the home with her little girl and was threatening her.

I wasn’t super far from the scene when the call came in, and there was another guy on patrol nearby. We both hit our sirens and pushed our vehicles past their breaking point. Not the usual careful response where you clear intersections and maintain safe distances. This was aggressive. We knew the situation, knew she wasn’t in her right state of mind, knew a child was in immediate danger. We passed cars quicker than we typically would, took corners harder, because every second mattered.

When we got there, we didn’t park a couple houses down like normal protocol. We dumped out right next door, lights still flashing, engines ticking from being driven so hard they were overheating. The other officer ran toward the front door on the right side of the house. I could see a big living room window on the left, and my tactical mind kicked in. I needed eyes inside to see what was happening.

As I ran up the sidewalk, the ground sloped gently uphill toward that window. It was a clear, sunny day, not too hot, not too cold. My M4 was already raised, red dot finding its target through the glass panes. I could hear a bird that had been startled from the tree branches above, its wings beating as it flew off. I saw a neighbor watching through a window across the street, probably wondering what was happening. There was a car stopped at the stop sign across from the house, blinker on to turn the opposite direction. Both neighbors on either side had no cars in their driveways, no kid toys in the yards.

I wasn’t tunnel-visioned whatsoever. Time didn’t seem to speed up. I was comfortable, calm, thinking outside the box about crossfire and backdrop, not only for me but for my partner. Any possible shots from inside the house. Any other responding units that might arrive. I knew bullets could deflect going through windowpanes, especially the lighter rounds we carried, but I was confident in the distance. As I got closer, I was already calculating how my trajectory would change, how the shots would start hitting slightly higher than where my red dot was placed.

That kind of spatial awareness doesn’t come from being cool or naturally gifted. It only comes with training and experience.

My barrel was almost touching the window when I posted up. Round in the chamber, finger on the trigger, slack out. I could see her clearly through the glass, and she had the little girl pressed against her chest, trying to use her as a shield. But I had a clean shot. I knew exactly where I could place it without endangering the child.

All of this happened in split seconds. My partner was at the front door, yelling, trying to break it down. I was ready to punch through a windowpane to make the shot even cleaner if I needed to, or throw myself through the bottom part of the window where it was weakest.

But then she set the child down. Walked to the front door. My red dot stayed on her the entire way, finger still ready. The lock clicked. The door opened. My partner rushed in, secured the situation, got the kid out safe, and returned her to her father.

It was over in seconds. No shots fired. No violence. But I had been ready. Completely ready. And that readiness came from a place the first case had carved out in me. A cold, clear conviction that if someone threatens a child, the calculation becomes simple.

When I had that shot, it was pure cold calculation. There was zero emotion behind it. It was all training. You can’t let emotion get in there too much in a moment like that. But I also had more conviction because of my previous experience. If someone threatens a kid, the calculation becomes simple. That weight isn’t going to be on my mind. I can pull the trigger guilt-free if it means getting that kid out of there safe.

My hands had learned a different kind of steadiness. Not just the mechanical precision my father had taught me, but the moral precision that comes from knowing exactly what you’re willing to do to protect the innocent.

When we got inside, the house was clean. The kid ended up being just fine, in good spirits to be back with her father after we removed her from her mother and got the mother the help she needed.

That’s what that first case did to me. It created a background conviction that runs through everything I do now. I’ll defend kids. I’ll defend the defenseless. And if someone threatens a child, the calculation becomes simple. Cold, maybe, but simple.

Now that I’m a father myself, I see my own boys differently because of what I know can happen to kids. When I discipline them and they run to their room crying, even when what I did was totally appropriate, it breaks my heart a little more than it probably should. That image of a kid alone in a room sits with me.

My wife and I made different choices because of what we’ve seen. We never did the cry-it-out method. We look them in the eyes when we talk to them, tell them we love them, then handle the discipline. Not to spoil them, but to make sure they know they can always come back to us.

The real question becomes: How do I prepare them for the world while protecting them from it? Most of the time, I have no idea what I’m doing. But I know that showing up matters. Being present matters. And sometimes the biggest fights are the quiet ones where you refuse to let someone slip through the cracks.

If you’re reading this and you’re carrying weight from your own service, your own calls, your own moments where you wonder what you missed, I want you to know something. It’s okay to carry that weight. It doesn’t make you weak. It doesn’t make you broken. It makes you human. It makes you someone who cares enough that those things are still on your mind.

You’re in the fight.

I know what it’s like when you share something heavy and people go silent. Expressionless. Maybe they move on from the subject quickly. Sometimes they just walk away, or they say “dang, dude, that’s rough” and then the conversation moves on. That can hurt, and that doesn’t make you crazy.

The vast majority of people, thank God, don’t understand what’s going on inside you. We don’t have war here. We don’t have the heaviest of the heavy weights. And that’s not to say that what other people carry isn’t real. Trauma is trauma, and it means something to each individual. But your experience is different, and that’s okay.

You don’t need to find the perfect person to talk to. Quit waiting for someone you can trust perfectly. Find someone you love who knows how to listen. Really listen. Someone who knows how to listen and not just try to fix everything.

Find someone who can validate that what you carry is heavy, even if they don’t fully understand it. Someone who can say, “I don’t quite get it, but I’m here and I’m listening. You’re not crazy. And what you went through was real.”

That’s enough. That’s more than enough.

But if you’re carrying something heavy and don’t know who to talk to, start somewhere. Write it down, even if it’s messy. Take a walk and talk out loud like someone’s listening. Pray the honest kind of prayers, not the polished ones. Try saying the hard thing out loud, even just to yourself in the mirror.

The weight gets lighter once it has words.

Sometimes the most important gate we can guard is the one between silence and speech. Sometimes the steadiest thing our hands can do is reach out, even when we don’t know if anyone will reach back.

I think about that kid sometimes, wonder if he ever got that burrito he was trying so hard to buy. Wonder if he learned that hunger isn’t just about food, that some hungers can only be fed by someone who sees you, really sees you, and refuses to let you disappear.

Some gates you guard with strength. Others you guard with attention.

That kid taught me the second kind matters more. The willingness to notice. To question. To trust your gut when something doesn't add up, even when everyone else has moved on.

Maybe you're the one who will notice next time. The teacher who asks why the homework stopped. The neighbor who wonders why the kid is always outside alone. The coach who sees flinching where there should be trust.

Sometimes that's all it takes. Someone willing to look a little longer.

Chapter 13: The Long Wait

Waiting can feel like a kind of dying. Especially when your hands have learned steadiness through years of crisis, and the answer still doesn’t come. When you’ve built your life around clear missions and defined objectives, and suddenly you’re suspended between one life and another with no control over which direction the gate will swing.

There was a long season where I didn’t know if the military was going to keep me, medically retire me, or just kick me to the curb with nothing. It dragged on for almost a year, each week another layer of plans that had to be held loosely because they might disappear with a single phone call. All that waiting for someone else to make a decision about my future, and then when the answer finally came, I’d have 15 days to get out.

That kind of timeline teaches your hands to hold everything like it might vanish, teaches your heart to hope and plan and let go all at the same time.

What started this whole medical process was a call to a toddler who had fallen backwards from a highchair. The child was maybe eighteen months old, his mother’s panic sharp and metallic in the air. I could see the potential for spinal injury in the way his small body lay too still.

I braced his head and neck with my hands, which meant kneeling on concrete that bit through my uniform, bent over at an angle that immediately felt wrong. The weight of my gear pulled everything forward, straps cutting into my shoulders like dull knives. He was crying, that glass-breaking wail of hurt and terror, but when I clicked my radio, it distracted him. I clicked it over and over, then let his tiny fingers work the button, his sobs turning to hiccups of fascination.

Ten minutes felt like an hour. I felt a twinge in my lower back, sharp and immediate, but more than that, wrongness that ran down through my legs. Something was bad, even though I didn’t understand what yet. Just the cost of doing the job right, of keeping this little boy still and safe.

It was later in Iraq, while being in the gun turret, that the real damage was done. You stand on a metal platform while getting bounced around and buffeted by stiff suspension, and the constant jarring of tires hitting holes. Hour after hour of that pounding, my back already compromised from that awkward position months before. That shock is what made my back end up herniating, a disc pushed up against nerves and choked some of them off like a garden hose with a kink in it.

It caused fiery pain down my legs that would come and go without warning. Some days they were cold, the sensation distant and wrong. Some days I couldn’t feel them at all, like they belonged to someone else. My run times all of a sudden got much longer after these incidents, my body compensating for damage I didn’t fully understand yet.

After surgery, my surgeon said no more running long distance for an extended time, and that’s what triggered the med board process. The gate between healthy and broken, between capable and limited, had been crossed without my realizing it until it was too late to go back.

Recovery after surgery was brutal. Nerves that had been dead for months started waking up, sending bolts of lightning through my body without warning. The pain was electric, white-hot, stealing my breath and bringing tears that I couldn’t stop. Being stuck at home made me feel more useless than the hospital ever did. At least there, being horizontal was expected. At home, life kept moving around me while I lay flat, counting ceiling cracks and listening to my family handle everything I should have been doing.

When I finally got vertical again, the world looked different. But my legs felt disconnected, alien. The nerves eventually went back to sleep, probably dying off. Now sensation is barely there, like my feet belong to someone else.

The silence from the Air Force was loud. Deafening. Every week that passed without clarity felt like a tightening around the chest, like trying to breathe through fabric that kept getting thicker. I would check my email first thing in the morning, the blue glow of the screen harsh against the pre-dawn darkness of our bedroom, my wife still sleeping beside me while I searched for answers that never came.


The worst part was going through that long timeline with nothing to do. Nothing that could affect the outcome. I could only wait for someone else to decide which direction it would swing. We had to learn how to not worry about what we couldn’t control. It was a huge learning experience, though I wouldn’t have called it that while I was living it.

Making everything more complicated, we had orders to Italy at one point, the emails sitting in my military inbox like a promise we weren’t sure we could keep. We were trying to sell our home, pricing it and showing it to strangers who walked through rooms where we’d built our first years of marriage. We didn’t know if we’d be moving across the world to an ancient country full of history and wine and adventure, or moving into my parents’ RV in a field in Texas.

Italy would have meant more adventure, which we had come to love through our military travels. We wanted to travel Europe, to walk through history and eat food that had been perfected over centuries and show our children that the world was vast and beautiful. We were excited, starting to plan and dream. We even started looking for homes near the base, imagining our life in a place where ancient stones held stories spanning centuries.

But it was shortly after that I had the conversation with my commander about the med board. We never sold the house or packed up for those orders. But it was on the horizon, and I was getting notifications that I had to get certain things done in order to get these orders pushed through. It was like trying to live in two different futures at the same time, never knowing which one would become real.

While I was on leave, the commander called. He said, “Rowdy, in the not-so-distant future, I could see a Chief Master Sergeant Rowdy, and I think you’d be a fine chief.”

His voice carried the weight of years of watching people, of knowing what leadership looked like when tested. That meant everything, it meant I had put the effort in and excelled like I wanted to. I had made staff sergeant within three years, ahead of most of my peers. But I let him know, “Thank you, but my body is done.” It’s not what I wanted. I wanted to do twenty or more. But my body was done, and no amount of willpower could change that reality.

On the form that gets sent out, I had only a few lines to capture everything. My commander had already advocated for me, stating that while I’d served with distinction, my body could no longer meet the demands of military service and the Air Force should medically retire me. He’d gone to bat for me, saying what I couldn’t bring myself to say. So I wrote that it had been my goal to serve twenty-plus years, that I loved what I did, but that with a heavy heart, I had to concur with my commander’s assessment. My body was done. That line stays with me: ‘It’s with a heavy heart, but I concur with my commander.’ He’d given me the dignity of an honorable exit when my body failed me, and all I had to do was agree.

That uncertainty shapes you. It forges you in ways that comfort never could. It makes you ask hard questions about where your peace comes from, what your hands are built to hold, what gates you’re meant to guard. It made me lean into Christ not because I was so spiritually mature, but because I had no other lifeline.

It’s easy to lead when the path is clear, when your hands know exactly what to do and the gates ahead are marked with clear signs. But real leadership, especially in a family, shows up when nothing makes sense. When you don’t have answers, when your kids are looking at you like, “What happens next?” and you have to look back with calm you don’t feel and say, “We’re going to be okay.”

Not long after we moved back to Texas, I was asked to serve with the youth at church. One day, watching young men act up during a lesson their teacher had put heart into, I asked them to stay behind. I knelt down in front of them and looked each one in the eyes.

“Guys, your Sunday school teachers are teaching you important life lessons here. Very important. Take my example. When I was getting taken out of the military, I had no marketable skills. I had young kids looking at me saying, ‘Daddy, what are we gonna do?’ I had a wife looking to me for support. And I had no answers. And I felt like the weight was going to crush me.”

“I went back to those life lessons my Sunday school teachers taught me, and it pulled me out. They let me know that I can go to God, that I can go to prayer and scripture. And you missed those lessons today. I implore you, next time, please listen. Because there will come a point in your life when the world is crushing you, when you feel like you have nowhere else to go, and when you feel like you have nobody standing beside you. It will remind you that Christ is standing there with you. And you can go to God and go to scriptures to pull you out of it. That’s where your strength will come from in those moments. Please listen.”

I don’t know if they even listened. But my hands had learned something in that moment, how waiting shapes you not just to endure, but to help others endure when their turn comes.

That pressure forged me. It didn’t just harden me; it hardened my resolve. It anchored me deeper into the kind of man I wanted to be, someone my family could rely on. And not just when I had answers. I wanted to be steady when I didn’t. Because that’s where trust is forged. In the wait.

We immediately started looking for land in Texas, scrolling through listings on a laptop screen, dreaming of space and cattle and building something that would last. The Saturday before leaving, we put in an offer on what later became the site of raising our family here in Texas. We couldn’t even find it exactly on the map, but just seeing the area around it, the rolling hills and oak trees and endless sky, we called the realtor and said, ‘Offer asking. We want it.’

It was a leap of faith made while everything else was uncertain, but somehow it felt like the first solid decision we’d made in months. We’d learned by then that real strength includes knowing when to reach for the hands reaching back, that your foundation isn’t just what you build but who stands behind you when you’re building it.

Now, we have a property with peaceful surroundings, and when I pull the truck through and pull it off to the side, I get out and stand by the gate. I just sit there and ponder. I realized just a couple weeks ago that it was a metaphor for fatherhood. I hope my boys know I’m waiting here at the red gate for them. And I’m not just sitting idly by, but I’m actively watching for them.

I want them to remember that when life gets hard, when they feel underwater, when things just don’t go their way and they need a place or an ear, that I’m not just sitting around. I’m actively watching for them. I’ll reach out for them.

That’s how the Father waits for us, too. The God who taught me to wait when I couldn’t see the path will be waiting for them too. The hands that learned to hold steady in uncertainty will be ready to embrace them when they come home.

And that’s the gift of the long wait: it teaches you that some things are worth the uncertainty, that some foundations can only be built in the space between knowing and not knowing, that some of the best gifts come wrapped in seasons you wouldn’t choose but wouldn’t trade.

The wait that felt like dying became the foundation for everything worth living.

Chapter 14: What We're Really Building
There’s a gate between dreaming and doing that most people never cross. They talk about building something lasting, about creating a legacy, about putting down roots. But when the concrete truck pulls up and the driver asks where you want it poured, that’s when you find out if your hands are ready to do more than just plan.

The day they poured our foundation, Texas was doing what Texas does best in summer, trying to melt everything that dared to stand still. The diesel engine of the concrete truck rumbled in the driveway, its massive mixer turning with a steady grinding that seemed to echo across the empty pasture. We knew we had maybe minutes before the concrete set too hard to leave our mark.

But as I pressed my palms into that warm surface, a familiar weight settled in my chest. Should I even be here? I was supposed to be on that helicopter the night it went down. Supposed to die with the rest of them. Instead, here I was, standing in Texas heat, building a life I wasn’t meant to have while good men would never get the chance to pour foundations for their families.

The wet concrete flowed like water from the truck’s chute, gray and thick, spreading across the wooden forms with a sound like heavy rain on dry ground. The smell hit us immediately, clean and sharp, like limestone and possibility mixed together. The scent of new beginnings that cuts through the heat and somehow purifies it.

“Quick,” Shay said, “hands in fast so we can get our prints.”

The concrete was warm against our palms, almost hot from the Texas sun, but still soft enough to accept the press of our hands. My hands, which had learned precision through years of training and steadiness through crisis, now learned what it felt like to literally lay a foundation. Not just talk about it, not just plan it, but to put my hands into something that would hold the weight of everything we were about to build.

Shay pressed one hand in next to mine, then we guided our boys to press one hand each into the wet surface. Four sets of prints, side by side, like a promise written in stone before we even knew what we were promising. Watching my sons’ small fingers spread in the concrete that had already begun to firm up, I wondered if those fathers had ever imagined moments like this. If they’d dreamed of building something permanent with their children.

The smell that rose from that fresh foundation wasn’t just cement and aggregate. It was the smell of new beginnings. Clean. Hopeful. Sharp in the heat but somehow pure, like the air after a thunderstorm washes everything clean. We were surrounded by cattle land stretching as far as you could see, green grass and green oaks dotted with stock tanks that caught the light like scattered mirrors. This was the smell of starting over, of putting your hands to work building something that would outlast the reasons you started building it.

But starting over meant leaving behind the only life I’d known. For six years, I’d had guaranteed income, clear purpose, a mission that mattered. Now I was betting everything we’d saved on a dream and a piece of land. It was hard watching our account go down with every check we wrote, even though we’d saved enough to do this right. The numbers were there but watching years of careful saving disappear into lumber and concrete still made my stomach tighten.

I stepped back and looked at those handprints, already darkening as the concrete set. In a few hours, they’d be permanent. Right there on the front porch foundation, extending out from what would become our bedroom doors, they’d be there every time we stepped outside. A daily reminder of the day we put our hands to work building something that would hold so many memories.

That’s when I understood something about the gate between dreaming and building. Once you cross it, once you put your hands in the concrete, there’s no taking it back. You’re committed. Not just to the house, but to everything the house represents. The life you’re going to live there. The family you’re going to raise. The legacy you’re going to leave. And the weight of wondering if you deserve any of it.

“Go stand where our bed will be,” I told Shay one day when the framing was up.

She walked through what would become our bedroom door, our youngest son riding her hip, and positioned herself where we’d calculated the bed would go. Then she turned toward the opening that would become French doors leading to the front porch.

That’s when I saw it. Not just the house we were building, but the life we were building. Shay standing in that doorway, holding our boy, looking out at huge blue Texas skies with clouds rolling through like ships on an endless ocean. All that green stretching to the horizon, cattle dotting the pasture like periods in a sentence that was still being written.

They’ll never see their kids grow up though. 

The house took four months total. Most of it went smooth, except for a month waiting on windows because COVID had everything backed up. When we finally moved in, we discovered that owning cattle land means sharing it with cattle, whether you want to or not. Suddenly, fencing wasn’t just about boundaries, it was about survival.

But a house is just shelter until you make it home. And making it home meant building boundaries that protected without imprisoning, gates that welcomed the right things and kept out the wrong ones.

The fencing came after the house was finished. We needed to fence around the house area, create a space where our boys could play without worrying about stepping in something unpleasant or getting too friendly with a bull.

I’m what you might call a fair-weather fencer. I learned early that there’s no point in stretching wire when the weather’s fighting you. The autumn air carried the scent of dying grass and wood smoke from somewhere distant, crisp enough to make work feel like pleasure instead of obligation.

My youngest was maybe two or three then, still small enough to ride in the carrier on my back but old enough to jabber constantly about everything we were doing. He’d babble and point and ask questions I couldn’t answer while I stretched wire and set posts, his weight familiar and comforting across my shoulders.

“Dad, what doing?” he’d ask for the fifteenth time that morning.

“Building a fence, buddy.”

“Why?”

“To keep the cows out and keep us safe.”

“Oh. Dad, what doing?”

And we’d start the whole conversation again. By lunch, I’d explained fencing roughly forty-seven times. He never seemed satisfied with my answers, but he never stopped asking either.

Sometimes he’d fall asleep back there, his little head bonking against my shoulder blade with each step, drooling on my shirt. Best work partner I ever had. My hands were learning to work while teaching, to build while explaining, to create boundaries while showing someone else how it’s done. Every fence post I set, every gate I hung, he was there watching. Learning that building something worthwhile takes time and patience and the willingness to start again when the weather’s right.

Those days on my back, watching me work, that’s where I hope he learned that building things is hard but worth it. That there’s a right way and a wrong way, and the right way usually takes longer. That your hands can be gentle with people and firm with materials, that the same strength that comforts a crying child can drive a fence post deep enough to hold against whatever tries to test it.

Too many friends won’t be building anything.

But even as I answered his questions and felt the satisfaction of teaching through doing, I wondered about those who trusted their leadership to make good decisions. The guys who went downrange believing the mission mattered, who never got the chance to carry their own sons on their backs while building something for the future. Was I worthy of my family’s trust the way they’d trusted theirs?

Every fence I built on this property, I built with one of my boys on my back or walking beside me. They didn’t do much of the actual work. They were too young, and fencing is hard enough without small helpers. But they liked being with me. They liked watching their dad work hard. They liked being part of something bigger than themselves.

And in watching them watch me, I realized something important: I’m not building this place for them to inherit. I’m building it to show them how to build. The land, the house, the fences, the gates, those are just the curriculum. The real education is in the doing, in the choosing action over comfort, in the willingness to put your hands to work creating something that matters.

If they want to keep this place when Shay and I are gone, that’s fine. But I hope they’ll feel free to sell it and use the money to build something of their own. Something that fits their families, their dreams, their vision of what home should look like. I hope they’ll take the lessons and leave the lumber, carrying forward the principles but adapting them to their own purposes.

Because I learned something building this life while carrying the weight of friends who never got the chance. The goal isn’t to create things worth keeping. The goal is to create foundations worth releasing.

That’s what real legacy looks like. Not the things you hold onto, but the foundations you pour for others to build upon. Not the gates you lock, but the ones you open. Not the hands that grasp, but the ones that shape and guide and ultimately let go. Concrete flows like water until it finds its form, then holds forever, just like the lessons we pass down.

Those handprints we pressed into the concrete that first day, they’re still there. Faded a little, weathered by Texas seasons, but permanent. Every time we step onto the front porch, we see them. A reminder that we built this place together. But more than that, a reminder that building this place doesn’t mean it’ll stand forever.

Now my boys race around that same foundation, their bare feet finding purchase on the warm concrete that holds our handprints. They chase each other across the surface where we first marked our claim, leaving new footprints in the dust that settles there. Different marks, temporary ones, but evidence of the life we built on top of what we poured together.

I want to be useful. I want my boys to look at what we built here and think not “I have to preserve this” but “I learned how to do this.”

Because the point isn’t the place or the memories. The point is the ability to create new places, make new memories, build new foundations for the next generation.

The point is teaching your hands to build something worth releasing, especially when you’re living a life that feels borrowed. Especially when you know that every foundation you pour, every gate you hang, every moment of peace you create carries the weight of those who never got the chance.

The point is making that borrowed time count.

Because there’s a gate between dreaming and doing, and we crossed it the day we pressed our hands into warm concrete. Now, years later, watching my boys race across that same foundation, I understand what we were really building all along. Not just a house. Not just a legacy.

We were building proof that borrowed time, when used with steady hands and grateful hearts, becomes the most sacred time of all. That those who didn’t make it home are somehow present in what we choose to build with the time they didn’t get.

Chapter 15: From Yellow to Red
Some gates teach you about welcome before you understand what welcome costs.
There's a yellow gate I still drive by sometimes, though it's been years since I last swung it open with eager hands. It stands at the entrance to a gravel drive that curves between old oak trees, leading to a house where I first learned that some places exist not just to shelter the people who live there, but to offer refuge to anyone who needs it.
The gate itself was nothing special, just painted metal weathered to soft yellow, hung between two metal posts that Nat had set himself. But when someone tells you to "turn at the yellow gate," and everyone knows exactly what they mean, you begin to understand that some landmarks matter more than their size suggests.
I always wanted to be the one to swing it open when we arrived, to feel the weight of metal moving on old hinges that squeaked with the particular protest of gates opened and closed thousands of times by hands that care enough to latch them properly. That squeak announced something important: you were crossing from the ordinary road into territory where different rules applied. Where abundance was assumed and welcome was given.
What lay beyond that gate was a world that moved at a different pace. The drive wound upward through scattered stones that crunched under our tires as we approached the house. Kathryn would be watching from the kitchen window, her face lighting up with genuine pleasure. Not the polite smile adults give children, but real joy, like your presence was a gift rather than an interruption.
The house was modest, but the life that spilled out from it wasn't. Their back porch overlooked gardens that stretched beyond what seemed reasonable for two people, but that was the point. Nat and Kathryn had learned something about abundance: when you have more than you need, the excess isn't waste, it's opportunity. Their garden produced enough tomatoes to feed many families, corn that stood tall in neat rows, beans climbing their stakes like green prayers reaching toward heaven. But the overflow was always intended for others. Neighbors knew they could stop by during harvest season and leave with armfuls of whatever was ready.
Nat would emerge from wherever he'd been working, dirt under his fingernails but his hands somehow clean when he greeted you. Those hands were something to study, weathered from decades of work, marked by small scars, but they transformed the moment they reached toward you. The same strength that could wrestle with stubborn machinery became impossibly gentle when he ruffled my hair.
This was my first lesson in a paradox that would shape my understanding of manhood: the strongest hands are often the gentlest ones.
They made you feel like family without making you feel like you owed them anything. There was always something good cooking, always a sense that you belonged there. Kathryn's hands moved through their kitchen with practiced grace, creating meals that stretched to feed whoever happened to be there, as if abundance was simply a matter of intention.
The yellow gate became a marker in my mental map of the world, representing stability and kindness and the possibility that adults could be trusted completely. When childhood worries weighed heavy, as they do for any kid growing up, I would think about that gate and know that somewhere nearby was another place where I belonged, where the door was always open.
That place affected me so deeply that I wrote a poem about it when I was young. I was not very good with rhyming and rhythm back then, but I remember working on it until I got it just right:
There's a yellow gate I used to know
 Where peace and kindness always grow,
 A place that made me feel so right
 Like everything would be alright.
The gate swings open, warm and wide,
 With something special safe inside,
 Where people make you feel at home
 No matter how far you may roam.
It taught me what good hearts can do
 When love shines honest, deep, and true,
 How simple kindness, freely shared,
 Can show a person that they're cared.
Now I drive by that yellow gate
 And think about the love so great,
 I hope someday I'll be the one
 To give what can't be undone.
Behind my gate, when I am grown,
 I'll make a place like I have known,
Where others find the peace I found
 When yellow gates were all around.
Even as a young person, I was trying to capture something I couldn't quite name but knew was precious. Looking back now, I can see that what I was sensing was the presence of people who had learned something about sanctuary.
I remember one afternoon sitting on their back porch with Nat when I was young. The place was tucked away from the main road, hidden behind oak trees and rolling pasture that made it feel like the rest of the world had dissolved into evening mist. You could hear the gentle lap of water from their pond near the house, mixing with the hum of bees working through their garden and the whisper of wind through leaves that sounded like secrets being shared.
That garden stretched beyond the porch steps like a daily promise, tomatoes heavy on the vine, corn standing tall and green, beans climbing neat lines. The abundance wasn't accidental. It was cultivated with the same intentionality that marked everything about how they lived.
Nat had this old alligator foot mounted on a stick that he'd keep by his chair, some curiosity from years ago. I was fidgeting with it, running my fingers over the rough, scaly texture, breathing in the smell of rich earth and growing things, when I worked up the courage to ask him something.
The air was still except for that gentle water sound and the rustle of leaves. In my young mind, this place existed outside of time, outside the rushing world beyond those trees.
"Nat, how do you become a man people can count on?"
He looked at me with weathered eyes, not surprised by the question, like he'd been waiting for someone to ask it. His hands stilled on the arms of his chair, and he was quiet for a moment, rocking slowly, watching the oak trees sway.
"Well," he said finally, his voice carrying gentle authority that made complicated things seem simple, "you just show up. Every day. When you say you're gonna do something, you do it. When someone needs help, you help if you can. And you treat people like they matter, because they do."
He paused, his weathered hands gesturing toward the garden, the gate, the whole sanctuary they'd created. "And you keep your gate open for folks who need a place to belong."
I didn't fully understand then how profound that answer was, how it would shape my thinking about manhood and responsibility. But I tucked it away, the way children do with wisdom too big for them to hold all at once.
Then Nat died.
Death changes everything, but not in the ways you expect when you're young. The details are fuzzy now, filtered through childhood memory, but the impact was sharp and clear. Nat was gone, and with him went something essential about that place beyond the yellow gate.
What I began to understand, though I wouldn't have had words for it then, was that death doesn't just take people. It promotes the living. Suddenly, Kathryn was the sole keeper of a tradition that had been built by four hands but now had to be maintained by two. The garden that had been their shared abundance became her responsibility alone.
We still visited Kathryn for a while after that, but something had shifted. The yellow gate still squeaked, the house still felt warm, Kathryn still made everyone feel valued, she still had a warm smile and hello for everyone. But some balance had been lost, some partnership that couldn't be restored.
Eventually, slowly over the years, the visits became less frequent until they stopped altogether. I don't remember the specifics, just that gradually we stopped turning at the yellow gate. It became another turn on the road, though I still felt that distant warmth of welcome when we passed. Not out of forgetting, but perhaps out of understanding, wanting to give Kathryn space in her time of loss, room to lean on her own children and family.
For years, that's all it was. A yellow gate that taught me something about hospitality when I was too young to understand what hospitality required.
But recently, driving past that yellow gate, I've started thinking about my own gate. Our red gate, chosen specifically to stand out, to make it easy for people to find us. "Turn at the red gate," I tell people now, the way they once said, "Turn at the yellow gate."
Our red gate has faded some over the years. The bright red paint has weathered to something more muted, mellowed by sun and rain and time. It's still clearly red, still distinctive, still functional, but it shows the wear that comes from being opened and closed thousands of times by hands that care enough to latch it properly. My hands now, where once they had been small and eager to swing Nat's gate open.
Standing at that gate recently, I found myself thinking about inheritance. Not the kind that comes with legal documents, but the deeper kind that passes from generation to generation through example and the simple act of paying attention to how life should be lived.
Nat had been on one side of his yellow gate, the side that offers refuge, that makes hospitality look effortless. Kathryn had shared that responsibility, and together they had created and maintained their home as sanctuary for anyone who needed it.
But standing at my own gate, I realized something profound: I'm no longer the kid walking through someone else's yellow gate, grateful for welcome but not responsible for providing it. I'm the man standing guard at the red gate, responsible for creating the kind of place that Nat had created.
Death had promoted me too, though not through loss but through time. The people who had once offered me refuge were gone or aging, and suddenly I understood that I'd inherited the responsibility, even if I hadn't yet earned the wisdom. I was learning to become the one others might look to for stability and kindness, trying to grow into the kind of open door that I had once received.
My boys are now the ones being welcomed, learning what hospitality means by watching how their parents treat people who come through our gate. They're learning about kindness and generosity, the way I learned those lessons sitting on Nat's porch. I'm trying to pass on what I received, though I know I'm still learning how to offer the same depth of sanctuary that Nat provided so effortlessly.
When I see our boys small hands swing the red gate open, I see the same eagerness I once brought to Nat's yellow one, not yet understanding what welcome costs, not yet knowing that someday these same hands will bear the responsibility of creating sanctuary for others.
This is what death teaches, if we're paying attention: it changes which side of the gate you're on. The people who once offered you refuge are gone or going, and suddenly you understand that becoming an adult isn't just about independence. It's about learning dependability. It's about growing into someone others can count on, someone whose gate stays open, even when you're still figuring out how to do that well.
It's both honor and weight, this inheritance. Honor because it means you were deemed worthy of carrying forward something valuable, even if you're still learning how. Weight because you understand, perhaps for the first time, how much intention and effort went into creating those feelings of safety and welcome that seemed so effortless when you were receiving them. Now you're the one trying to create that effortlessness for others, discovering it's anything but easy.
My hands, which once swung open Nat's yellow gate as an eager child, now rest on our red gate as a man trying to make our home the kind of place that yellow gate represented. The same hands, but carrying different purpose now, weathered by their own decades of work, still learning how to transform from strength to gentleness in the space of a heartbeat the way Nat's did.
This is how the important things survive. Not through grand gestures, but through the simple act of passing on what we've received, of becoming for others what others once were for us. The yellow gate may be forgotten by most, but what it stood for lives on in red gates and gates of every color, tended by hands that learned their purpose from other hands, creating new sanctuaries for new generations who need to know that some places in this world are safe, that some people can be trusted, that some gates open onto something better than what we left behind.
The yellow gate still squeaks on its hinges when the wind catches it just right. But now I understand it's not saying goodbye. It's saying thank you. And promising that somewhere down the road, a red gate stands ready to welcome the next child who needs to learn what sanctuary looks like, what abundance means, and how strong hands become gentle when someone needs a place to belong.

Chapter 16: When Quiet Becomes Complicity
I still have the quiet kid in me. I didn't kill or defeat that part of myself. I'm naturally more comfortable staying home with my family than seeking the spotlight. I'd rather be the peacemaker than the one stirring things up. It's not that I want to avoid responsibility. I just don't always believe my voice will make a difference.
But I've learned something about the weight of silence. Sometimes the world needs people willing to step into the uncomfortable space between what's easy and what's right, even when they're not natural leaders, even when they'd rather stay out of it.
Sometimes quiet becomes complicity.
Winter 2021, Washington state. Snow had transformed our yard into something from a Christmas card, pristine and peaceful. Shay and I stood at our screen door, phones in hand, watching our boys run wild in the white. They were throwing snowballs that exploded into powder, making snow angels with the kind of abandon that only comes when you believe the world is fundamentally good.
The cold pressed against the glass, our breath fogging the lower panes. Outside was all laughter and light. Inside, the air felt heavy with what Shay had just heard from someone she trusted back home in Texas.
"The mayor's about to resign," she said, scrolling through her phone for updates. "A mother caught him with her fifteen-year-old daughter. Told him to resign or she'd call the cops."
I felt my stomach drop, that particular sickness that comes when you realize evil has been operating in plain sight. My boys' laughter drifted through the door, their joy a sharp contrast to the darkness we were holding in our phones.
The resignation video appeared within minutes. We watched it together, still standing at that door, our boys' voices mixing with the mayor's practiced political language about mental health, about wanting to spend more time with family. All the usual phrases that say nothing while sounding like something.
"This is wrong," I said, the words quiet but hard.
Shay nodded. We both knew what this was. We both knew what he was trying to hide behind those empty words.
I looked at my boys again, making another snow angel, arms and legs spreading wide in trust that the ground would catch them gently. Then I thought about that fifteen-year-old girl. About every other girl in our hometown who might not be safe.
The screen door suddenly felt like a gate. On one side, my family's peace. On the other, a fight that would disturb that peace but might protect someone else's child.
"We can't just walk away from this," Shay said, not a question but a recognition of what we both already knew.
The tension wasn't between us. We were completely united, both understanding that crossing this line would be hard, might hurt our own family, but that some gates you have to cross even when you'd rather stay safe inside.
My phone felt heavier than it should have as I started typing the comment on his resignation post. We'd moved to the couch, but I kept getting up, pacing to the window to check on the boys, then back to type more. Each letter felt like a commitment, each word a step further outside the wire.
But first, I needed to call my parents. They needed to know what I was about to do, what it might cost them.
"Look," I told them, "if I do this, if I call him out publicly, we could all get blacklisted from the community. People could turn against you. And what about my little sister? She's still in school there, still building her life in that town."
There was barely a pause before they both responded, their voices overlapping on speaker phone.
"We don't care," they said, almost in unison. "Do what's right."
Not dramatic, not heroic-sounding. Just matter-of-fact. Do what's right.
That's when I understood something about the foundation they'd built in me. When you raise your kids to care more about character than comfort, they might actually choose character when it costs something. And when your parents are willing to risk their own comfort and their daughter's social standing for what's right, it removes any excuse you might have for staying silent.
With their blessing echoing in my mind, I went back to my phone and finished typing: "Are you sure this is why, or is it because you got caught with a child? You're a public official. You can't just block me and make this disappear. You need to answer."
My thumb hovered over "Post" for just a second. Once this went public, there was no taking it back. If I was wrong, if this was just a malicious rumor, I'd just painted a target on my whole family.
I hit post.
Within minutes, his response came through private message. All religious language and denial. "Why are you doing this? This isn't true. God bless you." Using God to cover lies made my skin crawl, but it also confirmed what I suspected. 
Then the other messages started.
Within an hour of that first comment, my phone started buzzing. Facebook Messenger mostly, but texts and emails too. Each notification felt like another crack in a dam that had been holding back years of silence.
"He did this to me too." "This happened when I was fourteen." "I never told anyone, but..."
Shay and I sat on our couch, phones in hand, reading these messages aloud to each other in disbelief. How had this gone on so long? How had so many known and said nothing?
"They were too scared," Shay said, and she was right. This was a small town where everyone knew everyone, where speaking up meant risking everything, where the mayor had power over jobs and reputations and futures.
Some victims felt more comfortable reaching out to Shay, especially since she'd grown up in that town, had friends there, was known and trusted. I was the quiet kid from outside town, but together we became something neither of us could be alone. She talked to people, drew out their stories with the gentleness of someone who understood the cost of speaking up. I became the public face, the one who'd take the heat, trying to shield my family from the backlash.
We made more posts encouraging people to come forward. Posted the Texas Rangers' information so victims could go directly to them. But they kept messaging us too, as if they needed that human connection before they could trust the system.
The mayor tried to make us feel crazy, sending message after message about how we were destroying his family. "Think of your own family," he wrote. "How could you do this?"
I wanted to scream. How could HE do this to those girls? How could he ask us to be complicit in protecting him?
The messages kept coming from him and his supporters, people who either didn't believe the accusations or didn't care. Each one trying to make us doubt ourselves, trying to make us feel like we were the ones doing harm.
But then another victim would reach out. Another piece of evidence would surface. Another girl would find the courage to speak up because she saw others doing it. That's what kept us going through the exhaustion and the pushback, knowing that our speaking up helped others find their voice.
Years later, people still reached out asking me to fight new battles related to this case. By then I was exhausted from it all. That wasn't my fight anymore. I'd stood up when no one else would, but at some point, others needed to find their own courage.
Being in Washington while this unfolded in Texas added another layer of complexity. We couldn't meet victims in person, couldn't sit across from investigators, couldn't feel the pulse of the town's reaction. Everything happened through screens and calls, digital gates we crossed again and again.
I was still active duty, working long shifts through the pandemic. The military was preparing my unit for possible deployment to the Seattle riots. Each time I left home for work, I carried the weight of this fight plus the reality that any shift could be my last, between COVID and law enforcement dangers.
But Shay and I stayed united through it all. We'd brainstorm what to post, when to push harder, when to let the Rangers do their work. She'd gather information while I was on duty. I'd make the public posts to draw fire away from her. It was completely a team effort that wouldn't have been possible without her. 
The investigation took months. The mayor kept postponing, kept delaying, hoping it would all go away. But the Texas Rangers are thorough, and the evidence was overwhelming.
He was charged with Indecency with a Child by Sexual Contact, a second-degree felony. The case was ultimately resolved as "unadjudicated," he avoided jail through legal complexities but became a registered sex offender. His political career was over. His ability to hurt children severely limited.
More importantly, the victims got to speak their truth. They got to see that someone believed them, that their pain mattered, that what happened to them wasn't acceptable.
I think about that day at the screen door often. My boys playing in snow, building their angels and forts in a world they believed was safe. Shay and I standing at our own gate, having to choose between preserving their peace and protecting other people's children.
That's the inheritance I want to leave them. Not just the ability to be quiet and steady and strong. But the wisdom to know when those qualities serve others, and when staying quiet becomes complicity.
Because there are moments when the hands that have learned to be gentle have to type words that will destroy someone's carefully constructed lies. When the voice that prefers silence has to speak for those who can't speak for themselves. When watching your own children play safely means remembering other children who aren't safe.
Sometimes the most important gates we guard aren't our own. Sometimes the quiet kid has to find his voice not because he wants to, but because he's the only one standing close enough to the gate to guard it.
The snow has melted many times since that winter day. My boys don't remember that particular afternoon, don't know that while they were making angels, their parents were deciding to fight demons. But someday, when they're old enough to understand, I'll tell them about the gate at the screen door. About the moment when keeping quiet would have meant letting evil continue.
I'll tell them that courage isn't the absence of fear or the desire for conflict. It's standing at your own screen door, watching everything you want to protect, and still choosing to step outside into the cold because someone else's child needs protecting too.
That's what I learned that winter: sometimes the greatest strength is knowing when to stop being strong in silence. Sometimes the most important work your hands will ever do is typing the words no one else will type, making the calls no one else will make, carrying the weight no one else will carry.
Not because you're a hero. But because quiet becomes complicity when you're the one who could speak up.

Chapter 17: Waiting at the Red Gate
The red gate stands between our place and the world, same as always. But today I'm not waiting for anyone to come home. They're all here. I can hear my boys in the distance, their voices carrying across the field as they work on some project that probably involves more mud than necessary.
I walk to the gate anyway, drawn by habit or maybe by something deeper. The afternoon light slants across the pasture the same way it did that day we pressed our hands into wet concrete, the same way it does every evening when I stand here.
That's when I see them, small handprints in the dust on the red paint. Three sets, different sizes, overlapping where my boys must have pushed it closed this morning when they went to check the horses. The smallest prints are down low where my youngest can barely reach. The older boys' prints are higher, already starting to spread across the rail where mine usually rest.
I place my hand over the smallest print. It disappears completely beneath my palm.
For a moment, I can't breathe.
Someday their hands will cover mine. Someday they'll be the ones standing here, watching for their own children to come home. The gate I'm guarding will become the gate they guard. The watch I keep will become the watch they learn to keep.
These same hands that once held steady on a rifle, ready to end one life to save another, now rest on this gate with a different purpose. Same protective instinct, different tools. The readiness is the same, to guard what matters most. But now I guard with presence, not violence. With patience, not force. With faithful waiting, not desperate action.
I think about all the gates in this book. The wire in Iraq that marked the boundary between safe and hostile. The door my partner walked through that he couldn't walk back from. The gate between dreaming and building that we crossed when we poured our foundation. The gate between quiet and complicity I crossed when I spoke up about the mayor.
Every gate taught me something about what hands are for. About when to hold tight and when to let go. About when to be strong and when to be gentle. About when to stay quiet and when your silence becomes part of the problem.
But this gate, this red gate, taught me the most important lesson: Sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is simply be there. Waiting. Watching. Ready.
My boys are walking toward me now across the pasture, probably heading in for dinner. Our middle son runs ahead, his hand reaching for the gate latch he can barely work. I watch his small fingers fumble with the chain, the same way mine once fumbled with my father's tools, learning by doing, growing stronger with each attempt.
"Dad, why do you always stand here?" he asks.
I could tell him about faithfulness. About the symbolism of gates and waiting and watching. About how love sometimes looks like a man standing still while the world spins around him.
But instead I just say, "So you'll always know where to find me."
He nods like this makes perfect sense, then runs toward the house, leaving his handprint in the dust again. His brothers follow, each touching the gate as they pass, adding their marks to the story.
I won't always be here to steady their hands. But I'll be in what they choose to build. In every gentle correction, every patient moment, every time they choose to stand at their own gates and wait for someone they love. The foundation I'm laying isn't in concrete or wood or metal. It's in the moments. The watching. The waiting. The choosing to be present when being absent would be easier.
Tomorrow there will be new handprints in the dust. New days of waiting. New moments of choosing patience over action, presence over absence, building over destroying. The gate will still be red, still visible from the road, still marking the place where our land begins and the world ends.
And I'll still be here. Not because I have to. Because this is what love looks like when it takes physical form. It looks like someone at a gate, watching the road, ready for whatever comes while hoping what comes is peace.
The sun is setting now, painting everything gold. I push the gate closed, the chain settling with its familiar rattle. Tomorrow I'll stand here again. And the day after. And the day after that. Because that's what you do at the gates that matter most.
You wait. You watch. You stay ready.
And you teach the next generation to do the same.

Chapter 18: When the Foundation Cracks
Through my truck's windshield, I could see my boys gathered in the backyard, notebooks spread across their laps, doing homeschool in the morning air. The youngest was probably half-listening, more interested in something he'd found in the grass. The older two were focused, heads bent over their work.
I sat twenty yards away in the driver's seat, phone in hand, staring at a screen that said 'Please wait, your host is joining the session.'
My first therapy appointment.
I wanted to throw up. Wanted to close the app and pretend I'd never made the appointment, never admitted I needed help, never crossed the gate into territory I'd spent my entire adult life believing real men never entered. Every instinct in my body screamed at me to run. Put the truck in reverse, drive somewhere, anywhere, wake up from this bad dream where everything I thought was solid had turned out to be something else entirely.
I won't tell you what caused the crisis. The specific details don't matter as much as what they revealed: truths I thought were permanent turned out to be something less. The reality I'd been living in wasn't the reality that existed. One moment I was standing on solid ground. The next, I was grasping for something that wasn't there anymore, like reaching for a handrail in the dark and finding only empty air.
Then my phone buzzed. A text from a friend.
My heart stopped when I read it. There was a veteran, someone I'd never met, who was struggling. He'd told my friend that the VA wasn't worth the hassle, that therapy was pointless, that he didn't see the point in trying.
I wasn't struggling with suicide. That's never been my battle. But reading that message, I understood something about that veteran completely. That feeling of being stuck. Of knowing you need help but being unable to reach for it. Of the weight pressing down so hard that even lifting your hand to make a phone call feels impossible. I knew that feeling. I was living it.
For one instant, I thought: This is my out. I can close this app, drive to wherever this guy is, be the one who rushes in to help. I can give up my time and drag him to get the help he needs.
In the next instant, the truth hit me: How am I going to drag him to help when I'm running from my own?
Something shifted. Instead of feeling like a failure for sitting in this truck waiting for therapy, I felt clarity. If I was going to help anyone else through their darkest times, I had to be honest about walking through my own. I couldn't stand at gates and tell others to cross if I wasn't willing to cross them first.
I typed back: 'Tell him to stop and wait. I'll text soon. Therapy is the hardest thing to admit needing, and I'm literally going into my first session right now. Send him my number.'
Then the therapist's face appeared on my screen, and I made myself stay.
That first session didn't fix anything. The therapist spent most of the time just talking, explaining what would happen in our sessions, how the process worked, what I could expect. He was settling me, building trust, showing me this space was safe. He was very good at it. I barely said anything. Just listened, nodded, tried to breathe normally while my heart hammered against my ribs.
But the therapist made me feel safe enough to come back and do the work.
After it ended, I sent a long message through my friend for the veteran I'd never met. Told him I didn't know if therapy would fix everything, but I felt safe there. Included my number.
I never heard back from him. I still check my phone sometimes, hoping.
Days earlier, I'd been ready to scrap this entire book.
The irony had felt too sharp. I'd spent years writing about quiet strength, about building foundations that hold when storms come, about being the rock others could depend on. And then my own foundation cracked. How could I publish words about standing firm when I could barely stand at all?
Late one night, I sat on the front porch with the manuscript in my lap. My family slept inside. The Texas darkness stretched out past the red gate, quiet except for distant cattle and the occasional rustle of wind through oak leaves.
I started reading.
At first, every page felt like accusation. Look at you. You wrote about foundations while yours was cracking. About steady hands while yours were grasping for a reality that wasn't there. Who do you think you are?
But somewhere in the middle of the night, the accusation transformed into something else.
I found myself lingering on certain passages. The lessons at the end of chapters. The truths I'd written without realizing I'd need them myself.
Some foundations are built with concrete and steel. Others are built with hands that know when to be strong and when to be gentle. The strongest foundations are built with both, steady enough to hold when the storms come, flexible enough to bend without breaking when the pressure mounts.
I'd written those words about my parents, about what they'd built into me before I knew I'd need it. Now I was discovering that the foundation didn't have to be perfect. It just had to hold. And somehow, despite the cracks, it was holding.
I kept reading.
A still mind is one that knows where to turn when the ground shifts beneath your feet. It's been trained to choose peace ahead of panic, to anchor itself in a deeper truth so it's not thrown by every wave that crashes over the bow.
I'd written about stillness in the storm, about deep water that stays calm even when hurricanes rage on the surface. Sitting on that porch, I realized I wasn't calm. The surface was chaos. But somewhere deeper, beneath the anxiety and the hurt and the uncertainty, there was still something anchored. Something that hadn't broken loose.
The pages turned.
The strongest fights are the ones that look like nothing's happening. The most important victories are the ones no one sees you win.
The still fight. I'd written about quiet battles, about showing up when no one was watching, about choosing patience over explosion when every nerve was frayed. That's what this was. A fight no one could see. Getting up each morning when getting up felt impossible. Sitting in a truck waiting for therapy when every instinct screamed to run. Choosing to stay instead of choosing to hide.
I turned more pages. Found another passage I'd almost forgotten writing.
Every time I feel like I'm about to accomplish something, life seems to knock me back. But every time I get knocked back, I learn something I couldn't have learned any other way. And somehow, I end up somewhere better than where I was headed.
There's a rhythm I've recognized in my life. A pattern. I dropped out of college feeling directionless, and that's when I found my purpose. That's when I met my wife. I was medically retired when I thought I'd serve twenty years, and that's when we found our land in Texas. That's when we built our home.
I was about to publish this book, finally ready to share what I'd learned, and that's when my foundation cracked.
I cannot see where this one is leading. I am still in the middle of it. But I've seen the pattern work too many times to stop trusting it now.
Then I found the passage that stopped me completely. From the chapter about the days my boys arrived:
What changed in me? I was naive, young and dumb, and had no idea what I was doing. But I learned that given effort, even I could learn how to do this father thing. Because I was going to be there.
My hands tightened on the pages.
I was going to be there.
Sitting on that porch, broken and questioning everything, those words hit like a physical blow. I'd written them years ago about becoming a father. About showing up even when I had no idea what I was doing.
And now, cracked and needing help I'd spent my whole adult life believing real men never ask for, I realized: being there doesn't mean being perfect. Being there means showing up even when you're broken. Maybe especially when you're broken.
I could be there for my boys while admitting I needed help. I could teach them about foundations while mine was cracking. Because the lesson wasn't "build foundations that never break." The lesson was "when they crack, you rebuild. And you don't hide the cracks. You show them how to work through them."
One more passage caught my eye. The deer.
A young doe stepped cautiously from the cool shadows of the oak trees into the sun-drenched meadow, her ears twitching at every sound as the tall grass swayed gently in the warm afternoon breeze, always ready to bolt back to the safety of the woods.
I'd written that sentence in Mrs. Edwards' class decades ago. A simple image that had stayed with me all these years. And sitting on that porch, I finally understood why.
The meadow is dangerous. Exposed. The place where predators can see you clearly. Every instinct tells the deer to stay in the shadows where it's safe.
But the meadow is also where the food is. The sustenance the deer needs to survive doesn't grow in the shadows. It grows in the open, in the light, in the very place that feels most dangerous.
That's what this season was. Stepping out of the shadows where I'd been hiding. Into the exposed meadow of admitting I needed help. Everything in me wanted to retreat. But the sustenance I needed wasn't in the shadows.
It was in the light.
I set the manuscript down and looked out past the red gate into the darkness.
I'd been so afraid that needing help meant the book was a lie. That I was a fraud for writing about strength while my foundation cracked.
But sitting there, I realized I'd misunderstood my own words.
The book was never about being strong. Every chapter I'd written was about what you do when you're not. Foundations that crack and hold anyway. Storms that rage while something deeper stays still. Quiet battles no one sees. Getting knocked down and trusting the pattern leads somewhere better. Stepping into dangerous meadows because that's where the sustenance is.
I'd written a book about struggle disguised as strength.
And now I was living proof that the lessons worked.
In the military, asking for help meant the end of your career. It was ingrained in our souls to never tell anyone we were struggling, to handle our demons in silence, to be the rock no matter how much pressure we were under.
I'd internalized that message so deeply that even years after leaving, the thought of asking for help felt like betrayal. Like admitting I'd been lying about who I was all along.
I almost didn't publish this book because of it. Almost let the shame of needing help erase everything I'd written.
But sitting on that porch, reading my own lessons back to myself, I understood why I had to.
Not because I had it figured out. Because I didn't.
If these words could reach me in my darkest moment, maybe they could reach someone else in theirs. If my own book could teach me what I needed when I needed it most, maybe it could do the same for someone I'd never meet.
This book isn't proof that I'm strong.
It's proof that the lessons hold when you need them most.
I'm still standing at my red gate.
Still cracked. Still rebuilding. Still in the middle of a story I can't see the end of yet.
But still standing.
And maybe that's enough. Maybe that's the whole point. Not arriving at some place where the foundation never cracks. But learning that when it does, the lessons hold. The pattern continues. The meadow, dangerous as it feels, is where the sustenance grows.
If you're reading this and your own foundation is cracking, I want you to know something.
You're not weak for needing help. You're not a fraud for struggling. You're not less of a man, or less of a woman, or less of anything for admitting you can't carry it alone.
The guy who wrote this entire book about quiet strength? I needed help. I am learning my own lessons all over again. And I almost didn't tell you because the shame runs that deep.
Because at the heart of every struggle, every mental health battle, every addiction, every attempt to cope in silence, is a thread of destruction called shame. It's the voice that convinces you the cracked foundation is a moral failure, that needing help means you are a fraud. It runs so deep it almost erased everything I've written.
But I am telling you anyway.
Not because the shame is gone, but because I've learned its name. And the antidote to shame isn't strength; it's vulnerability. It is the terrifying, powerful act of admitting, "I can't carry this alone."
If these words could reach me in my darkest moment, maybe they can reach you in yours.
Cross the gate. Step into the meadow. Ask for help.
That is the quiet fight. That is the true strength.
I'll be here, watching. Still cracked, but rebuilding.
Still standing.

Bonus Chapter: The Weight That Makes Beauty Possible
Why This Chapter Exists
Throughout this book, I've written about gentle strength, patient hands, and foundations that hold. Some readers might wonder: "But have you been tested? Where does this wisdom come from?"
This chapter answers that question. It shows what tried to break the foundation so you can understand why it had to be built so deep. It transforms every gentle moment in this book from nice advice into hard-won wisdom. When you understand what these hands have touched, every scene of patient fatherhood becomes profound rather than just sweet.
But this chapter contains graphic descriptions of death and trauma. If you're carrying your own demons, if you don't want to revisit darkness, or if you simply don't want to glimpse what lies behind the gentle moments, please don't read this. The hope and wisdom in this book already stand complete without it.
If it gets too heavy at any point, feel free to close the book and not finish reading. The last thing I want is to add to your weight.
This was a hard chapter to write. Not because of reliving certain experiences, but because I've gone back and forth on what details to put in and what details not to put in. This is where I landed: these stories with just enough detail to express the darkness without enveloping you in it. Just enough to show what the foundation had to withstand.
This is just for those who want to understand the full depth of what makes quiet strength so rare and precious.
There are gates we cross that change how we see everything that comes after. Some open onto beauty so profound it stops your breath. Others open onto darkness so complete it threatens to swallow everything you thought you knew about what hands are meant to hold, what eyes are meant to see, what foundations are meant to withstand.
This is just a sliver into the dark room. Some things I can't talk about yet. Some things I never will. But to understand why gentle strength isn't naive, why patient hands aren't weak, why the foundation had to be built so deep, you need to see what tried to break it.
You need to understand what tested the steel before it became silk.
The first gate opened on a highway at 2 AM, where I learned that the human body isn't as permanent as you think, and that some sounds will live in your memory longer than the people who made them.
I was riding along with State Police during college, expecting routine traffic stops and stories for applications. The night air through the cracked window carried that dry cold that cuts through your jacket and makes your lungs ache. Radio chatter provided the familiar rhythm of a quiet shift. Red and blue lights ahead should have meant another routine accident.
The scene stretched for two miles of interstate. The asphalt was still warm from the day's heat, but the air carried that metallic smell of twisted metal and something else, something sweet and wrong that coated your throat and wouldn't wash away.
We walked the shoulder in silence except for the crunch of broken glass under our boots. My flashlight beam caught an arm here, bone fragments there. A tuft of hair caught on a guardrail, moving slightly in the wind from passing traffic. Each discovery marked with a cone, collected with a care that felt both sacred and hopeless.
The truck driver was still sitting in his cab when we found him, engine running, diesel fumes mixing with the smell of what had happened. When they pulled him down to get his statement, the sound that came from him wasn't human. Like a wounded animal that doesn't understand why it's dying. Like grief given voice without language to contain it.
I remember recognizing even then that this should affect me more in that moment, and that it would later on. You could almost feel your soul change a little bit, but you also recognized that you couldn't let it change too much. In that environment, the way you have to cope with violence and darkness is often humor, dark jokes that probably sound callous to anyone who wasn't there. But humor becomes a shield, a way to process what you can't process yet.
That wail from the truck driver lives in your bones after you hear it. It echoes in quiet moments when you think you've moved past what you've seen, when you're standing at a peaceful gate watching for your family to come home safe.
Years later, watching my horse fight a barbed wire fence, seeing the cuts open across her flank, it all came flooding back. Not just one memory, but all of them. The scattered pieces on that highway. The helplessness of trying to collect what couldn't be made whole again.
But this time was different. This time, my hands could heal instead of just witness. This time, there was something I could do besides stand by and gather fragments.
I began to understand that these experiences weren't just breaking me down. They were building something in me. A deeper commitment to healing rather than hurting. Even carrying that sound, I held onto the idea that people are naturally good. This driver wasn't evil. He was broken by something that wasn't his fault. I chose hope in humanity instead of letting the weight destroy that faith.
But not every gate opened onto accidents or circumstances beyond anyone's control.
Another gate opened in a bathroom where someone had decided the world was too heavy to carry. It was late at night, the overhead light casting everything in harsh white that made the red on the walls look black. The air was thick and metallic; copper pennies mixed with something that made your stomach turn.
He'd pulled a hoodie over his head, maybe trying to contain what was about to happen, maybe trying to hide from pain that felt bigger than living. It didn't work. The ceiling told the story the hoodie couldn't hide. Brain matter and blood painting patterns above where he'd fallen. The floor slick with gore that had tried to escape under the door.
His body still shaking, still trying to breathe even though life had already left, the nervous system firing its last confused signals.
Processing something like that requires a different wrestling. Not the immediate coping of dark humor, but the longer work of turning beautiful everyday moments into spiritual conversations with God. This is how you keep the shadows at bay. You try to see the beauty in everything, attempt to relate how you can grow instead of being destroyed.
Seeing those eyes in that bathroom, I understood something about suffering that I couldn't have grasped before. It made me think about Christ on the cross, killed when He didn't have to do that for us, but He did. He's the one that's gone through everything, gone through more than anybody could ever imagine, and He still has hope. He still has perfect love. He still reaches out His hand.
If God can remember sin no more, maybe He can take away the darkness in your thoughts too. Maybe He can take those memories away when the time is right. Even if I can't do it as well as Him, maybe I can try to do it too.
But there was another loss that cut deeper in a different way. A father. A friend. An honorable guy with kids back home who would grow up without him. When you have to move a body like that, when your hands touch what used to be someone who wrote letters home and missed birthdays, you learn something about death that movies never show. The way it moves when you shift it, loose and formless, like flesh-colored water in a human-shaped container. Like everything that made him a person had leaked out and left behind just the parts.
And later, learning more about why we were there, about the lies that got us into that desert, I couldn't stop wondering: did his kids lose their father because politicians couldn't tell the truth? Did he die for something that wasn't even real?
That thinking can breed bitterness in you. It can breed darkness. The what-ifs and the waste and the weight of carrying someone else's lies while good people pay the price. But I had to make a choice about how to process that betrayal. I decided that was on them, not me. I hoped that somehow, in some way, we had made someone's life better over there. The politicians who sent us there under false pretenses, they'll answer to their maker, not to me.
I couldn't let their lies poison the foundation I was building for my family. Because I was learning that the real battle wasn't just about witnessing death. Sometimes it was about fighting for life even when you knew it was already lost.
There was the accident where I had to do CPR on someone who was already gone. The evening sun still made everything too bright, too clear, like the world wanted me to see every detail. His chest beneath my locked hands felt wrong, caved in places where the damage had already been done. The ribs were already broken, moving freely under the skin like pieces that would never fit together again.
When I did compressions, I could feel everything shift and grind, broken bones clicking against each other. That sickening movement with each push, knowing I was just moving broken pieces around inside a chest that would never work again. Count to thirty. Breathe twice. Count to thirty. Breathe twice.
But you can't quit. Your training won't let you quit even when your mind knows better, even when each compression feels like you're breaking more than you're fixing.
Now, anytime someone coughs up water at the pool, that wet, choking sound brings it all back. Not because he drowned, but because that's the sound of lungs trying to work when they can't. The wet rattle, the desperate hack. The taste of chlorine from the pool mixing with the memory of that morning. The futility of trying to restart what's already stopped.
All of these experiences taught me something crucial about the foundation that held: it wasn't built to keep me from experiencing evil. It was built to keep me from becoming it. Because witnessing destruction is one thing, but there's another shadow entirely.
There's a different weight I haven't told you about yet. The weight that comes not from witnessing destruction, but from being good at it. From knowing what seething rage feels like when it's focused and professional. From understanding what it's like to destroy something and feel good about it because that was your job, that was what you were trained to do.
I know what it feels like to be effective at violence. To have hands that know exactly how to break things, to have a mind that can calculate destruction with precision. And I know the satisfaction that comes with doing that job well, the righteous anger that can fuel you when you're pointed at the right target.
But I also know how that capacity can creep over you if you let it. How the ability for professional violence can start to feel natural, normal, even enjoyable. How the gate between using violence as a tool and becoming someone who seeks violence can be thinner than you think.
I hope that satisfaction in destruction isn't natural. Maybe I just need to believe it's not. But I know what it feels like, and I know how easy it would be to let that part of you take over when the world disappoints you, when politicians lie, when good people die for nothing.
What kept me from crossing that gate was watching my wife care for others. Witnessing her hear people's heartache and genuinely respond with compassion. She has her own trauma, her own shadows, but she chooses to lift herself and others instead of tearing down. Everybody has their own struggles, and seeing her decide to build rather than destroy, I knew I wanted to follow that example.
Coming home to Texas, establishing a home with my family, crystallized this choice for me. I wanted to create, not destroy. It drove me to start building homes for a while, because it made me recognize I wanted to be a builder in this world, not someone who tears things apart. And now, writing this book, putting these words down, trying to build something with language that might help someone else through their own darkness. Every story shared, every lesson offered is another act of construction instead of destruction.
Yes, I still need to maintain that capability for violence, need to keep that tool sharp to protect my family and those who need defending. But only when I have to. I'd much rather build.
This choice became the thread that held everything together.
The foundation held against all of it. Not just the trauma of witnessing evil, but the temptation to become it. Not just the weight of seeing destruction, but the pull of being the one who delivers it.
What drove me through all of this was thinking about my boys. I didn't want to be consumed by shadows for them. I wanted to be the light. I didn't want them to remember me being torn apart by difficult experiences, but processing and healing so they would know it's possible to do that. They were the driving force in choosing to hope over bitterness, in choosing to build rather than destroy.
The wrestling looked like pondering, turning beautiful everyday moments into spiritual conversations with God. Trying to find the beauty in everything, attempting to relate how you can grow instead of being destroyed. This is how you keep the void at bay.
Even now, too many years later, I still have friends who choose that final path. Every time I see a black ribbon on a badge on social media, I get terrified that another friend ended it without even reaching out. The struggle is wanting to see the signs, wanting to help, but losing people anyway despite everything you thought you knew about watching for them.
But this is exactly why the foundation had to hold. Someone needs to stand guard at the gates that matter most. Someone needs to be the father whose hands stay gentle even after they've touched things that could make them hard.
Someone needs to keep choosing light when shadows would be easier, building when destroying would feel justified.
When I teach my boys about using their hands to build and protect instead of hurt and destroy, I'm not teaching them because the world is safe. I'm teaching them because the world is dangerous, and they need to know it's possible to stay good despite what they might see or do. They need to know that choosing gentleness after you've seen what strength without love can do, that's the strongest choice you can make.
And now I finally understand something I couldn't see as a child, watching my father's hands transform from steel to silk as he reached toward that dying pilot. Something I couldn't grasp when I wondered how someone could be so patient, so tender, so present in another person's pain.
I've stood at that gate between light and darkness. The foundation held.
And now I know why my father's hands were so gentle on that pilot's arm.
He'd stood at that gate too.
