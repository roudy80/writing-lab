# Chapter 10: Frame and Void

Maria Webb | Luna Base, Sector C Observation Platform

February 18, 2028 | 14:33 Luna Standard Time

The ship didn't look like a ship yet.

That was Maria's first thought every time she came to the observation platform, the one on the outside of Habitat Ring C that you reached through an airlock sequence and a ladder that the engineers had bolted to the regolith with an informality that suggested they'd built it in an afternoon and hadn't gotten around to replacing it with something permanent. The platform was a steel grating attached to a lunar boulder that the base's early construction crews had converted into a foundation point, and it gave you a clear sightline across four kilometers of hard vacuum to the assembly framework.

What you saw from four kilometers was structure. Ribs of high-tensile aluminum alloy arranged in a spine that was already longer than the biggest thing humanity had previously launched into space, flanked by the permanent construction scaffolding that crawled with robotic arms and suited technicians reduced to glove-sized figures at this distance. The cross-sections weren't closed yet. The ship's habitation rings were visible as open frameworks, hoops through which you could see the stars. The drive assembly at the stern was present only as its mounting architecture — the cradles and heat exchangers and magnetic field generators that would eventually enclose the engine itself, which had not been fully designed yet. That was the part ARIA referred to, in its daily construction progress reports, as "drive system integration pending final propulsion specifications," which was engineering language for we haven't decided yet.

But the spine was there. The spine was undeniably there, and it was larger than Maria had expected even though she had been flying construction cargo to this facility for two years and had watched it grow component by component, and the gap between understanding something's scale from numbers and understanding it from sight was a gap that kept surprising her.

She stayed on the platform for eleven minutes, which was longer than the cold justified. Then she went back through the airlock.

* * *

Commander Patricia Novak's office was what a base commander's office looked like when the base commander had been too busy for three years to do anything about the way her office looked: a desk with a surface entirely covered by things that had been set there temporarily, three monitors, a folded lunar suit hanging from a hook that had probably been installed for a different purpose, and a whiteboard on the wall that had not been fully erased in what appeared to be several months. The most recent writing on the whiteboard was a schedule. The previous writing, visible through the new, appeared to be a technical diagram of some kind.

Novak was standing at the whiteboard when Maria came in, not looking at it but thinking at it, which was a distinction Maria had learned to recognize.

"Cargo manifest for February is short by eighteen percent," Novak said, without turning around. She said this the way she said most things: as a fact that needed acknowledging before anything else could be addressed.

"Supply chain issue at Cape Canaveral. The titanium alloy shortage is real. I've been reading the reports."

"The reports say three months. I think six." Novak turned. She was fifty-one, a naval engineer who had transferred to NASA in 2019 and who managed Luna Base with the organizational style of someone who had been responsible for keeping large expensive systems functional in remote environments for most of her professional life. She was not warm, exactly, but she was direct in a way that Maria found preferable to warm. "Your assignment rotation comes up in April. You're eligible for a transfer back to Earth duty."

"I know."

"Are you putting in for it?"

Maria had been thinking about this since December. She had an apartment in Houston she had not slept in for fourteen months. She had a sister in Connecticut and two nephews she'd seen twice since the impact, once in person and once on a screen. She had the accumulated small frictions of two years on a base where the population was 340 people and the social landscape had been mapped and remapped until everyone knew exactly who they were going to have dinner with and why.

"No," she said.

Novak nodded. She had the manner of someone who had expected this answer. "The drive specifications," she said. "You've read the current draft."

"I've read it."

"Your thoughts."

This was the reason Maria had been summoned, not the cargo manifest. The drive specifications were not her area of expertise in any formal sense: she was a pilot and a mission specialist, not a propulsion engineer. But she'd spent two years living with this project, flying its components, reading every briefing and technical summary that crossed her clearance level, developing an intuitive familiarity with the engineering landscape that Novak had been consulting her on for six months in the specific way that commanders consulted people who didn't have credentials but had judgment.

"The current draft gets us to eleven percent of light speed," Maria said. "Which gets us to Proxima Centauri in forty-one years at cruise. Crew would be in cryosleep for the transit."

"And the alternative."

"The alternative adds four years to the construction timeline and gets us to seventeen percent. Twenty-five years transit time. Meaningfully shorter for the crew's biological age at arrival."

"At a cost of four years we may not have."

The asteroid arrived in 2042. ARIA's latest projection put the probability of deflection at 61 percent, down from the 68 that had been the public-facing figure since 2025. The deflection mission was on track. The probability had declined because more precise orbital modeling had tightened the error bars on the trajectory estimate, which had not improved the estimate's optimism. If the deflection failed, the ship needed to be launched and at a safe distance before 2042.

"We have enough margin for four years if nothing else goes wrong," Maria said. "In my assessment."

"Nothing else going wrong is not a category I plan around." Novak sat down at her desk. "ARIA ran the optimistic scenario with the extended drive timeline at my request. Eighty-three percent confidence of launch completion before the impact window. With the current timeline it's ninety-one."

An eight-percentage-point difference on a one-time event.

"What does Hartley say?" Maria asked. Paul Hartley had moved from Senator Grant's staff to the program's technical advisory board six months ago. He was the one reading the propulsion engineering reports and translating them for the political stakeholders.

"Hartley says the decision is above his level. Which is correct." Novak looked at her. "I've sent my recommendation to the scientific board. I recommended the current timeline with the lower performance specification. I'm telling you this because you're going to be the one flying it."

Maria absorbed this.

"You're telling me before the decision is official."

"I'm telling you because you should know what you're being asked to fly and what it can do." Novak said this without apology. "Forty-one years. Proxima Centauri. If the deflection fails and we launch. You go in cryosleep. You wake up at the other end, if the biology works, and you're in your mid-seventies and you're looking at a star that isn't the sun and you've got four thousand people behind you who trusted you to get them there."

Maria looked at the whiteboard with its half-erased technical diagram and the schedule written over it.

"I know what I signed up for," she said.

"No," Novak said. "You signed up for seventeen years of cargo runs and briefings. You're now being told what comes after that." She paused. "I'm not asking you to decide anything today. I'm asking you to understand what you're deciding when the time comes."

Maria nodded. She stood to leave, then stopped.

"Amy Stokes," she said. "She's been passed to Elena Marsh's second round."

"I heard."

"She has two kids. Nineteen and twenty-two now."

"I know." Novak's voice was level. "That's not the selection criterion."

"I know it's not." Maria put her hand on the doorframe. "I was just saying."

* * *

She went back through the corridor to the pilot quarters, passing three technicians she knew by name and two she didn't, nodding to all of them with the practiced ease of someone who lived in a community of three hundred and forty people and had learned the social economy of a place where there was nowhere to go to be alone except the observation platform in the cold.

She sat on her bunk and looked at the ceiling, which was a standard-issue habitat ceiling and did not have anything particular to recommend it as a subject of contemplation.

Forty-one years. Proxima Centauri.

She thought about the observation platform and the spine of the ship against the stars and the robotic arms moving with the patient mechanical certainty of systems that didn't have opinions about what they were building.

She thought about her nephew's birthday cake, the chocolate one, whether her sister had gotten the right one.

She thought about the column of smoke and earth that had been rising from Massachusetts when she'd looked out the window of Flight 847 and understood, in the first clean moment before the professionalism took over, what the world had just become.

Two and a half years ago that had been a shock. It had sharpened, in the months since, into something more like a project. It was still a project. It was also, she was beginning to understand, the whole structure of the rest of her life, and she had known this for some time, and she was just today arriving at the specific version of knowing it that sat differently in the body than all the previous versions.

She lay back on the bunk and closed her eyes for three minutes, which was all she had before her next briefing.

Then she got up and went back to work.
