# Chapter 11: The People They Left

Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston

September 12, 2028 | 6:41 PM EST

The appeals had mostly failed.

Thomas had filed twenty-nine in the spring of 2026, following up on the three he'd submitted in January. Of those thirty-two, eight had been approved for partial restoration of original funding, which meant about a third of the original allocation and an indefinite timeline for the rest. Twelve were still in review, which was bureaucratic language for yes but not yet or no but not yet and was only distinguishable from the other by subsequent events. Twelve had been denied.

He had expected this. He had filed them anyway because filing them had produced something he hadn't entirely anticipated: a paper trail that was now being used, by three different community organizations in the outer ring neighborhoods, as the evidentiary foundation for a federal civil complaint filed in January 2028 through a pro-bono legal team at Northeastern University. The complaint alleged systematic de-prioritization of impact-affected communities in the reconstruction authorization process. It named the Office of Management and Budget and the Department of Housing and Urban Development and four other agencies.

Thomas was not the plaintiff. He was a witness and a source. He had provided documentation and had answered questions under oath in two depositions and had been careful, in both depositions, to answer exactly what was asked and nothing beyond it, because he was not a lawyer and because the lawyers had asked him to be careful and because careful was how he worked.

He had also, in the two years since the OMB re-allocation, quietly helped three other people start their own community information sessions in Roxbury and Dorchester and East Boston. Not officially. Not through any organization. He had given them the structure of the sessions, the methodology, the list of federal processes and their actual mechanisms versus their theoretical ones, and then stepped back, because the sessions worked better when they grew from the community rather than from him, and because he was aware that his profile, such as it was, was not an asset in every context.

Mrs. Petrovitch was back in her apartment. The Lamartine Street building had been cleared in November 2027, twenty-five months after the impact, with a partial funding restoration that had covered the structural work and left the interior finishes incomplete. She had moved back in anyway, had been living with unfinished walls and a kitchen that was functional but unfinished, had told him when he'd stopped by to check the structural work that it was fine, that she'd spent forty years in the building and she wasn't about to let unfinished drywall change that.

He'd said something about the drywall being finished eventually. She'd handed him a cup of tea and said that she hoped so, in the tone of someone who had recalibrated their relationship with eventually.

* * *

Ben Oakes had been a structural engineer before the impact. They'd met at a conference in 2022 and had stayed loosely in contact the way professionals stayed in contact: occasional emails, a few conference-adjacent beers, the kind of relationship that was professional rather than personal but that had a warmth to it.

On October 15, 2025, Ben had been in a meeting at a research facility in Pasadena when the shockwave hit. He had been recruited within the week, not to the Luna Base program but to something adjacent: a project at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory working on the asteroid deflection mission specifically, the hardware and software required to actually change the trajectory of a 340-meter object in space with enough precision to miss the Earth.

Ben had been the one Thomas had texted the afternoon of the impact. The reply had come three days later. Then nothing for eighteen months. Then an email, brief, saying that he was in a position where he couldn't talk about the work specifically but that he was glad Thomas was doing what he was doing.

The current contact had started in April 2028, when Ben had shown up at a structural assessment conference in Chicago that Thomas was presenting at, and had found him after the session and suggested they get dinner.

They'd had dinner. They'd talked about the work, Ben's and Thomas's, in the way that people talked about work when one of them had a clearance that limited what they could say. Thomas had come away from the dinner with the impression that the deflection mission was more complicated than the public-facing briefings suggested, and that Ben had wanted to see him for reasons that went beyond catching up, and that the reason hadn't been fully surfaced at dinner.

Ben called in September.

"I'm going to be in Boston in two weeks," he said. "Can we get coffee?"

"Sure."

"Not at a conference."

Thomas processed this distinction. "Okay."

"I have something I want to tell you. It's not classified. It's just something I think you should know, and I need to say it to someone who will understand it."

They met at a diner in Cambridge on a Saturday morning, a place Ben had suggested, and Thomas arrived first and watched Ben come in from the parking lot with the particular walk of someone who had been carrying something for a long time and was about to set it down.

"The deflection probability," Ben said, after the coffee had arrived and he'd been quiet long enough to make Thomas aware that the thing he was about to say mattered. "The official figure is sixty-one percent."

"That's what ARIA published in the last update."

"The internal figure, as of six weeks ago, is fifty-four percent." Ben held his cup in both hands. He was not looking at Thomas. He was looking at the table. "There's a targeting problem with the kinetic impactors. The asteroid is a rubble pile. You hit it in the wrong place, you get fragmentation instead of deflection. You get a debris field instead of a single object."

Thomas kept very still.

"The public figure is sixty-one," Ben said. "That's the optimistic scenario from the model that assumes the targeting problem is solvable. The internal figure accounts for the current probability of solving the targeting problem before the 2039 launch window."

"Is it solvable?"

"Probably. That's what we're working on." Ben looked up. "The problem is the timeline. We need to run a precursor mission to map the internal structure of the asteroid before we can finalize the targeting. The precursor mission launches in 2031. It gets there in 2033. That's two years of data processing before we have the internal structure map. Which means the final targeting parameters for the kinetic impactors can't be confirmed until 2035 at the earliest."

"Which is four years before the launch window."

"Four years is enough, if the data is what we need it to be." Ben finally drank his coffee. "I'm telling you this because I think you deserve to know. I think the people in your community sessions deserve to know. Not the internal probability figure, that's not mine to share. But the shape of the problem."

Thomas thought about the community sessions. About Dmitri, who had been asking more pointed questions over the past year, whose understanding of the political mechanics of the situation had sharpened considerably. About Nina, whose daughter was two now and who had started bringing her to the sessions because her regular childcare had fallen through and had kept bringing her because she didn't seem to find the sessions less valuable for the presence of a toddler.

"The ship," Thomas said.

"Yes."

"If the probability comes down further."

"The ship becomes more important. Yes." Ben set down his cup. "I'm not saying it's going to. I'm saying the ship is not just a backup for a worst case. It's a backup for a range of cases that's wider than the public messaging implies."

Thomas sat with this for a moment. Outside the diner window, Cambridge went about a Saturday morning: people walking dogs, a couple arguing quietly at a parking meter, a group of students moving with the directionless energy of people who had woken up recently and had not yet committed to a direction. People who did not know what Ben had just told him. People who were continuing to live their lives in the way that people continued to live their lives, which was the only available method.

"What do you need from me?" Thomas asked.

"Nothing," Ben said. "I just needed to tell someone who would hold it right."

Thomas thought about what holding it right meant. He thought about the thirty-two files in the redirected column and the eight partial restorations and the twelve denials and the twelve still pending. He thought about the federal civil complaint and the pro-bono team at Northeastern and Mrs. Petrovitch's unfinished kitchen walls.

He thought about Dmitri, who had started attending local planning board meetings, who had started asking the planning board questions that Thomas recognized as the next level of the questions he'd been asking in the community sessions.

"I'll hold it right," Thomas said.

They finished their coffee. Ben took the train back to wherever he was staying. Thomas drove back to Jamaica Plain and sat in his car outside the building where he kept his office and did not go in for a while.

He thought about the probability estimate. He ran the math in his head the way he ran structural calculations, looking for the load-bearing element, the thing the rest of it hung on. Fifty-four percent optimistic, internal, six weeks old. The precursor mission in 2031. The structure map in 2035.

He thought about nine billion people who were not on the ship.

He got out of the car and went in and opened the thirty-two project files and began reviewing the twelve that were still pending, because that was the work he could do, and doing the work he could do was the only response to information he couldn't act on that was worth anything at all.

At nine o'clock he closed the laptop and walked two blocks to the community center and unlocked it and turned on the lights and set up the chairs for the Thursday session, which was three days away, and then walked back to his car in the September dark.

He had not figured out what to do with what Ben had told him.

He suspected he was going to have to.
