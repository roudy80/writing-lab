# Chapter 12: Sovereignty

Dr. Marcus Webb | Mars Station Olympus

April 3, 2029 | 11:14 AM Mars Local Solar Time

The message from Earth had taken fourteen minutes to arrive and would take fourteen minutes to reply to, which gave Marcus twenty-eight minutes in which the conversation could not move forward, and which he was using to re-read the message a third time and look out the habitat window at the rust-colored plateau that had been his horizon for four years.

The message was from the Framework's liaison office in Geneva, which reported to Senator Grant's committee, and it was officially a "coordination update regarding Mars Station Olympus's operational and governance status." What it actually was, in Marcus's reading, was a question: are you still with us or not?

The question had been building for two years.

The Mars station had grown. That was the relevant fact that everything else derived from. In 2025, when the Boston impact had severed communications for four days and Marcus had convened the crew for their vote, there had been twelve people on the station and a hydroponics expansion that could support fourteen. There were now thirty-one people, a third hydroponic expansion underway that would support thirty-eight, a water extraction operation that had exceeded all projections, and a construction program that had added four new habitat modules and a workshop that the crew had taken to calling, with an informality that Marcus found both practical and revealing, "the yard."

They were not dependent on Earth supply. They had not been dependent on Earth supply for eight months. The last resupply cargo from Luna had contained components they'd requested but could theoretically have fabricated themselves, and three months from now, when the next fabrication printer came online, they would be able to fabricate them themselves.

Dr. Lena Shaw had done this. That was the accurate assessment and Marcus had been making it consistently in the crew reports he sent to Earth, which had become less frequent in their actual information content as the crew had become more capable and the specific nature of their daily operations had become less dependent on Earth expertise. Lena had taken the hydroponics operation from a survival measure to a genuine food system. She'd trained two crew members who were now running most of it themselves. She thought about the station's food independence the way Marcus thought about its operational independence: as a condition to be engineered into permanence.

The problem with independence was that it was not the plan.

The plan, the plan Marcus had voted for along with the other eleven crew members on October 19, 2025, had been to commit to becoming a permanent self-sustaining settlement. The plan had not specified the governance structure of that settlement. It had not specified whether a permanent self-sustaining settlement on Mars was a facility of the Framework for Coordinated Planetary Defense, an independent entity, or something in between that didn't have a name yet.

The Framework had a view on this. The message from Geneva expressed the view in diplomatic language that Marcus had learned to read as: you are a facility of the Framework, you receive Framework support and guidance, and your "operational independence" is a practical characteristic of a remote facility rather than a legal or governance status.

Mars had a different view. This was not Marcus's view specifically, although he held it. It was the view that had emerged from four years of thirty-one people solving problems on a planet where Earth was fourteen minutes away and the solutions had to be found locally or not found at all. The view was: we have been solving our own problems. We are good at it. We are going to keep doing it. The question of whether we do it as a Framework facility or as something else is a question that deserves a real answer.

The crew had been having this conversation informally for a year. Marcus had been managing the formal version of it with Geneva for six months. The message this morning was Geneva deciding it was time to have the formal version directly.

He hit reply and started typing.

* * *

Lena Shaw came to find him at noon, which she did on most days because they had both developed the habit of eating lunch at the same time in the main habitat common area. It was the kind of habit that developed in small communities where the schedules aligned whether you planned them or not.

"You sent a reply," she said. It wasn't a question. The station's outgoing communications were logged and the crew had access to the logs, which had been a policy decision Marcus had made in year one and that he'd found, consistently, to be worth the transparency cost.

"I sent a reply."

"What did you say?"

He'd said: the crew of Mars Station Olympus is committed to the mission's goals, including the development of human life beyond Earth. We believe that commitment is best served by governance structures that reflect the operational reality of a self-sustaining settlement rather than the assumptions of a dependent facility. We are willing to discuss what that means in practice. We are not willing to accept a framework that treats four years of demonstrated capability as a circumstance to be managed rather than a foundation to build on.

He told Lena the substance of this. She ate her lunch without commenting, which was her way of processing information that she needed to think about before she had an opinion.

"They'll say we're destabilizing the program," she said eventually.

"They might."

"Are we?"

He'd thought about this. The ship needed Mars. Not operationally, not yet, but the long-term plan included Mars as support infrastructure, a waypoint and a second base of human activity that made the interstellar mission less contingent on a single fragile Earth civilization. That was the plan as written by people who had been writing plans on Earth in 2025 with a particular picture of what Mars would be. The picture was outdated.

"We're not destabilizing it," he said. "We're insisting that the program account for what Mars actually is instead of what the 2025 projections assumed it would be."

"Which is what?"

"A civilization. A small one. Four years old. But a civilization."

Lena was quiet for a moment. She looked out the habitat window at the plateau, at the long afternoon shadows the Martian sun was casting across the equipment yard. "Thirty-one people," she said.

"Is where it starts."

She nodded slowly. She had the expression of someone who had been thinking about something for a long time and had just heard the sentence that named it.

"If we push this," she said, "and Geneva pushes back, we could lose the Framework support. The technical collaboration. The communication priority."

"We could."

"We'd survive it."

"Yes." He held her gaze. "We would survive it. The question is whether we want to spend the next thirteen years in that kind of friction with the organization that's trying to save the human species."

"As opposed to what? Spending the next thirteen years being managed as a dependent facility by an organization that's treating us like an inconvenience."

"That's the choice, more or less."

She looked at her lunch. "What does the crew think?"

"I'm going to find out this afternoon." He stood up. "I'm calling a meeting."

* * *

The meeting was in the main common area, all thirty-one crew members on the station, with two joining by video link from the water extraction site three kilometers away. Marcus had not prepared remarks. He'd found, over four years, that the crew responded better to conversations than to presentations, and that the conversations were more useful to him than the presentations anyway because the crew was genuinely intelligent and noticed things he didn't notice.

He described the Geneva message. He described his reply. He described the choice as he saw it: continue negotiating with the Framework from within its structure, or make a formal request for governance recognition as an independent entity.

The room was quiet when he finished.

Dr. Paulo Ribeiro, the station's geologist and the crew member most likely to ask the question everyone else was thinking: "What do we lose if we formalize independence?"

"Communication priority. Technical collaboration terms would need to be renegotiated. Potentially some supply chain support, though we're mostly past needing it."

"What do we gain?"

"Legal recognition that our governance decisions are ours to make. Formal status in the Framework's multi-sovereign structure as a distinct party rather than a facility." He paused. "Voice. In the decisions that are going to be made about what comes next."

A crew member from the back, a systems engineer named Adaeze who had been on the station for eighteen months and who spoke rarely but with the quality of someone who had thought carefully before she spoke: "The ship. If we're independent, what's our relationship to the ship?"

"That's the negotiation," Marcus said. "We want to be part of it. Not as a facility but as a partner."

"They might say no."

"They might." He looked at the room. "This vote is different from the one we took in 2025. In 2025 we were choosing between two operational modes. This vote is about who we are, legally and politically. I want to be clear about that."

The room was quiet again. Thirty-one people who had come to Mars as researchers and specialists and had become, in four years, something else, were making an assessment.

"Call the vote," Lena said.

The vote was twenty-eight to three for formal independence.

Marcus sent the message to Geneva that afternoon. It took fourteen minutes to travel. The reply would take fourteen more.

He went to stand at the habitat window and look at the plateau and think about what he'd just done. The sun was getting low, the Martian afternoon moving toward its long orange sunset. The equipment yard was quiet. Somewhere out there, in the cold dark between worlds, a 340-meter object was following its mathematics.

He'd just told the people trying to stop it that Mars was going to be a partner in the effort, not a facility.

He thought this was the right call. He also thought the next thirteen years were going to be considerably more complicated than the previous four.

He went back to work.
