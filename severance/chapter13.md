# Chapter 13: The Public Number

Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston

November 14, 2029 | 6:22 AM EST

The ARIA briefing dropped on a Tuesday morning and Thomas read it on his phone before he got out of bed, which was a habit he'd developed over four years of watching the numbers, and which he was occasionally aware was not a healthy habit.

The headline figure was 54 percent.

He read the figure three times. Then he sat up and read the full briefing, which was two pages and had the measured, careful language of an organization that had spent a week deciding how to release information it would have preferred to keep internal. The précis: the latest orbital modeling, incorporating new data from the preliminary observation campaign launched in 2028, had revised the deflection probability downward from the previously published 61 percent to 54 percent. The revision reflected tighter constraints on the asteroid's internal structure, which had complicated the targeting models for the kinetic impactors. The precursor mission, scheduled for launch in 2031, would provide the structural data required to finalize targeting parameters. ARIA's assessment was that the probability would improve once that data was available. The 54 percent figure was an honest accounting of current knowledge, not a final assessment.

Thomas sat with this for a while.

He thought about Ben Oakes, in the diner in Cambridge fourteen months ago. The internal figure is 54 percent. That's what Ben had told him, which meant the public number and the internal number were now the same number, which meant the information Ben had given him at professional and possibly legal risk was now on the ARIA website, and Thomas had been carrying it for over a year without knowing what to do with it, and now the carrying was over.

He got dressed. He made coffee. He stood at his kitchen window and looked at the street below, the Jamaica Plain morning assembling itself the way it always did: a woman walking a dog in the cold, a delivery truck blocking the crosswalk while the driver ran a package to a door, the Dunkin across the intersection already with a line six people deep. Life proceeding, the way it always did, the way it would keep doing until it didn't.

The civil complaint had settled in September.

Not fully. Three of the six agencies had reached a partial settlement: a commitment to revised authorization timelines and an independent monitoring office, which the pro-bono team at Northeastern had argued was a structural fix rather than a dollar-figure remedy. Thomas had reviewed the settlement terms and found them honestly modest, which was the accurate characterization of what three years of federal litigation produced when you were arguing process failure rather than harm. The monitoring office was real. The timeline revisions were real. The two agencies that hadn't settled were still in discovery, which would take another eighteen months at minimum, and the two agencies that had denied the complaint outright were on appeal.

Dmitri had called it a partial win. Thomas had called it a record and moved on, which was what you did when the record was as good as you were going to get and the work in front of you hadn't changed.

The Thursday session had grown. Forty-two regular attendees now, up from the original eleven, with a waiting list that Thomas maintained with the same spreadsheet discipline he applied to the appeals files. The sessions had evolved: less introduction, more working meeting. Dmitri ran a subgroup that focused on the city planning interface. Nina had started a parallel session specifically for parents with children under twelve, which met on Tuesday evenings and which Thomas attended once a month as a resource rather than a facilitator.

Thomas poured his coffee and sat at his kitchen table and opened his laptop.

By mid-morning the ARIA briefing had generated well over a thousand news articles. He looked at a sample. The range was what he'd expected: measured scientific reporting from the serious outlets, crisis-register headlines from the ones that understood their traffic model, and the specific flavor of political response that emerged whenever a federal agency published information that complicated someone's preferred narrative. Senator Grant's office had released a statement within forty minutes of the briefing, which was fast enough that the statement had been prepared in advance, which meant Grant's office had known this was coming. The statement was careful. It acknowledged the revised figure, it emphasized the precursor mission, and it used the phrase "honest and transparent assessment" three times in six paragraphs, which was the rhetorical equivalent of a doctor telling you that a diagnosis is preliminary before explaining what it might mean.

Thomas noted the statement and moved on.

His phone rang at 9:03. It was Ben Oakes.

"You saw it," Ben said.

"I saw it."

"I wanted to call before you saw it." A pause. "I should have called last night. I knew it was dropping this morning."

"You gave me the number fourteen months ago," Thomas said. "You didn't owe me a preview."

"I wanted you to hear it from me first."

Thomas looked at the window. The delivery truck had gone. The dog-walker was back, different dog. "The precursor mission. The structural data. You said if the data is what we need it to be, four years is enough."

"That's still true."

"And if it's not what we need it to be?"

Ben was quiet for a moment. "Then we remodel the approach. There are contingencies. This isn't binary."

"What's the contingency?"

"I can't talk about it specifically."

Thomas had learned, over three years of knowing Ben Oakes in this particular way, that what Ben couldn't talk about specifically was usually the thing that mattered most. He'd learned not to push on it, because Ben told him what he could when he could, and pushing on the gaps produced silence rather than information.

"The ship," Thomas said.

"The ship is on track. Luna Base construction milestone last month. The spine is closed. They're starting the habitation ring installation."

"I know. I've been following the briefings."

"The ship is real, Thomas. That's not in question."

Thomas thought about what was in question. Four thousand people out of nine billion. The math that he'd been carrying since the meeting in Cambridge and that he was still, three years later, carrying in the same way: too heavy to set down, too abstract to do anything with directly.

"The community sessions," Ben said. "How are they going?"

"Forty-two people Thursday. The civil complaint settled in September, partial terms."

"I saw that. The monitoring office is something."

"It's something." Thomas turned a pen over in his hand. "Ben, what does 54 percent mean in concrete terms. Not the institutional language. What does it mean."

A longer pause. "It means slightly worse than a coin flip, with real work ahead that could move it either direction. The targeting problem is solvable. The team working on it has made real progress. The precursor data is what we need to lock it down."

"And if the precursor data doesn't help."

"It will help. The question is whether it helps enough."

Thomas sat with this. Enough was a word that was doing a lot of work.

"Okay," he said.

"Okay?"

"Thank you for calling."

There was a pause, and Thomas understood that Ben had expected more from this conversation and was adjusting to not getting it. "How are you holding it?" Ben asked. The same language he'd used in the diner.

"The same way," Thomas said. "Right."

He hung up and sat at the kitchen table for a while with his coffee going cold. Then he opened a new spreadsheet and started a list.

* * *

The Thursday session that week was different.

Thomas had been facilitating community sessions for four years and he'd learned to read the room the way he'd learned to read structural assessments: by the quality of the silence before anything was said, by the body language of the people who came in early versus the people who waited in the hall until the last minute, by the specific way attention organized itself when something in the broader context had shifted.

Twenty-six of the forty-two were there. The rest had sent messages: some were watching the news, some were with family, some were doing what people did when a number got published that they'd been trying not to think about. The sixteen who'd stayed away weren't disengaged. They were processing.

Dmitri was in his usual chair, second row left. Nina was in the back with her daughter, who was four now and had developed the ability to color through an adult meeting without making noise, which was a skill Thomas genuinely admired. Three new faces sat near the door, which was where new people always sat, close enough to leave if they needed to.

Thomas didn't open with the ARIA briefing. He opened with what he always opened with: the work in front of them. The two agencies still in discovery. The appeals on the denials. The monitoring office implementation timeline, which he'd been tracking with the relevant staff contacts and which was three months behind its own published schedule, which was not surprising and was useful information to have documented.

He got about eight minutes in before a man named Warren in the fourth row raised his hand and said, "Are we going to talk about the briefing this morning?"

Thomas had expected this. "Yes," he said. "After the operational update."

Warren nodded and sat back. The room waited with the specific patience of people who are willing to do the thing in order but who need to know the thing is going to be addressed.

Thomas finished the operational update. Then he put down his notes.

"The ARIA briefing this morning revised the deflection probability to 54 percent," he said. "I want to be direct about what I know and what I don't know."

The room was quiet.

"What I know: 54 percent is the current scientific consensus based on the best available data, which is incomplete because the precursor mission that will provide the key structural data doesn't launch until 2031. What I don't know: whether the data from that mission will improve the probability, hold it where it is, or complicate it further. Those are the honest options and anyone who tells you differently is either reading something I'm not or telling you what they think you need to hear."

A woman in the third row, one of the three new faces, asked: "What does 54 percent mean for us? For the people not on the ship?"

Thomas had been asked versions of this question for four years and had given versions of the same answer. "It means the primary plan, the deflection mission, has a current probability that isn't where any of us would want it to be. The secondary plan, which is the interstellar ship, exists because the people who designed this response understood from the beginning that deflection isn't certain. The two plans are running in parallel. The ship isn't the backup in the sense of a plan you only think about if the primary fails. It's an active program that proceeds regardless of the deflection outcome."

"Four thousand people," said Dmitri. Not a question.

"Four thousand people on the ship. Nine billion on Earth. The deflection mission is what protects the nine billion, and that is the mission that all the political and technical energy is focused on. The ship is the hedge."

"A hedge that saves four thousand," Dmitri said.

"Yes."

The room sat with this. The four-year-old in the back was coloring. The fluorescent light in the corner hummed the way it always hummed.

"So what do we do?" Warren asked.

Thomas had been thinking about this since early that morning. He had a list. "The same thing we've been doing, but with more urgency. The monitoring office has to be held to its implementation timeline. The two agencies in discovery need to know that the public attention cycle generated by this morning's briefing is something we intend to use, not ignore. The appeals have deadlines and those deadlines matter more than they did yesterday."

"And the larger thing," Dmitri said. "The political conversation about the ship. About who's on it."

"That conversation is above what we can directly affect from this room," Thomas said. "It is not above what we can document. The record we've been building for four years is the record that exists when that conversation becomes impossible to avoid."

He believed this. He also knew it was incomplete, that a record was not the same as power, that documentation was a form of witness rather than a form of intervention. He'd been thinking about the gap between those two things since before the civil complaint was filed, and he didn't have a better answer than the one he kept giving, which was: do the thing you can do and trust that the doing accumulates.

After the session, Nina caught up with him in the hallway. Her daughter was asleep against her shoulder.

"The new people," she said. "Warren and the two in the front."

"I noticed."

"They're from a neighborhood group in Roxbury. They've been following the coverage and they want to know how we do what we do."

Thomas looked at the child on her shoulder, the specific dead weight of a sleeping four-year-old. "Tell them to come Thursday. We'll show them the methodology."

Nina nodded. She looked down the hallway, at the exit, at the street through the glass doors. "Do you think it gets better? The probability."

Thomas thought about Ben Oakes. He thought about the specific quality of Ben's voice in the diner, which was the voice of a man telling the truth carefully. "I think there are people working very hard on a very hard problem," he said. "That's as honest as I can be."

She nodded. She shifted her daughter's weight. She went out into the November dark.

Thomas locked up the community center and walked back to his car and sat in it for a while before starting the engine. The street was empty at this hour, the November cold coming through the glass, the city quiet in the particular way it was quiet at eleven on a weeknight.

He thought about the precursor mission. 2031. Data by 2033. Final targeting parameters by 2035. Four years before the launch window.

He thought about a coin flip with nine billion people on it.

He started the car and drove home and opened the new spreadsheet and kept working.
