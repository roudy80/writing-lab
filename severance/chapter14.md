# The Scattered Worlds: Severance

Chapter 14: Notification
Dr. Elena Marsh | Crew Selection Office, Houston
March 9, 2030 | 10:47 AM CST

The first notification went to a marine biologist named David Harmon, age thirty-four, who had scored in the ninety-second percentile on the behavioral assessments and whose cryosleep compatibility screening had come back with the three metabolic markers flagged red.

Elena drafted the letter herself. She had three templates prepared by the legal team: one for candidates in active screening, one for candidates who had passed medical clearance before the protocol update, and one for candidates who had been provisionally accepted. Harmon was in the third category. The third template was the most careful one. It was also the most inadequate.

She rewrote it.

The letter she sent explained the cryosleep compatibility protocol, the biological markers, the confidence intervals. It explained that incompatibility was a physiological finding rather than a reflection of qualification or merit. It explained the appeals process, which was real -- candidates with borderline results had a pathway to retest and to request review by the medical board -- and it explained what appeals had produced so far, which was honest information, which was: in the first 42 notifications, two candidates had successfully appealed based on borderline marker scores. Forty were confirmed incompatible.

It said that the selection team recognized the difficulty of receiving this information, and that counseling resources were available through the Framework's candidate support office.

It did not say: you have scored in the 92nd percentile and you are one of the best candidates we have reviewed and we cannot put you on the ship because your cellular metabolism will not survive forty years of cold sleep and there is nothing any of us can do about that.

Elena read the letter twice, made one change to the third paragraph, and sent it.

*

Kim Aldridge knocked on Elena's office door at eleven. She came in without being asked, which was a privilege she'd earned over three years of working in the same building with the kind of daily proximity that either became easy or broke entirely, and in their case had become easy.

"The Harmon notification went out," Elena said.

Kim sat down in the chair across the desk. She'd been running the cryosleep screening protocol for twelve months and had the specific look of someone who had processed a lot of difficult information and had developed the professional capacity to hold it without showing it while also not pretending it wasn't there. "How many are confirmed at this stage?"

"Forty-three confirmed incompatible in the first cohort. Sixty-one flagged for secondary review. The secondary review protocol runs another six weeks."

"That's above the midpoint of the original estimate."

"It is." Elena had done this math many times. The original estimate had been fourteen to twenty-two percent of the candidate pool. The first cohort was running at nineteen percent confirmed, with secondary review likely to push that to twenty-two or twenty-three. "The pool is large enough to absorb it. We have replacements moving through screening."

"The replacements are from a less screened initial pool."

"They're from the second selection cohort. The screening was applied from the beginning. Their cryosleep compatibility is better known at this stage than the first cohort's was."

Kim nodded. She was turning something over. Elena had learned to wait.

"I want to ask you something," Kim said.

"Go ahead."

"The candidates we're notifying. Harmon is one example. The people in this category -- compatibility issue, otherwise excellent candidates. What happens to them?"

"They're removed from the mission pool. The letter explains the appeals process."

"I mean what actually happens to them. If the deflection fails."

Elena looked at the window. Outside, the Houston morning was already bright and beginning to get warm, the particular light of a city that was warm earlier every year. "The same thing that happens to everyone not on the ship."

"Right." Kim looked at her hands. "I think about that a lot. I know we talked about it when we were setting up the protocol -- the compatibility issue in context of the alternative. But the abstract version and the specific version are different."

Elena understood this. She'd been living in the space between the abstract and the specific for four years. "We're not sending anyone to their death," she said. "We're telling them that the mission they applied for requires a biological characteristic they don't have. The deflection mission is the response to the threat. The ship is the hedge. We are building the best possible hedge."

"A hedge with twenty-two percent fewer of our best candidates than we selected."

"A hedge with four thousand viable candidates instead of four thousand candidates including some who won't survive the transit." Elena kept her voice level. "The alternative isn't better. A candidate who dies during the cryosleep period doesn't arrive. A ship that launches with eight hundred people who can't survive the journey is not a better hedge."

Kim was quiet for a moment. "I know," she said. "I know the logic. I keep knowing the logic."

"The logic is right."

"That's what makes it hard to stop thinking about." She stood up. "I'll have the secondary review preliminary results by Wednesday."

She left. Elena sat at her desk and looked at the letter queue.

Forty-three sent. Two hundred and twelve pending in the first cohort. She had a system for moving through them: twelve per day, each one reviewed, each one personalized at the third paragraph with whatever detail from the candidate's file made them specific rather than a template. It took an hour each morning. She had been doing it for eleven days and had not found a way to make it easier.

*

The candidate who called was not supposed to call.

The notification letters were clear about the contact protocol: responses and questions went to the candidate support office, which had a team and a process. The letter had Elena's office as the sender but it did not have a direct callback number. Somehow, a woman named Patricia Novak had found one.

Elena's assistant forwarded the call on a Friday afternoon with a note that said the woman had identified herself as Patricia Novak, candidate ID in the second round, current assignment as Commander of Luna Base. Elena had not reviewed Novak's file personally -- she was in the second cohort, earlier in the process -- but the Luna Base Commander's name was not one she needed to look up.

She took the call.

"Commander Novak," Elena said.

"Dr. Marsh." A pause. "I got the notification. I understand the protocol, and I know I'm supposed to use the support office. I'm not calling to appeal. I'm calling because I need thirty seconds of your time to ask one question."

Elena looked at the clock. She had four letters to finish. "Go ahead."

"Does this affect my Luna Base command."

A clean question. Elena had expected something different: a challenge to the screening methodology, a request to review her specific results, an emotional response to a notification she had clearly not expected. Instead, one direct question with no performance in it.

"No," Elena said. "Cryosleep compatibility is a mission crew criterion. Luna Base command is a separate command structure. Your position is not affected by this notification."

A pause on the other end. Elena waited.

"Good," Novak said. "That's what I needed to know." Another beat. "I know what the ship needs. I know what the mission needs. I've been building the thing for three years. I understand why I can't be on it."

Elena said nothing. There was nothing to add to that.

"The work continues," Novak said. "Same as before."

"Same as before," Elena said.

The call ended. Elena sat with the phone for a moment. Patricia Novak had spent three years building a ship she couldn't ride. She had called to ask one question, gotten a clean answer, and was done. The work continues. Elena wrote that in her notes.

She went back to the letter queue.

*

The twelve notifications for that day took an hour and twenty minutes. She worked through them in the order they appeared: a structural engineer from Vancouver, a physician from Nairobi, a linguist from Seoul, a former Navy pilot from Norfolk who had scored in the ninety-eighth percentile on every category except one. Each file had a name and a number and a photograph taken at the initial screening, and she looked at each photograph before she finalized each letter. It was not efficient. It was the minimum she owed them.

By the time she finished it was past six and the building was mostly empty. Her assistant had left a note on the door: Sofia called. Call her back when you can.

Elena called Sofia.

"How are you?" Sofia asked.

"Working."

"Still the notifications?"

Elena had told her daughter about the notifications in general terms, the way she'd told her about other aspects of the job: accurately, in the broad shape, without the specific files. "Still."

"How many?"

"We're through the first cohort. I'll finish the primary screening notifications by end of next week. Then secondary review."

Sofia was quiet for a moment. "Mom, can I ask you something."

"You can always ask."

"Do you think the deflection works?"

Elena looked at the window, the dark Houston evening on the other side of it. "I think there are good people working very hard on the targeting problem."

"That's not what I asked."

"I know." Elena put her feet on the desk, the particular informality she allowed herself only when she was alone in the office after hours. "I think fifty-four percent means the same as it always meant: it could go either way, and what we do in the next nine years will determine which way it goes. The deflection team is good. The precursor data will help."

"And if it doesn't help enough."

"Then four thousand people leave."

"And nine billion don't."

Elena had no answer to this that she hadn't given before, and Sofia knew all the answers she had, and they both knew it, so the silence that followed was not empty. It was two people sitting in the reality of a number that neither of them had made and couldn't change.

"Paul proposed," Sofia said.

Elena straightened up. "When?"

"Sunday. Dinner at that place on South Congress." A pause. "I said yes."

Elena sat with this for a moment. Her daughter was engaged. Her daughter was going to marry an environmental engineer named Paul who struggled with extended periods of limited social contact and who would, as far as Elena was concerned, spend his life in Drayfield or Austin or wherever the two of them made their life on a planet that she was helping four thousand people escape.

"I'm very happy," Elena said. And meant it entirely and also felt the particular vertigo of a person who holds two true things that don't fit comfortably together.

"I know," Sofia said. "I can hear it."

They talked for another twenty minutes. Elena asked about the ring, which Sofia described in the exact detail that people described rings to their mothers, and asked about Paul's family, and asked about the timeline they were considering. Normal questions. The questions a mother asked when her daughter called with good news.

After she hung up she sat in the office for a while in the quiet. The letter queue would be there in the morning. Two hundred and twelve notifications. One hundred and sixty-nine to go.

She was building a record. Each letter was a record that the process had been carried out with care. That the people on the wrong side of a biological threshold had been told directly, specifically, with the actual facts and the actual options. That they had not been a rounding error.

She closed the laptop and drove home.
