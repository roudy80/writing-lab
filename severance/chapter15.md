# Chapter 15: The Ring Closes

Maria Webb | Luna Base, Assembly Framework Observation Post Alpha

January 19, 2031 | 09:11 Luna Standard Time

The last section of Habitation Ring Two came in from the east on the back of a cargo hauler that had spent eleven days in transit from the L2 fabrication platform, and Maria watched it dock from Observation Post Alpha, which was a pressurized bubble of glass and aluminum bolted to the top of the maintenance tower at the base of the framework, and which had been her preferred place to be on mornings like this one.

The section was the size of a city block laid on its side, which was a comparison that didn't quite work because a city block had gravity and weight and permanence and this had none of those things in the conventional sense, but the scale was right. Forty-eight meters of curved aluminum-composite structure with the cooling channels already routed inside the wall and the interior framing in place and the connector flanges polished to the tolerance specs that the engineering team had spent three months arguing over. It came in on cold thrusters, patient and enormous, guided by the automated docking system and a team of six engineers in suits on the framework scaffolding who were there less to assist and more to bear witness, because the system was either going to work or it wasn't and human hands at this stage were observation rather than intervention.

It worked.

The flanges met. The connection indicators on Maria's wrist display cycled from amber to green in the sequence she'd been watching for. Somewhere out there in the vacuum a series of bolts that weighed less than a kilogram each were locking a structure that weighed eighty thousand metric tons into the permanent configuration it would hold for at least forty years and possibly forever, however long forever meant for a ship that was going to leave the solar system.

Habitation Ring Two was closed.

She stayed on the observation post for seventeen minutes after the docking team cleared the area. She had a briefing at ten and work to do before it, and she stayed for seventeen minutes anyway, which was the kind of decision she'd been making more frequently in the last year: choose the view. You could do the work later. The view only happened once.

* * *

The mission parameters had been locked in October.

Not the full mission, not the interstellar mission with its forty-year transit and its cold sleep and its arrival at a star that had only been confirmed as having planets nine years ago. That mission remained in the future in the way large permanent things remained in the future, certain in outline, specific in detail, still too large to hold all at once. What had been locked in October was the other mission: the asteroid deflection mission. The one that would happen first. The one that determined whether the interstellar mission was a hedge or a main event.

The parameters: a crew of six aboard the modification vessel Phoenix, launched in 2039, intercept in 2041, deflection operation over a period of four to six weeks, return transit depending on the operational outcome. Maria was the mission commander and had been officially designated as such for three months, which had been enough time for the designation to feel real rather than provisional.

She had spent those three months learning everything that was known about a rubble-pile asteroid designated 2024 XR19 by the catalog and called "the problem" or "the object" by the people who worked with it daily, because giving it a name made it easier to discuss and harder to think about clearly. The internal structure was the variable. The precursor mission, an unmanned survey vehicle called Compass, was en route and would arrive in 2033, and the structural data from Compass would determine the final targeting protocol for the kinetic impactors she was going to place.

She had read every paper on rubble-pile asteroid dynamics published in the last eighteen years. She had talked to the targeting team at JPL for a combined total of forty hours over three months. She had a very clear picture of what she was being asked to do and a very clear understanding of how many variables in that picture were still blank.

Novak had told her: I'm telling you because you should know what you're being asked to fly and what it can do.

She knew. She kept knowing, the way you kept knowing things that were too large to metabolize all at once.

* * *

The briefing was in the main conference room, which was a room with a long table and a wall-sized display currently showing a real-time rendering of the assembly framework from three angles. The rendering was Maria's doing, or rather the doing of an engineer named Brooks who had built it at her request after she'd gotten tired of the two-dimensional schematics that made the ship look like an engineering drawing rather than a thing that existed.

The thing existed. The rendering showed that clearly.

Six people around the table: Novak, Maria, Dr. Harrison from the technical advisory board who had come up from Earth for the week, and three engineers whose names Maria knew and whose expertise she trusted. The agenda was the habitation ring completion milestone and the timeline revisions that came with it.

"Ring Two closed this morning," Novak said. She said it the same way she said everything that was not a question: as a fact that required acknowledgment before proceeding. "Ring Three integration begins in April. If the fabrication platform stays on schedule, which it has, for the last two quarters, Ring Three closes in November. We enter 2032 with all three habitation rings complete and the drive assembly integration as the remaining major structural work."

"Drive assembly is the long pole," Harrison said. He was the kind of scientist who had stopped being primarily a scientist at some point and had become primarily a person who explained science to decision-makers, and he was very good at it. "The propulsion specifications are locked but the integration tolerances are tighter than anything we've built before. Eighteen months minimum, twenty-four months if there are fit issues."

"There will be fit issues," Maria said.

"There will be fit issues," Novak agreed without inflection. "We're planning for twenty months and hoping for seventeen. That puts final integration completion in mid-2033 at the optimistic end."

"Which leaves six years for outfitting, crew loading, and final systems integration before the impact window," Harrison said.

"Six years and two months," Maria said.

Harrison looked at her. "You've been counting."

"I've been counting since October."

The room absorbed this. Maria had made a decision in October, when the mission designation was confirmed, to stop doing the mental work of not counting. The timeline was what it was. There was margin and the margin was real, and the work to be done within the margin was defined, and knowing exactly how much margin existed was more useful than maintaining a professional detachment from the arithmetic.

Six years and two months to final systems integration. Then crew boarding, which was a three-month operation for four thousand people. Then departure. Then whatever came after departure, which was someone else's problem in the temporal sense and her problem in every other sense.

"The Compass mission," Maria said. "Any updates on transit status."

"On track," Harrison said. "Nominal trajectory. The first signals from the approach phase should reach us in mid-2033."

"Same window as final integration."

"Give or take."

Maria made a note. The asteroid's internal structure and the ship's completion would become known at approximately the same time. She found this either a coincidence or a piece of planning she hadn't been told about, and the operational timeline suggested the latter. Someone had aligned these arrivals deliberately, which meant someone had decided it was better to know the targeting requirements and the ship's readiness status at the same moment rather than sequentially. She agreed with that decision and made a note to find out who had made it.

* * *

After the briefing she went to the pilot simulator for three hours.

The simulator was the closest thing on Luna Base to the interior of the Phoenix, which itself didn't exist yet except as a set of components in various stages of fabrication on Earth and at the L2 platform. What existed was a pressure vessel approximately the right diameter fitted with the command interface design the Phoenix would use, and Maria had been in it for an average of nineteen hours per week for three months.

The simulation today was approach and parking orbit insertion at a rubble-pile object of specified mass and cohesion. She had run the same simulation forty-one times. Each run produced different variables within the specified parameter range, and each run required her to make decisions that would, in a real approach, have consequences for the operation. The simulation was designed to fail in new ways. Today it failed in a way she'd seen before, a navigation error in the parking orbit that produced an uncontrolled drift toward the object's surface, and she managed it the same way she'd managed it the last time it appeared, which was methodically and in the right order, and she got the vehicle back to stable orbit with six minutes of simulation time remaining.

She stayed in the simulator after the run ended. The display showed the post-run analysis, the decision tree rendered as a diagram with her choices marked and the alternatives calculated. She'd made sixteen decisions in the run. Thirteen were rated optimal. Two were rated acceptable. One was flagged for review.

She looked at the flagged decision. She'd seen the flag before on other runs, a choice to prioritize station-keeping over instrument calibration in a specific two-minute window. The simulation's evaluation model considered the other choice optimal. Maria consistently chose the station-keeping. She'd been thinking about why for two months.

The answer she kept arriving at was: because the simulation's model assumes the instrument calibration matters more than the station-keeping in the specified window, and I think the model is wrong, and I think the margin for error on station-keeping in approach proximity is smaller than the model accounts for, and I would rather lose two minutes of calibration data than risk a drift event.

She wrote this down in the notes section of her run log.

It was possible she was wrong. It was possible the simulation's model was right and she was introducing a systematic error into her approach protocol. This was the problem with simulations: they told you what their model preferred, not what reality required. She was going to run a variant tomorrow in which she took the model's preferred choice and see what happened. She'd done this ten times before with this specific decision point and the model's choice had produced worse outcomes six times, better outcomes twice, and equivalent outcomes twice.

She logged the run, powered down the simulator, and went to the cafeteria.

Novak was at a corner table with a plate of food and a stack of reports. Maria took her tray and sat down across from her.

"The simulation flag," Maria said.

Novak didn't look up. "The station-keeping choice."

"You've been reviewing my logs."

"I review all the mission commander logs." She turned a page. "I agree with your choice."

Maria ate. The cafeteria was half full at this hour, the lunch crowd thinning toward the afternoon shift changeover, the low hum of four hundred people's daily routine going about its business. A base that had housed forty people when it was built in 2021 now housed four hundred and eleven, with another ring of habitat modules in planning that would bring it to six hundred and fifty by 2034. The base had grown the way the ship had grown: one section at a time, each one adding mass and permanence, until the thing it was building toward was no longer a concept but a fact.

"The Ring Two closure this morning," Maria said.

"I saw." Novak set down her report. She looked at Maria with the direct assessment that was her default mode of attention. "You were on the observation post."

"Seventeen minutes."

Novak was quiet for a moment. Then: "When I was running submarine operations, I had a commander who told me that every time you watch something become permanent, you should actually watch it. Not check the indicators and move on. Watch it."

"Sound advice."

"He died in 2022. Heart. Never saw any of this." She picked up her fork. "I think about that sometimes."

Maria thought about the ring closing, the flanges meeting, the green indicators in sequence. The thing that had become permanent this morning would still be permanent when Novak was dead and when Maria was dead and when whoever eventually piloted this ship to another star had done so and whatever came after that had come after that. The permanence operated on a scale that was difficult to hold in the mind.

"I'm going to get us to that asteroid," Maria said.

Novak looked at her.

"I know the probability. I know what the precursor data might show. I'm going to get us there and I'm going to place those impactors and we're going to move that rock." She said it the way she said things she'd finished deciding: without emphasis, as information. "I want you to know that."

Novak was quiet for a moment. "I know," she said.

They ate in the cafeteria with three hundred and ninety-eight other people who were building the same thing for the same reason and who were not going to stop.
