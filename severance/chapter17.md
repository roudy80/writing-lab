# Chapter 17: The Limits of the Record

Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston

October 8, 2032 | 6:15 PM EST

The federal civil complaint reached its final disposition in August, which meant that Thomas had spent seven years building a record and had arrived at the record's end: two partial settlements, one denial upheld on appeal, three agencies under the monitoring office's revised oversight framework, and a legal document that was approximately nine hundred pages long and would be cited in subsequent litigation in Dallas and Detroit and Portland and three other cities where the same pattern had produced the same outcome and the same community organizations had done the same work and arrived at the same partial, honest, inadequate result.

He had expected this. He had filed the original appeals anyway, and had supported the pro-bono team at Northeastern, and had provided documentation through two depositions and four follow-up requests, because the record was worth building even if the record's end was partial. The monitoring office was real. The timeline revisions were real. The nine hundred pages were real.

He told the Thursday session in September. Forty-eight people by then, the waiting list cleared, the room at capacity in a way that required them to arrange chairs along the walls. He described the final disposition in the same terms he described everything: what it was, what it wasn't, what came next.

Dmitri had asked: what comes next?

Thomas had said: the monitoring office. The appeals that are still active in other cities. The political conversation that is going to have to happen eventually about the selection process and the four thousand.

The room had been quiet in the specific way it was quiet when something had been said that everyone was thinking and no one had wanted to say first.

* * *

The Compass mission was eighteen months from arrival.

Thomas followed it the way he followed everything related to the asteroid and the deflection probability: in the early mornings before the workday started, reading the technical summaries published by ARIA and the analysis pieces published by the research institutions that had been granted access to the mission telemetry. Compass was traveling at the edge of the communications window, its signals taking four minutes to reach Earth, and the mission team at JPL published weekly status updates that were precise and brief and always included the same last line: nominal operations, all systems within parameters.

He had started reading those updates in 2031 when Compass launched. Eighteen months of nominal operations, all systems within parameters. He found the regularity of it reassuring in the specific way that regular reporting was reassuring: it meant nothing had gone wrong yet, which was the condition required for something to eventually go right.

The probability had not moved.

Fifty-four percent, published in November 2029, confirmed in the 2030 and 2031 annual assessments. The 2032 assessment was due in December and the technical journalists who covered the story had been cautious in their speculation, which typically meant they'd been briefed on something they weren't publishing yet. Thomas had read four pieces in September that described the targeting problem in more detail than previous coverage had, which typically meant the people being interviewed were preparing the ground for an announcement.

He didn't know what the announcement would be. He'd stopped trying to know things before they were knowable three years ago, when Ben Oakes's private briefing had become public knowledge and Thomas had understood that the gap between what he knew in advance and what he could do with that knowledge was not smaller than the gap between what he knew publicly and what he could do. The knowing-early hadn't helped. The working-steadily had.

He drove to the community center on a Tuesday evening for the October session. The last leaves were coming off the oaks on Lamartine Street, the particular October quality of the light that Boston produced, the city going amber and cold. He unlocked the center, turned on the lights, set up the chairs. Thirty-eight chairs for the Thursday session, three additional along the wall.

He stood in the empty room for a moment.

The chairs were set up. The room was the same room it had been for six years. The same fold-out tables, the same folding chairs with the scratch on the third-row left that had been there since 2026, the same whiteboard with the same set of markers that the same man from the Dorchester group always capped wrong. The room had hosted three hundred and thirty-eight sessions. Thomas had kept count in a spreadsheet the way he kept count of everything that mattered.

The keeping count was not nostalgia. It was the habit of a man who believed that the record had value independent of the outcome, and that the value accumulated, and that you could not know in advance which specific entry in the record would matter to someone for whom it became the reference point they needed. The nine hundred pages of the federal complaint had already been cited four times. Someone was going to cite those three hundred and thirty-eight sessions at some point. He didn't know for what.

Dmitri came in at 6:30, half an hour early, which was Dmitri's signal that he had something to say before the session.

He sat down in the second row. He'd been attending the Thursday sessions for six years and had moved, over those six years, from a man who asked hard questions to a man who was helping generate the questions others would ask. He ran the planning board subgroup. He had been elected to the neighborhood council in 2031 and had been reelected in 2033. He was the kind of person the community sessions produced when they worked, which was the outcome Thomas had never been able to fully plan for but had learned to recognize when it appeared.

"I've been talking to the monitoring office staff contact," Dmitri said.

"I know. I've been following the exchanges."

"They're not enforcing the timeline revision on the East Boston development review. The agency is sixty days past the required consultation period and the monitoring office sent one letter."

"I saw the letter."

"One letter in sixty days. The monitoring office has enforcement authority. They have it in the charter. They're not using it."

Thomas sat down across from him. "What are you going to do about it."

Dmitri looked at him. This was the shift in their relationship, the one that had been developing for two years: Thomas asking Dmitri what he was going to do about it rather than answering the question himself.

"I'm going to take it to the city council in November. Not the neighborhood council — the city council. The monitoring office charter has a city council notification provision that's never been used. I want to use it."

"That makes the monitoring office a city-level political issue."

"It should be a city-level political issue. It was created in response to a federal process failure. The city had standing in that process and they've been treating the monitoring office as someone else's accountability structure."

Thomas thought about this. Dmitri was right about the notification provision. He was also right about the political dimension. Elevating the monitoring office to city council consideration would produce coverage and attention and pressure that the monitoring office staff might or might not welcome, and which the agencies it monitored would definitely not welcome.

"The agencies will push back hard," Thomas said.

"I know."

"The monitoring office staff might push back too. They're federal appointees. City council pressure on a federal structure is a complicated jurisdictional play."

"I know that too." Dmitri had the quality Thomas had always valued in him: he had thought it through before he spoke. "The alternative is an enforcement mechanism that doesn't enforce. Which is no mechanism at all."

Thomas looked at the chairs. Thirty-eight chairs and three along the wall. Six years of sessions and nine hundred pages and a monitoring office that was sending one letter in sixty days.

"The record we've been building," Thomas said. "The complaint, the settlements, the monitoring office, the documentation. You understand that the record has value independent of whether it changes what the agencies do in the next six months."

"I understand that."

"And you also think that the record isn't enough by itself."

"I think the record was the right tool for the right problem," Dmitri said. "The problem has evolved."

Thomas sat with this for a moment. He had been thinking about this for two years and had not said it, because saying it was a commitment to a different kind of work, and he had been careful, over six years, to do only the work he was sure he could do well.

"After November," he said. "When you take it to city council. Come back and tell me what you need."

Dmitri nodded. He understood what Thomas was offering.

* * *

The session started at eight. Thirty-nine people. Three new faces. The usual work: monitoring office status, active appeals, the two cases in other cities where Thomas's documentation had been referenced by local advocacy teams. At nine o'clock, after the operational items, Thomas opened the floor.

"The Compass mission reaches arrival in early 2034," he said. "The structural data from that mission will either improve the deflection probability or complicate it. We're going to know more by the middle of next year than we've known at any point since 2025."

The room listened.

"The thing I want to name for this group is that more information is better than less, and whatever the information shows, it doesn't change the work in front of us. The deflection mission is either going to succeed or it isn't. The ship is either going to launch or it isn't. In either case, the record we've built here matters. In the case where the ship launches, it matters because the people who get on it will make decisions that affect all of us, and those decisions should happen in a context where our documentation exists and is cited and is part of the record of what this period cost the people who lived through it."

He paused.

"In the case where the deflection succeeds and the ship doesn't launch, the record we've built is the record of a community that refused to be erased during a crisis that tried to erase it. That has value regardless of what comes next."

The room was quiet.

"In the case where neither happens," he said, and stopped.

He'd never finished that sentence in a session before. He'd always stopped before it. He stopped again.

"We keep working," said a woman in the back whose name was Rosa and who had been attending for three years and who said things in four words that he spent eight sentences arriving at. "Same as before."

"Same as before," Thomas said.

The session ended. He locked up. He walked to his car. He sat in it for a while, the October cold coming in, the Lamartine Street trees bare now, the streetlight making pools of orange on the wet pavement.

He thought about Compass, traveling through the dark toward a rubble pile that would either be moved or not be moved, carrying instruments that would tell the targeting team what they needed to know and possibly what they didn't want to know.

He thought about the nine hundred pages.

He thought about Dmitri and the city council and the work that was still in front of them, the different kind of work.

He started the car and drove home. He had a session to prepare for Thursday and a monitoring office timeline to document and a call to make to the Dorchester group about their parallel complaint. The record wasn't finished. It was just in a new chapter.

He went inside and opened the laptop and started working on it.
