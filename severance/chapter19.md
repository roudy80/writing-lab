# Chapter 19: The Record and the Room

Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston

March 4, 2034 | 8:30 AM EST

The city council hearing was scheduled for nine AM and Thomas had been awake since four, which was not unusual. What was unusual was that he had nothing left to prepare.

The binder on his kitchen table was complete. The documentation was organized. The testimony he'd agreed to give was three pages, double-spaced, and he had read it so many times that he could have delivered it from memory in the dark. He had revised it eleven times since January, which was one more than he'd counted when he started keeping track. The eleventh revision had been this morning at four-thirty, in the kitchen, with the city still dark outside and the radiator doing what the radiator did in March, which was make noise while achieving only partial warmth.

He made coffee and stood at the window and watched Jamaica Plain come awake in the cold. A delivery truck blocking half the street while the driver moved boxes with the efficiency of someone who'd memorized which building got what. A woman in a yellow coat walking a large dog that was clearly not interested in walking, planting its feet every third block in a negotiation it was losing. The bodega on the corner putting out its sidewalk display, the owner moving with the unhurried precision of someone who had done exactly this every morning for twenty years and expected to do it for twenty more.

Normal life. The particular insistence of it.

He'd been thinking about the hearing for three months, since Dmitri had called in November to say it was happening. The Committee on Housing and Reconstruction had agreed to hold a formal session on the monitoring office's enforcement record — a thing that had not happened before, that Thomas had not entirely believed would happen until Dmitri sent the official notice with the council letterhead and the date.

It had taken Dmitri two years to get here from that November conversation when he'd said, in the community center, that he was going to take it to the city council. Two years of working the notification provision in the monitoring office charter. Two years of building relationships with three council members whose districts overlapped with the outer ring neighborhoods. Two years of attending planning board meetings and housing committee sessions and neighborhood forums with the same patient presence that Thomas had modeled, without ever quite teaching it, by doing it himself for the six years before Dmitri started.

Thomas had not been in the room for most of those two years. He'd been available — had answered questions, reviewed draft testimony, suggested sources. But the work had been Dmitri's, and Nina's, and Warren's, and Rosa's, and the dozen other people who had built the Jamaica Plain sessions into something that now had three satellite sessions in Roxbury and Dorchester and East Boston, each with its own facilitators, each running with a methodology that was recognizable as the Thursday sessions but that had grown past what Thomas could have designed alone.

This was the outcome he'd been working toward and had never quite been able to plan for. He was not sure what to do with it, which was a feeling he associated with things going right.

* * *

Dmitri picked him up at eight-fifteen. They drove to City Hall in Dmitri's car, which was newer than Thomas's and had working heat, and they talked about the testimony in the specific way you talked about a thing you'd both already finished thinking about: checking the edges, not the center.

"The enforcement gap," Dmitri said. "You're going with the East Boston numbers?"

"The East Boston development review and the Roxbury consultation period. Both documented. Both with the monitoring office's own correspondence in the record."

"They'll say staffing."

"They'll say staffing." Thomas had thought about this response for a month. "I'll say the charter doesn't have a staffing exception for the consultation requirement. The staffing argument is real — they genuinely don't have enough people, and that's not a fabrication. But I want to be precise about the difference between an explanation and an excuse. An explanation says why something happened. An excuse says it therefore doesn't matter. The communities waiting on those consultations weren't waiting any less because the monitoring office was understaffed."

Dmitri nodded. He drove. Outside, the highway fed into surface streets, the approach to the government district, the old buildings alongside the newer ones that had gone up in the years when the reconstruction funding was still moving.

"Ben Oakes," Dmitri said.

Thomas looked at him.

"You haven't mentioned him in four months."

"He's gone quiet. Since the Compass data landed."

"That's not a good sign."

"It might not mean what it looks like." Thomas turned back to the window. The city moved past. "Ben goes quiet when the work is at a stage he can't discuss. The Compass data gave the targeting team a precise structural map of what they're trying to move. Precise map means precise problem. When you've been working with a range of possible scenarios and you suddenly have the actual data, you stop being able to talk generally. You have to work specifically."

"What does that mean for the probability."

Thomas thought about how to say this accurately. "The official figure is forty-three percent. I think the real figure is lower. I think Ben knows something about the targeting problem that he can't tell me right now, and I think that's why he's gone quiet rather than calling with nothing to say." He said it the way he said things he'd decided needed to be said. "The Phoenix mission is real. Maria Webb is the commander. The mission launches in five years. The question of whether it succeeds is a different question from whether the mission is happening."

"Does the community need to know that."

"The community needs to know what I know. I've been giving them that for nine years." He paused. "Not today. Today is the monitoring office. But the next session after today, I want to talk about how we engage the probability question. Forty-three percent is public. People are doing the math. I'd rather we be the place where they do the math out loud than the place where they're afraid to."

They parked and walked to the building. The hearing room was already half-full.

* * *

Thomas had testified in depositions and in federal proceedings. A city council hearing was smaller and less formal, which made it more human, which made it harder in a specific way the federal proceedings hadn't been. He could see the council members' faces clearly. He could see the people in the gallery — community members, some of whom he recognized, some he didn't, a few with children drawing in notebooks or looking at phones with the studied indifference that meant they were paying close attention.

He gave the testimony.

Three pages, double-spaced. He looked up from the paper more than he looked at it, because the testimony was addressed to the council but it was for the room, for the people who had been waiting nine years for this to be said in a place with official letterhead and a public record.

The enforcement gap was real. He gave the numbers. East Boston consultation period: sixty-two days past the required window, one letter sent. Roxbury development review: four months of non-compliance, two letters, no escalation to the inspector general as the charter required after ninety days. The escalation provision had never been used. Not once, in four years of the monitoring office's operation.

He said: the monitoring office was created because a federal process failed the communities most affected by the Boston impact. It was created because those communities needed an accountability structure that would hold federal agencies to their own timelines. If the monitoring office itself cannot be held to its own enforcement obligations, we have replaced one accountability failure with another, and the communities that were promised relief have received paperwork instead.

He said: I am not asking for the monitoring office to be defunded. I am asking for it to be funded adequately and required to enforce. Those are different arguments and I want to be clear which one I'm making.

He said: the record exists. Nine years of documentation, nine hundred pages of federal complaint, two partial settlements, a monitoring office that was supposed to be the permanent institutional response to what those settlements couldn't fix. What we're asking today is whether the city intends to make the record matter.

He sat down.

The questions were what he'd expected: some hostile, some genuine, some aimed at the monitoring office staff who were in the room. Dmitri gave a separate testimony that was more direct about the specific correspondence dates. Nina sent a written statement that was read into the record. Rosa was in the gallery and said nothing and was one of the most important people in the room.

By noon it was over. The committee chair said they would issue a formal report within sixty days. Thomas noted the sixty days in the same mental file where he'd been noting response windows for nine years.

* * *

They went to a diner near City Hall. Dmitri ordered the soup. Thomas ordered coffee and something he ate without tasting. They sat with the morning settling around them.

"That was something," Dmitri said.

"It was a hearing."

"It was the right room. Finally in the right room."

Thomas thought about this. The right room. He'd been thinking about rooms for nine years — which rooms the work could reach and which it couldn't. The federal complaint had reached the rooms where lawyers worked. The community sessions had reached the rooms where neighbors worked. Today had reached the room where the city officially examined itself.

Each room was different. Each room had produced something different. The question that followed him was whether the rooms added up to something, or whether they were just rooms.

"What's next for you," he asked. He meant it directly.

Dmitri looked at him with the look of someone who had been waiting for the question to be asked naturally rather than forced. "Council seat. 2036."

Thomas had known this was coming. Had sensed it forming for a year. Still felt the slight internal recalibration when a thing you'd anticipated arrived. "Which district."

"Ours." Dmitri wrapped both hands around his coffee. He was fifty-three now, had been in Boston since 1998, had built his life around a city that had been half-destroyed and was still rebuilding. "The incumbent is retiring. There's a field forming. I've been asked."

"By whom."

"The neighborhood council. Three of the community members whose families I've worked with for four years. One of the council members whose office has been our best contact on the enforcement question." He looked at Thomas steadily. "I want your honest read."

Thomas considered what he actually thought, which required separating it from what he would have assumed he'd think before he understood what Dmitri had become in the last four years. "I think you're the right person for it. I think the communities that need representation in that room most are the people who've been in our sessions — and you came from where they came from, which is different from representing them, and matters." He paused. "I also think the work will change when you're inside the room instead of outside it. There are things the inside position allows that the outside doesn't, and things it prevents."

"And you've always been outside the room."

"By choice. Because it's where I was most useful and because I knew it." Thomas looked at his coffee. "You're going to be more useful inside. You've been building toward it for four years. The organizing work, the relationship work, the enforcement documentation — that's the credential for the room you're trying to get into."

Dmitri nodded slowly. He looked out the diner window at the City Hall building. "Will you help."

"Yes. Differently than before. But yes."

They paid. They walked out into the March cold and stood for a moment on the sidewalk before going to separate cars.

"Ben will call when he can," Dmitri said.

"He will."

"And until then."

"Until then we do what we've always done." Thomas looked at the building, at the street, at the flat gray sky over the city. "The record isn't finished. It's just in a new room."

* * *

He drove back to Jamaica Plain. He had a Thursday session to prepare and a monitoring office timeline to update and a call to make to the East Boston group about the hearing transcript. The work didn't stop because the morning had been significant. He had learned this a long time ago and was still learning the depth of it.

He parked outside the community center and sat in the car for a moment. The March cold was coming through the glass. The street was quiet at this hour. The bodega owner was still out, had moved the sidewalk display inside for the midday lull and would put it back out in two hours, as he did every day.

Normal life. Its insistence.

Somewhere above the flat gray sky, following the mathematics it had been following since before this city existed, a 340-meter object continued its long fall toward a specific date in October. And on the moon, invisible in daylight, a ship was being built. And in Houston, Elena Marsh was assessing candidates for the seats on that ship. And in Geneva, Sarah Grant was holding a coalition together across languages and competing interests and the specific political friction of people who agreed on the goal and disagreed on everything else. And somewhere in JPL, Ben Oakes was working a targeting problem he couldn't talk about yet.

Thomas got out of the car. He unlocked the community center. He went inside and started setting up the chairs.

He set up twenty-eight of them, which was how many the room held comfortably, and then he pulled out two more because there had been thirty-one at the last session and he was not going to be the reason someone couldn't sit down.
