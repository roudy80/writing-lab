# Chapter 20: The Number They Expected

Senator Sarah Grant | Washington, D.C. / Geneva

April 17–21, 2034

The revised probability figure dropped at midnight, and Sarah had been awake for it.

Not because she'd known exactly when ARIA would post the update, the agency had been characteristically precise about not specifying a release window, which was itself a form of communication to people who knew how to read it, but because she'd been awake anyway, working through the Framework's legal exposure on the cryosleep redesign question, and the notification had arrived on her phone while she was reading a structural engineering brief she only partly understood.

Forty-three percent.

Down from forty-six, which had itself been down from fifty-four, which had been down from sixty-one, which had been the number that had started everything: the emergency treaty sessions, the OMB re-allocations, the Luna construction authority, the selection process, all the rest. Nine years of institutional response to a single probability estimate, and the estimate kept moving in one direction.

She read the ARIA release three times. It was careful, as ARIA releases always were: thorough in explaining what the Compass data had revealed about the asteroid's internal mass distribution, honest about the narrowed targeting window, precise about what forty-three percent represented and what it didn't. It did not say: the mission all of this was built around is now more likely to fail than succeed. It said: the current estimate reflects the best available data and the targeting team continues to work toward solutions that may improve this figure before the Phoenix launch window.

Sarah set her phone down and looked at the ceiling of her Washington apartment and thought about forty-three percent for ninety seconds. Then she got up, made coffee, and began drafting the day.

* * *

Paul Hartley was in her office at eight with his tablet and the posture he'd developed over five years of managing political crises, not alarmed, not calm, but precisely between them where the useful work happened.

"European delegations want an emergency Framework session," he said. "I've had messages from Lindqvist's office since three AM. The Brazilians are drafting a formal inquiry. The Indians have gone quiet."

"The Indian silence is worse than the Brazilian drafting," Sarah said.

"Usually. The Chinese response is expected in twelve hours, science ministry is reviewing the data first, which is actually a good sign. They don't review before responding when they've already decided."

"Good. What's the press saying."

Paul glanced at his screen. "Mixed accuracy. The number is being reported as a probability rather than a certainty, which is better than 2025. The commentary is another matter. Three major op-eds arguing the probability drop proves the Luna program was the wrong investment, that resources should have gone to Earth resilience. Two arguing it proves we need to spend more on the ship. One arguing forty-three percent is still better odds than most major geopolitical decisions and the panic is disproportionate."

"I agree with the last one. It will be read least."

"The French delegation," Paul said, "is going to table a motion to pause the cryosleep development program pending probability reassessment. They've been looking for a procedural lever for eight months."

"They know the cryosleep program is already in a redesign cycle."

"They know. The motion isn't about the cryosleep program. It's about whether the Framework can hold together when the primary justification for it keeps moving in the wrong direction." Paul had the quality she valued most in her staff: he said the thing that needed saying without performing the difficulty of saying it. "The motion is a signal. It's asking who else in the room has also been looking for a procedural lever."

Sarah stood and walked to the window. Washington assembling itself in the morning, the specific energy of a city that ran on decisions. "Don't let the emergency session be called without me controlling the agenda. Thirty-six hours."

"I'll tell Lindqvist."

"Tell him also that a motion to pause any component of the program in response to a probability shift is the most expensive procedural mistake the Framework could make right now. Tell him I'll make that case publicly if I have to."

"He knows you will."

"Good. Then he knows what the motion costs before he tables it."

* * *

She spent the four days before the Geneva session working the coalition the way she'd been working it since 2026: not by persuading people to believe things they didn't believe, but by finding the outcome that let each party do what they actually needed to do.

The work was different every cycle. The coalition was the same people, but the circumstances had shifted, the probability down, the program longer in operation, the political costs of both commitment and withdrawal different than they'd been a year ago. What she was managing was not a static alliance but a living arrangement, and living arrangements required constant tending.

She talked to fourteen delegations in four days. Some calls were five minutes. The Brazilians required two hours across three conversations because their equity concern, who was in the selection pool relative to their national population, was a genuine concern that deserved a genuine response, not a political management response. She gave them a commitment to expedited review of the nationality distribution formula, which was something she had the authority to give and that addressed what they actually needed.

The Indians were quiet because they were running their own structural analysis of the asteroid data and wanted to know if their figures matched ARIA's before they said anything publicly. She arranged a direct technical exchange between their science ministry and the JPL team. When their numbers came back consistent with ARIA's, the Indians came into the session already decided. That was the work: removing the reasons for uncertainty before the room assembled.

The Chinese were working the cost formula. An extended timeline meant extended cost, and the distribution formula hadn't been designed for extension. She spent four hours on this with their diplomatic team, which was four hours of careful arithmetic and careful language, and arrived at a revised formula that was workable for both sides without giving away what she couldn't give away.

Lindqvist called the night before the session.

"The French motion," he said.

"I know about it."

"They have four committed votes for tabling it. If it reaches the floor, the procedural discussion alone costs us a day. And a day creates space for other delegations to position."

"I know that too." Sarah looked at the draft agenda on her laptop. "The motion is going to be tabled. I'm not going to stop them from tabling it. What I'm going to do is make sure it fails quickly and cleanly, so that the failure is the signal rather than the process."

"How."

"By making the case before the motion, not in response to it. I want thirty minutes at the top of day two, before the floor opens. Uninterrupted." She paused. "Can you give me that."

A pause on his end, Lindqvist calculating. He had been the Framework's co-architect from the beginning, had more invested in its survival than anyone except Sarah, and understood that his ability to give her thirty minutes on day two was a function of his relationships with eight other delegation leads. He was calculating those relationships. "Yes," he said. "You have thirty minutes."

"That's all I need."

* * *

The session ran three days in Geneva. Day one was technical testimony: Kim Aldridge remote, Novak in person, the engineering leads who had designed the cascade modification explaining, with precision and without defensiveness, why a correctly-designed modification had produced an interaction they hadn't modeled. Sarah watched the delegations during this testimony. She was less interested in the content, she knew the content, than in what the content produced on the faces in the room.

What she saw was: people who had been looking for a reason to doubt were not finding the testimony reassuring, because the testimony was honest about the failure, and honesty about failure reads as evidence of more failure. People who had been committed were reading the same honesty as evidence of a program mature enough to find its own problems. The room was doing what rooms always did: sorting itself along lines that were already there.

Day two, thirty minutes before the floor opened.

She stood at the front of the room and said the thing she had been saying in various forms for nine years, this time without qualification.

She said: the forty-three percent figure is a data point in an ongoing calculation. It represents our best current understanding of a complex physical system. It is not a verdict.

She said: the testing process found a problem at small scale that would have been catastrophic at full scale. This is the definition of a working safety system. The alternative is finding the problem with four thousand people in suspension during a forty-year transit, and we are not in a position to find the problem there.

She said: every delegation in this room made a commitment based on the understanding that this program would encounter problems and solve them. We have encountered a problem. We are solving it. The question before this session is whether we honor the commitment we made, or whether forty-three percent is the number we decided in advance would be enough to abandon it.

She looked at the room. She let the silence be silence.

"The French motion," she said, "will be tabled. I'm not going to argue that it shouldn't be tabled. I am going to argue that the consequences of its passage are ones this coalition will spend the next three years recovering from, because the recovery cost of abandoning a program at this stage is higher than the cost of continuing it. I've done the arithmetic. I have it available for any delegation that wants to review it."

The motion was tabled that afternoon. It received four votes in favor, eight against, with one abstention. The discussion lasted eleven minutes.

The session ran through day three. The communiqué was three pages. The Framework reaffirmed its commitment to the program, directed quarterly targeting updates from JPL, and established joint monitoring on the cryosleep redesign timeline. Sarah had drafted the communiqué before the session opened.

* * *

Lindqvist found her in the corridor after the final signature.

"You held it," he said.

"We held it."

He handed her a glass of wine from somewhere. He was good at that, producing the right thing at the right moment, which was one of the qualities that had made him effective for thirty years of multilateral work. They stood by the window that looked out at the lake.

"Forty-three percent," he said. "Do you believe the JPL team can improve it before Phoenix launches?"

Sarah thought about what she actually believed. She had been separating what she believed from what she needed to communicate for nine years, and the separation had become reflex. This was the moment to let them be the same thing.

"I believe the targeting team has more precise data than they've ever had and is working with more precision than they've ever been able to work with," she said. "Whether that translates to a better number depends on the asteroid's internal structure in ways that are beyond anyone's control." She paused. "What I actually believe is that forty-three percent is not the final number. I don't know if the revision is up or down. I think the team is competent enough to find out, and I think they're going to tell us before Phoenix launches."

"And if the revision is down."

"Then we have a harder conversation than this one. And we have a ship."

Lindqvist looked at the lake. The water was doing what the lake always did: holding the sky and not caring about anything reflected in it. "You've been saying that for nine years. 'We have a ship.'"

"Because it keeps being true."

"Does it ever stop feeling like enough?"

She thought about this honestly. "It stopped feeling like enough sometime around 2028. What it feels like now is: it's what we have. And what we have is more than we had in 2025, which was nothing. So I stay with it."

He nodded slowly. He had the quality of someone who understood that answer completely and found it both insufficient and correct, which was how she felt about it herself.

She flew home the next morning. Eleven hours. She spent nine of them working, the quarterly Senate report, the appropriations preliminary, the equity review commitment to the Brazilians, and two of them looking out the window at the Atlantic below, at the water moving with the patient enormity of something that had been doing this since before anything that would be affected by what was coming.

Forty-three percent.

She had held the coalition together. The work continued.

She opened her laptop and started on the Senate report, because the work in front of her was the only work that mattered.
