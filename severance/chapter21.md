# Chapter 21: What the Map Shows

Maria Webb | Luna Base, Phoenix Mission Operations

February 3, 2035 | 11:40 Luna Standard Time

The internal structure map of 2024 XR19 was forty-eight gigabytes of processed telemetry and it had been sitting in Maria's classified briefing folder for six days before she had four consecutive unscheduled hours to read it properly.

She read it in the mission operations room with the door closed. The room had been storage before the Phoenix designation made it politically justifiable to claim, and it still smelled faintly of lubricant and the mineral dust of lunar regolith that worked its way into everything on the base regardless of seal quality. She had a desk, a good display, and three chairs she never used. It was the right size for the work — large enough to think in, small enough to belong to the thinking.

The map was remarkable. That was the first thing she thought: that the Compass mission had produced something genuinely remarkable. A three-dimensional density model of a 340-meter rubble pile generated from transit-phase radar tomography and spectrographic analysis, at a resolution the mission planners had called optimistic when they'd set the parameters and that had turned out to be achievable. The false-color density gradient ran from deep blue at the surface through green and yellow toward orange at the core concentrations, with three distinct high-density regions rendered in red that the analysis team had labeled M1, M2, and M3.

M1, M2, and M3 were the problem.

She read the summary first, then the methodology, then the data, then the conclusions, then back to the summary to see if it said the same thing it had said before she knew the data. The summary was honest. The three mass concentrations were distributed asymmetrically within the core, offset from the geometric center by a margin that the original targeting models had assumed would be smaller. The distribution wasn't catastrophic. It wasn't fragmentation-risk different. But it had shifted the optimal impact vector — the angle and velocity that would produce maximum momentum transfer without triggering disaggregation — from the parameters that JPL had been planning against for three years.

The targeting team had been working the revised vector for four months. Three solutions in the appendix, all viable, all within the operational parameters of the Phoenix mission as designed. None with the margin the original vector had carried.

She counted the word margin eighteen times in the main document.

She pulled up the simulation environment and loaded the revised targeting parameters.

* * *

She ran nine simulations before she stopped. Each used a different combination of the three viable solutions and parameter variations she'd developed from her understanding of the approach geometry. All nine produced successful deflection outcomes within the mission's success criteria. The variability was in the margin: how much error in impactor placement before the outcome fell below threshold.

Original pre-Compass model: plus or minus four kilometers on impactor placement. Wide enough to hit half-awake with a navigation fault running.

Revised models, using actual internal structure data: the best of the three appendix solutions gave her plus or minus 2.3 kilometers. The most conservative gave 1.8.

She sat with 1.8 for a while.

It was achievable. She had placed payloads to tighter tolerances in training and in real approaches. The Phoenix navigation system was designed for precision that exceeded what the original mission required. 1.8 kilometers was tight, but it was within what she and her crew could execute.

What it was not was comfortable. And she had learned, in twenty years of flying, that the distance between achievable and comfortable was exactly the distance you needed to understand before you climbed into the vehicle.

She made a note in her mission log: Request update on navigation system calibration specs for revised vector approaches. Specifically CEP at revised intercept range under asymmetric approach angles that M1/M2/M3 distribution requires. This affects simulator configuration and needs to be in the training record before April certification review.

She sent it and closed the briefing folder.

* * *

She told them on a Thursday.

The crew of six in the mission operations room with the door closed: Cole at the navigation station, Harrison and Anand along the side wall, Reyes and Stone together the way they were always together after two years of training as a paired team, Priya Anand standing because she almost always stood in briefings, which Maria had noticed in the first month and had stopped remarking on because it was simply how Anand thought.

Maria walked them through the Compass data the same way she'd walked through it herself. Summary, methodology, map, numbers. No editorial until the numbers were on the table. She had learned that telling people how to feel before they'd seen the data was a way of managing their response rather than informing it, and she needed their actual responses, not managed ones.

The map came up on the main display. M1, M2, M3 in red, the asymmetric distribution visible to anyone who looked at it against the model's geometric center.

No one said anything while she finished the walkthrough.

"One point eight to two point three kilometers," Cole said when she finished. He was looking at the display with the precise attention that meant he was working the problem rather than reacting to it. "That's the revised operational margin, depending on which solution JPL certifies."

"Correct."

"That's achievable," Anand said. Flat. Statement of fact, no reassurance built in, no performance of confidence. Just what was true.

"It is achievable," Maria said. "It requires us to be exact in ways the original mission design gave us more room to be approximate. The nav system is rated for it. Our training needs to reflect it." She looked at the room. "I want honest assessments. Not optimism. Not alarm. What does this change for each of you, specifically?"

Harrison spoke first, which she'd expected — he processed fastest out loud. "The approach profile. The asymmetric mass distribution creates torque during the impactor strike. The active compensation window is going to be shorter than we've been training against. I want to model that in sim before I give you a number, but my estimate is the window is about sixty percent of what we've been assuming."

"Schedule it with Cole. This week."

Reyes — who almost never spoke first in group settings and who always said something worth waiting for — said: "The survey pass before deployment. The M1/M2/M3 positions need to be confirmed at intercept range. The Compass data is transit-phase. There will be variance from the model at close approach. How much survey time do we have before the deployment window opens?"

"Fourteen hours at the certified standoff distance. If we push to the closer approach option, we gain three hours of survey time and lose two point one kilometers of standoff." Maria had worked this out the previous night. "My instinct is the standoff option and the shorter survey. I want your read."

Reyes and Stone exchanged the look of people who had been thinking about the same problem from the same angle for long enough that their conclusions had converged without discussion. "Fourteen hours is enough," Stone said. "If the radar return is clean. Rotation-induced interference at that range is the scenario we'd need to plan for."

"Anand," Maria said.

"Already noted," Anand said. "I'll add a contingency to the sensor array protocol. Should have a draft for your review by Friday."

They worked for two more hours. The conversation was technical, specific, and entirely free of the quality Maria had been monitoring for: the performance of calm. People who were performing calm had a slight lag before they spoke, a slight excess of measured tone. None of her crew had that. They were engaged with the problem because they were the right people for the problem and engagement was their natural response to it.

When the session broke she kept Cole back.

"The probability," he said. He'd known that was why she'd kept him.

"The public figure is forty-three percent. Based on what I'm reading in the targeting team's unclassified communications and what I know about the mass distribution data, I think the real figure is lower." She said it directly because Cole needed the direct version and because anything less would be a form of condescension. "I'm not in a position to give you a number. What I can tell you is that the map changes how I think about our operational margin."

"Not the probability."

"Not the probability — that's JPL's calculation and they're more equipped for it than I am. What it changes is my understanding of the distance between what we need to do and what we're capable of doing. And that distance is tighter than the original mission design assumed."

Cole nodded. He was looking at the map, at M2 offset from center in the core.

"We can close that distance," he said.

"That's why I told you," Maria said.

She closed the briefing room and walked back through the base to the pilot quarters. The base was settling into its afternoon routine around her — shift change at the cafeteria, the ventilation running its standard cycle, the muffled sounds of construction still ongoing on the outer sections.

She carried 1.8 kilometers with her. She had been carrying the Phoenix mission for three years and she would carry it for four more, and she had learned that the things worth doing were usually the things that required you to carry something heavy long enough that you stopped noticing the weight. You just moved with it. You built your strength around it. And then one day you were in the place the carrying had been taking you toward, and you either had what you needed or you didn't.

She was going to have what she needed. She was going to make sure of it in every simulation session and every debrief and every calibration run between now and launch.

She went to the simulator and started the approach sequence from the beginning, this time using the tightest of the three certified solutions and the worst-case radar conditions.

She ran it until she got it right. Then she ran it again.
