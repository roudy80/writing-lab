# Chapter 26: The Cost of Holding

Senator Sarah Grant | Geneva

April 3–5, 2036

The emergency Framework session was called on April third, seventeen days after the Luna Base incident reports cleared the agency review and became public record.

Sarah had known it was coming since March sixteenth, the morning after the reports cleared. She had spent the seventeen days between knowing and the session in the same way she spent every interval before a difficult negotiation: understanding what each party in the room actually needed, as distinct from what they were saying they needed, and running the calculation she never ran out loud about what she was willing to give up.

In April of 2036, the answer was: more than in any previous year.

* * *

Day one was technical.

Novak presented in person. Kim Aldridge appeared by secure link from Houston, her voice carrying the specific quality of someone who had spent three weeks preparing to describe a failure she felt responsible for to an audience that would use the description to make political decisions. Sarah had spoken with Kim once before the session, briefly, to tell her that the most useful thing she could do was be precise and that precision would serve better than any other quality she might bring into the room.

Kim had said: I know. That's all she'd said. Sarah had believed her.

The incident analysis unfolded over four hours. The cascade modification, its design rationale, the testing history, the second human trial's sixty-two-day run without anomaly. Then the morning of March fifteenth: the construction crew on the outer ring section running a structural brace installation, the vibration propagating through the hull, the modified thermal regulation architecture's response.

The French delegation lead interrupted twice. Both interruptions were good questions, he had done his technical homework, which was either the sign of a delegation genuinely concerned about the engineering or the sign of a delegation that had decided to make the engineering its political lever and had prepared accordingly. Probably both.

"The modification was certified before the trial," he said. "By whom, and on what basis."

Novak answered without consulting her notes. The certification had been conducted by the Luna Base engineering team and reviewed by the Framework's technical advisory board. The vibration analysis had been conducted against the original thermal regulation architecture, because the modification had not yet been fully characterized for its changed vibration response at the time the construction work order was approved. "The two analyses were conducted by teams working in parallel," Novak said. "The integration of their findings was not completed before the work order was issued."

"The left hand did not know what the right hand was doing," the French delegation lead said.

"The left hand and right hand were using different system models," Novak said. "This has been corrected. The post-incident protocol now requires integrated analysis for any construction or maintenance work within the vibration proximity of active cryogenic systems."

Sarah watched the room during this exchange. The delegations that had come in skeptical were not being reassured, because the testimony was honest about the failure and honesty about failure read as evidence of more failure to people who had already decided to be skeptical. The delegations that had come in committed were reading the honesty as evidence of a program mature enough to find and address its own problems.

The room was dividing along lines that had been there before the session started. Her job was to prevent that division from becoming the outcome.

The afternoon session was devoted to engineering analysis. Kim walked through the cascade mechanism, the modification's design rationale, and the cascade behavior at forty-pod scale versus the projected behavior at fourteen hundred pods per ring. The technical briefing was thorough and, Sarah thought, slightly too thorough, Kim had prepared for the depth of the most technical questions she might receive and as a result was giving more technical detail than most delegations would retain. This was a common error in expert testimony. The experts knew which details mattered and couldn't fully model how much less their audience knew.

During the break Sarah pulled Paul aside. "The afternoon session. The technical briefing."

"Too detailed," he said.

"For most of the room. But there are two delegations who are going to make the technical record the basis for their political position, and Kim's thoroughness is what will make their position defensible or not." She thought about it. "Leave it. The detail is protective, even if it's more than most people will absorb."

Day one ended without a vote or a motion. That was correct. Day one was for the facts.

* * *

Day two was the political session. Sarah had thirty minutes at the top of the day, at Lindqvist's arrangement.

She used them.

She said: the forty-three percent probability is a data point, not a verdict. She said: the testing process worked, the failure was found at small scale, with forty subjects, not at operational scale with four thousand. She said: every delegation in this room made a commitment based on the understanding that the program would encounter problems and solve them. This is a problem being solved.

Then she said the thing she'd been deciding for three days whether to say.

"I want to address directly the option that I know several delegations are considering and that none have yet raised on the floor," she said. "The option is a complete redesign of the mission architecture to eliminate cryosleep."

The room shifted. Not the specific quality of surprise but the specific quality of a subject arriving that had been present in every private conversation and was now public.

"A conscious-crew mission," she said. "Four thousand people awake for the full transit. The ship would need to be significantly redesigned, larger life support capacity, different social and psychological architecture, a construction timeline extension." She paused. "I am not proposing this option. I am naming it, because I believe it is better for this room to make this decision with the option explicitly on the table than to spend the next six months managing it as a subtext."

Lindqvist, who had known she was going to say this, she had told him the night before, said from his seat: "What is the engineering estimate for such a redesign."

"Novak," Sarah said.

Novak, who had also been told: "Eighteen months to two years for the redesign and reconstruction of the life support and habitation architecture. The ship's structural, propulsion, and navigation systems are unaffected."

"The departure window," the Brazilian delegate said.

"Under a conscious-crew redesign, the departure window shifts to 2044. Two years after the asteroid impact date." Sarah held the room's attention. "Which makes the Phoenix deflection mission the load-bearing element. If the deflection succeeds, the 2044 departure is viable and the mission profile is unchanged. If the deflection fails, the 2042 impact date precedes the departure window."

"So we're betting everything on Phoenix," someone said.

"We were always betting on Phoenix," Sarah said. "The ship has always been the contingency for the scenario where Phoenix doesn't succeed. The question is whether the contingency has a cryosleep system or a conscious-crew system, and what each of those costs in time and risk."

The discussion ran six hours. She answered questions. She let Novak and Kim answer the technical questions. She let the Brazilians make their equity argument about the selection criteria and took notes because the equity argument was legitimate and would need to be addressed in the redesign regardless of which system was chosen.

The French motion came at hour four: a formal pause of the cryosleep development program pending a comprehensive safety review.

Sarah did not block it. She let it be tabled. She spoke to it for eight minutes, arguing that a pause of the cryosleep development program was the correct decision if the decision was that cryosleep would not be the mission architecture, that a pause contingent on ongoing redesign was not the same as a pause contingent on the status quo.

The motion came to a vote at the end of day two. The room voted eleven to two to table the motion for decision on day three, in conjunction with a decision on mission architecture.

Day two ended with no architecture decision. That was also correct. Day two was for the argument.

* * *

Day three was the vote.

Sarah had spent the night, three hours of it, the rest on work, talking with five delegations individually. Not managing them. Listening. There was a version of coalition work that was about managing people toward predetermined outcomes, and a version that was about understanding what they actually needed and finding the outcome that met enough of it.

She was doing the second version. It was slower and it produced more durable results.

The Chinese cost formula had been the hardest conversation. Four hours over two nights, a revised formula that was workable for both sides without giving away what couldn't be given away. The Indian delegation had run its own structural analysis and independently arrived at a conclusion consistent with Kim's model. The Brazilian equity provisions had been incorporated into the amendment language for the architecture decision. The European bloc needed the science-based framing rather than the political framing, which was satisfied by leading the vote with Novak's engineering determination.

The vote on mission architecture: eleven in favor of conscious-crew redesign, two abstentions, one against.

The one against was a delegation whose government had changed since the last review cycle and whose new leadership hadn't developed a position that could survive the political cost of publicly supporting the program. Sarah noted it in the communiqué margin. She expected the position to shift before the next review.

The Framework voted to formally transition the mission architecture to conscious-crew design. The cryosleep integration program was suspended indefinitely. The construction timeline was extended. The departure window was adjusted. The interstellar ship would carry four thousand people awake across forty years of deep space, and the people responsible for designing what that meant would now have to design it.

* * *

Lindqvist found her after the final signature.

"You held it," he said.

"We held it." She accepted the wine he was somehow always able to produce. "Eleven to one. The Chinese cost distribution took most of the second night."

"How much did we give them."

"Less than they asked for and more than they expected. The formula is workable." She looked out the window at the lake. The water was indifferent to what had just happened in the room behind them, which was the quality of bodies of water that she had always found useful to look at after difficult negotiations. "The conscious-crew design changes everything Elena Marsh has been building for nine years. The selection criteria have to be rebuilt. The one hundred and ninety people who received incompatibility determinations may be eligible again."

"Does she know."

"The formal notification goes to her office today. She's been preparing since Friday."

Lindqvist looked at his wine. "Forty years. Awake. I hadn't thought through what that actually means until Novak described the life support requirements."

"It means the ship has to be a city," Sarah said. "Not a vessel. A city. Where people live and work and build something over four decades. That's a harder design problem than a vessel where everyone is asleep."

"Is it solvable."

"Every design problem is solvable if you have good people, adequate time, and clear requirements." She paused. "We have good people. We have adequate time if Phoenix succeeds. The requirements are now clearer than they were yesterday, because at least we know what we're designing for."

"And if Phoenix doesn't succeed."

"Then we have a more complex problem, and we will address it when we know the dimensions of it." She finished her wine. "I've stopped asking whether our solutions are enough. I ask whether we're doing the work as well as it can be done. The answer to that question is yes. The answer to the other question doesn't help anyone."

She flew home the next morning. Eleven hours. She worked for nine of them and spent two looking out the window at the Atlantic below.

The water moved with the patience of something that had been doing this for four billion years and would keep doing it regardless of what happened in the rooms above it.

Eleven to one. The program survived. The work continued.

She opened her laptop and started on the Senate briefing, because the next conversation was already waiting and the work in front of her was the only work that mattered.
