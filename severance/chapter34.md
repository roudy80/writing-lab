# Chapter 34: Fracture Lines

Senator Sarah Grant | Washington, D.C. / Geneva

June 12, 2040 | 07:22 AM EST

The coalition had been holding for fifteen years, which was longer than anyone had reasonably expected, and in June of 2040 Sarah watched it begin to crack along the lines she had known, since approximately 2028, that it would eventually crack along.

The crack was not dramatic. It was not a single vote or a single speech or a single government's withdrawal. It was the specific accumulation of smaller things that coalition cracks were always made of: a bilateral agreement between two Framework members that should have gone through the joint technical committee but didn't; a third member's response to that agreement, which was technically a notification but carried the tone of a grievance; a fourth member's response to the third member's response, which required four hours of Sarah's staff time to prevent from becoming something it could not be allowed to become.

She had been managing these accumulations for fifteen years. She was good at it. She was also, in June of 2040, tired in a way that was different from the previous varieties of tired she had been.

Paul came in at seven-thirty. He had been her chief of staff for six years now, and in that time he had developed the specific economy of a person who had been managing high-stakes situations long enough to lead with what mattered.

"The Australians have formally notified Geneva of their intent to reduce their Framework contribution by eighteen percent," he said, setting a tablet on her desk. "The notification cites domestic political constraints. The actual cause is the prime minister's coalition partner, who has been running on a platform that the program is resource misallocation."

"I know the platform." Sarah had been watching Australian domestic politics for eight months, since the coalition partner had first started gaining traction. "What's the timeline on the reduction?"

"Ninety days. They're framing it as a temporary adjustment pending their domestic budget review."

"It's not temporary."

"No." Paul sat down. "The Koreans are watching the Australians. If the Australian reduction goes through without consequence, Seoul will be under pressure from their opposition to do something similar. The South African government is in a different situation, their reduction pressure is coming from the left rather than the right, argument that the program prioritizes global elite survival over regional resilience investment."

Sarah read the notification on the tablet. It was carefully worded, as Framework notifications always were, but the careful wording could not conceal what it was: the first formal contribution reduction from a major partner since the program's inception.

"What does the program actually need from the Australians," she said.

"Their contribution covers approximately three percent of the conscious-crew habitation redesign. The redesign is seventy-two percent complete. A three percent reduction in funding at this stage is manageable. A three percent reduction that signals permission for others to reduce is not manageable."

"Then the Australian reduction is not about three percent."

"No. It's about whether the coalition holds." Paul looked at her steadily. "It's been fifteen years. Phoenix is en route and has been dark for two months because that's what transit looks like. The public can't see the mission. They can see their own governments' budgets. There are sixteen elections in Framework member states in the next twenty-four months."

Sarah set the tablet down. She looked at the window. The Washington morning was assembling itself in the way it always assembled itself, and she had been watching it from this window for nine years and she understood something about it that she hadn't understood in year one: the city was not the work. The city was the place where the work happened, and the work was what made the city legible. Without the work, the city was just a city.

"Schedule calls with the Australian foreign minister and the prime minister's office," she said. "I want them in the same week, the foreign minister first, to understand the actual constraint, then the prime minister's office to see what room there is."

"And if there's no room?"

"Then we design the response to the reduction to make it as visible as possible that this is what choosing less looks like." She said it without heat, because heat was not useful. "Not punitive. Factual. The construction milestone that moves. The habitation module that doesn't get completed on schedule. The number of selected crew members whose departure is delayed. We make the cost concrete and specific and we let their domestic politics do the rest."

Paul was writing. "The Geneva session next month."

"I'll be there in person. The French have been reliable but they have elections in October and I need to know what the new government will look like before I can plan around it." She picked up her coffee. "The Chinese?"

"Stable. They're watching the Australians the same way everyone is, but their domestic calculation is different, the program is popular with the technical class, which is the class their government needs right now."

"The Indians?"

"Solid. Their science ministry has been running a parallel crew selection track in coordination with Elena Marsh's office and they're invested in the outcome of that process. Hard to withdraw from a program your people are selected for."

"That's by design." It was, in fact, by design, one of the structural decisions Sarah had made in 2030, when the crew selection process was taking shape, was to push for broad geographic distribution in the early selection cohorts precisely because selected families created a different political constituency than abstract program support. "What's the total crew selection status?"

"The pool is closed at four thousand, three hundred and twelve. The governance framework review is ongoing, Elena's office expects final ratification by the pool in September. Crew boarding begins in late 2041 as the ship construction completes."

"The ship completion date?"

"Novak's office is projecting February 2042 for full systems certification. That gives a launch window of March through May, with the March window optimal."

Sarah did the math. February certification. March launch window. October 2042 impact date. Six months. Six months between launching the ship that carried four thousand people and the possible arrival of the thing the ship was partly an answer to.

"Phoenix," she said.

"Still on transit. The intercept window opens in November. We won't have results until January at the earliest, the communication lag from that distance is significant."

"I know." She thought about Maria Webb, somewhere out there in the dark, running the mission the way she ran everything: completely. "The Australian situation. If we can't contain it , "

"The program survives a three percent reduction. It doesn't survive the fracture that follows."

"I know." She stood up. "I'm going to call Erik Lindqvist first. Before the Australian foreign minister, before anyone. I want to know what Geneva looks like from his angle before I start making calls."

Paul looked slightly surprised. In fifteen years of this work, she had almost never called Lindqvist before a crisis developed rather than during it. He had been a reliable partner, the most reliable partner, but she had always maintained the protocol of managing the problem before bringing it to the alliance.

"He's been doing this as long as I have," she said. "And he's been watching what I've been watching. He may see something I don't."

She picked up her phone. Outside the window, the city was doing what it always did.

She called Lindqvist.

* * *

He answered on the second ring, which told her he had been expecting the call.

"The Australians," he said.

"The Australians," she confirmed. "What are you seeing from your end?"

"The same fracture lines you're seeing. Fifteen years is a long time. The thing that held the coalition together in 2025 was fear, an immediate, comprehensible fear. What holds it together now is inertia and investment and the specific calculation that withdrawing is more costly than continuing." He paused. "That calculation is still correct for most members. But the margin has narrowed."

"What would you do with the Australians?"

"I would make the cost of the reduction visible in the specific areas their domestic politics care about. Not punitive , "

"Factual."

"Yes." A pause. "Sarah. Is the program going to hold together for two more years?"

She thought about what she actually believed, which was a different question from what she needed to project. "I believe it holds through the Phoenix result. If the deflection succeeds, the political calculation changes and the coalition becomes easier. If the deflection fails , " She stopped. "If the deflection fails, the program becomes essential in a way that makes withdrawal politically impossible for any member government. The impact date is the deadline everyone understands."

"Either way," Lindqvist said.

"Either way, we have two years. I intend to use them."

A pause. "I'll hold the European bloc. You know that."

"I know. Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Win the Australians back."

She ended the call. She sat for a moment with the phone in her hand.

Fifteen years. She was fifty-eight years old and had been doing this specific work for fifteen of them and she was going to do it for two more, and at the end of those two more years either the asteroid would have been deflected or it wouldn't and either the ship would have launched or it wouldn't and either the coalition would have held or it wouldn't, and she was going to have done everything she was capable of doing in the space between this morning and that outcome.

She called the Australian foreign minister's office and asked for a time.
