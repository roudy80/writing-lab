# Chapter 37: Dark

Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston

December 2040 through January 2041

Phoenix would not be back until September.

That was the thing Thomas had been carrying since October, when he had looked up the return trajectory and done the math and understood that the mission — which had launched in September 2039, reached the asteroid in November 2040, and was now burning back toward Earth — would not arrive at Luna Base until September 2041. Ten months of return transit. Ten months of Phoenix being somewhere in the dark with whatever it had done, the results invisible to everyone on Earth until the trajectory data accumulated enough to show which way the asteroid was actually going.

The trajectory data was publicly available. ARIA updated it every six hours on the agency website. Thomas had looked at it once and decided not to look at it again, not because he couldn't read it — he had learned enough orbital mechanics in fourteen years of monitoring office work to understand what the numbers meant — but because the numbers updated too slowly to be useful and checking them would become a habit he didn't want.

He had developed, over the years, a good sense of which habits were worth developing.

* * *

Thursday sessions in December ran through the holiday week, which they always had. Thomas had never closed the sessions for holidays, on the theory that the people who needed the sessions most were often the people with the fewest reasons to celebrate the holiday. The December attendance was usually lower than the fall sessions — not much lower, because the regulars came regardless, but the walk-ins were fewer.

This December the walk-in rate held at the fall average. He noticed this and did not comment on it.

Dmitri came to the December session on the third Thursday, which he rarely did — his council work had expanded since his reelection and his Thursday availability was sporadic. He sat in the back and participated in the record review and stayed afterward when most people had left.

"The Phoenix result," Dmitri said. He had the directness he always had when he was asking something he actually wanted to know the answer to. "When do we find out?"

"The mission is on return transit. The trajectory deviation from the deflection attempt will be measurable from ground observation within three to four months. The full result will be confirmed when the tracking data accumulates."

"So spring."

"Preliminary data in spring. Confirmed by summer, probably."

Dmitri looked at him. "You're not watching the data."

"No."

"Why not."

Thomas thought about how to answer this honestly. "Because I've spent fourteen years building a record that I could actually affect. The trajectory data is a record I can't affect. Watching it doesn't change it."

Dmitri nodded slowly. He had the quality of someone who understood the distinction between things you could affect and things you couldn't, which was part of why he had been good at the council work.

"The law," Dmitri said. "The implementation."

"The federal agency is standing up the consultation framework. First review cycle starts in March. I've been in contact with Watts about the Roxbury assessment backlog." Thomas looked at his notes from the session. "There's work to do. There's a lot of work to do."

"There always is."

"Yes."

They sat for a moment in the empty room. The chairs were in their usual configuration — the semicircle that allowed everyone to see everyone else, that he had been setting up for fourteen years and that had become, over time, not something he arranged but something that was simply how the room went.

"The ship," Dmitri said.

"Boarding in late 2041. Launch window March 2042."

"Six months before the asteroid date."

"Yes."

Dmitri looked at the chairs. "My son is in the candidate pool."

Thomas looked at him. He had not known this.

"He applied in 2038," Dmitri said. "He made the first cut. He's in the final pool." He said it the way he said most things: directly, without performance. "He's thirty-one. He's an agricultural systems engineer. He spent two years on the Luna Base agricultural module." He paused. "He's exactly the kind of person the mission needs."

"He is," Thomas said.

"I know." Dmitri looked at the window. The December night outside was cold and clear, the sky the specific deep black of a Boston winter night after rain. "His mother is not in the pool. She doesn't want to go." A pause. "He's going. She knows he's going. We've all known for two years that he's going."

Thomas didn't say anything, because there was nothing to say that would add to what Dmitri had said.

"The work," Dmitri said, pulling his coat on. "The work continues."

"Yes."

Dmitri left. Thomas sat in the empty room for a while, with the chairs and the quiet and the knowledge that outside, in the dark, a mission was making its way back from a place no human had ever been before, carrying the result of the only answer anyone had been able to devise.

* * *

In January, Ben Oakes called.

Thomas picked up before the second ring. He had been keeping the phone close since November, not because Ben had said he would call but because the kind of proximity that produced phone calls was something Thomas had learned to maintain.

"I can't tell you anything official," Ben said. He sounded tired in the way he always sounded tired when he was carrying something that required precision. "I want you to understand that. What I can tell you is what's public."

"Go ahead."

"The preliminary tracking data from the Goldstone and Arecibo arrays has been running since mid-December. There's a trajectory deviation. We expected a deviation — that's what the mission was for. The deviation is real and it's measurable."

"Is it enough."

"The analysis isn't complete. The confidence interval on the deviation magnitude is still wide — we need another three months of data to narrow it. What I can tell you is that the preliminary figure is within the range that the targeting team calculated as achievable under Solution Two." He stopped. "That's all I can tell you. I'm sorry. I know it's not the answer."

"It's better than no data."

"Yes."

Thomas looked out the window at the January street. "The preliminary figure is within the achievable range," he said, carefully. "That means it's possible."

"It means it's possible." Ben's voice had the quality it had when he was being as honest as the available data allowed. "Three months. The spring analysis will be more definitive."

"I'll wait."

"Thomas. Whatever the spring analysis shows —"

"I know," Thomas said. "Whatever it shows, the ship is launching. I know."

A silence. "Yes."

"That was always the plan," Thomas said. "The deflection mission gives us the best chance and the ship gives us continuity regardless. Both of those things are true simultaneously. I've known that since 2026."

"I know you have."

They stayed on the line for a moment. Outside, a car passed slowly on the January street, its headlights moving across the wall.

"Thank you for calling," Thomas said.

"Every time," Ben said.

The call ended. Thomas sat with the phone in his hand for a while, with the January dark outside and the preliminary trajectory data sitting somewhere in a database at Goldstone, showing a deviation that was within the achievable range.

Within the achievable range.

He got up and made tea and sat back down and looked at the window.

Three months.
