# The Scattered Worlds: Severance

Chapter 5: Triage
Thomas Rourke | Boston Reconstruction Zone
November 19th, 2025 | 8:15 AM EST
Three weeks after the impact, the city had divided itself into two places that occupied the same geography and had almost nothing else in common. Thomas had started thinking of them as Boston-that-was and Boston-that-remained, and the line between them ran roughly along the Route 128 corridor, though it shifted daily as the assessment teams pushed further into the damage zone and the evacuation orders crept further out.
He was standing at that line now, or close enough to it, on a stretch of Commonwealth Avenue where the building facades on one side still had their windows and the building facades on the other side did not. The pressure wave had done this, arrived from the west like weather and expressed itself in glass: everything facing the impact direction had lost it, everything perpendicular or sheltered had kept it. Walking this street felt like walking through two different versions of the same moment, one in which October 15th had been an ordinary Tuesday and one in which it hadn't.
His shoulder had been properly reset four days after the impact by a paramedic at a field station in the Common. It still ached in the mornings. He'd started thinking of it as a useful reminder, a calibration point for what discomfort actually meant. The people in the zone to the west didn't have shoulders that ached in the mornings.
The people in the zone to the west, the ones within roughly thirty miles of the Quabbin crater, were not a category Thomas spent a lot of time in, because the numbers were large enough to be meaningless and he'd found that meaningless numbers didn't help him do the work in front of him. What helped was the specific: this block, this building, this load-bearing wall that had developed a crack suggesting the foundation had shifted two inches to the northeast. Specific things could be assessed and addressed. The large numbers just sat there.
He pulled out his notebook and added Commonwealth Avenue, 800 block, facade assessment needed, east-facing structural survey to the list of things that needed doing and wouldn't get done this week because there were not enough people to do them.
* * *
The firm had reassigned him to the emergency assessment team the week after the impact, which meant he was now doing in a semi-official capacity the same thing he'd been doing unofficially since the afternoon of October 15th: walking through damaged buildings and telling people whether they were going to fall down. His license made it official. His experience made it useful. The sheer volume of damaged structures in greater Boston made it overwhelming.
His supervisor on the assessment team was a woman named Carol Briggs, a structural engineer twenty years his senior who had done disaster assessment work after the Northridge earthquake and two Gulf Coast hurricanes and who had the particular economy of motion and speech that came from having done difficult things many times. She ran the team's daily briefings out of a FEMA field office set up in a parking structure in Newton that had survived the pressure wave intact, and she delivered bad news the way she delivered all news, which was without preamble.
"Federal assessment authority ends at the 128 perimeter," she told them that morning, indicating the map on the wall behind her. The map had a color gradient that Thomas had learned to read over the past three weeks: white for assessed and cleared, yellow for assessed and restricted, orange for assessed and condemned, red for the zone where assessment teams didn't go yet because the structural situation was too unstable to enter safely. The red covered roughly the western third of the map.
"Anything inside the perimeter is federal jurisdiction under the emergency authority declaration. Which means our assessment findings in that zone go to FEMA, FEMA routes them to the Army Corps, and the Army Corps decides what gets demolished and in what order." She paused. "It also means the people whose buildings are in that zone don't get to talk to us directly about what we find. They talk to their congressional representatives, who talk to FEMA, who eventually talk to the Army Corps."
"How long does that process take?" asked someone at the back, a younger engineer whose name Thomas hadn't learned yet.
"Months," Carol said. "Possibly longer for the buildings that aren't falling down immediately, because the ones that are falling down immediately take priority."
Thomas had been in enough government processes to understand what months meant in practice: it meant the people whose buildings had been condemned were living somewhere temporary and couldn't plan beyond the temporary because they didn't know yet whether their neighborhood was coming back. It meant business owners couldn't decide whether to reopen or relocate. It meant whole communities were suspended in a kind of administrative limbo while the federal machinery processed the information that the assessment teams were generating.
He understood why the system worked this way. The scale of the damage required centralized coordination. Individual property decisions couldn't be made without understanding the larger infrastructure picture: utility lines, load-bearing party walls, historic district considerations, the interdependencies of a dense urban environment. You couldn't just tell one building owner their building was safe if the building next to it was going to be demolished, because demolition had structural implications for everything adjacent.
Understanding why the system worked this way didn't make it easier to look at the faces of the people standing outside the 128 perimeter asking him what he'd found in their building.
* * *
He had found, early on, that the most useful thing he could do outside of his official assessment work was to be the person who explained things. Not the decisions, those weren't his to explain. But the reasoning. Why the crack in that wall meant what it meant. Why the building next door could stay up while this one couldn't. Why the timeline for structural decisions was what it was and what the process looked like from the inside.
People wanted to understand. That was the thing that surprised him, though in retrospect it shouldn't have. In the immediate aftermath of the impact, when he'd been helping pull people out of debris and organizing gas evacuations, the dominant need had been physical: safety, water, warmth, information about where family members were. Three weeks in, the dominant need had shifted. People were physically safe. They had shelter, provisional and uncomfortable in many cases but shelter. What they needed now was to understand what was happening to them and why, and nobody in the official structures had time to explain it.
He started doing it informally, standing outside the assessment sites while Carol and the team worked inside, answering questions from whoever had gathered on the sidewalk. He was careful about what he said. He never shared specific findings from the assessment reports, which were federal property, and he never made predictions about outcomes, which were beyond his authority to make. What he did was explain structural engineering in plain language. How buildings worked. What kinds of damage were fixable and what kinds weren't. Why a building could look intact from the outside and be condemned on the inside. What the assessment process involved and approximately how long each stage took.
Word spread. By the second week he was getting calls from community groups who wanted him to come to their neighborhood and hold information sessions. He went to two of them on evenings when Carol's team had finished early, sitting in community centers that smelled of institutional coffee and collective anxiety, drawing diagrams on whiteboards to explain load transfer and foundation stress to rooms full of people who owned buildings and needed to understand what might be happening inside them.
He wasn't sure if it helped in any material sense. The buildings were what they were. The federal process was what it was. But people left those sessions looking different from how they'd arrived, less like they were waiting for something to be done to them and more like they were waiting for something they at least understood.
Carol found out about the community sessions and didn't say anything for three days. Then she told him to keep doing it, and to tell people that the assessment reports were public record and could be requested through FEMA's online portal once they'd been processed. A small thing. It gave people something concrete to do.
* * *
The press conference happened on a Tuesday, which was how Thomas learned about 2042-Delta.
He'd been avoiding news coverage since the impact, not deliberately at first but because the work filled the available hours and the evenings he'd been using for community sessions and the nights he'd been using for sleep. He knew the broad shape of it: the asteroid had been a fragment, the main body was still out there, there was an impact probability and it wasn't small. He'd absorbed this information the way you absorbed background radiation, as a fact about the environment that didn't change the work in front of you.
The press conference put specific numbers to it and the specific numbers changed something.
He watched it on his phone during a lunch break, sitting on a piece of rubble on the edge of a cleared lot in Allston where a three-story row house had been demolished the week before. The NASA administrator spoke first, then the head of the planetary defense program, then a scientist whose name Thomas didn't catch who walked through the orbital mechanics in terms designed for a general audience. 340 meters. 68% probability. October 2042. Energy release that the scientist carefully declined to describe in apocalyptic terms but that translated, when Thomas ran the math on his phone afterward, to something in the range of a hundred thousand megatons.
He sat with that number for a while.
The row house behind him had been three stories of brick, built in 1923, home to two families for a hundred years before a forty-meter rock had ended that arrangement. The crater at Quabbin was fourteen kilometers wide. The rock that had made that crater was roughly one-ninth the diameter of the one currently being tracked.
He tried to picture what 340 meters meant at the velocities involved and found he couldn't build an image that his brain accepted as real. His brain kept returning to the column over western Massachusetts, the one he'd watched from the Common, the one he'd been watching for three weeks in the form of a persistent haze in the western sky that was the dust still settling out of the upper atmosphere. That had been forty meters. He could not build a version of that column that was 340 meters.
His phone buzzed. Carol, asking where he was. He typed back a location and stood up and brushed the dust off his jacket and went back to work.
On the way he passed a woman standing outside a building on the condemned list, the orange notice still taped to the door from last week's assessment. She was in her sixties, wearing a winter coat, holding a cardboard box with some items she'd apparently retrieved from inside despite the notice. She looked at him as he passed. He stopped.
"That building's been condemned," he said. "It's not safe to enter."
"I know. I was quick." She shifted the box. "It's my mother's. She's eighty-three and she's in a shelter in Brookline and she wanted her photographs."
Thomas looked at the building. He'd done the original assessment himself. The foundation had shifted and one of the load-bearing walls had cracked through in a way that made the structural situation unstable but not immediately catastrophic, which was a meaningful distinction. Unstable but not immediately catastrophic meant it was safe enough for a quick in-and-out if you knew what you were doing, which this woman clearly hadn't but had gotten away with anyway.
"Is she all right? Your mother?"
"She's fine. Angry. She lived in that building for forty years and they put a notice on the door and nobody told her anything." The woman looked at the orange notice. "Do you know what happens to it? Eventually?"
"It depends on the structural assessment and the federal process. The report goes to FEMA and then to the Army Corps. They make the final determination."
"How long does that take?"
"Months."
She nodded slowly, absorbing this. "And if they decide to demolish it?"
"Then it gets added to the demolition schedule. There are a lot of buildings on that schedule right now. It could be six months before they get to this one."
"So she waits six months to find out if her home is gone."
"Yes. I'm sorry."
The woman looked at the box of photographs in her arms. "There's going to be another one," she said. "That's what they're saying. In 2042. A bigger one."
"That's what the probability suggests. There's still a chance it misses."
"A 32% chance." She'd done the same math he had, apparently, on the same timeline. "My mother is eighty-three. She won't be around for 2042 either way. But I will. And my kids will." She looked up at him. "Are they going to fix it? The ships they're building?"
Thomas thought about the honest answer to that question and the useful answer and the answer he was actually qualified to give. "I don't know enough about the ships to say. I build buildings. They're building something I don't really have the technical background to evaluate."
"But you think they'll work?"
He thought about the column over Massachusetts. He thought about the number he'd been sitting with on the rubble pile. He thought about Carol's team, moving methodically through the damaged city, generating reports that went into a federal process that would eventually produce decisions that would eventually be communicated to the people whose lives were on hold waiting for them.
"I think a lot of very capable people are working very hard on it," he said. "I think that's what I've got."
She nodded as if this were more useful than she'd expected and walked away with her box of photographs.
* * *
That evening he went to a community session in Jamaica Plain, a neighborhood that had taken moderate damage, enough that a significant number of buildings were in the yellow assessment category, restricted rather than condemned, which meant residents could enter but couldn't occupy. The distinction mattered enormously to the people living out of it.
The room held maybe fifty people, a cross-section of a dense urban neighborhood: renters and owners, longtime residents and people who'd moved in two years ago, people with nowhere else to go and people who had family they could stay with and were choosing to stay local anyway out of some combination of stubbornness and attachment. Thomas recognized the type. He was one of them.
He explained the yellow category. He explained what structural restriction meant in practice and what the pathway to clearance looked like and how long it typically took. He explained what residents could do to move their building up the priority list for reassessment. He answered questions for an hour and twenty minutes.
Near the end, a man in his forties who hadn't spoken yet raised his hand. "I heard they're going to redirect the reconstruction funding. To the ship program."
The room went quiet in a particular way.
Thomas had heard the same thing, in the form of a rumor moving through the assessment teams, something about a federal budget review and competing priorities. He didn't know if it was true. "I haven't seen anything official on that," he said carefully.
"My neighbor works for the city," the man said. "He says the federal money that was supposed to come for the reconstruction phase is being reviewed. That the Luna-Ark program is pulling from the same emergency authorization."
"I genuinely don't know," Thomas said. "What I know is the assessment work is continuing and the demolition schedule is continuing. If funding changed, it would affect the later phases. The immediate safety work is already authorized."
It wasn't much of an answer. The room knew it. But there wasn't a better one available, so he gave what he had and people filed out into the November night with their questions unanswered, which was the same condition they'd arrived in, just with a better understanding of why.
He walked back to his car through streets that were mostly dark, the streetlights in this part of the city still running on reduced power from the grid damage, pools of orange light with long shadows between them. A few windows showed the blue flicker of televisions. In one ground-floor window he could see a family at a dinner table, just the silhouette of it, people sitting together in the specific posture of a shared meal.
He thought about the woman with the box of photographs. About her mother's forty years in a building with an orange notice on the door. About the man in the community session and his neighbor who worked for the city.
He thought about the ships. About the people Elena Marsh was selecting for them, whoever those people were, making their assessments about who was psychologically fit for a forty-year journey while Thomas was making his assessments about which buildings were structurally fit to stand. Different work. The same underlying question: what gets to continue and what doesn't.
He didn't know yet that the funding rumor was true. He would find out in six weeks, from a news report, sitting in the same temporary apartment he'd been in since October 16th. The emergency reconstruction authorization had been partially redirected. Not eliminated. Partially redirected, toward the Luna-Ark program and the infrastructure it required.
The official language was careful about it. Competing priorities. Long-term species survival versus short-term reconstruction. The mathematics of a 68% impact probability applied to a rebuilding program that would be meaningless if the impact occurred.
The mathematics were correct. Thomas would understand that, when he thought about it clearly. The mathematics were correct and the people making the decision were not wrong and the fifty people in that Jamaica Plain community center were going to wait longer for their buildings to be cleared, and both of those things were true at the same time, and he was going to have to figure out how to hold both of them.
He got in his car and drove back to the temporary apartment and did not figure it out that night.
