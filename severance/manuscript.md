# The Scattered Worlds: Severance

Chapter 1: Sonic Boom
Maria Webb   Commercial Flight 847
30,000 feet above Massachusetts
October 15th, 2025   2:47 PM EST
Maria Webb was thinking about her nephew's birthday cake, whether she'd remembered to tell her sister to order the chocolate one and not the vanilla, when the sky split open and tried to kill everyone on board.
The sound came first. Not through the cockpit windows, which were rated for bird strikes and lightning and the pressurized indignity of weather systems, but through the airframe itself: a concussive crack that she felt in her back teeth and her sternum and the base of her skull simultaneously, like standing too close to a cannon. The Boeing 737 didn't lurch so much as it was shoved, a hundred tons of aircraft knocked sideways by something that had no business being in the atmosphere at 2:47 on a Tuesday afternoon.
Her body knew what to do before her mind caught up. Left hand on the yoke, right hand cycling through the overhead panels. Twelve years flying F/A-18s off carrier decks had built a reflex architecture that activated before conscious thought, and it activated now, not the panicked grab of a surprised civilian, but the systematic assessment of someone who had practiced this moment until it lived in muscle memory. Altitude: dropping. Airspeed: climbing toward the red. Engines: both reading nominal, which made no sense because the aircraft was behaving like an engine had blown.
"What the hell was that?" Jake Morrison, her co-pilot, had both hands on his own controls before he finished the sentence. Good man. He didn't freeze.
"I've got it," Maria said. "Talk to me. What's the atmosphere doing?"
"Pitot tubes are reading inconsistent. I think there's a pressure differential across the fuselage." Jake was cycling through his displays with the focused speed of someone who'd also trained to not think about being afraid. "Like something changed the air pressure outside. Fast."
Maria pulled back on the yoke, bleeding off the airspeed before it climbed into structural concern territory. The aircraft responded sluggishly, not mechanically, the controls felt fine, but the air itself had gone wrong, had thickened or destabilized into something that was fighting her inputs. She'd felt weather like this once, skirting the edge of a supercell over the Gulf of Mexico, where the atmospheric gradients got steep enough to make an aircraft feel drunk. This was worse.
"Boston Control, this is Flight 847." She kept her voice at the steady, controlled pitch of a controller talking down a student on a bad approach. Not calm exactly (calm was an emotion, and she didn't have room for emotions right now) but measured. Professional. The voice that got things done. "We've experienced severe uncharacterized turbulence and are declaring an emergency. Request immediate vectors to nearest available field, over."
Static. Not the crackle of interference or the dropout of a weak signal, but a complete and absolute dead silence that went on long enough to mean something had gone wrong with Boston Center itself, not just her radio.
"Jake, try guard frequency. Try everything."
"I am." He was already running through the emergency frequencies, his voice carrying the tight, controlled quality of a man who had realized the problem was larger than their aircraft. "Nothing on 121.5. Nothing on the military guard. VHF is dead. I'm getting some HF traffic but it's all noise." He paused. "It's a lot of people all trying to talk at once."
Maria looked out her left window.
She had spent twenty years looking out of aircraft windows. She had watched thunderstorms build from the flight deck, had seen the green flash of a Pacific sunset from 41,000 feet, had flown through the northern lights once on a repositioning flight over Canada that she still dreamed about sometimes. She knew what the sky was supposed to look like from thirty thousand feet above New England on a clear October afternoon. She knew the browns and golds of autumn foliage from altitude, the blue-gray geometry of the Connecticut River, the coastal shimmer of Long Island Sound to the south.
What was rising from the western horizon was not that.
It was a column. That was the only word for it, a column of earth and vapor and something darker that rose with the impossible vertical speed of a nuclear test, climbing through the troposphere and still climbing, fanning at the top into an anvil shape but not a storm anvil, nothing as ordinary as weather. The color was wrong: a deep brown at the base shading through gray to near-black at the upper edge where it was entering altitudes where aircraft didn't fly. Even from this distance, Maria could see the thing was still growing, still pushing outward, the pressure wave from the base event visibly rippling across the landscape below as a ring of darker shadow, trees bending, the glint of windows shattering in towns whose names she couldn't read from up here.
"Impact event," she said. The words came out with a clinical flatness that surprised even her. Not because she felt nothing (she could feel something enormous building behind the professionalism, the same way a pilot feels a stall building before the aircraft knows it's stalling) but because the only useful thing to do right now was fly the airplane.
"How big?" Jake asked.
"I don't know. Big." She pulled her eyes off the horizon and back to the instruments. "Quabbin Reservoir area, maybe. Somewhere in central Massachusetts." She did the math. The column was at least sixty miles wide at the base. "Figure a lot."
The aircraft shuddered again, a long rolling vibration that was the pressure wave catching up to them at the speed of sound. Maria rode it with small yoke corrections, keeping the wings level, buying altitude that she was going to need if the atmosphere got any stranger below them. She could feel the passengers in the back, a hundred and forty-three of them, business travelers and families and a college soccer team she'd overheard in the boarding line, as a kind of weight in her chest, a responsibility that had been theoretical two minutes ago and was now the most concrete thing in the world.
"Nearest field," she said. "What have we got?"
Jake pulled up the charts. "Bradley International, Windsor Locks, Connecticut. Forty miles southeast. If Boston Center is down, they might still have radar."
"Try them."
"Bradley Approach, Bradley Approach, this is Delta Flight 847 declaring emergency, do you read?"
A pause. Then, from what sounded like a controller who was also watching the western horizon: "Flight 847, Bradley Approach. We have you on primary radar. Be advised we have multiple aircraft requesting emergency handling. You are number four in the pattern."
Number four. Maria exhaled something that wasn't quite a laugh. "Bradley, 847. We are fuel-sufficient but experiencing atmospheric instability from the impact event. Request expedited handling."
"Understood, 847. You're moved to number two. Expect ILS runway 24. Winds are 270 at 14, gusting 22. Be advised we are seeing unusual pressure readings on all our instruments and do not have a reliable altimeter setting."
No reliable altimeter setting. Maria digested this. "Understood, Bradley. We'll fly the approach on barometric and visual. 847 out."
She turned to Jake. "We're going to do this the old way. Eyes and instincts."
"I love it when you say reassuring things like that."
"Monitor airspeed. Call any variance over five knots. Don't let me get slow."
"Copy." He paused. "Maria. That column. How many people do you think were under it?"
"Later." She said it firmly, not unkindly. "Put it away until we're on the ground. Then we can be humans again."
He nodded and went back to his instruments.
The descent into Bradley took eighteen minutes that felt like considerably longer. The air was unstable in ways that the books didn't cover   not random turbulence but a kind of persistent wrongness, as if the atmosphere had been rearranged and hadn't settled into its new configuration yet. Maria flew it on feel as much as instruments, making continuous small corrections that kept the aircraft stable through shear gradients that her airspeed indicator couldn't fully capture. The column on the western horizon kept growing in her peripheral vision. She kept not looking at it.
The runway came up through the haze with three hundred feet of ceiling to spare   not comfortable, but workable. She greased the landing out of pure stubbornness, the same way she'd always landed on carriers: not because the situation called for precision, but because precision was all she had to offer the universe right now, and she was going to offer every bit of it.
The tires kissed the concrete.
"Nice," Jake said quietly.
"Get the passengers off and get them inside. I want everyone away from windows." She was already running the shutdown checklist, hands moving through the familiar sequence while her eyes tracked the emergency vehicles converging on the runway. "And get someone to tell me what's on the news."
* * *
The federal processing center filled Concourse B within an hour of the last aircraft landing. Folding tables, temporary barriers, agents in DHS windbreakers who had clearly been called in from something else and were working from hastily printed instruction sheets. Maria recognized the flavor of it from her Navy days: institutional improvisation, the kind of organized chaos that emerged when a plan met reality and both of them had to compromise.
She'd spent the hours since landing doing three things. First, she'd helped her cabin crew get the passengers settled   not an easy task when CNN was broadcasting on every terminal monitor and the broadcasts were not reassuring. Second, she'd found enough satellite signal to call her sister in Tampa and establish that her family was fine, that the impact had been localized to central Massachusetts, and that her nephew's birthday party would, presumably, still happen. Third, she'd watched the news.
The casualty estimates were climbing faster than the anchors could report them. The impact site was the Quabbin Reservoir area   she'd been close on her estimate   and the kinetic event had released energy that the physicists being hastily assembled on camera were comparing to multiple nuclear weapons simultaneously, though they kept walking that comparison back because it implied things about radiation that apparently weren't accurate. The blast wave had leveled structures for thirty miles in every direction. The overpressure pulse had damaged buildings as far as Providence. And then there was the ejecta, the debris cloud, the atmospheric injection of particulates that the climate scientists were already running models on with expressions that suggested the models were not delivering good news.
Boston. She'd flown that route four times a week for three years. She knew the city from the air the way she knew her own apartment   every familiar landmark, the harbor, the greenway, the stadium. She'd watched it shrink below her dozens of times, turning from a city into a geometry into a smudge on the horizon.
It was gone now. Not completely   the impact wasn't on the city itself   but the city she'd known was gone, replaced by something that was still being understood.
"Captain Webb?"
She looked up. The agent at the processing table   a young woman with the harried competence of someone who had stopped being surprised by her day   was holding Maria's pilot's license and looking at it with an attention that went beyond routine screening.
"That's me."
"I need you to step aside for a moment. Someone wants to speak with you."
* * *
The man in the Air Force uniform was Colonel David Reeves, and he was sitting in a room that had been a family waiting area ninety minutes ago and was now something that resembled a military command post in the early stages of assembly. Tablets and printed manifests covered the low tables. Two other officers were on phones in the corner. Through the window, Maria could see the flight line, where aircraft were being catalogued with a thoroughness that went beyond routine emergency management.
"Captain Webb." Reeves stood when she entered, extended his hand. "I'm told you landed a 737 on visual approach in marginal conditions without incident while whatever that was " he gestured vaguely westward " was happening. That's your voice on the tower recording."
"That's my voice," she agreed.
"Before that, twelve years Navy. F/A-18s. Carrier qualified." He glanced down at something on his tablet. "You have a particular citation for a controlled emergency landing on the USS Truman in force-six conditions. Night. Single engine."
"The other engine came back online at about fifty feet. I'd call it less impressive in retrospect."
"I wouldn't." He gestured to the seat across from him. "Sit down, Captain. I want to talk to you about what comes next."
She sat. Through the concourse window, she could see the processing line, could see her passengers still waiting, and Jake at the far end talking to another pair of agents. She noted that the agents talking to Jake were taking notes. She noted that the agents in this room were not.
"What comes next for me specifically," she said, "or for everyone?"
Reeves looked at her steadily. He was maybe fifty, with the kind of face that had decided a long time ago not to be readable. "At the moment those two things are connected."
"All right."
"The object that impacted central Massachusetts was a fragment of a larger body." He said it with the flat precision of someone delivering information that hadn't yet been converted into comprehensible public language. "The larger body is still out there. We've known about it for approximately nineteen months. We've been calling it 2042-Delta."
Maria was quiet for a moment. Outside, a fuel truck rolled past the window, unremarkable. "Nineteen months," she said.
"The impact probability went from low to high over a period of several weeks when new orbital data came in last year. There was a window   a short window   when deflection might have been possible using existing technology. That window closed before the political process caught up with the science." He said this without inflection, as if the judgment embedded in it was so obvious as to not require emphasis. "What struck Massachusetts today was a fragment that separated from the main body during a close solar pass. An unmodeled event."
"And the main body."
"Is still on its original trajectory. Current models give a 68% probability of Earth impact in seventeen years."
Sixty-eight percent. In seventeen years. She did the arithmetic on what that meant for the world she'd been flying through for the past two decades, for the cities she knew from the air, for her sister's house in Tampa and her nephew who wanted chocolate cake.
"What happens in seventeen years if the 68% scenario plays out?"
"Extinction-level event. Main body is approximately three kilometers in diameter. The fragment that hit today was roughly forty meters." He let her do the volume math. "There will be survivors. The human species will persist in some form. But civilization as a continuous enterprise " He stopped. "The models aren't kind."
"And what happens in the other 32% scenario?"
"We get to have this conversation about something else." He almost smiled. "We're not betting on that."
Maria looked at her hands in her lap. Good hands   steady hands, the kind that people had written things about in her performance reviews. They looked normal. "So what are you building?" she asked. Because that was obviously what came next. "And how many pilots do you need?"
For the first time, something moved in Reeves's expression. "Right now? Every single one we can find."
* * *
By the time Maria was escorted to the military transport on the south end of the field, the sun was sitting on the western horizon and the column from the impact was a feature of the sky, not a temporary intrusion   a dark smear that had spread east on the upper-level winds and dimmed the afternoon into something that felt more like late November than mid-October. She'd said goodbye to Jake, who was being processed for a different conversation with different officers. She'd sent another message to her sister. She'd looked at the news one more time and then decided not to look at it again for a while.
The transport held fourteen people, most of them pilots, judging by the quality of the silence they were all keeping. The kind of silence that came from people who processed things professionally and had not yet switched out of professional mode. A woman across from Maria caught her eye and didn't quite smile.
"Navy?" the woman asked.
"Was. You?"
"Marine Corps. Amy Stokes." She had the bearing of it, even in civilian clothes. "Two tours in the Hornet, four in the Super. I was flying cargo out of Providence when it hit."
"Maria Webb. Commercial out of Miami. I was inbound to Boston."
Amy looked at her with something that wasn't quite sympathy. "How close?"
"We were in the pressure wave. Had to bring it down visual at Bradley."
"And they still recruited you?"
"They recruited me because of it, I think."
Amy nodded slowly, and they both looked out the small oval window at the landscape moving beneath them, the lights of Connecticut and then Long Island, the dark expanse of water, and somewhere ahead the Florida coastline that was their destination. The other pilots around them were having similar quiet conversations in pairs, the preliminary taking-of-inventory that happened when competent people found themselves thrown together by events and needed to establish, quickly and without ceremony, who everyone was.
"Did they tell you what we're building?" Amy asked.
"They told me enough."
"Seventeen years."
"Give or take."
Amy was quiet for a moment. Outside, the first stars were appearing in the darkening sky to the east, away from the impact's haze. "I have two kids," she said. "Eight and eleven."
Maria didn't say anything, because there was nothing useful to say. In seventeen years, Amy's children would be twenty-five and twenty-eight. Old enough to understand what was happening. Maybe old enough to do something about it.
"That's why," Amy said, answering her own unasked question. "That's why I got on the transport."
Maria looked out at the stars. She was thinking, for no particular reason, about the first time she'd flown at night from a carrier, the particular loneliness of cutting the catapult shot and going from ship-deck noise to absolute black sky, the instruments the only reality until her eyes adapted. The way the horizon disappeared. The way the stars took over the entire world.
She'd thought that was the most alone a person could feel.
She'd been wrong about that, she suspected. But the training for it had been useful.
The transport flew on through the darkening sky, carrying its cargo of people toward whatever came next, while behind them the column from the impact continued to spread across New England like a permanent bruise on the face of the world.
It was the first day of a new chapter in human history.
Maria Webb had started it thinking about birthday cake.
She ended it wondering what seventeen years was actually long enough to build.
  END OF CHAPTER 1

# The Scattered Worlds: Severance

Chapter 2: Ground Zero
Thomas Rourke | Downtown Boston
October 15th, 2025 | 2:47 PM EST
Thomas Rourke was reaching for his wallet when the sky turned white and the world cracked in half.
He'd ducked into the coffee shop on Washington Street during his lunch break, the same routine he'd followed for two years since joining the architecture firm six blocks away. Large coffee, black. Maybe a sandwich if he was feeling ambitious. Today he'd been feeling ambitious the Henderson project was finally moving forward after months of delays, his boss had actually smiled at him in the morning meeting, and it was one of those October afternoons in Boston that felt like the city was showing off, all amber light and cold clean air.
The barista, a college kid named Emma with paint-stained fingers and the tired eyes of someone working their way through art school, had just handed him his cup. He was reaching for his wallet. That was the last normal moment he would ever be able to remember clearly.
The light came first. Not a flash, not lightning, nothing with an end to it. Pure white, total and sourceless, as if someone had replaced the world with its own negative. Thomas stood frozen in a universe without shadows, coffee in one hand, wallet in the other, while Emma's smile dissolved into confusion.
Then the shockwave.
The front windows blew inward all at once, not shattering so much as ceasing to exist, replaced in the same instant by a wall of pressure and noise and flying glass that lifted Thomas off his feet and threw him backward into the pastry case hard enough to fold the metal display shelf. His coffee cup was somewhere. His wallet was somewhere. Everything hurt in a way that was still catching up to him, the pain running a half-second behind the events that were causing it.
The building shook. Not the polite swaying of the minor seismic events he'd felt during a semester abroad in California, but something deeper, a resonance in the foundations that his architect's training recognized as wrong, as the frequency that old brick-and-mortar wasn't designed to absorb.
He found Emma behind the counter, curled small with her hands over her head. Alive. Conscious. Bleeding from a cut above her eyebrow that looked worse than it probably was, the way scalp wounds always did.
"Emma." He got a hand on her shoulder. "Are you hurt? Anything broken?"
She looked up at him with the expression of someone whose mental model of the world had just been deleted and not yet replaced. "What was that?"
Thomas didn't answer because he didn't know, and the structural cues he was reading from the walls around him were demanding his attention. The crack running from the northeast corner down toward the floor was getting longer as he watched. The ceiling in the back had developed a new geometry. The building was intact for now, but intact and stable were different things, and this building was only the first one.
"We need to get outside."
They picked their way through the debris to the door and stepped into a city that had become unrecognizable in the space of thirty seconds.
Every window on Washington Street had blown out simultaneously, leaving the building facades raw and dark-socketed, brick and concrete suddenly exposed the way a face looks wrong without eyebrows. Cars had stopped at random angles, some with their airbags deployed, drivers standing beside them with the particular stillness of people who don't yet know what to do. A city bus had jumped the curb and come to rest against a fire hydrant, water arcing up in the pale October sunlight with an absurd cheerfulness. Down the block, a construction scaffold had partially collapsed. From somewhere to the south, a car alarm was cycling through its entire repertoire in a loop.
And to the west, above the roofline, rising above everything the column.
Thomas had studied structural failure in school, had written a thesis on load distribution and progressive collapse. He had a technical vocabulary for most things that could go wrong with built environments. He had no vocabulary for what he was looking at. A column of material was climbing the sky above western Massachusetts at a speed that registered as wrong before his brain could classify it as anything else brown at the base, shifting to dark gray and then near-black near the top, still expanding, still climbing, reaching for altitudes that buildings and birds and aircraft occupied and going past them without slowing.
The Quabbin Reservoir area. He knew that geography. Fifty, maybe fifty-five miles west. He'd driven through there with his ex-wife on a leaf-peeping trip three years ago, winding two-lane roads through reservoir-country forest, a general store that sold apple cider and had a hand-painted sign about fresh eggs. Whatever that column was rising from, it had replaced everything he remembered about that drive.
He stood there for a moment on the sidewalk, broken glass around his shoes, holding Emma's elbow because she seemed like she needed it, and he understood with the calm clarity that sometimes arrives in the middle of impossible events that the world he had woken up in this morning was gone. Not damaged. Not disrupted. Gone.
Then someone up the street started shouting for help, and Thomas put the larger understanding away and moved toward the sound.
* * *
The man in the business suit was pinned under a section of metal awning that had been ripped from a storefront and twisted into something that bore no relationship to its original purpose. He was conscious, which was good. He was wedged in a way that suggested moving the debris without care could cause more damage than leaving it, which was bad.
Thomas assessed the geometry. The awning's main beam had come down across the man's legs but hadn't gone through them it was weight, not penetration. Remove the weight carefully and the man could move himself. He looked around and registered that a small crowd had materialized from the chaos, the same instinct that had brought him running now bringing others: a woman in hospital scrubs, a teenage boy, two men in work gear who had the useful look of people who spent their days moving heavy things.
"On three," Thomas said, and nobody questioned it, which told him something about what disaster did to the normal social friction of strangers. He positioned the construction workers at the main beam. "Lift straight up, don't drag it. You two when it moves, pull him clear, toward you, watch his legs."
"I'm a nurse," said the woman in scrubs. Margaret, she told them afterward. "His name is Frank. Frank, keep looking at me."
"One, two, three."
Thomas's left shoulder screamed. He'd dislocated it once in a rugby match and the scar tissue had never entirely forgiven him, but adrenaline was a useful chemical and the beam lifted. Frank came free. Margaret was already kneeling beside him, her hands moving with the practiced efficiency of someone who'd triaged injuries before.
"Pulse is good. Probable fracture in the left tibia but it's not compound. He needs a hospital but he's stable for now."
Thomas straightened and looked down the street. There were more people down there who needed what Frank had needed. He could see at least three other situations from where he was standing, and he could hear more, the particular sounds of people in trouble filtering through the general noise of a city that had stopped working.
"Can anyone here do what you just did?" he asked Margaret.
She looked at him steadily. "I've got maybe an hour of useful supplies in my bag. After that I'm improvising."
"That's an hour more than most people. Come with me."
The group grew as they moved down the street. Emma stayed close, having apparently decided that Thomas was a more reliable landmark than anything else available. A subway engineer named David joined them after emerging from a maintenance access hatch in the middle of the sidewalk, blinking in the changed light. Two college students. A retired firefighter who hadn't had his gear in fifteen years but still remembered what to do with his hands. By the time they reached the next person who needed help, there were eleven of them, and by the time they reached the one after that, there were nineteen, and Thomas had stopped being surprised by this because it turned out that it was simply what people did.
The smell reached them about forty minutes in. Natural gas, the sulfur additive sharp and unmistakable, coming from somewhere under the road.
"Ruptured main," David said immediately, stopping and holding up his hand to stop the group. He'd worked Boston's subway infrastructure for fourteen years and knew the utility grid under these streets the way Thomas knew load calculations. "Could be one break or it could be a cascade. We can't know from up here."
"What does that mean for us?" asked Emma.
"It means we don't go further into this block," Thomas said. He turned to David. "How far does the risk area extend?"
David squinted west, doing calculations Thomas couldn't follow. "Old infrastructure here. Cast iron mains, most of it, pre-1960s stock that the city never got around to replacing. A pressure wave that size could have fractured lines across half the district. We're talking potentially a dozen separate leak points."
"And if someone lights a cigarette," Margaret said.
"Or a generator kicks on. Or a car fires up," David added. "Anything with a spark."
Thomas thought about the people they'd passed who were sitting in their cars. The ones trying to restart dead engines. The ones who'd already gotten their cars running. He thought about the restaurants in this district, every one of them with gas lines running to their kitchens, and the chefs who were probably standing in those kitchens right now wondering what had happened and reaching for familiar routines.
"We need to get people away from this area," he said. "Not just our group. Everyone on these blocks."
"That's an actual job for actual emergency services," the retired firefighter said. His name was Phil. "Where are the actual emergency services?"
Nobody answered, because nobody had seen any. The sirens they could hear were distant and already sounded overwhelmed, the kind of emergency response that was dealing with something larger than it had been built to handle.
"Then it's us," Thomas said. "Phil, you know how to clear a gas emergency."
"I know how to clear a gas emergency with proper equipment."
"You know more than anyone else here. Take the east side of the street, we'll take the west. Knock on every door, get everyone moving south toward the Common. Don't let anyone run engines, don't let anyone use electrical switches, tell them to leave their phones off if the phones are still working."
Phil looked at him for a long moment. "You're an architect."
"Structural engineer, technically. But right now I'm just the person who knows what a gas leak can do to a city block."
Phil looked at him the way contractors looked at him on job sites when they were deciding whether the person holding the drawings actually understood the drawings. Thomas had been on the receiving end of that assessment enough times to know the only thing that passed it was looking like you already knew the answer to the question being silently asked.
He apparently passed, because Phil turned to the east side of the street without another word. Thomas turned to the west.
The next ninety minutes were the most exhausting of his life, including the summer he'd spent doing AmeriCorps construction work in Mississippi and the winter he'd crammed for his structural engineering license in six weeks. Door to door through a city that smelled like a gas station and sounded like the last act of something, knocking on doors that sometimes opened and sometimes didn't, delivering variations on the same message: there may be a gas leak, we need you to leave the building, no you cannot take your car, yes it is serious, no I am not from the city, I'm just someone who knows about gas mains, please come with me.
Some people came immediately. Some argued. A few slammed their doors, which Thomas couldn't afford to spend time on. An elderly woman in a third-floor apartment had lost her stairwell railing to the shockwave and needed two people to get her down safely, Margaret from below and Thomas from above while his shoulder tried to renegotiate the terms of their working relationship.
They moved maybe three hundred people out of those two blocks in ninety minutes. Thomas had no way to know if it was enough, or if the gas situation was as dangerous as David had assessed, or if fire department units would arrive in the next ten minutes and make all of it beside the point. You did the thing in front of you with the information you had. Every structural engineer learned eventually about the gap between design documents and what actually happened when load met reality.
No explosion came. The blocks sat quiet and gas-smelling in the October afternoon while their former occupants joined the southward flow toward the Common, and Thomas filed this under wins and kept moving.
* * *
Boston Common had been a public park for nearly four centuries, which meant it had seen a great deal of history and had strong opinions about none of it. It absorbed the two thousand displaced residents of the surrounding neighborhoods with the same indifference it had shown to colonial militias and political protests and the occasional outdoor concert. Tents were going up in the corners where park maintenance kept equipment. The Red Cross had arrived with blankets and water that was going to run out before the night did. A man with a bullhorn was trying to organize people by district so that families who'd gotten separated might find each other.
Thomas sat on a bench near the Frog Pond and let his left shoulder stop pretending it was fine. Margaret had wrapped it in something improvised from her kit, not a proper sling but better than nothing, and the adrenaline had finally metabolized enough for the pain to assert its full personality.
Emma sat beside him. David on the other side. Their group had dispersed across the Common in ones and twos as people found family members or found corners to be alone in, but these two had stayed, which Thomas supposed made them friends now, the way disasters compressed the normal timeline of acquaintance into something unrecognizable.
"My wife is in Cambridge," David said. He'd said it before. He would probably say it again.
"Cambridge took less damage," Margaret said, from somewhere behind them. She'd found someone's portable radio and had been translating its fragments of news into human language for the past hour. "The pressure wave attenuated going east. They're saying everything within thirty miles of the impact site is a total loss, but thirty-five miles out it drops off fast. Cambridge is probably forty-two miles."
David nodded slowly, working through the arithmetic of relief.
Thomas pulled out his phone. The screen was cracked from when he'd hit the pastry case, a spider-web fracture across the upper corner, but the thing still worked, and the cellular signal had flickered back to life in the past twenty minutes as emergency systems came online. He had sixteen missed calls and a string of texts from his parents in Connecticut, from his college roommate in New York, from coworkers who'd been elsewhere in the city when it happened. He'd sent the same three words to all of them before the signal died again: alive, Washington Street.
He scrolled to a number he'd had in his phone for six years and almost never used. Stared at it. The time delay to Mars was currently somewhere around eight minutes each way, which meant any call was really a recording. He composed a text instead, because texts felt less like a performance.
Just alive. Boston was bad. Thinking of you. Let me know you got this.
He sent it and put the phone away and looked at the sky, where the column from the impact had spread into a general overcast that was stealing the remaining light from the October afternoon, turning everything the color of old pewter.
Around him the Common filled with the sounds of a city doing what cities had always done at the end of catastrophes: the low murmur of people finding each other, the occasional sharp cry of recognition when a search ended well, the quieter sounds when searches ended otherwise. A group of children near the bandstand had started a game that had no rules Thomas could identify, the way children will organize themselves when the adults are otherwise occupied. An elderly couple sat on a bench twenty feet away holding hands with the practiced ease of people who had been holding each other's hands through difficulties for a very long time.
Thomas was an architect. He spent his professional life thinking about how buildings met the ground, how structures transferred load, how the things people built were supposed to outlast the people who built them. He was good at thinking about permanence.
What had hit Massachusetts today was a forty-meter rock. The briefings that would come later would give it a precise diameter and a precise mass and a precise velocity and a precise energy-release figure in megatons, and all of those numbers would be correct and none of them would capture the experience of standing on Washington Street watching the sky go wrong.
But the rock that had hit today was not the rock the astronomers had been worried about. Thomas didn't know that yet. Nobody in Boston Common knew it yet. They were still inside the first catastrophe, the comprehensible one, the one with a single cause and a specific location and a brown column still climbing the western sky.
The second fact was making its way toward them from JPL and the observatories that had stayed online through the atmospheric disruption, working through the institutional channels that would eventually produce a press conference and a number: 68% probability. Seventeen years.
But that was still hours away, and for now Thomas Rourke sat on a bench in Boston Common with a dislocated shoulder and a cracked phone and people on either side of him who had been strangers this morning, and watched the October light die under a sky that would never look quite the same again.
He'd helped pull nine people out of debris today. He didn't know yet that this would become the skill most relevant to his life for the next two decades. He didn't know yet that the city he'd spent three years learning, block by block, coffee shop by architecture firm by subway stop, would become the laboratory where he would understand what it meant to be left behind while the species figured out how to leave.
He just knew that it was getting cold, and the blankets were running out, and the people around him were going to need something warmer than hope to get through the night.
He stood up, told his shoulder to manage itself for a while longer, and went to find out who was organizing the blanket distribution and what they needed.
The man with the bullhorn was named Ray, and he was overwhelmed in the specific way of someone who had expected to be organizing something much smaller. Ray was a parks department employee who had been on shift when everything happened, which made him the de facto authority over Boston Common by virtue of being the only person there who technically worked there. He had a radio that reached other parks department staff and not much else.
"I need people who know how to talk to crowds without making them panic," Ray said when Thomas introduced himself. "And I need someone to figure out where the next water is coming from because we have maybe two hours left."
Thomas organized both. The crowd-talking turned out to come naturally to David, who had spent fourteen years making PA announcements in subway stations and had the particular skill of projecting calm authority into spaces full of anxious people. The water situation was harder. Thomas spent an hour working through the logic of it: the mains in this part of the city might or might not be intact, the fire hydrants they'd passed earlier were flowing which meant pressure somewhere in the system, the parks maintenance building had a utility connection that might be testable. He got David to help him locate the connection, tested it, found it clean enough for now, and got Ray to announce the location.
By nine o'clock the Common had settled into the uncomfortable rhythms of an improvised shelter. Fires had been built in the open areas away from the trees, using wood from a maintenance pile that Ray had unlocked without being asked by anybody with authority to unlock it. The Red Cross volunteers who had arrived in the late afternoon were running on their training and their adrenaline, cataloging the injured and doing what they could with what they had. The man with the cracked phone was texting his wife in Cambridge; the elderly couple on the bench had accepted tea from a thermos pressed on them by a college student who had more thermoses than blankets and had prioritized accordingly.
Thomas sat down again and let himself be still for a while.
He thought about the Henderson project, which had been important enough this morning to put a small satisfied knot in his chest during the meeting. He thought about the building he'd been designing: twelve stories, mixed residential and commercial, seismically upgraded foundation, blast-resistant lower-floor glazing per the new post-2020 codes that everybody in the industry had rolled their eyes at when they came in. He thought about how the building had been designed to handle impacts that the code-writers had imagined as theoretical, and how this afternoon had removed the word theoretical from that sentence.
He thought about the column still visible against the darkening sky to the west, now lit orange and bronze at its lower edge by whatever fires were burning in the impact zone. Fifty-five miles. He'd driven through there. He'd bought apple cider from a hand-painted sign.
He pulled out his cracked phone and checked for a reply from the number he'd texted earlier. Nothing yet. The delay to Mars was sixteen minutes round-trip right now, he knew that vaguely from news coverage over the years, which meant that even if the message had gotten through and a reply had been sent immediately, he was still waiting. He put the phone back in his pocket.
He didn't know yet about the 68%. He didn't know yet that the rock that had done this today was a fragment, a piece that had sheared off the main body during a solar approach, an unplanned preview. He would learn that tomorrow, from a press conference, sitting in this same park with a shoulder that had been properly reset by a paramedic and a cup of coffee that someone had produced from somewhere and handed to him without explanation.
Tonight he only knew what he could see: a city that had been intact this morning and was not intact now, full of people who were managing, more or less, because people generally managed more than you expected when you put them in the same place with a problem that needed solving and removed the normal social architecture that kept them from talking to each other.
He checked his phone one more time. Still nothing from the Mars number. He didn't know enough about the relay infrastructure to know if that meant the message hadn't arrived, or had arrived and not been read, or had been read and the reply was still crossing the distance between worlds at the speed of light, eight minutes out and counting.
He put the phone back in his pocket and watched the fire across the Common, and waited.

# The Scattered Worlds: Severance

Chapter 3: Red Horizon
Dr. Marcus Webb | Mars Station Olympus
October 15th, 2025 | 9:23 AM Mars Local Solar Time
Marcus Webb was three sentences into his weekly report when Earth stopped answering.
It happened mid-transmission, which was the thing that made it wrong from the first second. Morrison had been talking, the familiar eight-minute-delayed voice of Houston Flight Director Greg Morrison, reliable as a clock for three years, walking through the agenda items for their Wednesday window. Standard atmospheric data requests. A note about the upcoming equipment resupply timeline. Then, mid-sentence, the carrier signal dropped to nothing and stayed there.
Marcus waited. Solar interference sometimes produced dropouts of a few seconds. He'd learned patience in the communication chair the same way he'd learned patience for everything else on Mars: because impatience cost energy the station couldn't afford, and the planet was indifferent to how urgently you needed something to happen.
Two minutes. Five. Eight, which was the round-trip light delay, the minimum time for a reply to any transmission. Then eight more.
"Houston, Mars Station Olympus. We appear to have lost your signal mid-transmission. Please confirm status, over."
He already knew the answer wasn't coming. The quality of the silence was different from a technical dropout. A dropout had texture, residual carrier hiss, the sound of a channel that existed but was temporarily blocked. This was the silence of a channel that had simply ceased to be.
"Dr. Reeves." He kept his voice level, reaching for the intercom without taking his eyes off the console displays. Dr. Owen Reeves was their communications engineer, and if there was a technical explanation Marcus had missed, Reeves would find it in about ninety seconds. "Communications center. Now, please."
Reeves arrived still holding a coffee bulb, took one look at Marcus's expression and one look at the displays, and set the coffee down with the careful deliberateness of someone who had just decided they might not pick it up again for a while.
"All green on our end," Reeves said, moving to the secondary console without being asked. "Transmitter array nominal. Dish alignment confirmed. Signal integrity across our entire system is..." He paused, cycling through diagnostics. "Perfect. We're broadcasting fine. There's just nobody receiving."
"Try the backup channels."
Reeves was already trying them. ESA's ground control in Darmstadt. The Deep Space Network station at Goldstone in California. Madrid. Canberra. The emergency military relay frequencies that were supposed to be hardened against any terrestrial disruption. Each one returned the same answer, which was no answer at all.
"Marcus," Reeves said quietly, "this isn't our equipment."
"No." Marcus stood and straightened his jacket, a habit left over from the days when straightening his jacket meant he was about to walk into a room and say something difficult. "Get everyone to the main conference room. Emergency protocols."
* * *
The twelve members of Mars Station Olympus filled the conference room with the particular quality of silence that came from people who were scared and were trying not to show it in front of each other. Marcus had learned to read that silence over three years of small crises: equipment failures, a medical emergency in year two, the dust storm that had kept them station-bound for nineteen days. He knew how to work with it.
He laid out the facts the way he always laid out facts: in order, without editorializing, with the uncertainty clearly marked.
"At 09:47 local time we lost contact with Houston mid-transmission. All backup communication channels are non-responsive. Equipment diagnostics show no fault on our end. We have been unable to reach any Earth-based station on any frequency, including emergency military relays that are designed to survive significant terrestrial disruptions." He paused. "We don't know why. We are continuing to attempt contact."
Dr. Owen Reeves, from the communications seat: "How long until we'd expect natural signal restoration if this were a solar event?"
"No unusual solar activity on our monitors. If it were a coronal mass ejection significant enough to knock out global communications, we'd have seen the precursor readings."
Dr. Nora Finch, their station physician, from the far end of the table: "Are we considering the possibility of a ground-based event? Something that damaged the infrastructure on Earth's end?"
The room went very still. Marcus had been considering exactly that possibility for the past forty minutes and had been trying to find an alternative explanation that fit the data equally well. He hadn't found one.
"It's one of the scenarios we have to consider, yes."
"What kind of ground-based event takes out every relay simultaneously?" That was Dr. Paul Garrett, their structural geologist, the crew member who most reliably asked the question everyone else was thinking but not saying. "Goldstone, Madrid, Canberra, military frequencies. You're talking about a global infrastructure failure. What does that?"
Nobody answered because the answers were all large and terrible and none of them wanted to be the person who said them out loud first.
Marcus let the silence sit for exactly long enough, then: "We maintain station operations. Normal duty rotations continue. Dr. Reeves stays on communications. Everyone else keeps to their assignments. We don't know what's happened and we don't act as if we do until we have actual information."
"And if the information doesn't come?" Garrett asked.
"Then we'll deal with that when we reach it. Right now, we work with what we have."
The meeting broke up with the particular efficiency of people who needed something to do with their hands. Marcus stayed behind after the others filed out, looking at the communication console through the window of the conference room, watching Reeves work through another systematic scan of every frequency they had access to. The displays glowed steady green. All systems nominal. Broadcasting into a void that wasn't sending anything back.
* * *
The first fragment of signal arrived fifty-nine hours after the blackout began, picked up by Reeves at 3:14 in the morning station time during a frequency scan he'd been running on rotation since the silence started.
Marcus was in the communications center inside four minutes, still pulling on his jacket. The recording was weak, bouncing off the ionosphere in ways that compressed and distorted the audio, but the words were there if you listened three or four times.
"...major impact event confirmed, western Massachusetts... casualty figures not yet released pending... infrastructure collapse across six states... all civilian aviation grounded..."
The signal dropped back into static.
Marcus played it again. Then again. Then he sat with it for a moment before keying the all-station intercom.
"This is Webb. All hands to the conference room."
He played the recording for the crew at 3:30 in the morning and watched their faces while they listened. The twelve of them in that room had chosen Mars over Earth for different reasons. Some for the science, some for the challenge, some because the particular personality type that volunteered for four-year Mars assignments tended to find Earth slightly too crowded and slightly too loud. They all had family down there. Parents, siblings, friends, partners. The people whose faces they'd last seen through a departure terminal window, who'd grown smaller and then disappeared as the transport climbed away from Earth.
"Impact event," Nora Finch said when it finished. Not a question. Processing.
"Western Massachusetts," Reeves said. "That's Boston area. Greater Boston metro has four million people."
"Had," Garrett said, and immediately looked like he wished he hadn't.
Marcus let them sit with the word for a moment. Then: "We're going to keep scanning. There will be more fragments as emergency systems come back online and more transmitters reach the ionosphere. We'll build a clearer picture over the next several days. Until then, we don't know the scale. We don't know anything beyond what that recording said."
"My brother is in Worcester," said Dr. Anne Sato, their atmospheric systems engineer. She said it to the table, not to anyone in particular, the way you say a thing that needs to be said because keeping it inside has stopped being an option.
"I know." Marcus kept his voice steady. There was nothing else useful to say. "I know. All we can do right now is keep listening."
* * *
Over the following four days, the fragments accumulated into something approaching a picture, though it was a picture with large sections still missing and the edges still unclear.
The object had been approximately forty meters across. A fragment of a larger body, the broadcasts kept repeating, which was a phrase that nobody on the station talked about directly but that Marcus noticed everyone noticing. A fragment of a larger body. Meaning the larger body was still out there. Meaning this was not the whole story.
The impact site was the Quabbin Reservoir watershed in central Massachusetts. The energy release had been equivalent to somewhere between 250 and 320 megatons, depending on the source of the estimate and how much of the broadcasts they'd managed to catch clearly. The pressure wave had caused structural damage across six states. Casualty estimates in the early fragments had said millions; later fragments were more careful with language in ways that suggested the earlier estimates had been, if anything, conservative.
Global communication infrastructure had been disrupted by two separate mechanisms, and Marcus had spent part of the third day working through the physics of both. The impact had produced enough atmospheric ionization to interfere with radio propagation across the northern hemisphere for the first several hours, not a true electromagnetic pulse the way a nuclear detonation would generate one, but enough plasma disruption in the upper atmosphere to degrade satellite links and HF communications across a wide area. More significantly, the physical destruction of the northeastern United States had severed the undersea fiber cables and satellite ground stations concentrated in that corridor, which handled a disproportionate share of global internet traffic. The Northeast wasn't just a region. It was infrastructure. When it went dark, it took significant portions of the global communications backbone with it. Systems were coming back online gradually as backup routing activated across Atlantic cables further south, which was why the fragments were getting clearer. Within another week, Marcus estimated, they'd have something close to a real connection again.
He kept a private log during those four days, separate from the official station records. Not because the content needed to be hidden but because the official log was for facts and this was for something else. What he wrote in it was mostly questions. Questions about the resupply mission timeline and whether the political infrastructure to authorize it would survive what Earth was going through. Questions about what self-sufficiency actually meant for a station designed as a temporary research post rather than a permanent settlement. Questions about the phrase a fragment of a larger body, and what the scientists at JPL and ESA were doing right now, and what they were finding.
On the third day Lena Shaw knocked on his door after the dinner rotation and asked if she could sit down, and he said yes, and she pulled out her own tablet and walked him through the hydroponics expansion she'd been modeling since the second day of the blackout. She'd already done the work. She'd already run the numbers. She was showing him a plan, not raising a problem, which told him something important about Lena and something important about the crew.
"How long have you been working on this?" he asked.
"Since the first morning," she said. "When the backup channels didn't answer."
He looked at the projections. A thirty-six percent increase in caloric output from the existing greenhouse footprint, achievable with materials currently on station, requiring approximately 900 hours of labor spread across the crew over eight weeks. A second phase using the northern water ice deposits that would push production to something like actual sustainability. A third phase that Lena had labeled speculatively, which involved dome expansion and outdoor cultivation beds under UV-filtering panels, which was either visionary or irrelevant depending on how the next year went.
"The crew doesn't know you've built this," he said.
"No. I wanted to show you first."
"Why?"
She thought about it. "Because if I showed everyone, it would feel like a decision that had already been made. I wanted you to decide if it should be a decision first."
Marcus looked at the plan for a long time. Then he thanked her and said he'd think about it. She left, and he sat with the tablet, and after about twenty minutes he closed it and went to bed, and lay there in the dark listening to the life support systems and thinking about the difference between preparing for something and choosing it.
In the meantime, the twelve of them were running Mars Station on the information they had and the uncertainty they couldn't resolve, and Marcus was watching his crew handle it with the kind of careful professionalism that told him they were all spending their off-hours somewhere else entirely.
It was Dr. Lena Shaw who said what everyone had been circling around. She said it in the corridor outside the greenhouse module, not in a meeting, not officially, which was how the most important things on the station usually got said.
"Marcus. If it's as bad as it sounds, what does that mean for the resupply mission?"
The resupply mission. Scheduled in eleven months. The transport that was supposed to bring fresh equipment, replacement parts for systems showing wear, four new crew members, and the materials they needed to expand the hydroponics bay to support a larger population.
Marcus had been running that calculation since the second day of the blackout. "I don't know yet."
"If Earth's infrastructure is as damaged as the fragments suggest, and if the casualty figures are in the range we're hearing, the political and economic capacity to run a Mars resupply mission in eleven months..." She didn't finish the sentence.
"I know."
"We have consumables for fourteen months at current population. Eighteen if we reduce non-essential power consumption and tighten the food ratios. Beyond that, we need either the resupply or we need to have significantly expanded our own production capacity."
"I know, Lena."
"So we have maybe six months to decide which problem we're solving."
She went back into the greenhouse and he stood in the corridor for a moment, listening to the air recyclers and the distant hum of the reactor and the twelve small sounds of twelve people going about the business of staying alive on another planet.
* * *
He called the meeting five days after the first fragment, when they had enough information to have a real conversation and not enough to have a complete one. He laid out the supply situation the way Lena had laid it out to him, without softening the numbers. He laid out the communication trajectory, his estimate that they'd have reliable contact with Earth within another week. He laid out what they knew about the impact and what they didn't.
Then he asked the question.
"We have a decision to make about how we use the next six months. Option one: we operate as a research station awaiting resupply, maintaining our current mission parameters and trusting that Earth's response capacity will recover in time. Option two: we treat the resupply as uncertain and begin immediately expanding our self-sufficiency. That means redirecting research capacity to food production, construction, infrastructure. It means acting as if no help is coming even if help does come."
"What does option two look like in practice?" Garrett asked.
"We expand the hydroponics bay using materials already on station. We begin extracting and processing water ice from the northern deposits we mapped last year but haven't touched because we didn't need to. We start fabricating component parts for the systems most likely to fail and that we can't improvise replacements for." Marcus paused. "We start thinking of this station as a permanent settlement rather than a temporary research post."
The room was quiet.
"That's a significant shift in what we are," Nora said.
"Yes."
"If we go to option two and the resupply arrives on schedule, we've spent six months doing work that wasn't necessary."
"Correct. We'll have a better-supplied, more resilient station and we'll have delayed some research timelines."
"And if we stay with option one and the resupply doesn't come?"
Marcus let the question sit.
Anne Sato spoke from the far end of the table, the first time she'd said anything in the meeting. "My brother is in Worcester. Was in Worcester." She corrected herself with the flat precision of someone who had already done the internal accounting. "I've been thinking about this since the first day. About what it means to be here while that's happening there. About whether anything we do on this station changes anything about that."
Nobody interrupted her.
"I think option two is the right answer. Not because the resupply won't come, but because we've been operating as if Mars is temporary and Earth is real, and I don't think that's true anymore. I think we need to start operating as if both are real. As if this place matters on its own terms."
Marcus watched the rest of the crew while Anne spoke, reading faces. He'd spent three years learning how twelve people handled stress and uncertainty and disagreement, which of them went quiet when they were processing and which went quiet when they were withdrawing, which ones needed to be asked directly before they'd say what they actually thought. What he saw now was eleven people listening with the attentiveness of people who had already been thinking the same thing and were waiting for permission to say it.
"Are there objections to option two?" he asked.
Dr. Peter Walsh, their electronics engineer, raised a hand. "Not an objection. A question. If we commit to option two, what does that mean for the research mission? Do we abandon it?"
"We deprioritize it temporarily. The atmospheric research, the geological survey work, the long-baseline observations, all of that continues at reduced capacity. Nothing gets abandoned. We just stop treating it as the primary purpose of being here."
"And the primary purpose becomes what, exactly?"
Marcus thought about how to answer that honestly. "Staying alive. Building something that doesn't require Earth's permission to exist."
Walsh nodded slowly. "Okay."
"I'll take a vote," Marcus said. "Everyone gets one. Write it on paper, fold it once, pass it to me. I'll count them and report the result."
He'd expected more deliberation. There was less than he'd anticipated. People wrote quickly, as if they'd already decided and were only waiting for the formal mechanism. The slips came back to him in under two minutes, eleven of them folded identically, and he unfolded each one and read it and set it face-down on the table.
Eleven votes for option two.
He hadn't written his own yet. He did it now, on a separate slip, looked at what he'd written, and added it to the pile.
"Unanimous," he said. "We begin expansion protocols tomorrow, 06:00 station time. Task assignments by end of day today. Lena, I'll want your hydroponics plan distributed to the full crew this afternoon."
Lena nodded without looking surprised, which meant she'd had the plan ready to distribute since the night she'd shown it to him.
The meeting broke up quickly, people moving toward their quarters or their stations with the purposeful energy of a crew that had been given something concrete to do, a problem with actual edges. Marcus stayed until the room was empty, watching them go through the window of the conference room, then walked to the observation port at the far end of the module.
Outside, the Martian afternoon was doing what Martian afternoons did: the light flat and reddish and without warmth, the dust on the plain stirring in low eddies, the sky the color of a bruise fading toward brown at the horizon. The nearest feature was a low ridge about four kilometers out that the crew had named, without any official authorization, the Seam, because of the way a dark mineral stratum ran through it like a stitch. Marcus had hiked to the Seam twice in four years, once for a geological sample run and once because he'd needed to be somewhere that wasn't the station for an afternoon. Both times he'd stood at the base of it and looked back at the habitat domes and felt the full weight of the distance between that collection of pressurized modules and everything else that had ever mattered to him.
Earth was up there somewhere in the afternoon sky, washed out by the diffuse Martian daylight, invisible until evening. A point of light. The third planet. Home, or what had been home, or what was still home depending on how you defined the word.
He'd voted for option two. He'd known he would before he sat down to write it. What surprised him was something else, something he was going to have to be honest with himself about before he could trust his own judgment on what came next.
When he'd unfolded those eleven slips and read them, one after another, he'd felt something he hadn't expected.
Relief.
Not at the crisis itself. Not at the suffering that had produced this moment. But at the decision. At the clean, uncomplicated clarity of twelve people choosing to be here, to make this place real on its own terms, to stop holding Mars as a temporary inconvenience between them and the lives they'd left behind. He'd been carrying a low-grade discomfort for most of three years that he'd never quite named, and when the last slip came back and he read it and set it down, the discomfort was gone.
He wasn't sure what that said about him.
The broadcasts had kept repeating one phrase that he couldn't stop turning over. A fragment of a larger body. Every source that mentioned the impactor described it that way, as if the phrase were a technical designation. And technically it was. But what it meant was that the thing that had killed however many people were under that column in Massachusetts was not the thing the astronomers had been watching. It was a piece that had broken off. The larger body was still out there, still moving, still on whatever trajectory the orbital mechanics dictated.
He didn't know the numbers yet. The fragments from Earth hadn't carried anything specific. But he'd spent four years tracking celestial mechanics as part of his station's atmospheric research program, and he understood enough about orbital dynamics to know that a body large enough to produce a forty-meter fragment during a solar approach was a body that warranted serious attention. The kind of attention that would require, among other things, people who were not on Earth.
Marcus was an astronomer by training. He'd come to Mars because the atmospheric research was genuinely interesting and because he'd wanted, in some part of himself he didn't examine too closely, to stand somewhere that wasn't Earth and look at the stars without the interference of a thick wet atmosphere and eight billion neighbors. He'd gotten both. He'd also gotten three years of learning what it took to keep twelve people alive in a place that would kill them efficiently and without malice if any of a hundred systems failed.
He filed the question about his relief somewhere he could find it again. He was going to need to keep examining it, because the relief was real and the situation that had produced it was terrible and those two things were both true and he needed to hold both of them without letting either one crowd out the other.
The afternoon light shifted, the sun dropping toward the western horizon at its unhurried Martian pace, the shadow of the habitat extending slowly across the rust-colored ground. In the greenhouse module he could see Lena already at work, the green of the grow lights warm through the translucent panels, life being methodically maintained against a landscape that had never produced any.
Tomorrow they would start building something. He didn't know yet exactly what it would become. But he knew, with more certainty than he'd felt about anything in a long time, that building it was the right thing to do.
The larger body was still out there. Somebody needed to be somewhere other than Earth when it arrived.

# The Scattered Worlds: Severance

Chapter 4: The Weight of Selection
Dr. Elena Marsh | Crew Selection Office, Houston
November 3rd, 2025 | 8:44 AM CST
The job had a title that sounded clinical and a reality that was something else entirely: Dr. Elena Marsh, Lead Behavioral Specialist, Luna-Ark Crew Selection Program. What it meant in practice was that she was the person who decided which families got to live.
She had accepted the position three weeks ago, two days after the Boston impact, sitting in the office of Dr. James Hartley at JPL while the television in the corner replayed the column rising over central Massachusetts for the fourteenth consecutive hour. Hartley had laid out the numbers the way he laid out all numbers, with the flat precision of someone who had spent thirty years delivering bad news to funding committees and had developed a specific affectlessness for it. The object that had hit Massachusetts was approximately forty meters across. The object they were now tracking, the one the fragment had broken from during a close solar pass eight months earlier, was closer to 340 meters. Impact probability for October 2042: 68%. Energy release if it hit: somewhere in the range of 120,000 megatons, enough to end agricultural civilization in the northern hemisphere within two years of impact.
Elena had sat with those numbers for a moment. Then she'd asked what he needed from her.
What he needed, it turned out, was someone to build a system for selecting 4,000 people from the roughly nine billion currently alive on Earth, evaluate their psychological fitness for a forty-year journey in a closed environment, and do it without the process becoming either a lottery or a eugenics program. The political pressure was already building from every direction: governments wanting national quotas, corporations wanting to protect key personnel, religious groups demanding representation, geneticists arguing for optimized diversity. Someone had to hold the line between all of it and produce a crew that could actually survive the journey.
She'd said yes. She wasn't sure, three weeks later, whether that had been courage or a failure of imagination about what the job would actually involve.
The first application file was open on her screen. A family from Austin, Texas. Father, forty-one, civil engineer with a specialty in pressurized structure design. Mother, thirty-eight, emergency medicine physician. Two children, sixteen and fourteen. The family had submitted their application within forty-eight hours of the public announcement, which told Elena something about them, though she wasn't yet sure what. Prepared, maybe. Or frightened enough to move immediately. Sometimes those were the same thing.
She pulled up the psychological screening protocol she'd drafted over the past two weeks and looked at it for a long moment. Eighteen assessment categories. Stress response under isolation. Conflict resolution tendencies. Attachment patterns. Tolerance for uncertainty. She'd built it from the existing literature on long-duration spaceflight psychology, submarine crew studies, Antarctic winter-over research, and her own fifteen years of clinical practice. It was, she believed, a solid instrument.
It was also going to break people's hearts at a scale she was having trouble fully conceptualizing.
She closed the Austin family's file without reading further and went to find coffee.
* * *
The selection office occupied the third floor of a building on the JSC campus that had previously been used for astronaut training administration. It had the particular institutional character of government facilities that had been repurposed in a hurry: desks that didn't quite match, whiteboards with the ghost-marks of previous projects still visible under fresh paint, a conference room that smelled faintly of the carpet cleaner someone had used to address whatever the previous occupants had spilled.
Her team was small for the scale of the work. Dr. Kim Aldridge, thirty-three, ship psychologist and Elena's most capable hire, who had come from a background in long-duration spaceflight research at the University of Colorado and who had the useful quality of being able to hold large amounts of difficult information without visibly buckling under it. Three intake coordinators who handled the administrative processing of applications. A data analyst named Greg Foster who had built the selection database and who Elena suspected was running his own family's application through it on the side, though she hadn't confronted him about it because she wasn't sure she wanted to know.
"First batch of psychological assessments came back overnight," Kim said, setting a tablet on Elena's desk. "Sixty-three families from the initial application pool. I flagged the ones I think need a second look."
Elena picked up the tablet. Kim had flagged nineteen of the sixty-three, which was a higher rate than Elena had expected. "Walk me through your reasoning on the flags."
"Most of them are stress-response issues. The impact was three weeks ago and a lot of people are still in acute trauma response, which makes the psychological assessments unreliable right now. They're answering questions about long-term resilience from inside a crisis, which skews everything." Kim sat down across from her. "My recommendation is we retest the flagged group in ninety days and weight the second assessment more heavily."
"We may not have ninety days of flexibility in the timeline."
"I know. But if we select crew based on trauma-state assessments we're going to end up with people whose actual psychological profiles are different from what we measured. That's a mission risk."
Elena looked at the flagged files. Nineteen families, each one a stack of forms and questionnaire responses and interview notes that represented real people who had real reasons for wanting off this planet and real reasons they might not survive the journey. "What's the split on the non-flagged group?"
"Thirty-one that I'd advance to the next stage. Thirteen that I'd remove from consideration based on psychological grounds alone, separate from any medical or technical screening."
"What grounds?"
Kim was quiet for a moment, choosing words. "Three families with patterns consistent with coercive family dynamics. Two with what look like significant substance dependence histories that weren't disclosed in the initial application. The rest are various combinations of impulse control, conflict escalation tendencies, and what I'd call magical thinking about the mission itself."
"Magical thinking how?"
"They've idealized it. The journey, the destination, the whole thing. They're not applying because they've made a rational calculation about survival odds. They're applying because they've decided this is a spiritual calling or an adventure or some kind of chosen-people narrative. Those people are going to have a very hard time in year six when the reality of forty years in a closed environment stops being abstract."
Elena thought about that. It wasn't wrong. But it raised a question she'd been turning over since she took the job. "Is the alternative better? Someone who's made a purely rational calculation about survival odds has already accepted that Earth probably ends. Living with that knowledge for forty years in a closed environment has its own psychological profile."

Elena set the tablet down. Outside the window, the JSC campus was moving at the particular pace of an institution that had been given an impossible mandate and was trying to staff up fast enough to meet it. Apparently the engineering team is concerned about the interaction between certain psychological profiles and extended suspension." She paused. "They want us to add it to the assessment matrix."
Elena felt something shift in her attention. "What kind of interaction?"

Kim's expression was careful, the look of someone who had noticed something and wasn't sure yet whether to say it. "It's the first time the medical team has flagged a psychological component to the cryosleep question. Previously they've been treating it as purely an engineering problem."
"Send me the full note."
"Already in your inbox."
Elena waited until Kim left, then opened the email. Two short paragraphs from the medical subcommittee, asking behavioral science to begin factoring cryosleep compatibility into the screening criteria. She read it twice, then called Hartley.
"James. The cryosleep note."
"I saw it."
"They're asking me to screen for suspension compatibility. The technology doesn't exist yet."
A pause. "The design concept calls for rotating suspension cycles. Five hundred crew awake, five hundred under, switching every five years over the forty-year transit. The biology is...still theoretical. We have short-duration mammal studies. Human multi-year suspension is the part we're working toward."
"So you're asking me to screen people for a procedure that hasn't been proven."
"I'm asking you to keep the question open. If cryosleep doesn't pan out, the ship design changes entirely. Fully conscious crew for forty years. That's a different psychological profile than what you're currently building for, and I'd rather know now than in fifteen years."
Elena looked at her assessment matrix. Eighteen categories, none of them built around the question of who could stay sane for four decades in a sealed environment with no escape and no news from home. "I'll add it to the framework. But I'm noting in the record that I'm screening for a theoretical requirement."
"Noted."
She hung up and pulled up the Austin family's file again.
* * *
Her daughter called at 6:15, while Elena was still at her desk working through the second batch of files. Sofia's face on the phone screen had the slightly too-bright quality of someone who had been crying recently and had made an effort to look like she hadn't.
"Mom. Are you okay?"
"I'm fine. Working late. Are you okay?"
"I've been watching the news." Sofia was twenty, studying bioengineering at UT Austin, and had her father's habit of leading with the practical question when what she actually wanted to talk about was something harder. "They're saying the impact probability is 68%."
"That's the current estimate, yes."
"And you're the one choosing who gets on the ships."
"One of the people choosing. There's a whole program."
"But you're running the psychological side."
"Yes."
Sofia was quiet for a moment. On the screen Elena could see her daughter's dorm room behind her, the organized chaos of a twenty-year-old's living space, textbooks and a half-eaten container of takeout and a poster of a molecular structure Elena couldn't identify from this angle. The ordinary archaeology of a life in progress.
"Are we on the list?" Sofia asked."
Elena had been waiting for this question since she took the job. She'd prepared an answer for it, something measured about how the selection process worked and why she couldn't discuss individual cases. The prepared answer dissolved when she actually looked at her daughter's face.
"I haven't looked," she said. Which was true. She had deliberately not run her family's profiles through the assessment matrix, partly for ethical reasons and partly because she wasn't ready for the answer.
"Dad's a structural engineer," Sofia said. "That's exactly what they need for the ships. And you're the person building the selection criteria. And I'm..." She stopped.
"You're what?"
"I looked up the crew profile requirements online. Bioengineers are on the priority list. Agricultural bioengineering specifically, which is basically my entire major." She said it with the careful flatness of someone reporting a fact they hadn't decided how to feel about yet. "So we probably qualify. All three of us."
Elena looked at her daughter on the screen and thought about the families she'd been reading about all day. The ones who would qualify and the ones who wouldn't and the ones she hadn't gotten to yet who were sitting in their own homes having some version of this same conversation.
"Sofia. I'm not going to have this conversation tonight. Not because I'm avoiding it, but because I genuinely don't know enough yet to have it honestly. There are seventeen years between now and the ship launch. A lot can change."
"A lot can not change. 2042-Delta can hit exactly where they think it's going to hit."
"Yes. It can."
"So what do we do?"
Elena thought about the families she'd been reading about all day. Some of them had answers to that question. Some of them had submitted applications within hours of the announcement, had clearly been thinking about it before the announcement, had organized their lives around the possibility in ways that suggested a certain kind of personality. She wasn't sure yet whether that personality was an asset or a liability on a forty-year mission. She was going to spend the next seventeen years finding out.
"You keep going to school," Elena said. "You finish your degree. You live your life. The ships don't launch for seventeen years and a lot of the people who will be on them haven't been selected yet and won't be for years. There's nothing to decide tonight."
"But you're thinking about it."
"I think about it all day. Then I try to stop thinking about it when I go home." She looked at the files on her desk. "I'm not very good at the second part yet."
Sofia was quiet for a moment. "Dad doesn't know you took the job."
"Dad knows I took the job."
"He knows the title. He doesn't know what it actually involves." It wasn't a question. Sofia had always been good at reading what was happening at the edges of her parents' conversations, the things that were technically said and technically understood and technically not the whole picture.
"I'll talk to him this weekend," Elena said. "Really talk to him."
Another silence. Then: "Mom, are you okay? With the job, I mean. Actually okay."
Elena looked at the files stacked on her desk, the Austin family on top, forty-one and thirty-eight and sixteen and fourteen, application submitted within forty-eight hours of the announcement.
"I don't know yet," she said. "Ask me in a year."
After she hung up she sat without working for a while, which she'd been carefully avoiding since the job started. The scale of it came anyway. She let it come, then set it down and pulled the Austin family file back to the center of her desk.
The father's stress response assessments were excellent. The mother's conflict resolution scores were among the highest in the batch. The sixteen-year-old's profile was still developing, expected at that age, indicators pointing toward adaptability. The fourteen-year-old had flagged on isolation tolerance, the subcategory about limited social variety, which made sense for someone who'd probably never gone more than a week without seeing friends.
She made a note: retest in six months. Advance pending medical screening. Move to the next file.
At nine o'clock she put on her coat and walked out through the empty office. In the parking lot she sat for a moment before starting the car, looking at the November sky. Out there somewhere, invisible, a 340-meter rock was following a trajectory that seventeen years from now would settle the question one way or another.
She started the car and drove home.

Thomas Rourke | Boston Reconstruction Zone
November 19th, 2025 | 8:15 AM EST
Three weeks after the impact, the city had divided itself into two places that occupied the same geography and had almost nothing else in common. Thomas had started thinking of them as Boston-that-was and Boston-that-remained, and the line between them ran roughly along the Route 128 corridor, though it shifted daily as the assessment teams pushed further into the damage zone and the evacuation orders crept further out.
He was standing at that line now, or close enough to it, on a stretch of Commonwealth Avenue where the building facades on one side still had their windows and the building facades on the other side did not. The pressure wave had done this, arrived from the west like weather and expressed itself in glass: everything facing the impact direction had lost it, everything perpendicular or sheltered had kept it. Walking this street felt like walking through two different versions of the same moment, one in which October 15th had been an ordinary Tuesday and one in which it hadn't.
His shoulder had been properly reset four days after the impact by a paramedic at a field station in the Common. It still ached in the mornings. He'd started thinking of it as a useful reminder, a calibration point for what discomfort actually meant. The people in the zone to the west didn't have shoulders that ached in the mornings.
The people in the zone to the west, the ones within roughly thirty miles of the Quabbin crater, were not a category Thomas spent a lot of time in, because the numbers were large enough to be meaningless and he'd found that meaningless numbers didn't help him do the work in front of him. What helped was the specific: this block, this building, this load-bearing wall that had developed a crack suggesting the foundation had shifted two inches to the northeast. Specific things could be assessed and addressed. The large numbers just sat there.
He pulled out his notebook and added Commonwealth Avenue, 800 block, facade assessment needed, east-facing structural survey to the list of things that needed doing and wouldn't get done this week because there were not enough people to do them.
* * *
The firm had reassigned him to the emergency assessment team the week after the impact, which meant he was now doing in a semi-official capacity the same thing he'd been doing unofficially since the afternoon of October 15th: walking through damaged buildings and telling people whether they were going to fall down. His license made it official. His experience made it useful. The sheer volume of damaged structures in greater Boston made it overwhelming.
His supervisor on the assessment team was a woman named Carol Briggs, a structural engineer twenty years his senior who had done disaster assessment work after the Northridge earthquake and two Gulf Coast hurricanes and who had the particular economy of motion and speech that came from having done difficult things many times. She ran the team's daily briefings out of a FEMA field office set up in a parking structure in Newton that had survived the pressure wave intact, and she delivered bad news the way she delivered all news, which was without preamble.
"Federal assessment authority ends at the 128 perimeter," she told them that morning, indicating the map on the wall behind her. The map had a color gradient that Thomas had learned to read over the past three weeks: white for assessed and cleared, yellow for assessed and restricted, orange for assessed and condemned, red for the zone where assessment teams didn't go yet because the structural situation was too unstable to enter safely. The red covered roughly the western third of the map.
"Anything inside the perimeter is federal jurisdiction under the emergency authority declaration. Which means our assessment findings in that zone go to FEMA, FEMA routes them to the Army Corps, and the Army Corps decides what gets demolished and in what order." She paused. "It also means the people whose buildings are in that zone don't get to talk to us directly about what we find. They talk to their congressional representatives, who talk to FEMA, who eventually talk to the Army Corps."
"How long does that process take?" asked someone at the back, a younger engineer whose name Thomas hadn't learned yet.
"Months," Carol said. "Possibly longer for the buildings that aren't falling down immediately, because the ones that are falling down immediately take priority."
Thomas had been in enough government processes to understand what months meant in practice: it meant the people whose buildings had been condemned were living somewhere temporary and couldn't plan beyond the temporary because they didn't know yet whether their neighborhood was coming back. It meant business owners couldn't decide whether to reopen or relocate. It meant whole communities were suspended in a kind of administrative limbo while the federal machinery processed the information that the assessment teams were generating.
He understood why the system worked this way. The scale of the damage required centralized coordination. Individual property decisions couldn't be made without understanding the larger infrastructure picture: utility lines, load-bearing party walls, historic district considerations, the interdependencies of a dense urban environment. You couldn't just tell one building owner their building was safe if the building next to it was going to be demolished, because demolition had structural implications for everything adjacent.
Understanding why the system worked this way didn't make it easier to look at the faces of the people standing outside the 128 perimeter asking him what he'd found in their building.
* * *
He had found, early on, that the most useful thing he could do outside of his official assessment work was to be the person who explained things. Not the decisions, those weren't his to explain. But the reasoning. Why the crack in that wall meant what it meant. Why the building next door could stay up while this one couldn't. Why the timeline for structural decisions was what it was and what the process looked like from the inside.
People wanted to understand. That was the thing that surprised him, though in retrospect it shouldn't have. In the immediate aftermath of the impact, when he'd been helping pull people out of debris and organizing gas evacuations, the dominant need had been physical: safety, water, warmth, information about where family members were. Three weeks in, the dominant need had shifted. People were physically safe. They had shelter, provisional and uncomfortable in many cases but shelter. What they needed now was to understand what was happening to them and why, and nobody in the official structures had time to explain it.
He started doing it informally, standing outside the assessment sites while Carol and the team worked inside, answering questions from whoever had gathered on the sidewalk. He was careful about what he said. He never shared specific findings from the assessment reports, which were federal property, and he never made predictions about outcomes, which were beyond his authority to make. What he did was explain structural engineering in plain language. How buildings worked. What kinds of damage were fixable and what kinds weren't. Why a building could look intact from the outside and be condemned on the inside. What the assessment process involved and approximately how long each stage took.
Word spread. By the second week he was getting calls from community groups who wanted him to come to their neighborhood and hold information sessions. He went to two of them on evenings when Carol's team had finished early, sitting in community centers that smelled of institutional coffee and collective anxiety, drawing diagrams on whiteboards to explain load transfer and foundation stress to rooms full of people who owned buildings and needed to understand what might be happening inside them.
He wasn't sure if it helped in any material sense. The buildings were what they were. The federal process was what it was. But people left those sessions looking different from how they'd arrived, less like they were waiting for something to be done to them and more like they were waiting for something they at least understood.
Carol found out about the community sessions and didn't say anything for three days. Then she told him to keep doing it, and to tell people that the assessment reports were public record and could be requested through FEMA's online portal once they'd been processed. A small thing. It gave people something concrete to do.

The press conference happened on a Tuesday, which was how Thomas learned about 2042-Delta.
He'd been avoiding news coverage since the impact, not deliberately at first but because the work filled the available hours and the evenings he'd been using for community sessions and the nights he'd been using for sleep. He knew the broad shape of it: the asteroid had been a fragment, the main body was still out there, there was an impact probability and it wasn't small. Maria Webb had been flying for twenty years and she had never once done a preflight checklist three times. She did it three times now, not because she distrusted herself but because the cargo behind her was forty tons of precision-machined titanium structural components bound for a shipyard on the Moon, and the margin for error in this particular line of work had recently become much smaller than anything she'd encountered in commercial aviation.

They had compressed it to twenty-two weeks of the most concentrated training she'd ever experienced, and even that had felt like too little. The first time she'd docked a simulator to a virtual lunar platform she'd overshot by eleven meters and spent twenty minutes repositioning. The second time she'd overshot by three. By week sixteen she was hitting it clean. Whether clean in a simulator translated to clean in actual lunar orbit was a question she was about to answer.
"Primary systems nominal," ARIA reported through her headset, the AI's voice carrying the calibrated neutrality of a system that had no stake in the outcome but was paying close attention anyway. "Trans-lunar injection burn scheduled for forty-two minutes mission elapsed time. Lunar orbit insertion burn in approximately sixty-eight hours. Cargo manifest confirmed. Fuel margins at 9.4% above minimum for injection burn, 12.6% above minimum for lunar orbit insertion."
Amy Stokes, strapped into the co-pilot's seat, was running her own checklist on the backup flight systems with the focused economy of someone who had already accepted that this was the job now. They'd flown together twice in the three weeks since their recruitment at Bradley, orientation runs to low Earth orbit, nothing like this, and Maria had learned enough about Amy to trust her in a cockpit, which was the primary thing she needed to know.
"ARIA, confirm collision avoidance parameters for lunar approach," Amy said. "Traffic control is reporting three cargo vessels ahead of us in the pattern."
"Collision avoidance active. Tracking Cargo-9, Cargo-11, and Cargo-12 on approach vectors. Automated separation protocol maintains minimum 3.2-kilometer spacing during orbital insertion phase. Manual override available if required."
Maria studied the flight plan on her primary display. The trajectory ARIA had calculated would take them in a long arc away from Earth, building velocity before the insertion burn that would intercept lunar orbit roughly three days from now. She understood the mathematics of it. She'd been studying orbital mechanics for three weeks with the compressed urgency of someone who needed to know things yesterday. But understanding the mathematics and feeling the reality of them were different things. She was about to travel 385,000 kilometers to a construction site on the Moon. Last month she had been thinking about her nephew's birthday cake.
"Tower, Cargo-15 ready for launch," Amy called. "Crew secured, AI flight systems active, cargo manifest uploaded to Luna Base traffic control."
"Cargo-15, you are go for launch. Weather optimal, no traffic conflicts. Luna Base reports ready to receive. Safe flight."
The engines ignited.
Maria had experienced a lot of g-forces in her career, from carrier catapults to test flights that had pressed her into her seat at angles a commercial passenger would find alarming. This was different in duration rather than magnitude, not the violent shove of a catapult but a sustained, relentless push that continued for nearly eight minutes while the spacecraft climbed through atmosphere and kept climbing, the blue of the sky darkening through indigo to black while the pressure stayed constant against her chest.
"First stage separation," ARIA announced. "Second stage ignition nominal. Velocity 24,800 kilometers per hour and climbing."
Then the engines cut and Maria weighed nothing.
She had done parabolic flight training. Forty-five seconds of weightlessness at a time, enough to practice procedures and get comfortable with the disorientation. Forty-five seconds had not prepared her for the sensation of it becoming permanent. Her inner ear, which had spent twenty years being correct about which direction was down, was now simply wrong, and it was going to keep being wrong for the next three days, and there was nothing to do about it except let the wrongness become the new normal.
"Main engine cutoff," ARIA reported. "Orbital velocity achieved. Trans-lunar injection burn in thirty-nine minutes."
"ARIA, display current orbital parameters," Maria said, partly because she wanted the information and partly because having something to look at helped.
"Current altitude: 208 kilometers. Orbital velocity: 27,740 kilometers per hour. Trans-lunar injection burn in thirty-nine minutes will increase velocity to 39,540 kilometers per hour. Lunar orbit insertion burn in approximately sixty-eight hours."
Amy was already running the post-launch systems check, moving through it with the methodical precision that zero gravity required. Every action had to be deliberate. There was no down to keep a dropped tool from falling to the floor, only the direction it drifted until it contacted something. Maria watched her and catalogued the technique, the small adjustments of body position and tether management that made the difference between competent and dangerous in this environment.
"Radiation monitors," Maria said.
"Nominal. Cosmic ray flux below average for the season. No solar activity predicted in the next 60 hours." Amy pulled up the space weather overlay. "We picked a good window."
"We'll see how good the window looks once we're outside the magnetosphere."
"Trans-lunar injection in sixty seconds," ARIA announced.
Maria gripped her armrests, not from fear exactly, but from the trained habit of having something to hold when the engines fired. The burn lasted four minutes and eighteen seconds, pressing her gently but continuously against her restraints, and when it ended she was committed. No airport within a few hundred miles. One destination. No alternatives if something went wrong during orbital insertion.
She watched Earth shrink in the rear display and did not let herself think about everything that was still on it.
* * *
Eighteen hours into the coast, Maria was discovering that long-duration spaceflight was largely an exercise in managing discomfort and boredom in approximately equal measure.
Her back ached from the pilot's couch in a way that the zero-gravity environment made impossible to fully address. Shifting position helped temporarily, but there was no comfortable arrangement for a spine that had spent forty years expecting gravity to organize things. Her sinuses were congested from fluid redistribution, the same effect that made her face look slightly puffy in the mirror and would get worse before it got better. The food had been engineered for nutritional completeness and for packaging that worked in zero gravity and for essentially nothing else.
She had flown F/A-18s off carrier decks in sea states that would have kept most pilots on the ground. She had done test flights in aircraft that were, by design, operating outside their certified envelope. She had, three weeks ago, flown a 737 through the pressure wave of an asteroid impact and landed it intact. None of that had prepared her for the specific grinding tedium of three days in a metal can with recycled air and nowhere to go.
"ARIA, status," she said.
"Mission elapsed time: 19 hours, 31 minutes. Velocity: 35,620 kilometers per hour. Distance from Earth: 138,000 kilometers. Distance to lunar orbit insertion: 248,000 kilometers. All systems nominal. Water recycling efficiency at 95.3%, within acceptable parameters."
Amy had replaced the primary water filter assembly as a precaution, which had taken forty minutes in zero gravity versus the ten it would have taken on the ground and had produced a floating screw that required another eight minutes to locate. Small maintenance tasks were like that up here. Everything cost more than expected.
"How are you doing?" Amy asked from her station, not looking up from the systems display she was monitoring.
"Fine. Bored."
"That's good. Bored means nothing is wrong."
"I know. I'm not complaining about the bored."
Amy glanced over. She had the particular quality of stillness that Maria had learned to associate with people who were genuinely comfortable in small spaces, who could sit with their thoughts for extended periods without the thoughts becoming a problem. Maria was less sure she had that quality. She was going to find out.
"ARIA is picking up Luna Base traffic control," Amy said. "You want to listen in?"
Maria nodded and Amy routed the feed to the main speakers.
The traffic coordination coming from Luna Base had the surface texture of air traffic control: the same clipped efficiency, the same layered simultaneous conversations, the same background hum of a system managing many moving things at once, but the content was different in ways that took a moment to parse. No weather. No runway assignments. Three-dimensional approach vectors with altitude components that required constant adjustment. Docking sequences that ATC never had to consider. And the sheer volume of it: over two dozen active spacecraft in various orbital phases around the Moon, all needing coordination, versus the handful that had been the historic peak at the International Space Station.
"ARIA, display Luna Base configuration," Maria said.
The AI generated a schematic overlay on her navigation display: the primary construction platform surrounded by subsidiary platforms, cargo staging areas, crew habitats, fuel depots. And at the center of it, visible even at this resolution, the skeletal framework of the first ship. It looked like the bones of something vast and not yet alive, ribs and spine implied but not yet enclosed, the suggestion of enormous scale without the surface to confirm it.
"How did they build all that in three weeks?" Amy said.
"They didn't. Luna Base has been under construction for eleven months. The ship framework went up in the last six." Maria studied the schematic. "They knew the impact probability was rising. They started before the announcement."
"So someone knew."
"Someone always knows. The question is who gets told and when."
Amy was quiet for a moment. "Does that bother you?"
Maria thought about the briefing room at Bradley. Colonel Reeves and the 68% and the transport to Cape Canaveral arranged before she'd finished saying yes. The way the whole thing had been structured, the speed of it, the obvious preparation behind the apparent urgency. "It bothers me slightly less than the asteroid does," she said. "Ask me again in a year."
* * *
Sixty-six hours after leaving Earth, with Luna filling their forward windows and the insertion burn forty-three minutes out, the emergency call came.
"All stations, all stations, this is Luna Base Traffic Control. Cargo-11 is reporting attitude control failure. Unable to align for orbital insertion burn. All inbound traffic hold your burns until further notice."
Maria was already pulling up Cargo-11's telemetry before the transmission finished. The ship was ahead of them in the approach pattern, roughly sixty kilometers out, and the telemetry was not encouraging: tumbling at 4.1 degrees per second on pitch and yaw, the attitude control system throwing fault codes that suggested a thruster cluster had failed. A spacecraft tumbling at that rate couldn't safely fire its main engine. The burn would go in whatever direction the engine happened to be pointing when the firing command executed, which could be anything from a corrective burn to a direct vector into the lunar surface.
"Fuel margins if we delay burn by ten minutes," Maria said.
"Margins reduced to 8.3% above minimum," ARIA responded. "Emergency maneuver capability significantly reduced. Extended delay not recommended."
"Cargo-15, Luna Base," Amy called. "We are thirty-eight minutes from our insertion window. What is the timeline on Cargo-11?"
"Cargo-15, stand by." A pause, during which Maria could hear the controllers working the problem, voices overlapping in the controlled urgency of people who had trained for this but were doing it for real for the first time. "Cargo-11 is attempting attitude recovery with secondary thrusters. Telemetry shows partial response. Estimated stabilization time unknown."
Unknown was the word Maria did not want to hear with thirty-eight minutes on the clock and 8.3% fuel margin if they waited.
"Options," she said to Amy.
"Adjust our approach vector to increase separation distance from Cargo-11's current drift trajectory. Buys us more time before their situation becomes our situation."
"ARIA, compute adjusted approach vector with fifteen-kilometer clearance from Cargo-11's projected drift cone."
"Computing. Adjusted vector requires 0.8-degree course correction and reduces insertion burn efficiency by 1.2%. Resulting fuel margin: 6.9% above minimum. Within acceptable parameters."
"Do it."
ARIA fired the maneuvering thrusters, a brief gentle push that shifted their trajectory almost imperceptibly. On the navigation display, their projected path moved slightly away from the cone of possible positions that Cargo-11 might occupy in the next forty minutes.
"Luna Base, Cargo-15," Amy reported. "We have adjusted approach vector for separation clearance. We can hold our insertion window if Cargo-11 achieves stabilization within twenty-five minutes."
"Copy, Cargo-15. Cargo-11 is reporting secondary thruster response improving. Tumble rate now 2.9 degrees per second and decelerating."
Maria watched the numbers on Cargo-11's telemetry feed. The tumble rate was dropping, slowly, the secondary thrusters fighting the angular momentum that the attitude control failure had let accumulate. It was slow work. The physics of it were not forgiving.
"Tumble rate 1.8 degrees per second."
"Insertion window in twenty-two minutes," ARIA noted.
"Tumble rate 0.9 degrees per second."
"Cargo-11, Luna Base. Telemetry shows attitude stabilization within tolerance. You are cleared for delayed insertion burn on our mark."
"Luna Base, Cargo-11. We copy. Standing by for your mark."
"All stations, Luna Base Traffic Control. Cargo-11 has achieved attitude stabilization. Inbound traffic cleared to resume insertion burns on individual windows. Cargo-15, you are go for insertion burn at your scheduled time."
Maria let out a breath she had not been entirely aware of holding. "ARIA, confirm insertion burn parameters."
"All nominal. Burn in fourteen minutes, nine seconds. Duration four minutes, forty-three seconds. Post-burn orbital velocity 5,923 kilometers per hour."
"Copy."
Amy looked across at her. "Nice vector adjustment."
"That was ARIA."
"You told ARIA to do it."
Maria didn't answer because the burn was fourteen minutes out and she wanted those fourteen minutes for the checklist.
* * *
Stable lunar orbit. Two hours to docking.
The Moon outside the window was not the Moon she had looked at from Earth her entire life. That Moon had been a disk, flat and familiar, something to navigate by or to set a romantic scene. This Moon was a world, close enough that she could see the surface texture of individual craters, the sharp-edged geometry of a landscape that had no weather to soften it, the terminator line cutting hard across ancient regolith in a way that made the shadows look architectural rather than natural.
Luna Base resolved from a bright point to a recognizable structure as they closed the distance: the primary platform and its subsidiaries, the crew habitats, the fuel staging areas, and at the center of it the ship framework that she'd seen on the schematic and that was, in reality, significantly more affecting than a schematic.
It was large. She had understood this intellectually. The numbers, 2.5 kilometers when complete, three rotating habitat rings, a crew of a thousand, were real numbers she had processed and stored. But numbers and presence were different things, and the presence of the framework, even incomplete, even just ribs and spine without the hull to fill it in, was something the numbers had not prepared her for. It occupied the space around Luna Base the way a cathedral occupies a city block: not just filling it but changing the character of everything nearby.
"ARIA, construction timeline projection," she said.
"Based on current material delivery rates and construction schedules, first vessel completion projected at sixteen years, four months. Second vessel at eighteen years, nine months. Projections assume continued supply and no major interruptions."
Sixteen years. The asteroid arrived in seventeen.
"Cargo-15, Luna Base Traffic Control. Welcome to lunar orbit. Proceed to docking vector Charlie-6 for Platform Delta-4. Automated guidance active."
"Luna Base, Cargo-15. ARIA is interfacing with your traffic management system. Estimating one hour, fifty-four minutes to docking."
Maria watched ARIA take over the final approach, the maneuvering thrusters firing in small precise bursts to match velocity with the docking platform while the robotic arms and other spacecraft moved around them in the choreography of a facility that never stopped. The precision required was beyond human reflexes in both speed and accuracy. This was what the AI was for, here as it had been in the cockpit above Massachusetts three weeks ago: not to replace her judgment but to execute within it faster than her hands could move.
"Docking clamps engaged," ARIA announced. "Cargo-15 docked at Platform Delta-4. Cargo transfer systems ready."
Maria unstrapped and floated to the forward window. Through it she could see the cargo handling systems beginning to move, robotic arms reaching for the titanium components in her hold, each beam precisely tagged and destined for a specific location in the framework visible through the upper port. Her forty tons of materials would become part of that structure. In sixteen years and four months, if the projections held, those beams would be inside a ship between the stars.
"Hell of a thing," Amy said, floating up beside her.
"Yes."
"Does it feel real to you yet?"
Maria watched the robotic arms work in the silence outside the window, moving materials with the patient mechanical certainty of systems that didn't have opinions about what they were building or why. Behind them, the lunar surface stretched gray and sharp-shadowed to the horizon. Above it, barely visible in the black, Earth hung like a marble someone had set down and forgotten.
"It feels real," she said. "I'm not sure real is the right word for it, but it feels real."
She pushed off from the window and went to begin the post-flight checklist. There was a return cargo manifest to review, a maintenance cycle to sign off on, and a debrief with Luna Base operations before the next launch window opened. The work was what it was. She did it.

Chapter 7: What the Numbers Don't Count
Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston
January 9, 2026 | 7:14 AM EST

The news didn't break like something shattering. It came in the way most permanent things came: quietly, on a Thursday morning, in a report that had been technically available since Tuesday and that Thomas only found because he'd been awake since four and had run out of other things to read.

The headline was careful. Emergency Supplemental Authorization Re-Allocation: Luna-Ark Infrastructure Designated Priority Tier 1. He read the actual text, not the summary, because he'd learned in the three months since October that the summaries left out the load-bearing parts. It was forty pages. He read all of them.

By the time the sky outside his temporary apartment had gone from black to gray, he'd read it twice and written down the numbers. Not the round numbers from the press conference slides, the ones that were calibrated for television: $220 billion. Not that figure. The actual project-by-project breakdown that someone in the Office of Management and Budget had assembled with the specific thoroughness of a bureaucrat who understood that their work would outlast the political cover that commissioned it.

Forty-three percent of the emergency reconstruction authorization, the money that had been designated in October for exactly this, rebuilding what the impact had destroyed, had been redirected. Not eliminated. The paperwork was careful about that. Redirected. Reallocated to priority tier infrastructure in support of the Luna-Ark program: launch facility upgrades, rare-earth extraction contracts, materials processing facilities in Nevada and Utah and three other states that happened to contain the right minerals and the right senators.

Thomas put the printout on the kitchen table and looked at it for a long time.

The mathematics were correct. He'd thought this through in October, standing on the Common in the dark, and he still thought it this morning. A 68% probability of a 340-meter impact event in seventeen years was not a problem that could be managed by ignoring it in favor of more immediate concerns. The reconstruction was necessary. The ships were more necessary. Any honest analysis arrived at that conclusion, and he'd spent his professional life respecting honest analysis.

He also knew, in a way that had nothing to do with the analysis, exactly which buildings this was going to affect.

He got in his car and drove to Jamaica Plain.

*

The assessment office he ran out of a stripped classroom in a former school building had been through a few iterations since October. In the first weeks it had been crisis infrastructure: cots on the floor, power strips run from a generator, people sleeping in shifts while others worked. Now it was something more like an ordinary office, if you ignored the fact that the windows had been replaced with plastic sheeting and the building's structural status was itself "conditionally cleared pending further inspection," a category Thomas had coined because the alternatives were worse.

Carol Briggs was already there when he arrived. She was his supervisor in the technical chain of the assessment program, a woman in her late fifties who had spent thirty years in structural engineering and who had, in the three months since the impact, developed a quality Thomas associated with field surgeons: a complete separation between the clinical assessment of a situation and the human reality of what the assessment meant. She handed him a coffee from a thermos she kept on her desk.

"You saw the report," she said.

"OMB release. Read it this morning."

"I read it Tuesday." She sat down at her desk and pulled up her work screen. "Figured you'd get there."

"How bad is it for us?"

She gave him the numbers she'd worked out. The affected projects were mostly in the outer ring, the neighborhoods furthest from the impact site that had damage without total destruction, the places where remediation and selective reinforcement were possible in a way they weren't in the crater zone. Jamaica Plain was in the outer ring. Roxbury. Parts of Dorchester. Thirty-two projects that had been conditionally approved and funded, that had contractors in place, that had families waiting on them.

Thirty-two projects that were now in the redirected column.

"Timeline?" Thomas asked.

"Undefined. Which is worse than a number." She looked at her screen. "Indefinite pause pending resource availability. That's the language."

He thought about Mrs. Petrovitch on the third floor of the Lamartine Street building, the one with the orange notice and the forty years of photographs. She'd been in a church basement in Roslindale since October. Indefinite pause was not a term she had the resources to absorb.

"Is there an appeals process?"

"There's a process. Federal review, sixty-day response window, low success rate on projects under a specific dollar threshold." Carol looked at him with the expression of someone who had worked in government long enough to know exactly how appeals processes worked. "Useful for projects above a hundred and fifty million. Most of ours are under forty."

"I'm going to file anyway."

"I know you are." She said it without judgment. "I'll sign whatever you need me to sign. Just know what you're filing into."

He spent the morning at his desk preparing the documentation for three of the thirty-two projects, the ones with the strongest individual cases, the clearest photographs, the most complete structural assessments. He worked methodically, the way he always worked, with the deep focus that had gotten him through structural engineering school at twenty-three and through the first weeks after October at forty-one. He built each case carefully, because careful cases were the only kind that had any chance at all, and even careful cases didn't have much.

At noon he walked two blocks to the community center on the corner of Green and Perkins.

*

He'd been holding these sessions since November, informally at first, then with a regular time and a regular room. They were not official. Carol had mentioned once, obliquely, that certain people at the program's administrative level were aware of them and had chosen not to comment, which Thomas understood as tacit tolerance rather than approval. He was not sure he cared about the distinction.

The room held twenty-two people this Thursday. He recognized most of them. The man in the back who'd asked about the federal assessment process three sessions ago, who was now asking follow-up questions that showed he'd done his own research in the interval. The woman with the baby, who came because her building's assessment was still pending and who stayed after each session to ask him specific technical questions in a way that suggested she was a lot more technically literate than her file indicated. Two young men from the apartment building on Boylston, the one that had been cleared but whose landlord had stopped accepting rent payments and was clearly running an insurance play, a situation Thomas had been trying to figure out how to address through channels that were increasingly theoretical.

He'd started these sessions explaining the assessment process: what the orange notices meant, what the sequence from assessment to authorization to remediation looked like, what the federal paperwork required. That was still part of it. But the conversation had shifted over three months into something larger, a running community seminar on the mechanics of how decisions got made: how reconstruction money moved, how the bureaucratic sequencing worked, where the appeals points were, what kinds of pressure actually moved things and what kinds were absorbed without effect.

He told them about the OMB re-allocation today. He'd decided on the drive over that he wasn't going to handle it carefully, soften it, give them the version that was designed to prevent panic. They were adults. They'd been living in church basements and temporary housing for three months. They had earned the actual information.

The room was quiet when he finished.

Then the man in the back, whose name was Dmitri, said: "So we were emergency until we weren't."

"The emergency designation is still active. The funding level changed."

"Because the ships are the actual emergency."

"The ships are a response to a 68% probability event that ends with a 340-meter object hitting the planet. Mathematically, yes, the ships represent a higher-priority emergency."

"And the people in this room are." Dmitri stopped. He rephrased. "The people in this room are lower priority than that."

Thomas looked at him. "The people in this room were never in the ship selection pool. The selection pool is four thousand people out of nine billion."

The room digested this. The woman with the baby, whose name he'd learned was Nina, said: "Who is in the selection pool?"

"Specialists. Professions the mission requires. Psychological fitness assessments. Physical health screening. There's a federal process."

Thomas didn't answer immediately, because the honest answer was complicated. He knew someone in the selection process. He'd gotten a reply, finally, to the text he'd sent on the afternoon of October 15th, from a number with a Boston area code that was currently, as of three months ago, not in Boston. The reply had been brief: I'm okay. Working on something. Talk when I can.

He hadn't heard from Ben since.

"I know someone who was recruited," he said. "Pilot. Not selected yet, as far as I know."

"But people like us," Nina said. She wasn't being pointed about it. She was asking a real question. "People who had lives here, who are trying to put those lives back together, who don't have whatever qualifications the selection requires. What happens to us?"

Thomas had been turning this over since October. He had an answer that was technically correct and he had the feeling that was building in him, had been building since that morning, that the technically correct answer was increasingly insufficient.

"The plan is that the ships go, and they carry what's necessary to restart human civilization if the worst happens, and meanwhile reconstruction continues here for whatever time we have. Best case, the mission succeeds, the impact is deflected, and we've built a permanent deep-space infrastructure in the process. Worst case, the ships leave with four thousand people and the rest of Earth takes the impact."

He paused.

"That's the plan as written. The mathematics behind it are sound."

Dmitri said, very quietly: "And you believe in it."

Thomas looked at the man with the photographs of Minsk and the forty years on Lamartine Street and the family in the church basement, and the thirty-two projects in the redirected column, and Mrs. Petrovitch's orange notice, and the appeals process with a sixty-day response window and a low success rate.

"I believe the mathematics are sound," he said.

Nobody asked him to finish that sentence.

*

He filed the three appeals before five o'clock. He submitted each one into the federal portal, received the automated confirmation receipt, and noted the sixty-day response windows in his calendar on three dates in March that already felt like geological time.

On his way out he stopped at the door and looked back at the classroom. The plastic sheeting in the windows had been there since October. Someone had taped a piece of paper to the wall with the community center's schedule. Below it, someone else had taped another piece of paper with a handwritten list of what he realized, after a moment, were the names of the thirty-two projects in the redirected column. They weren't organized by any criterion he could immediately identify. They were just the names, listed in the order they'd appeared in the OMB document.

He didn't know who had put the list there or when.

He stood looking at it for a moment, then put on his coat and went out into the January cold.

His phone buzzed. He looked at it. Not Ben's number: an email from a federal address he didn't recognize, subject line "Re: Emergency Supplemental Coordination, Community Liaison Request." He opened it while standing on the sidewalk in the dark. Someone in the Luna-Ark program's public affairs infrastructure was assembling a community outreach initiative to explain the re-allocation to affected populations. They were looking for credentialed experts who could help facilitate.

He read the email twice. He put his phone in his pocket. He did not reply.

He got in his car and sat in the cold for a minute, engine off, looking at the building with the list in the window.

He started the car. He drove back to the temporary apartment. He ate something. He opened his laptop and pulled up the thirty-two project files and began drafting the documentation for the other twenty-nine.

Outside, a city that had been taken apart was putting itself back together by increments, the way things always put themselves back together: one building at a time, one decision at a time, with insufficient resources and too much paperwork and the particular human stubbornness that kept doing the work anyway because the alternative was to stop doing the work.

Thomas Rourke was very good at not stopping.

He was starting to understand that this was going to be a problem.

Chapter 8: The Vote That Wasn't One
Senator Sarah Grant | Geneva, Switzerland
January 22, 2026 | 9:03 AM CET

Sarah Grant had been in enough rooms where history was decided to know that history did not, in her experience, look like history while it was happening. It looked like a scheduling conflict and a bad audio connection and twelve people with different time zones and competing constituencies trying to agree on language that would mean the same thing in six different legal frameworks.

She was on the third floor of the Palais des Nations, in a committee room that smelled of lemon cleaning solution and the accumulated paper of a hundred previous negotiations, when the resolution came to a preliminary vote and passed fourteen to three.

She allowed herself exactly two seconds of something that might have been relief, then went back to work.

The resolution was called the Framework for Coordinated Planetary Defense and Human Continuity, which was the kind of title that had been assembled by committee to be so broad that no single government could object to it without appearing to object to planetary defense or human continuity, which was the point. What it actually did was establish the legal basis for the Luna-Ark program as a multi-sovereign undertaking, which meant the funding and labor and materials would come from every signatory nation, which meant no single nation could withdraw from it without triggering a cascade of treaty obligations that their domestic legal systems were not equipped to handle quickly.

Sarah had spent nine weeks writing the treaty obligations into the framework at precisely those load-bearing points. She was a United States Senator from Ohio, which was not the most obvious posting for international treaty architecture, but she was also, as of October 16th, the chair of the newly-established Senate Select Committee on Planetary Defense, which had been created with the speed that congressional bodies created things when the alternative was explaining to constituents why they hadn't. She had been given authority she had not previously had, a staff that had been assembled from borrowed personnel in four days, and a problem that had no precedent in the history of human governance.

She had, in her assessment, approximately six months before the political will calcified and the competing national interests that were currently suppressed by shock and solidarity reasserted themselves. She intended to use all six months.

"Senator Grant." Her deputy, a young man named Paul Hartley who had not slept in what she estimated was forty hours, appeared at her elbow with a tablet. "We have a problem with the Chinese language provision on crew nationality distribution. Beijing's team is saying the current language implies a quota system that conflicts with their constitutional framework."

Sarah set down her coffee. "Tell them the language doesn't imply a quota, it establishes a minimum representation floor, which is a different legal concept, and ask them to show me specifically which provision of their constitutional framework conflicts with a minimum floor as opposed to a maximum ceiling. They'll need an hour to look it up. We'll have lunch."

Paul wrote this down. He'd learned in the six weeks he'd worked for her that she gave instructions in the format of the complete action sequence, not just the first step, because the first step was obvious and what she needed him to know was what came after it.

"There's also a press request from the Times," he said. "They're asking about the OMB re-allocation announcement. Quote from the piece in progress: 'Critics argue the redirection of reconstruction funds to the Luna-Ark program represents a de facto abandonment of impact survivors in favor of a speculative long-term infrastructure project.'"

Sarah looked at the quote.

"Tell them I'll do a statement in writing, no interview. Give them this: 'The Framework for Coordinated Planetary Defense is the most important insurance policy in human history. Insurance policies require premiums. The premium in this case is a fraction of our existing emergency authorization, and the coverage it provides is the survival of our species. The mathematics are not complicated.' Don't add anything to it."

Paul looked uncertain. "It's going to read as dismissive of the reconstruction concerns."

"It's going to read as direct." She picked up her coffee. "The dismissive version would be not responding. This is me saying: I know the math, I've looked at it carefully, and I've made a decision. They can agree or disagree with the decision. They can't say I didn't make it."

She had never been in a situation where the gap between politically possible and necessary was this large, or where the timeline for closing it was this short.

He was a tall Swede who had been working on climate infrastructure for fifteen years and who had, in her observation, a very good instinct for when to push and when to wait. "That's why we're finishing the build quickly."

"Her methodology is sound. I've reviewed it. The questioning is coming from people who want the selection to be a lottery, which I understand the impulse behind, but a lottery doesn't produce the skill distribution the mission requires." She picked up her jacket from the chair. "Tell your parliament that if they have specific methodological concerns, they can submit them in writing to Dr. Marsh's office through the Framework's scientific advisory board. That's the channel. That's the process."

"And if they push harder?"

"Then they're welcome to explain to their constituents why they want the asteroid deflection mission crewed by people selected at random instead of people selected for capability." She pulled on the jacket. "I've never lost that argument."

She had not, in fact, lost that argument. She'd made it four times in the last three weeks to four different constituencies, and she expected to make it many more times over the next sixteen years, and she expected to win it each time, because the argument was correct and because she had learned over eleven years that correct arguments, delivered clearly and without apology, tended to prevail over incorrect arguments delivered with passion and volume.

What she had not figured out, and what she thought about in the quiet hours of these long Geneva nights, was what to do about the arguments that were also correct. Thomas Rourke was not wrong. The residents of Jamaica Plain were not wrong. The thirty-two projects in the redirected column were not wrong. The mathematics of a 68% impact probability did not stop being correct because of the human cost of acting on them. The human cost did not stop being real because the mathematics were correct.

She had made her decision. She was going to continue making it. She was also going to hold the thing she didn't yet have language for: the awareness that making the right decision and doing right by everyone it affected were not always the same operation, and that she was in a role that required the former and could not always deliver the latter, and that this was a cost she'd accepted when she'd taken the job and that accepting a cost was not the same as not feeling it.

Paul reappeared. "The Chinese team says they'll need ninety minutes, not sixty."

"Then we have ninety minutes. Get me Marsh's office on the line. I want an update on the cryosleep compatibility screening."

"The what?"

"The cryosleep screening. It's in the Chapter 4 supplemental. Ask for Dr. Aldridge if Marsh isn't available."

Paul wrote this down. He was getting better at writing things down and worse at sleeping, which she recognized as the standard trajectory of people working on problems with seventeen-year deadlines that felt, day to day, like they were due in three weeks.

She went back to the table. The room smelled of lemon cleaning solution. Outside, the winter sky sat flat and gray over the lake.

Somewhere above that sky, on a trajectory that seventeen years of work might or might not change, a 340-meter object was doing what objects in space did: following the mathematics. The mathematics didn't care about the resolution that had just passed, or the appeals process in Boston, or the selection pool, or the thirty-two projects in the redirected column, or anything else that happened in the rooms where human beings tried to organize themselves in response to a universe that organized itself differently.

The mathematics were correct. Sarah Grant was going to hold that, and keep building, for as long as it took.

She pulled the next document toward her and got back to work.

Chapter 9: Acceptable Margins
Dr. Elena Marsh | Crew Selection Office, Houston
March 3, 2027 | 9:17 AM CST

The cryosleep compatibility data had been sitting in her secure folder for six days before Elena opened it, which was not because she had forgotten it was there.

Dr. Kim Aldridge had sent it with a subject line that was careful in the specific way that subject lines were careful when the sender knew the content would be difficult: "Preliminary Cryosleep Compatibility Analysis, Review at Your Discretion." Elena had read the subject line on a Thursday evening, had noted it, and had spent the six days since clearing other things from her desk. The Austin family had moved to medical screening. The Okafor family required a second-round behavioral assessment. There were forty-three files in various stages. She'd been busy.

The methodology was Kim's and it was sound. Twelve months of preliminary biological screening for the initial 1,200 candidates in the first selection cohort, cross-referenced with what was known about cryosleep compatibility from animal studies, the three failed human trials in 2019 and 2021, and the partial success of the Nagoya protocol in 2024 that had kept a medical patient viable for eleven days before he'd been revived. The data set was thin. The extrapolations were careful. The conclusions were Kim's, signed, with the specific precision of someone who had thought about how the conclusions would land before she wrote them down.

Estimated incompatibility rate: 14 to 22 percent.

Elena read this figure and set down her coffee and looked at it.

Fourteen to twenty-two percent of the candidates in the first selection cohort were biologically unlikely to survive a cryosleep period of the duration the mission required. Not certain to die. Unlikely to survive. Kim had built confidence intervals around the figure that were appropriately cautious given the data quality, and the intervals were wide, and the figure could be as low as nine percent or as high as thirty-one, but the center of mass was in that fourteen to twenty-two range and Kim's analysis of the biological markers was solid.

"Yes."

"And the candidates who've already advanced?"

"Same. Flag, don't remove. Nothing changes in their status until we have the full picture."

Kim nodded. She was making notes.

"One more thing," Elena said. "The incompatible candidates. The ones who test positive for the markers." She chose the next words carefully. "What are their odds if they don't make the ship? I mean survival odds, if the impact occurs."

Kim looked at her.

"Same as everyone else not on the ship," Kim said.

"Right." Elena stood up. "So we're not doing them any favors by removing them from the pool if we can't replace them with someone better. Write that into the analysis. I want the full picture, including what incompatibility means in the context of the alternative."

That was Maria's first thought every time she came to the observation platform, the one on the outside of Habitat Ring C that you reached through an airlock sequence and a ladder that the engineers had bolted to the regolith with an informality that suggested they'd built it in an afternoon and hadn't gotten around to replacing it with something permanent. What you saw from four kilometers was structure. Ribs of high-tensile aluminum alloy arranged in a spine that was already longer than the biggest thing humanity had previously launched into space, flanked by the permanent construction scaffolding that crawled with robotic arms and suited technicians reduced to glove-sized figures at this distance. The cross-sections weren't closed yet. The ship's habitation rings were visible as open frameworks, hoops through which you could see the stars. The drive assembly at the stern was present only as its mounting architecture, the cradles and heat exchangers and magnetic field generators that would eventually enclose the engine itself, which had not been fully designed yet. The spine was undeniably there, and it was larger than Maria had expected even though she had been flying construction cargo to this facility for two years and had watched it grow component by component, and the gap between understanding something's scale from numbers and understanding it from sight was a gap that kept surprising her.

She lay back on the bunk and closed her eyes for three minutes, which was all she had before her next briefing.

Then she got up and went back to work.

We enter 2032 with all three habitation rings complete and the drive assembly integration as the remaining major structural work."

"Drive assembly is the long pole," Harrison said. He was the kind of scientist who had stopped being primarily a scientist at some point and had become primarily a person who explained science to decision-makers, and he was very good at it. "The propulsion specifications are locked but the integration tolerances are tighter than anything we've built before. Eighteen months minimum, twenty-four months if there are fit issues."

"There will be fit issues," Maria said.

"There will be fit issues," Novak agreed without inflection. "We're planning for twenty months and hoping for seventeen. That puts final integration completion in mid-2033 at the optimistic end."

"Which leaves six years for outfitting, crew loading, and final systems integration before the impact window," Harrison said.

"Six years and two months," Maria said.

Harrison looked at her. "You've been counting."

"I've been counting since October."

The room absorbed this. Maria had made a decision in October, when the mission designation was confirmed, to stop doing the mental work of not counting. The timeline was what it was. There was margin and the margin was real, and the work to be done within the margin was defined, and knowing exactly how much margin existed was more useful than maintaining a professional detachment from the arithmetic.

Six years and two months to final systems integration. Then crew boarding, which was a three-month operation for four thousand people. Then departure. Then whatever came after departure, which was someone else's problem in the temporal sense and her problem in every other sense.

"The Compass mission," Maria said. "Any updates on transit status."

"On track," Harrison said. "Nominal trajectory. The first signals from the approach phase should reach us in mid-2033."

"Same window as final integration."

"Give or take."

Maria made a note. The asteroid's internal structure and the ship's completion would become known at approximately the same time. She found this either a coincidence or a piece of planning she hadn't been told about, and the operational timeline suggested the latter. Someone had aligned these arrivals deliberately, which meant someone had decided that it was better to know the targeting requirements and the ship's readiness status at the same moment rather than sequentially. She agreed with that decision and made a note to find out who had made it.

*

After the briefing she went to the pilot simulator for three hours.

The simulator was the closest thing on Luna Base to the interior of the Phoenix, which itself didn't exist yet except as a set of components in various stages of fabrication on Earth and at the L2 platform. What existed was a pressure vessel approximately the right diameter fitted with the command interface design the Phoenix would use, and Maria had been in it for an average of nineteen hours per week for three months.

The simulation today was approach and parking orbit insertion at a rubble-pile object of specified mass and cohesion. She had run the same simulation forty-one times. Each run produced different variables within the specified parameter range, and each run required her to make decisions that would, in a real approach, have consequences for the operation. The simulation was designed to fail in new ways. Today it failed in a way she'd seen before, a navigation error in the parking orbit that produced an uncontrolled drift toward the object's surface, and she managed it the same way she'd managed it the last time it appeared, which was methodically and in the right order, and she got the vehicle back to stable orbit with six minutes of simulation time remaining.

She stayed in the simulator after the run ended. The display showed the post-run analysis, the decision tree rendered as a diagram with her choices marked and the alternatives calculated. She'd made sixteen decisions in the run. Thirteen were rated optimal. Two were rated acceptable. One was flagged for review.

She looked at the flagged decision. She'd seen the flag before on other runs -- a choice to prioritize station-keeping over instrument calibration in a specific two-minute window. The simulation's evaluation model considered the other choice optimal. Maria consistently chose the station-keeping. She'd been thinking about why for two months.

The answer she kept arriving at was: because the simulation's model assumes the instrument calibration matters more than the station-keeping in the specified window, and I think the model is wrong, and I think the margin for error on station-keeping in approach proximity is smaller than the model accounts for, and I would rather lose two minutes of calibration data than risk a drift event.

She wrote this down in the notes section of her run log.

It was possible she was wrong. It was possible the simulation's model was right and she was introducing a systematic error into her approach protocol that would cost her something on the actual mission. This was the problem with simulations: they told you what their model preferred, not what reality required. She was going to run a variant tomorrow in which she took the model's preferred choice and see what happened. She'd done this ten times before with this specific decision point and the model's choice had produced worse outcomes six times, better outcomes twice, and equivalent outcomes twice.

She logged the run, powered down the simulator, and went to the cafeteria.

Novak was at a corner table with a plate of food and a stack of reports. Maria took her tray and sat down across from her.

"The simulation flag," Maria said.

Novak didn't look up. "The station-keeping choice."

"You've been reviewing my logs."

"I review all the mission commander logs." She turned a page. "I agree with your choice."

Maria ate. The cafeteria was half full at this hour, the lunch crowd thinning toward the afternoon shift changeover, the low hum of three hundred people's daily routine going about its business. A base that had housed forty people when it was built in 2021 now housed four hundred and eleven, with another ring of habitat modules in planning that would bring it to six hundred and fifty by 2034. The base had grown the way the ship had grown: one section at a time, each one adding mass and permanence, until the thing it was building toward was no longer a concept but a fact.

"The Ring Two closure this morning," Maria said.

"I saw." Novak set down her report. She looked at Maria with the direct assessment that was her default mode of attention. "You were on the observation post."

"Seventeen minutes."

Novak was quiet for a moment. Then: "When I was running submarine operations, I had a commander who told me that every time you watch something become permanent, you should actually watch it. Not check the indicators and move on. Watch it."

"Sound advice."

"He died in 2022. Heart. Never saw any of this." She picked up her fork. "I think about that sometimes."

Maria thought about the ring closing, the flanges meeting, the green indicators in sequence. The thing that had become permanent this morning would still be permanent when Novak was dead and when Maria was dead and when whoever eventually piloted this ship to another star had done so and whatever came after that had come after that. The permanence operated on a scale that was difficult to hold in the mind.

"I'm going to get us to that asteroid," Maria said.

Novak looked at her.

"I know the probability. I know what the precursor data might show. I'm going to get us there and I'm going to place those impactors and we're going to move that rock." She said it the way she said things she'd finished deciding: without emphasis, as information. "I want you to know that."

Novak was quiet for a moment. "I know," she said.

They ate in the cafeteria with three hundred and ninety-eight other people who were building the same thing for the same reason and who were not going to stop.
