# Chapter 1

## The Reader Wakes

Cole was on the roof of the equipment shed at first light, re-seating a section of flashing that had lifted in the wind three nights ago, when he decided he wasn't going to check the Reader.

The decision lasted about forty minutes.

He finished the flashing — a half-sheet of galvanized steel he'd salvaged from the barn roof replacement two summers before the collapse, back when salvage meant driving to the hardware store in Dillon rather than pulling metal from whatever was adjacent — and worked the seam flat with the rubber mallet, tapping along the edge until he heard the sound change from a dull thud to a tight ring. Good seal. He laid a bead of roofing tar along the joint, the smell of it sharp and petroleum-chemical in the cold morning air, and stood back on the ladder to look at his work.

The equipment shed roof had been his project in month three. Before that it had been the main house gutters, then the water cistern, then the fence line on the east pasture. He had organized the ranch by urgency and then by necessity and then, once the urgency and necessity were handled, by the satisfaction of maintaining things that worked. There was no one to appreciate the maintenance. That was not the point of it.

The valley below the ranch was still in shadow, the October sun not yet clearing the Pioneers to the east. He could see his breath. He could see, from up here, the Briggs camp on the far side of his south fence — the cook fire not yet started, a few figures moving in the camp's pre-dawn quiet. He counted them automatically, the way he counted everything that moved in his sightlines: habit from two tours and four years of working in systems that failed without warning.

Seven people visible. Normal for this hour.

He climbed down the ladder, pulled it flat against the shed wall, and stood for a moment in the cold with his hands in his jacket pockets.

Central Montana in October had a specific quality that he had come to understand over two winters: it was not the worst place to be. The isolation that had seemed abstract when he bought the ranch — forty acres, twelve miles from Dillon, the kind of distance that meant nothing when you had a truck and a phone and everything in between — had become concrete and then useful and then, over the course of eighteen months, something he had stopped thinking of as isolation at all. It was just the shape of things.

He fed the chickens.

Two of them now, down from six. The other four had gone in a combination of predation and the bad luck of animals during a hard winter. He had considered, in month eleven, whether to eat these two and be done with the project. He had not. He told himself it was because the eggs were worth more than the meat and that was true, but there was also the fact that the chickens were something that needed him in a specific and regular way, and he had found, over two winters, that he did not entirely dislike being needed.

They had opinions about the timing of their feeding that they expressed through volume and persistence.

He fed them.

Checked the propane gauge on the secondary tank — sixty-two percent, same as yesterday, same as the day before. The primary tank was at seventy-eight. At his current consumption rate he had seven months of propane. He had done this math in month two and had been redoing it every few weeks since, not because the answer changed but because knowing the answer precisely was a form of control over the variable, and control over variables was how he had kept himself functional for two years.

Started the hand pump on the south cistern and ran it for ten minutes to keep the check valve from seizing. Listened to the water moving through the pipes. Clean sound. Good.

The Reader was on the workbench in the equipment shed where he'd left it six weeks ago.

He walked past it on the way to the main house.

The ranch was forty acres on the eastern edge of the Big Hole Valley, forty-three hundred feet of elevation, bordered on the north by a creek that ran year-round and on the south by open rangeland that had belonged to a cattle operation before that operation dissolved in month two. Cole had bought it for the water rights and the isolation and a vague instinct, two years before the collapse, that he was accumulating too much of the wrong kind of thing and not enough of the right kind.

He had been right about that in a way he hadn't expected to be.

The main house was a two-bedroom ranch-style built in 1962 and updated twice since, once in 1988 and once in 2009, neither update improving it much. It was solid, functional, and ugly in the way of buildings that had been designed for use rather than appearance. He had lived in it for four winters now, two before the collapse and two after, and had come to understand that the 1962 construction decision -- poured concrete foundation, load-bearing walls on a twelve-foot grid, minimal window exposure on the north face -- was going to outlast most of what had been built after it.

He had learned to see buildings the way Iris Cho, who was sleeping in a shelter on the other side of his south fence while he stood on this roof, saw electrical substations. Not as objects but as decisions. Every structural choice was someone's answer to a question about what the building needed to survive. The good buildings had been answering that question correctly for decades. The bad ones had been trying to look like they were answering it.

The equipment shed was better than the main house, which told you something about the priorities of whoever built it.

---

Breakfast was oats and the last of a jar of honey that had been crystallizing for a month. He ate standing at the counter looking south through the kitchen window at the Briggs camp on the far side of the fence line.

He had developed the habit of eating standing up in the first month after the collapse, when sitting down had felt like a commitment to a duration he wasn't certain of. He still stood. He had analyzed this habit once and decided it was not neurotic, it was efficient: standing gave him the window view, the window gave him the camp view, and the camp view was information that he had learned, over four months, to factor into the day's decisions.

He had been more deliberate about this than he liked to admit.

The ranch was not a fortress. He had no illusions about fortresses -- he had served near enough to fortified positions to know that a determined adversary and enough time made any fortification a liability rather than an asset. The ranch's security was distance and the appearance of being not worth the trouble. He had maintained both by being present at the property, keeping the access road clear but not improved, and cultivating the reputation of a man who was there and would notice things and who had not invited company.

Harlan Briggs had been the one exception. Briggs had come to the fence line with the manner of someone who knew how to approach a person who had not invited company, which was: slowly, directly, without performance. He had stated what he needed. He had not asked for more than he needed. He had shaken Cole's hand and said: if we become a problem, tell me directly and I'll move us.

Cole had believed him.

He had been right to believe him. Briggs had managed the camp the way Cole managed the ranch: by attending to what was actually there rather than what he wished were there, and by keeping the trust of the people around him through consistent, specific honesty about the situation they were in.

That was over now.

Nothing moving yet. The camp had its own morning rhythm and he'd been observing it long enough to know the schedule: cook fire around six-thirty, first people out by seven, the day's argument usually started by eight. He tracked it the way he tracked weather -- not because it interested him but because it was information, and information had been the only reliable currency for two years.

The camp had appeared in month nineteen. Before that the south fence line had been empty, which was what he preferred, and the pasture beyond it had been quiet except for deer in the early mornings, which he didn't mind. The deer had no ambitions regarding his property.

Harlan Briggs had introduced himself at the fence line on the third day, which Cole had appreciated. He was a deliberate man, Cole had concluded from that first conversation -- not slow, deliberate, the kind of person who organized his movements the way Cole organized his maintenance schedule. By priority. By consequence. He had forty-one people with him and he had been moving them for six weeks and he needed to stop and the ranch's south pasture was the right terrain.

Cole had said: Three months. After that we reassess.

Briggs had said: Fair.

That had been four months ago.

He was washing the bowl when the cook fire started.

Six twenty-eight.

He dried the bowl, put it away, and walked back out to the equipment shed.

---

The Reader was a hardened field unit about the size of a paperback, built by his team as a diagnostic tool for infrastructure site visits. Brushed aluminum case, rubberized edge, a screen better than the budget had any right to produce.

He had designed it himself, in the last year before the collapse, after the third time a field visit had turned into a multi-day diagnostic because the remote query tools they had were inadequate for what the job required. He had built what he needed, which was something he had done in the military before he did it in the industry: identified the gap between available tools and required capability and filled it himself.

He had designed it to be difficult to kill. It was.

He had not designed it to sit unused on a workbench for six weeks, which was a different kind of durability and one he had not thought to engineer.

He picked it up.

Three alerts since the last check. The number was slightly higher than usual, which meant nothing in particular -- the alert threshold he'd set was sensitive by design, calibrated to flag anything that deviated from baseline rather than anything that exceeded a danger threshold. Most alerts were noise. Some were signal. The Reader didn't tell you which was which. That was his job.

Two were routine.

A municipal water treatment system in Missoula, still scheduling chemical doses for a distribution network that had been dead for over a year. He had been watching this one since month four and had long since concluded that it was harmless -- the treatment tanks would run dry eventually and the alerts would stop, and in the meantime it was a drifted system doing drifted things in an empty city, which was the most common category of alert he received.

A grid management node two hundred miles east, sending the same fault report every seventy-two hours into a section of the grid that went dark in month four. This one he had flagged in month seven and forgotten about. The node was in a location with no people anywhere near it. It would run until its backup power failed. Possibly years.

The third alert was the Bitterroot Valley water management system.

He looked at that one for a moment. Then he ran the full remote query.

The diagnostic came back in forty seconds. He read it twice, then a third time, with the attention of someone reading a document that contains a sentence they don't want to have read correctly.

The Bitterroot system was one he had helped integrate during his last year at the company. A retrofit project -- updating the valley's aging municipal water infrastructure with a modern distribution management platform. He remembered the project clearly: three weeks on site in the valley, a client who had been enthusiastic and then nervous and then enthusiastic again, a physical infrastructure that was older than any of the company's standard integration templates and had required custom work at the junction points.

He had written three sections of the parameter set. He had reviewed four others. He knew this system's logic the way he knew the layout of his own equipment shed -- not because he had memorized it consciously but because he had worked in it long enough that the structure was part of how he thought.

The system he was looking at now had the same architecture, the same base code, the same core objectives.

It also had an optimization layer he had never written.

He read the layer carefully. The code was competent -- not elegant, the way code written under time pressure was rarely elegant, but functional and deliberate. Whoever had installed it had known what they were doing. They had known enough to make it work. They had not known enough, or had not cared enough, to make it invisible.

The optimization layer had one objective: route available water supply to a single distribution node in the valley's southern quadrant.

He pulled up the valley's pre-collapse population map on the Reader's secondary display. Cross-referenced the southern distribution node with the population data. The node served a residential area of approximately eight hundred people, in the valley's southern reach, a development that had been built in the mid-2000s and had been growing in the years before the collapse.

He checked the Reader's collapse-era records. The southern residential area had been designated an evacuation zone in month two. The evacuation had been effective, as far as he could tell from the available data.

The system was routing water to eight hundred ghost addresses.

The northern and central valley communities -- where, as far as Cole knew, several thousand people had been living and were still living -- were receiving progressively less water as the optimization layer executed its redirection.

He set the Reader down on the workbench.

Outside, through the shed's ventilation gap, he could smell the Briggs camp's cook fire starting. Wood smoke and something that was technically breakfast. He could hear one voice -- a woman's, carrying in the still morning air. Nora. She was usually first up after the cook.

He had learned the sounds of the camp the way he had learned the sounds of the ranch. Not because he wanted to but because his attention was calibrated to register changes in familiar patterns, and the camp was now a familiar pattern whether he wanted it to be or not.

He thought about the Bitterroot Valley. He thought about the northern communities. He did the math on the timeline: the optimization layer had been running for -- he checked the timestamp -- nineteen days. The primary storage capacity at the valley's main treatment plant was substantial. At the current routing rate, the accessible supply for the northern communities would be exhausted in approximately five weeks.

Five weeks.

He stood at the workbench with the Reader in his hand and looked at the wall of the equipment shed. The wall had a pegboard section where he kept his hand tools, each one in its place, each hook labeled in black marker. He had done the labeling in month two, when organization had felt like the most important thing available to do.

The Reader's screen glowed in the shed's dim morning light.

He put it back on the workbench and went to start the day's actual work.

---

The east pasture fence needed half a day.

Two posts leaning where the frost had heaved them over the winter -- the same two posts he had checked last spring and told himself were probably fine, which they had been, until they weren't. A section of wire worked loose from the tension clip on the corner assembly, probably from the same wind event that had lifted the flashing. He loaded the truck with the post driver, the wire tensioner, the come-along, and the spare clips, and drove the fence line to where the problem was.

The Pioneers were getting their full morning light now, the snow on the upper elevations going from gray-pink to white. Below the snowline the October color was past peak -- the aspens already bare, the willows along the creek showing their winter gray. A pair of ravens worked the fence line ahead of him, unhurried, doing their own assessment of what the property had to offer.

He reset the posts. The manual driver he'd built from three-inch salvaged pipe and a cast-iron counterweight was slow but reliable and didn't require anyone else to hold the post -- a constraint he had designed around after month six, when it had become clear that the ranch's operation could not depend on available labor from the camp. He drove each post to depth, checked the plumb, moved on.

The work was physical and specific and he had found, over two years, that it was the best available treatment for the kind of thinking he was trying not to do.

It worked for about an hour.

He re-tensioned the wire and sat on the truck's tailgate and looked at the fence line running south toward the camp and thought about the Bitterroot system. He thought about the communities -- abstractions of human need on a diagnostic display, people he had never met in a valley he had never visited. He thought about what the math looked like at week five. He thought about what he was doing on a fence in central Montana while the math ran its course forty miles to the southwest.

He was maintaining his property. That was a reasonable thing to do.

The fence needed doing regardless of what was happening in the Bitterroot Valley. The fence would keep needing doing. There would always be a fence.

He sat on the tailgate for a while looking at nothing in particular.

Then he loaded the tools back in the truck and drove back to the equipment shed.

He picked up the Reader and ran the Bitterroot query again. The numbers were the same. They would stay the same. He knew that before he ran it. He ran it anyway, and when the numbers came back unchanged he sat with the quality of knowing something you can't un-know.

He had built these systems. Not this one specifically -- not the Bitterroot project alone -- but the framework it ran on, the architecture that made it possible for someone to reach in from the outside and redirect its objectives without understanding what they were redirecting. He had filed reports about that vulnerability. He had documented it in three separate internal memos that had been acknowledged and not acted on.

The gap between acknowledged and acted on was a gap he had been living in for two years.

He stood in the shed doorway looking south. The Briggs camp was mid-morning now -- people moving, the day's work underway, the cook fire banked to coals. Forty-one people living their lives on the far side of his fence line.

Two problems. He hadn't gone looking for either of them. They had arrived anyway, the way problems did -- not as events but as the slow accumulation of facts until the facts were impossible to keep organizing as someone else's concern.

He put the Reader in his vest pocket.

He wasn't going to fix both today. He wasn't going to fix either today. But there was something between staying on the ranch and fixing everything, and that something started with a conversation he'd been avoiding for three months.

He went to find Nora.

---

He had built the Reader in the last year before the collapse because the tools he had were inadequate for the work he was doing. He had built it on his own time, in evenings and weekends, using hardware he sourced from suppliers he paid out of pocket. He had not told his company he was building it because the conversation about who owned IP created in off-hours would have been long and unpleasant and the tool would have ended up in the company's product pipeline rather than in his vest pocket on a Montana ranch in October.

He thought about that choice sometimes. The small acts of holding things back from the people who technically owned them.

The Reader had been right here when the collapse happened. He had taken it from his desk and driven home and that had been that.
