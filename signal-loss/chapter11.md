# Chapter 11

## Dillon

They passed through Dillon on the second day.

He had known this would be difficult and had not planned well enough for how difficult it would be. Not the logistics -- he had routed around the main Drift concentration, through the outskirts on the old highway alignment, and the logistics were fine. The difficulty was the people.

Eight of the twenty-three were from Dillon.

He had known this in the abstract. Nora had told him when they were planning the route: eight people from the town, two more from properties in the surrounding county. He had noted it and moved on because he had been thinking about the route and the route was what he could control.

What he couldn't control was the passage through the outskirts of a place that eight people had called home, looking at the buildings still standing and the streets still laid out and the cars still in the lots and the intact emptiness of a place that looked lived-in and wasn't.

The Drift was visible in the town's center. He could see it from the old highway -- the movement in the grid streets, the slow aggregate mass of Dillon's former population moving in the particular forward-leaning drift that he had been observing from his ranch for months from a comfortable distance. From here, close enough to read individual figures, it looked different.

He had not prepared the group for this.

He should have. He had known eight people were from here. He had treated it as a logistical variable and not as what it was, which was people passing through their former lives.

The failure mode was a familiar one -- he had seen it in himself before, in the Army and in the industry, and he had not entirely eliminated it despite being aware of it. He processed things technically first. He identified the logistical and security implications of a route and managed around them. He was good at this. He was less good at identifying the human implications of a route -- the way a road through a specific place would land on specific people -- and managing around those.

Briggs had been better at this than he was. Briggs would have told the eight people from Dillon what was coming and given them time to prepare and probably sat with them while they drove through. He would have made it a group experience rather than an individual one.

Cole had not done this. He was doing it now, approximately, at the last possible moment.

He stopped the truck at the top of the rise where the town was visible.

He got out. He stood for a moment with his back to the view, looking at the people in the truck bed.

He said: We'll take ten minutes here if you need it. No one goes below the ridgeline. After that we move.

He went around to the front of the truck and stood looking north, away from the town.

He could hear the group behind him. the quiet of people who had been given permission to stop moving. After a moment he heard footsteps on gravel, then quiet voices. Then a sound that might have been crying and might have been the wind.

Nora came and stood beside him.

That was the right thing to do, she said quietly.

It was late, he said.

Yes. But you did it.

He looked at the road north. At the mountains beyond.

I was thinking about the route, he said.

I know.

I should have been thinking about the people.

She was quiet for a moment.

You're learning, she said. She said it without condescension -- just an observation. A clinical note.

They stood in silence for the ten minutes. Behind him the quiet voices moved through whatever they needed to move through, and he held the space for it without being able to fully understand what he was holding.

Then Nora said: Time.

It was not an impatient word. It was the word she used in medical situations when the time available for something had elapsed and what remained was the next thing. She had said it the same way -- same register, same function -- when she had determined that a situation was past the window where a particular intervention would help. Not resignation. Precision.

He had heard her use it twice before.

Both times it had been exactly right.

He got back in the truck. The group got back in the truck bed, eighteen of them since five had stayed in Nora's truck. Their faces had the quality of people who had just put something down that they'd been carrying for a long time, and were not sure yet whether they were lighter or just empty.

He drove south through the outskirts.

The old highway ran through the commercial strip on Dillon's edge -- the gas stations, the fast food buildings, the auto parts stores with their lots still holding the cars of people who had stopped there in month one and never come back. A hardware store Cole had been to three times before the collapse. A grain elevator that had been one of his landmarks when he was still driving to town.

He drove past all of it and kept his eyes on the road.

He allowed himself one look at the hardware store. One glance, the kind that registered without dwelling. The parking lot held four cars, two of which he recognized from trips he had made before the collapse. A memory of a specific errand -- the fall of 2022, an October morning not unlike this one, buying a compression fitting for the cistern inlet that had been giving him trouble for a week. He had stood at the counter and talked to the clerk for ten minutes about nothing in particular and driven home.

He had not known the clerk's name.

He looked back at the road.

---

South of the commercial strip the road went through a transitional zone -- light industrial, storage units, a feed supply operation with its yard still stacked with pallets that had been delivering nowhere for two years.

The hostile group came out of a tire shop.

Six people. On foot, emerging from the shop's bay door with a purposeful movement of people who had been watching from inside and had decided the arriving column was worth engaging. Two of them carrying rifles, carried low but visible. The others spread in a loose line across the road.

Cole stopped the truck. He assessed the situation in the time it took to stop.

Six people, two visible weapons. Posture: aggressive but not committed -- the line across the road was a demand rather than an attack formation. No vehicles visible, which meant they were either local to this spot or had left their vehicles elsewhere, and local to this spot meant they had probably been here long enough to know the area and not long enough to have significant backup.

Marcus was already off the motorcycle.

He had positioned himself between the approaching group and the truck's cab in the time Cole was still stopping. Both hands visible, posture the careful neutral of someone who had done this before. He was nineteen years old and he stood there like someone who had been standing in difficult positions for most of those nineteen years.

Cole got out.

He stood beside Marcus and looked at the six people and let them finish arriving.

The lead man was in his forties, heavy in the shoulders, wearing a vest that had been a hunting vest before it became something else. He stopped twelve feet from Cole and looked at the trucks and at the people in the truck beds and did his own assessment.

Where are you going? he said.

South, Cole said.

Where south?

We're looking for viable resettlement terrain, Cole said. Not in this town. We're passing through.

What do you have?

What we need to get where we're going.

The lead man looked at the trucks. At the twenty-three people. At the motorcycle and Marcus.

You've got fuel, he said. I can smell it.

We have what we need to reach our destination.

The lead man looked at the two with rifles. The rifles didn't move. Cole assessed: they were waiting for direction rather than running their own initiative, which meant they would follow the lead man's decisions and the lead man's decisions were the variable.

Cole looked at the lead man steadily.

We're not stopping, Cole said. We're passing through. We're not a threat to this location and we have nothing to leave behind. He kept his voice flat and informational. If you let us pass, we're gone and you have your road back. If you don't let us pass, you have a problem you didn't have before we got here.

He let that sit.

The lead man looked at him. Cole met the look without escalating it.

Thirty seconds of quiet.

Then the lead man moved out of the road. He didn't say anything. He just stepped to the shoulder and looked at something else, and the others followed his lead, and Cole got back in the truck.

They drove through.

In the truck, Nora said: You've done that before.

Yes, Cole said.

Military?

And after. You learn what actual aggression looks like and what performance looks like. Those were performing.

What if they'd been actual?

He looked at the road.

Different conversation, he said.

She looked at him.

You'd have shot them, she said.

If they'd given me cause. Yes.

She turned back to the road. She didn't say anything else about it. He appreciated that.

---

Iris Cho stopped the column that evening.

She had been in the second truck since they left the ranch, and Cole had been aware of her in the way he was aware of all twenty-three people -- as a presence, a variable, a person who had made the decision to come and who he had a responsibility to. He had not had a real conversation with her.

She had been watching the landscape with a specific attention he had not noticed until she called a stop.

She was out of the second truck and walking toward a utility installation on the south side of the road before the trucks had fully stopped. Cole got out to follow.

The installation was a substation. Transformer bank, switching gear, the standard architecture of a regional power distribution node. Fenced. Two years of unmaintained weathering on the equipment. A signage panel on the fence showing the standard utility company warning.

And running.

Cole had clocked it as he drove past -- the hum of active equipment was something he registered automatically, the same way he registered sound anomalies underground. He had filed it as battery backup.

Iris was at the fence looking at the equipment with her head slightly tilted, the way people looked at things they were reading rather than just seeing.

This substation is running, she said. It shouldn't be. The grid in this section has been down for fourteen months.

He pulled out the Reader. Queried what he could reach from here.

Nothing in his credential set. The substation was operating on infrastructure he hadn't worked on.

Something's keeping it powered, Iris said. She looked at the transformer bank, at the switching gear, at the feed cables running south. It's not battery backup -- the load profile is wrong. Something upstream is actively supplying this node.

She looked at Cole.

Someone knows what they're doing, she said.

How do you know? he said. That it's not battery backup.

Because she was already explaining it -- the vocabulary of a retired electrical engineer looking at a system she understood intimately. The load profile, the current draw, the feed configuration. She was reading it the way he read code: not the surface, the underlying logic.

He listened to her explain it.

When she finished he said: Can you tell how long it's been running like this?

She looked at the equipment again.

The weathering on the new terminations is consistent with about eight months of exposure, she said. Someone modified this eight months ago.

He noted the timestamp. He put it beside the Bitterroot system's modification timestamp in his head.

Eight months.

He made a note in the field notebook and got back in the truck. But he was thinking about the substation long after it was behind them, and the thinking had a shape to it that the Bitterroot problem alone had not had.

Someone had been doing things in this region for eight months.

---

South of Dillon, the road opened up and the Bitterroot Valley's northern approach began to resolve.

He had been watching the terrain change since they left the commercial strip. The land here was different from the ranch country -- more cultivated even in its abandoned state, the evidence of agricultural investment in the soil quality and the drainage structures and the irrigation infrastructure that ran along the roads. Someone had spent money making this land productive. The money was gone. The land remained.

He drove through it and thought about systems that outlasted the intentions that created them.

Nora had fallen asleep in the passenger seat. She slept the way she did everything -- efficiently, without waste. Her head was against the window and her breathing was even and the medical notebook was in her lap with her hand on it.

He had been watching her for four months without fully understanding what he was watching. He was beginning to understand it now.

She was the kind of person who was fully present in the room she was in. Not performatively -- she didn't announce her presence, she didn't manage it. She was simply there, completely, in a way that made the room different than it was before she was in it.

He had been trying to be not-present in every room he was in for two years.

The fence line policy. The careful maintenance of distance. the discipline of being available without being inside.

He looked at the road south and thought about what it meant that twenty-three people were in the truck beds behind him and that this was different from being alone in a specific way he hadn't fully accounted for.

---

The valley opened south of the canyon and the Bitterroot ran parallel to the road a hundred yards to the east, the October river clear and cold, running over cobble and the occasional boulder that had come down from the mountain section in some previous high-water event. He had grown up near a river -- the Fox, in Illinois, nothing like this -- and had carried a preference for moving water without fully knowing he carried it.

Moving water was clean water, usually. Moving water was alive. He had been drinking from this river's tributaries for two years, filtered, and it had been fine. He trusted the river.

---

He drove out of Dillon's outskirts and back into the open country and the group in the truck beds slowly returned to themselves.

He could feel it in the drive -- the quality of a convoy that had been holding its breath and had released it. Not dramatic. Just: the tension that had been there for twenty minutes in the commercial strip and the tree line and the hostile group encounter was no longer there. The road was open and the valley was ahead and the day was October-cold and clear.

Marcus came back to convoy speed fifty yards back.

Nora looked up from the topo map.

How many days since you've been in a town? she said.

He thought about it.

Four months, he said. I drove to Dillon in June for a parts run. Before that -- He paused. Spring. March or April.

She nodded.

Did it feel strange? Being back in it?

He thought about the hardware store parking lot and the four cars he had recognized and the clerk whose name he hadn't known.

Some of it, he said.

She went back to the map.

It'll be different when you've been away longer, she said. Places that were yours stop being yours. They become places that were yours. That's a different thing.

He thought about this for a while.

He thought: the ranch is still mine.

He thought: for now.
