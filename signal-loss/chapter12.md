# Chapter 12

## The Substation

They went back to the substation the next morning.

Cole and Iris, while the group held in the barn. He had told Nora where they were going and when to expect them back. She had looked at him the way she looked at things she was professionally concerned about, and then she had said: Take the radio and use it. He had taken the radio.

He had been thinking about Iris since Dillon, where she had stopped the column at the substation and had been correct about what she was seeing. In the two days since, he had added observations to his initial read of her: she was precise in her movements, the economy of someone who had spent years working in confined electrical infrastructure where unnecessary motion was a safety risk. She asked questions only when she needed information she didn't already have, which made her questions worth answering carefully. She had the quality he associated with the best technical professionals he'd worked with -- she was interested in the problem rather than in her own performance on the problem.

She was also, he was beginning to understand, ill.

He had not asked. She had not said. But he had been watching people manage conditions for two years and he knew the difference between fatigue and managed fatigue, and what Iris was managing was not nothing. He filed it and moved on because it was her information to share and she had not shared it.

She moved around the substation's exterior with the systematic attention of thirty years of doing this. Not looking at the equipment the way a person looked at a thing -- reading it, the way Cole read code. She narrated as she worked, which he appreciated. The professionals who narrated were the ones worth learning from.

Primary feed cables running south from the transformer bank, she said. New terminations. She crouched at the base of the bank. The compression fittings are weathered consistent with maybe eight to ten months of exposure. These were not here when this substation was last serviced normally.

She stood and moved along the bank to the switching gear.

The original feed came from the north, she said. Standard configuration for this type of node -- it received power from the regional transmission network and stepped it down for local distribution. The northern feed is dead. Not failed -- disconnected. Someone disconnected it deliberately, with the right tools and the right knowledge, and then connected a new feed from the south.

Cole looked at the feed cables running south. Out of the fence, into the terrain. Trees, open hillside, nothing visible from here.

What's the source? he said.

Something to the south with enough generation capacity to run a regional distribution node. Not battery backup -- the load profile is wrong for backup, the current draw is too steady, too high. Whatever is supplying this is running continuously and has been running continuously since these terminations were made. She looked at Cole. That's not a generator. That's an installation.

He pulled out the Reader.

One system responded from this location that he hadn't reached before -- a distribution management platform, the architecture familiar, the parameter set entirely new. He read it with the attention it deserved.

Post-collapse timestamp. New objectives. Sophisticated code, the kind that came from someone who understood the systems they were modifying at a foundational level rather than a configuration level.

Optimize power distribution for residential and operational continuity within defined service area boundaries, he read.

Not pre-collapse objectives. These had been written after everything fell apart, by someone who understood what the system could do and had decided what they wanted it to do. the vocabulary of the objectives -- residential and operational continuity, defined service area -- was the vocabulary of professional infrastructure management. This was not improvised. This was planned.

He showed Iris the display.

She read it completely before responding. Then: Someone's running a private grid. They've used the remaining physical infrastructure as a backbone and they're generating their own power to supply it.

And they know what they're doing, Cole said.

They know exactly what they're doing. She looked back at the equipment. This modification required knowledge of grid segmentation techniques, an understanding of the specific node architecture, and access to the right materials and the right tools. There's no amateur improvisation here. This is textbook emergency grid segmentation, which is a technique I've seen used twice in thirty years of field work -- both times in genuinely emergency situations, by utility engineers who had planned for it in advance.

Planned in advance, Cole said.

The specific configuration she's used here, Iris said without elaborating on the she, requires preparation. You can't do this on the fly. You have to know in advance what you're going to do and have the materials staged. He or she or whoever, she corrected herself, had this planned before they did it.

Cole looked at the Reader. At the access log for the distribution management system: one credential set, first entry fourteen months ago.

Fourteen months ago, he said.

Before the worst of it, Iris said.

Before the worst of it. Yes.

She looked at the feed cables running south.

Are you going to go find it? she said.

Yes.

She looked at him with the steady attention she brought to things that mattered.

When you do, she said, ask them about the load calculation. The sizing of the generation source relative to the distribution load. That will tell you more about what they're planning than anything else will.

. He did not know yet that he would need to carry it without her.

---

They walked back to the barn in the mid-morning.

Cole carried the Reader and the notes he had made and the weight of a picture that had gotten more complicated with every piece Iris had added to it. He had arrived at the substation that morning with a theory: the Bitterroot system and the substation were connected, modified in the same window, by the same credential. He was leaving with confirmation and more detail than he had expected.

Iris walked beside him. She was not slow, exactly, but she was managing her pace with the specific deliberateness of someone who was attending to something he was not going to ask about directly.

What are you thinking about? he said. The size of it. You looked at the generation calculation.

The generation source is sized for growth, she said. Not just for the current installation -- for a substantially larger population than the current installation serves. Whoever built this was not planning to serve fifty people for a year. They were planning to serve several hundred people for a decade.

Cole walked with this.

That's infrastructure thinking, he said. Long-horizon planning.

It's what you do when you're building something that's meant to last. She looked at the field to the west, the mountains beyond. The technical difficulty of building this correctly, in the middle of the collapse, while managing whatever else they were managing -- that's not a small undertaking. This is someone who was thinking about this before the collapse made it necessary. Who had a plan and executed the plan.

That's not a prepper, he said.

No. Preppers build for themselves and stop. This is scaled for community. For a specific community of a specific size with a specific set of service requirements. She paused. Someone knew what community they were building for.

Or what community they were planning to build.

Yes, Iris said. That's the other possibility.

They walked for a while.

When you get there, she said. When you go to find them. Take the technical picture seriously. Not just the decisions they've made -- the technical quality of what they've built. That will tell you whether they can actually do what they're claiming to do or whether they're working at the edge of their capability and holding it together with effort they may not be able to sustain.

Cole looked at her.

What do you think you'll find? he said.

She thought about it.

I think you'll find someone who built the right thing for the wrong reasons, she said. Or the right reasons with the wrong implementation. And you'll have to decide whether the right thing outweighs the wrong that got it built.

Cole walked.

That's not a technical problem, he said.

No, Iris said. It's not.

They reached the barn. The group was doing the things that groups did at midmorning during a halt -- maintenance, conversation, the small productive motions of people who had learned to use available time. Rosa was in the truck bed with the notebook. Marcus was checking the motorcycle. Nora was with two members of the group who had shown symptoms she had been monitoring.

Cole stood at the barn's edge and looked at all of it for a moment.

He thought about what Iris had said: the right thing for the wrong reasons. He thought about the optimization layer and the eight months of water redirection and the northern communities with no framework for understanding why their supply was failing.

He thought about what the right thing outweighing the wrong would require.

He went to help with the prep for the afternoon's movement.

---

They drove south in the afternoon.

The barn was three hours behind them and the valley was somewhere ahead and Cole had spent the drive thinking about the substation and about Iris's analysis and about the fourteen systems he was building toward understanding.

He thought about infrastructure as language.

The systems he could read -- the Bitterroot system, the substation, the logistics network Marcus navigated -- were all expressions of decisions. What to optimize for, what to prioritize, who to serve. The decision was embedded in the parameter set and the parameter set ran the system and the system produced outcomes for the people inside it.

If you could read the parameter set, you could read the decision.

The optimization layer in the Bitterroot system: route water south, deprive northern communities, serve a defined population at the expense of an undefined one. That was the decision, embedded in the code.

The substation modification: disconnect from the failing regional grid, connect to a private generation source, provide power to a defined service area. That was the decision.

Both decisions made by the same person, eight months ago, in service of the same objective.

The objective: build a viable community at the expense of the surrounding region.

He thought about whether that was the right framing.

The surrounding region had not been viable in the absence of the managed community's infrastructure. The power the substation provided was power that the regional grid was not providing. The community was not extracting from a working system -- it was running a system in the vacuum left by systems that had failed.

But the water was different. The water had been taken from communities that needed it. The northern communities had not failed -- they had been failing because of a decision made by someone else.

That was a different moral category.

He drove and thought about the difference between filling a vacuum and creating a shortage.

He thought about how to say this clearly when he was in the room with whoever had made these decisions.

He thought about what Iris would have said about it, which was probably: say it directly and see what they say.

He thought about Iris.

He looked at the road ahead.

---

On the walk back to the barn, Iris said: What are you going to do with this?

He thought about how to answer.

Go south, he said. Fix the Bitterroot system. Then find whoever is running the private grid and have a conversation.

What kind of conversation?

The kind where I find out whether what they're building is something that helps the valley or something that helps itself at the valley's expense.

Iris was quiet for a moment.

Those aren't mutually exclusive, she said.

No, Cole said. They're not.

She looked at the terrain ahead -- the barn visible half a mile off, the group's activity visible even at this distance, the smoke from the morning cook fire rising in the still air.

I've been thinking about infrastructure since I retired, she said. What it means for it to outlast the people who built it. She paused. Most of the infrastructure we built was for growth. Bigger, more, faster. It was designed for a world that kept expanding. She looked at the substation behind them. This one wasn't.

No, Cole said.

Whoever built this was thinking about what remained viable when the expansion stopped.

She thought about that.

That's a different kind of engineering problem, she said. Not bigger. Sufficient. She paused. I find that I have more respect for it than I expected to.

Cole looked back at the substation. At the transformer bank running cleanly, the feed cables disappearing south.

So do I, he said.

---

They walked the fence perimeter before they left.

Iris's idea. She wanted to look at the outgoing feed line from the south side, to see if the conduit burial depth was consistent with a planned installation or an emergency one.

It was consistent with a planned installation. The conduit was buried at the correct depth for the soil type and the ambient temperature range. There was a pull box at the thirty-meter mark, appropriately sealed. The conduit ran true south without the lateral deflections that emergency installation often produced.

This was planned before the collapse, Iris said.

Or in the very early months, Cole said. The weathering is consistent with about two years of exposure at the surface connections.

She crouched at the pull box and looked at the conduit entry point.

Someone ran this, she said. As in: ran it themselves, in the field. Not a contractor crew.

How do you know?

The bend radius at the entry is slightly tighter than code minimum. A licensed contractor wouldn't do that. Someone who was doing it themselves, under time pressure, knowing the code but not worrying about the inspection. She stood. You learn to read these things.

He looked at the conduit disappearing into the earth.

Someone running cable in a field alone, she said. In the early months.

He thought about that image.
