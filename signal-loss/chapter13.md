# Chapter 13

## Purcell's People

He spotted the three vehicles on the afternoon of the third day.

They were moving on a parallel road to the east, visible in gaps between tree lines when the terrain opened up. Not pursuing -- pacing. Maintaining a consistent position relative to the group's movement that told Cole they were tracking rather than approaching.

He watched the gaps for forty minutes before he said anything to Marcus.

Marcus disappeared.

He came back twenty minutes later with the quality of someone who had gotten close to something without being seen.

Three vehicles, he said. Seven people. A flag on the lead truck's antenna -- the same flag Purcell had on the operations tarp at the camp. A hand-drawn design, some kind of stylized symbol that Cole had registered and filed when he first saw it without attaching significance to it.

Cole looked at the tree line to the east.

The flag was information. It told him that Purcell had given his group an identity marker before they left -- that there had been enough organizational preparation for the reconnaissance to include branding. A man who sent a reconnaissance team out with a flag was a man who was already building an organization rather than just leading a group of survivors.

What are they doing? Cole said.

Watching. Marcus paused. The guy in the lead truck has a notebook. He's been writing things down every time your column stops. He's clocked the fuel stop, the canyon crossing, the substation halt. He writes it down, your column moves, he writes when you move and which direction.

Logging the route.

Your route specifically, Marcus said. Not just where you go. What you do when you stop. How long it takes. What resources you access.

Cole thought about this.

Purcell is doing a capability assessment, he said. He wants to know what I know and whether it's worth what he'd have to pay for it.

Nora was in the passenger seat. She had been listening.

He's going to try to recruit you, she said.

He's going to try to use me, Cole said. Which is different from recruiting me.

Is it?

Recruiting means he accepts my terms. Using means he establishes the terms and presents them as an offer. He paused. He doesn't know enough yet to establish the terms correctly, so he's gathering information.

She looked at him.

You've thought about Purcell a lot, she said.

I've worked with Purcell before, he said. Different name. Same operational logic.

He told Marcus to maintain visibility to the eastern column while providing nothing usable. Marcus received this as a straightforward instruction and went to execute it with the ease of someone who had been uninformative professionally.

---

They camped that night in a grain storage facility.

The facility was off a county road, a metal building with a concrete floor and enough space for the trucks and the group. Marcus had found it, as Marcus found most things, by going ahead and coming back with specific information. Cole had run the Reader at the location and found nothing concerning.

The evening was useful. People cooked, checked their gear, had the small practical conversations of a group that had been moving together long enough to develop the rhythms of shared work. Cole moved through it attending to what needed attending to and leaving alone what didn't.

At nine o'clock, Marcus appeared at Cole's shoulder.

I talked to the eastern column, he said.

Cole looked at him.

I told you to maintain visibility and provide nothing usable.

I maintained visibility and provided nothing usable. I also gathered information, which you didn't tell me not to do.

Cole looked at the jaw, which had a new bruise.

The information cost you something, he said.

One of the seven didn't appreciate the conversation. The other six were fine. He touched the jaw. It's fine. More importantly: Purcell is moving in five days. He's already organizing. He heard from someone that the valley communities are intact and he's committed to establishing a presence there before anyone else does.

Five days, Cole said.

And he knows about you, Marcus said. Not your name. He knows someone in the group has technical knowledge of the valley's infrastructure systems. Someone who was able to identify and potentially fix what's wrong with the water supply. He paused. He wants that person. He has been building toward that want for three days.

Cole looked at the Reader in his vest pocket.

He thinks I modified the Bitterroot system, he said.

He thinks whoever is heading to the valley is connected to the infrastructure modifications. He doesn't know how and he doesn't know who specifically, but he's operating on the assumption that the person he wants is in this group.

And he'll arrive in the valley five days behind us.

Five days if he moves when he said he would. He looked at Marcus's jaw. The one who hit you -- that's Purcell expressing something?

That's one of Purcell's people expressing something Purcell wouldn't express directly, Marcus said. He seemed satisfied with that answer.

Cole thought about the five-day window. He thought about the Bitterroot timeline, which had compressed since the previous morning. He thought about the valley communities and whoever was holding them together and whatever was running the southern infrastructure, and about Purcell arriving in five days with forty people and a flag and a notebook full of capability assessments.

Nora appeared.

She looked at Marcus's jaw and said: Come here, and Marcus came, and she examined it, and said: Nothing broken. She handed him a cold pack from the medical bag. Then she looked at Cole.

Five days isn't much, she said.

No.

You need to fix the system and understand the southern situation before he arrives.

Yes.

She looked at the grain facility, at the group settled for the night.

Then we move early, she said.

We move at dark, Cole said. The timeline on the Bitterroot system just got shorter.

She nodded and went back to the medical bag.

Marcus held the cold pack against his jaw and looked at Cole.

What's the shorter timeline? he said.

Six days, Cole said. Maybe five.

Marcus considered this.

So we have five days before the water is gone and five days before Purcell arrives, he said.

And we're two days from the valley.

Marcus looked at the cold pack.

That's close, he said.

Close is workable, Cole said.

---

Cole spent the evening of the third day doing something he rarely did: talking to the group.

Not a briefing. He had given briefings. This was different -- he sat with the groups of people who had gathered at the barn's edges in the way of people who were processing a difficult day, and he let the conversations find him.

He talked to a woman named Elena who had been a teacher before the collapse and who had been running what she called an informal curriculum for the children in the camp. She wanted to know what he thought they would find in the valley. He told her what he knew and what he didn't know. She listened and said: I've been telling the children that a place exists where things are being managed. She looked at Cole. You're the managed things.

He talked to a man named Robert who had been a veterinarian and who had been managing the camp's few remaining animals and who had concerns about disease vectors in the valley communities. Cole took the concerns seriously, which Robert noticed, and which produced a further conversation about what the Reader could and could not tell them about the valley's public health infrastructure.

He talked, briefly, to Eddie.

It was the first real conversation they had had, rather than exchanges of specific information. Eddie said: You've been carrying this alone since we left the ranch.

Cole considered this.

I've been carrying the technical picture alone, he said. Everyone else has been carrying their parts.

Eddie looked at the barn's interior.

Rosa told me you looked at her notebook today, he said.

She showed me her resource tracking. It's good work.

She's been doing it since month three. He paused. I didn't teach her. I mentioned that I was tracking things and she started doing it. He looked at Cole. She was twelve. She just started doing it.

She's useful, Cole said. Not as a category -- specifically useful. She sees things the way the Reader sees things, through the numbers.

Eddie nodded.

She's going to want to understand the Reader, he said. Fully. When things settle down enough. She's going to want to understand how all of it works.

Cole thought about Rosa's three questions and her column notebook and the way she had filed his answer about whether the person who modified the Bitterroot system would try to stop him.

I know, he said.

It was the beginning of a different kind of conversation. Neither of them knew that yet.

---

The eastern column was three vehicles at dawn, two by midday.

Marcus reported this at the midday halt. The column had dropped one vehicle overnight -- not retreating, repositioning. The two remaining vehicles had moved closer, into a sightline that let them track the group's movement more precisely.

Tightening the surveillance, Cole said.

They're interested in something specific, Marcus said. They were tracking our fuel stops in the first two days. Now they're more interested in where we sleep and how long we rest.

They're calculating our pace, Cole said. Our rate of movement versus their rate of movement.

He thought about what Purcell knew.

Purcell knew Cole had Reader access to the valley's infrastructure. He knew Cole had credentials. He knew Cole was moving toward the valley and had been moving toward it efficiently. He was calculating whether he could get there first, or at the same time, and what position that would put him in.

The answer was: he could not get there first if Cole was moving at his current pace. The reconnaissance was telling him this.

So Purcell was not planning to beat Cole to the valley. He was planning to arrive shortly after and understand the situation Cole had created.

That was actually a reasonable strategy.

Cole thought about how to use it.

If Purcell arrived in the valley two days after Cole had fixed the Bitterroot system and established relationships with the northern communities, Purcell would arrive into a situation that was already organized. He would not be building the situation -- he would be joining one. That was the constraint Cole wanted.

The constraint required the northern communities to be organized enough, and Cole's relationships with them solid enough, that Purcell's arrival changed nothing structural.

He had four and a half days, approximately, to accomplish this.

He drove and thought about the tasks.

First: fix the water. That was the first thing and the thing everything else depended on.

Second: understand the southern community well enough to know whether it was an asset or a threat to the valley's future.

Third: establish enough relationship with whoever was running the northern communities that the framework for what the valley was and what it needed was clear before Purcell arrived to offer his version of it.

Fourth: be in the valley when Purcell arrived, because Purcell would make his assessment of the situation based on who was present and who understood the situation and who had already built trust with the people who mattered.

The fourth one was the hardest. He didn't have the relational capital yet. He was going to have to build it fast.

He thought about the conversations he'd had in Victor and the crossroads settlement. He thought about the quality of those conversations -- people who had been managing incomprehensible situations and had just been given a comprehensible explanation of one piece of it.

That was the beginning of trust. Not sufficient, but a beginning.

He drove south.

---

They camped that night in the open, the trucks pulled into a field access track, a small fire at the center of the group.

Cole sat at the fire's edge and worked through the Reader data. The Bitterroot query, the accelerating drawdown, the Purcell surveillance three vehicles to the east. The substation credential. The fourteen-system picture he was building.

Rosa came and sat near him.

She had her notebook, open to a page dense with small writing. He had seen her writing in it every day since the ranch. He had not asked what she was writing.

He asked.

She looked at him with the evaluating look she had, the one that was older than fourteen.

A record, she said. She showed him a page.

It was a daily log. Date, location, conditions, medical status -- her own, recorded in Nora's notation system, which she had apparently learned by observation. Resources available. Notable events.

You've been keeping this since the ranch? he said.

Since month three, she said. Dad taught me. He said if you don't write things down, the important things get mixed up with the unimportant things and eventually you can't tell them apart.

Cole looked at the notebook.

That's good advice, he said.

She closed it and looked at the fire.

Do you think it'll be better, she said. Where we're going.

He thought about how to answer honestly.

I think the valley has what it needs to be better, he said. Whether it becomes better depends on what the people in it decide to do.

That's not really an answer, she said.

No, he said. It's not.

She thought about that for a moment.

Dad says you're the kind of person who fixes things, she said. He says those people are rare.

Cole looked at the fire.

I fix systems, he said. Systems are easier than people.

She was quiet for a moment.

Maybe, she said. But you have to fix the systems before the people can fix themselves.

He looked at her.

That's a pretty good answer, he said.

She shrugged.

I write things down, she said.

---

The camp that night had a different quality from the previous nights.

He could not have said exactly what the difference was. The same twenty-three people, the same trucks, the same equipment. But the camp had been traveling together for four days and four days of traveling together in difficult conditions produced a specific social chemistry that the first night had not had.

He watched it from the watch position. The way people had stopped negotiating space with each other and had settled into the natural arrangement of a group that had been functioning together long enough to have an arrangement. The way the cook fire was handled now without discussion, the same two people doing the same things without anyone assigning them. The way Marcus sat at the group's edge and was no longer completely outside it.

---

The Purcell surveillance continued through the afternoon.

Cole watched it in the rearview on the stretches where the eastern road was visible. The three vehicles maintained their distance -- not closing, not falling back, the pace of professional surveillance rather than casual following. Purcell had sent people who knew what they were doing.

He thought about what Purcell was doing with the information. Building a picture of the route, the resources accessed, the group's size and capability. Building a model of what they would find at the valley and what Cole's specific role in the valley would be.

He thought about Purcell arriving in the valley five days after the group and finding whatever Cole had left for him to find.

He thought about what he wanted Purcell to find.

A working water system. A community in the northern valley that was organized and had a council that was not dependent on any single person. A situation where Purcell's specific skills -- organizing, negotiating, managing resources -- were genuinely useful but not uniquely necessary.

He thought about what it took to build that in five days while also fixing the water system and figuring out what the southern installation was.

He thought: I need the valley's people to be more ready for Purcell than Purcell is ready for them.

He thought about the northern community leaders and how they would receive what he was bringing.

He thought: whoever was running those communities might be ready.
