# Chapter 14

## The Road Doesn't Hold

The washout was five miles north of the valley.

He had known it was a possibility. The road through the mountain section ran alongside a creek that had been flooding annually before the collapse, and without two years of maintenance the drainage culverts had been overloaded and the road had gone with them on one side. Not a complete loss -- the creek hadn't taken the whole road, just the shoulder and three feet of the driving lane. But three feet of missing road was enough to stop the trucks.

He stopped and got out and looked at it in the afternoon light.

He stood at the gap for two minutes. Not looking at it -- building a model of it. Width, depth, the creek's current velocity, the substrate visible in the crossing. The composition of the remaining road on each side, whether the edge was stable or would erode further under a vehicle's weight. The angle of approach and departure from both directions.

He thought about the people in the truck beds behind him. Twenty-three people who had made this trip on the assumption that he had a plan. He had a plan. The plan had encountered the washout.

He thought about the plans he had made in the Army that had encountered equivalent obstacles. the discipline required in those situations was not improvisation -- improvisation was what you did when you had no plan and no options. What he was doing was adaptation: a plan encountering new information and incorporating it, the structure of the plan intact, the execution adjusting.

The distinction mattered. Improvisation was chaos managed after the fact. Adaptation was design responding to new constraints. He was adapting.

The model told him: impassable for the trucks as currently configured. The Forest Service road was the answer but added time he did not entirely have.

The Reader's timeline numbers sat in his head alongside the model.

The gap was clean -- the road had gone, the creek had moved laterally to fill the space, and what remained was a clear crossing problem: enough road on either side, not enough in the middle. Twenty feet of gap, the creek running through it at about eight inches of depth over a rocky bottom.

Marcus was already off the motorcycle, standing at the edge of the gap, looking at the creek bed.

We could wade it, Marcus said.

We could. The trucks can't.

They could if you bridged it.

With what?

Marcus looked at the road ahead, at the tree line to the west, at the truck beds. He was doing the same assessment Cole was doing, which was the most useful quality he had: he looked at problems the way Cole looked at them.

There's a Forest Service road about six miles south, Marcus said. Takes a longer route but comes back to the main road on the valley side of this washout.

How long does it add?

Two, maybe three hours depending on the condition.

Cole pulled out the Reader. He ran the Bitterroot query.

The numbers were wrong.

Not wrong in the sense of bad data. Wrong in the sense that the timeline had jumped. The drawdown rate had doubled -- something upstream had changed, a valve state or a physical pressure event, something that had cascaded through the system and accelerated the optimization layer's effect. The timeline he had been working from -- four and a half weeks -- had just become six days.

He looked at the numbers for a moment.

Nora had come up beside him.

How long to the facility? she said.

Twenty miles on the main route. Twenty-six on the Forest Service detour.

And the timeline?

Six days, he said. Maybe less.

She looked at the washout. At the Reader. At him.

We go tonight, she said.

Yes.

On the Forest Service road.

In the dark.

She looked at the road behind them -- the group at the trucks, the light going flat.

I need thirty minutes to do medical checks before we move again, she said. Rosa's numbers need tracking and I have two people showing dehydration markers I don't like.

Thirty minutes, he said.

She was already walking back to the trucks.

He watched her go and thought about the thirty-minute window. Thirty minutes in cold weather with a group that had been in the trucks all day -- he had the sequence: the medical checks, the water distribution, the explanation of the night operation's structure. She would have it organized before the thirty minutes was up.

He went to check the Forest Service road's first mile on foot, with Marcus, to know what they were driving into before they drove into it.

---

They moved at ten o'clock.

Twenty-three people in two trucks and one motorcycle on a Forest Service road in the dark in October. He had organized them the way he organized difficult operations -- giving each person a specific role and a specific expectation, which reduced the cognitive load by converting uncertainty into procedure. Nora managed the two most vulnerable group members, checking in by radio every thirty minutes. Eddie tracked road condition from the second truck cab and radioed warnings ahead. Marcus ran lead. Cole drove.

The group had surprised him, over four days, in specific ways he had not predicted. He had expected attrition -- the people who realized on day two that the road was harder than they had understood and who would have gone back if going back were an option. No one had mentioned going back. He had expected friction -- the social compression of twenty-three people in close quarters under stress, the irritabilities and small conflicts that were the normal exhaust of group dynamics. There had been friction. It had been managed.

He had not managed it. Nora had managed it. She had been doing it with such economy of intervention that he had been watching for days before he understood what he was watching: the small conversations, the placements during rest stops, the way she organized sleeping arrangements that kept certain people apart and other people together. The social architecture of the group was her work.

He had been thinking about the logistics. She had been thinking about the people.

Simple structures. Simple roles. Simple enough to hold together in the dark on bad road.

Marcus on the motorcycle with the better of the two headlamps running at full brightness, thirty yards ahead. Cole in the lead truck, Nora navigating with the topo map and a flashlight she held at the map's edge to preserve night vision. The Forest Service road was worse than rough -- rutted, the drainage ditches on both sides overgrown and filling the road's shoulders, the kind of road that had been maintained on a schedule and had not had that schedule for two years.

He drove at fifteen miles an hour because fifteen miles an hour was the fastest you could drive this kind of road and still react to what it threw at you. The group in the truck beds was quiet. The road required attention and attention imposed a kind of silence.

The night was very dark. Montana dark, the kind of dark that people from cities didn't prepare for -- not the urban dark of closed curtains and streetlight bleed, but the actual absence of light across forty miles of empty country. The headlamps lit a cone ahead and nothing else.

Eddie Garza rode in the cab on the second truck. Cole had moved him forward at the start of the night drive because Eddie had been to this valley before and knew the roads, at least in outline, and a second set of informed eyes in the cab was better than a second set of uninformed ones.

At the two-hour mark, the lead truck hit the rut.

He felt it before he heard it -- the front right corner dropping into a drainage cut that the headlamps had not resolved until they were in it, the suspension compressing hard against the stop. Then the sound: a heavy crack from the right front wheel well.

He stopped the truck immediately.

Got out. Marcus was already back from ahead, off the motorcycle, headlamp aimed at the wheel well.

The shock mount had pulled, Cole said.

Marcus looked at it.

Is it driveable?

Not safely. Not at night on this road.

Iris came from the second truck. She had been in the cab with Eddie and she came forward with the unhurried purposeful movement of someone who had already assessed the situation from a distance.

She had been in the second truck since Dillon -- quiet, attentive, doing what she did which was observe and file and intervene when intervention was warranted. Cole had noticed that she intervened rarely and correctly, which was a more useful combination than intervening often.

She carried a small toolkit on her belt -- not a large one, the kind you could improvise with and the kind that contained the most useful ten percent of what a proper field kit held. He had noticed it on the first day. He had not asked about it because the presence of a toolkit on someone's belt was self-explanatory.

She looked at the wheel well. She looked at the angle of the shock absorber against the mount. She looked at what tools they had.

Twenty minutes and I need two people to hold the truck at an angle, she said.

Cole organized the group to hold the truck. He pulled the tool kit from behind the seat and gave Iris what she asked for. He held the light while she worked.

She didn't explain what she was doing. She did it. He watched her hands in the headlamp beam moving with the precise economy of someone who had been working with physical systems for thirty years and had the confidence that came from knowing the difference between what would hold and what wouldn't.

Sixteen minutes.

She came out from under the wheel well and stood and looked at the suspension component.

It'll hold, she said. Don't hit anything else.

He thanked her. She handed him back the tools and got into the second truck without ceremony.

They drove on. The road got better for a mile and then worse again and then better as they came off the mountain terrain and onto the valley's approach. At three in the morning they came off the Forest Service road onto the valley floor.

And the valley was dark.

He had expected lights. Small ones, the minimal light of communities running on generator power or wood fire. The hydro installation on the tributary creek that served the northern valley had been functional in the pre-collapse records. Communities that were viable had lights, in his experience.

The northern end of the valley was dark.

He stopped the truck and ran the Reader. The Bitterroot system's drawdown was complete. The northern communities' accessible water supply was gone.

He sat with that for a moment.

Then he drove toward the dark.

He had expected this as a possibility and had prepared for it and still felt the weight of it. The northern communities had been surviving for two years. They were not gone.

But the dark was not nothing.

---

He drove the first hour of the Forest Service road in an alert state that bad roads in the dark required.

The state was not anxiety -- he had driven bad roads in worse conditions in worse vehicles and had come to understand that anxiety was not useful in these situations and that the useful state was the alert state, which was: full attention on what was immediately in front of the vehicle, the headlamps showing forty feet of road, the decisions made at the limit of that forty feet rather than at the horizon.

He drove at the speed the road required and no faster.

Marcus was ahead with the better headlamp. Cole had the radio open and Marcus's voice came through it at the problem points -- the drainage cuts, the places where the shoulder had slumped into the ditch, the one section where an elk trail had been worn across the road surface and the wheel ruts from years of elk crossings had created a washboard that required specific speed management.

The group in the truck beds was quiet. This was their third night moving and the quiet had a different quality from the first night's quiet -- less unprocessed anxiety, more managed fatigue. The experienced quiet of people who had learned what the moving required.

Nora's radio check came at thirty minutes.

Medical status: stable. No changes. Rosa is awake and reading her notebook. Cole -- is there any reason to think the timeline is going to compress further tonight?

Not from inside the facility, he said. The system I fixed will run correctly now. If anything changes it'll be external.

Understood, she said.

The road crested a ridge and the valley appeared below them -- not the near valley, the Bitterroot, but the intermediate terrain, the foothills draining west toward the valley floor. He could see, in the far distance, the faintest indication of the valley's presence: a specific quality of dark that was different from the dark of the mountains.

Forty minutes, he estimated.

He drove.

---

He was four miles out from the valley floor when he stopped the truck for a moment at a pull-off.

Not because anything required stopping. Because he needed a moment to sit with what the Reader was telling him.

The Bitterroot system's drawdown was complete. Northern communities: no accessible supply. He had known this was coming since the timeline compressed. He had been driving toward it with a focus from someone managing toward a solution.

He sat with the fact of it for thirty seconds.

People in the northern valley had been rationing water for weeks. They had been making decisions about what to use water for and what to go without. Some of those decisions had had medical consequences. Some of those consequences were irreversible.

He had not caused this. He had not been able to prevent it -- he had not known about it until the problem was already weeks old. The timeline from finding the problem to being in a position to fix it had been as short as he could make it.

None of that changed what had happened.

He put the truck back in gear.

He was four miles from the place where he could fix one variable in a situation with many variables. He was going to fix it. Then he was going to understand the rest of the variables and deal with them in sequence.

He drove toward the dark.

---

The Forest Service road had once been a logging road before it was maintained and before the logging ended. He could read this in the grade changes -- too steep in sections for a road built for regular traffic, acceptable for the occasional heavy equipment that had used it seasonally. The sections where the grade was steepest were the sections where the road's edge had eroded most, the drainage structures failing on the steeper grades first.

He drove these sections at ten miles an hour and kept the wheel straight and did not think about anything except the next ten feet of road.

This was not a bad way to exist for stretches. The narrowing of attention to the immediately physical, the immediate specific problem of this section of road in the dark. He had done it in the Army on difficult terrain and had found it then, as he found it now, clarifying.

The wide-angle problem -- the Bitterroot system, the unknown party, the timeline, Purcell -- was still there. It would be there when the Forest Service road ended. For now: ten feet of road.
