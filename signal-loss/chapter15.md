# Chapter 15

## The Valley Floor

The first community was Stevensville.

He drove in slowly with the headlights on dim and the Reader in his lap running a continuous query. Not because he expected trouble -- he expected people, which was its own kind of complexity -- but because an approach to a community he had no information about required more attention than open road.

He had known, in the abstract, that there were communities in this valley. Numbers on a population map, potential beneficiaries of a fix he needed to make. The abstraction had been adequate for planning purposes.

The communities in the northern Bitterroot Valley had been agricultural and small-city before the collapse. Farms, small towns, the light industrial support of an agricultural economy. Not wealthy, not particularly visible nationally, the kind of place that existed in large numbers across the rural west and that had not been a primary focus of the collapse's attention -- the collapse had been loudest in the dense population centers, and places like this had experienced it as a slow withdrawal of supply chains and services rather than as a sudden catastrophic event.

They had had time to adapt, which meant they had adapted. The communities that had time to adapt were the ones that were still there.

The problem with abstractions was that they were adequate for planning until you arrived at the actual thing. The gap between the abstraction and the actual thing became the primary variable at the point of contact.

The actual thing was: a working community of over forty people who had been losing their water for three weeks and had not abandoned the place or turned on each other. That was not a small thing.

He got the false haven warning first.

The Reader pinged a system he hadn't queried -- an emergency management platform, still broadcasting on a public-facing channel, displaying shelter availability and resource status for the Stevensville area. He looked at the display.

The system was showing: shelter available, water available, medical supplies available.

He stopped the truck. Looked at the display. Looked at the dark town ahead.

He ran a full diagnostic on the emergency management system.

The status data was eighteen months old. The system had been broadcasting continuously since month six with the last information it had been given, which was the status at the time of the last update before the person managing it stopped updating it. The shelter it was advertising had burned in month eight -- he couldn't know that from the system, but the system's update timestamp told him the information was stale, and stale enough to be wrong.

He looked at the dark town.

Someone who didn't know to check the timestamp would drive into Stevensville following the emergency broadcast and find none of what the broadcast promised. That had probably happened already, multiple times. The broadcast was still running, still promising, still sending people toward resources that didn't exist.

He turned off the truck's radio.

Why'd you do that? Marcus said from outside the window.

Cole showed him the Reader display. The emergency broadcast, the timestamp, the gap.

Marcus looked at it.

So there's a system telling people to come here for resources that aren't here, he said.

There are probably dozens of systems like this still running, Cole said. The emergency management network was designed to broadcast continuously without human input once activated. Nobody's turned them off because nobody's been managing them.

People have been following them.

Some of them, yes.

He put the Reader on the dash and drove into Stevensville. When the man with the lantern came out from the central block, Cole was ready for it.

---

Curtis Hall.

The remaining council. Sixty-eight people when the second wave hit, forty-three now. He said this with the brevity of someone who had given this number enough times that it had become a fact rather than a grief.

They went inside -- into the central block, a hardware store that the community had been using as their main gathering space, the tool displays still on the walls, shelving cleared for living space. People were awake despite the hour, which meant they had been running extended watches -- the sign of a community under sustained stress.

Cole laid out the Bitterroot situation at a folding table with Curtis Hall and two other people Hall introduced as the remaining council members. He gave them the technical picture. He watched their faces as he explained what the optimization layer was doing and what he could do about it.

The relief was visible and real and had in it a specific quality that Cole found difficult to look at directly: the relief of people who had been managing an incomprehensible situation and were being told that it had a comprehensible cause. The incomprehensible was harder to bear than the merely terrible.

Curtis Hall had the quality of someone who had been carrying a weight for long enough that the weight had become invisible to him -- not because it wasn't there but because he had integrated it so completely into his normal functioning that he no longer registered it as separate from himself. Cole had seen this before in people who had been managing sustained crisis. The weight showed in specific ways: the slight economy of expression, the careful brevity of descriptions that were actually long stories, the way you answered a direct question without prelude because prelude had been compressed out by months of dealing with things that required immediate response.

When Cole told him what the optimization layer was and what it had been doing, something shifted in Hall's face -- the weight redistributing. It was not relief exactly. It was the adjustment of someone who had been carrying the wrong answer and had just been given the right one.

Can you fix it? Curtis said.

Yes. I need to get to the facility.

We know where it is. We've sent people twice. The security system is active -- they couldn't get past the gate.

I have credentials for the security system, Cole said. I worked on the original installation.

A long pause at the table.

Curtis looked at him. How long will it take?

A few hours once I'm inside. I'll need to run a full diagnostic before I can write the reversal. Maybe more if there are complications.

Are there going to be complications?

Cole thought about the backdoor. The credential that had been accessing the system for eight months. The unknown organized party forty miles south.

Possibly, he said.

Curtis looked at him for a long moment.

Okay, he said. Come in. We'll feed you and you can tell me what else we should know.

---

Nora spent two hours in Stevensville while Cole talked with Curtis Hall.

He watched her work from across the central block.

She moved from person to person. Not randomly -- she was doing an assessment too, but her assessment was built from individual cases rather than from the aggregate. She asked about specific people. She looked at specific symptoms. She made specific notes. And by the time she had moved through the central block she had created a dozen individual human connections, each one substantive enough that the person she had spoken to would remember it.

He filed this under: things I don't know how to do.

He watched her work from across the central block while Curtis talked.

She had a way of moving through a community that was different from how Cole moved through a community. He assessed. He catalogued. He built the picture from the outside in -- identifying the structural features, the resource distribution, the power dynamics, the potential threat vectors.

Nora moved from person to person. Not randomly -- she was doing an assessment too, but her assessment was built from individual cases rather than from the aggregate. She asked about specific people. She looked at specific symptoms. She made specific notes. And by the time she had moved through the central block, she had not just assessed the community's medical situation -- she had created a dozen individual human connections, each one substantive enough that the person she had spoken to would remember it.

She moved through the community the way she moved through the camp -- with the systematic attention of a medical professional doing a rapid assessment. Not intrusive, just present. She found the cases she needed to find and did what she could with what she had and made notes in her notebook about what she didn't have.

Cole watched her work from the edge of the central block while Curtis briefed him on the valley.

The water situation had been developing for three weeks. The northern communities had been attributing it to infrastructure failure -- a reasonable assumption. None of them had the technical background to distinguish between a broken system and a redirected one. The symptoms were the same from the outside.

The southern community had water throughout, Curtis said. Consistent supply. No shortage. He said it with the careful neutrality of someone who had been thinking about what that consistency meant.

How organized are they? Cole said.

Curtis considered.

They've been the most organized community in the valley since month three, he said. They took in a lot of people early. They've managed their resources well. He paused. They don't share. They trade. There's a difference and they're very clear about the difference.

What do they have that the northern communities don't?

Knowledge. They know things about the infrastructure -- the power systems, the water distribution -- that they shouldn't know from available public records. We've tried to negotiate for information sharing and the answer has always been the same: we'll trade for what we need, we don't give information away.

Cole looked at the Reader.

I need to go to the facility tonight, he said.

Curtis looked at him.

It's three in the morning, he said.

The timeline I'm working from just compressed significantly, Cole said. I need to fix the system before it causes damage I can't reverse.

Curtis looked at him for a moment, then nodded.

I'll have someone guide you to the facility road, he said.

---

Curtis Hall told him about the southern community over the food they shared in the central block.

Cole was eating standing up, the way he ate when he was managing multiple things at once, and Curtis had pulled a chair up and was talking in the low, measured way of someone who had been holding information for a long time and was deciding how much of it to give and to whom.

They've been organized since month three, Curtis said. That's the thing that stands out. Most of us were still figuring out survival in month three. They were already consolidating. They had resources they shouldn't have had and they had a plan for the resources.

What kind of resources?

Power. Water, consistently, when everyone else was managing with what they could get. Food stores that were more than what their initial population would have had. Curtis looked at his hands. We assumed they had been preppers. It's the simplest explanation for someone who had too much too early.

But? Cole said.

But preppers don't build for community. They build bunkers and they don't invite people in. These people were inviting people in from the beginning -- selectively, their own criteria, but actively recruiting. Growing the population deliberately. He paused. That's institution building. That's someone who had a plan for a specific kind of future.

Cole thought about the credential. The substation. The fourteen systems Iris had helped him understand.

Did you ever go there? he said.

Twice. The first time, month six, I went to try to establish a trade relationship. They were willing to trade. Specific terms, specific commodities, no information sharing. The second time, month ten, I went to try to negotiate water access when our supply started dropping. He looked at Cole. They told me the supply situation was a regional infrastructure issue they were also monitoring and that they had no ability to address it from their position.

They lied to you, Cole said.

Or they had a specific definition of address, Curtis said. He said it without particular anger -- the flat precision of someone who had been managing a difficult situation long enough to be past the emotional response to it.

Cole looked at the assembled valley community eating the food that had come from Nora's group's supplies and from Stevensville's stores and thought about what Curtis Hall had been managing for ten months.

I'm going to go talk to them, Cole said.

I know, Curtis said. When?

After I fix the system. Tomorrow or the day after.

What do you want from them?

An accounting, Cole said. And then -- he paused. Depends on what the accounting looks like.

Will you tell me? he said. What they say. What you decide to do with it.

Yes, Cole said.

Curtis nodded and ate his food.

He had the quality of someone who had learned to trust the next competent person who showed up and had been waiting a long time for one to arrive.

Cole thought about that quality and about what it cost to hold it for ten months.

He thought about what it would mean to not give Curtis Hall a straight accounting.

He would give him the straight accounting.

---

The community's medical situation was worse than he had expected and better than he had feared.

Nora told him this at the four-hour mark, when she had finished her circuit of the Stevensville central block.

Waterborne illness in three cases, she said. Early stage, manageable. Dehydration markers in eight more, which will resolve now that the water is clean. Two people with conditions that were being managed poorly because of the water situation that are now going to be managed better. She paused. One case that is not going to be resolved by clean water.

Who? Cole said.

She showed him the notebook.

He looked at the name.

Curtis Hall's wife, she said. She has been managing a condition for eight months that required consistent clean water access as part of the treatment protocol. The access wasn't consistent. She paused. I can stabilize her with what I have. I can't reverse what the eight months cost.

Cole sat with this.

Curtis Hall knew this, she said. He's known for a while. He just didn't have a framework for understanding why the water access had been inconsistent.

Cole thought about Curtis Hall's careful neutrality when he talked about the southern community. About the word suspicious used without visible anger.

He thought about how to carry this into the conversation with Vera Solis.

He would carry it directly. Curtis Hall's wife's name was in Nora's notebook. It was going into the accounting.

He stood at the central block's edge and looked at the Stevensville community -- forty-three people who had held together through two years and a water crisis and were now in the tentative quality of people who had gotten something back and were not yet certain it would hold.

He thought about what it would take to make it hold.

He thought about the southern installation with its consistent power and its managed infrastructure and its two hundred people who had been provided for while the northern communities had not.

He thought about what the valley needed the installation to be.

He went back to work.

---

He was at the facility gate for four minutes before he went through it.

Not hesitation. Assessment. He ran the Reader, checked the security system's current state, verified that his credentials were intact and had not been revoked. He looked at the camera positions and thought about who was watching the cameras and what they were seeing and what they were deciding to do about it.

The decision they had made, apparently, was to open the gate.

He thought about the choice that represented. Someone with administrative access to this facility had seen his truck on the approach road and had decided: let him in. Not: alert the facility's security protocol. Not: use the backdoor to lock the distribution parameters before he could reach them. Let him in.

He had been thinking about this choice for the entire drive from Stevensville.

The most parsimonious explanation was that whoever had been maintaining this facility wanted the northern communities to have water back and lacked the ability to fix it themselves without revealing their credential set. So they had waited. And when someone arrived with the right credentials and the evident intention of fixing the system, they had opened the gate.

Waiting for someone specific.

He thought about the printout and the note and the word thorough.

He drove through the gate.

---

Curtis Hall had been a county commissioner before the collapse. He had said this in passing, not as a credential but as a piece of context: I was used to managing public resources with insufficient funding and political interference, so this isn't entirely different.

Cole had laughed at this. He had not laughed much in two years and the laugh had surprised him slightly.

Hall had looked at him with the expression of someone who had said something that was supposed to be funny and had been uncertain whether it would land.

It landed, Cole said.

Hall nodded.

Good, he said. We need more of that around here.
