# Chapter 16

## The Valley Towns

He left Nora in Stevensville.

The leaving was harder than he had expected, which was information about something he was not yet ready to examine. The logic was clear -- she was needed there, he was needed south of there, the allocation of resources to where they were most useful was the most basic operational principle he had. The difficulty was not the logic. The difficulty was something underneath the logic that he noted and set aside.

He took Marcus and Eddie and drove south toward the facility.

They passed through two more northern communities on the route.

The first was Victor. Thirty people in buildings that had held four hundred -- the compression of a community that had lost most of itself to two years of wave infections and displacement and the accumulated cost of living without adequate water. A man at the road's edge, watching their trucks come in, carrying the wariness of someone who had been watching unfamiliar vehicles for two years and had learned that wariness was the right default.

Cole stopped. Got out. Explained.

He had given this explanation four times now and had gotten better at it each time -- watching what landed, adjusting the framing, learning which entry points into the technical picture were accessible from where people were standing. The man listened with the focused attention of someone who had been trying to understand an incomprehensible situation and had not yet found the right frame for it.

When Cole finished, the man said: So it wasn't a failure.

No.

Someone changed it.

Yes.

He was quiet. Cole let the quiet be.

We lost eight people in the last three weeks, the man said. Not all from the water. But the water didn't help.

Cole heard this. He put it where it belonged, in the accounting he had been keeping since he understood what the optimization layer had been doing. Eight people in this community. More in the other communities up the valley. More he didn't have numbers for yet.

I'm going to fix it tonight, he said. You'll have water by morning.

The man stepped back from the road and let them pass.

---

Eddie came forward at the second community.

He had been in the second truck since Stevensville, and Cole had not had a full conversation with him during the movement. He had been building toward it. He had known since the canyon crossing that Eddie had relevant knowledge, the engineering background Cole had been watching surface in pieces. He had not yet given Eddie the full context to offer it.

The second community was a crossroads settlement -- forty people in a farm supply building, the shelving cleared for living space, the smell of grain and diesel still present after two years of different use. Cole stopped to give the briefing.

Eddie got out with him.

He stood slightly back while Cole spoke, looking at the settlement's water distribution infrastructure with the attention Cole had seen twice before. Professional attention. An engineer reading a system.

After the briefing, back at the truck, Cole said: Tell me what you see.

Eddie looked at him.

The distribution system serving this community, he said, is a type I used in small municipalities in the 2010s. A modular platform for communities under five thousand population. I worked with this platform for four years. I know its architecture the way you know the Bitterroot system's architecture.

Cole waited.

If you're going to modify the distribution management software at the facility, Eddie said, there are three physical parameters you need to align. He described them -- specific valve configurations, a pressure baseline, a flow measurement point that would register the change and either confirm or reject it. Each description was precise and correct in a way Cole could verify against his own technical knowledge.

This is better than anything the Reader gave me remotely, Cole said.

Why didn't you say it before?

Eddie looked at the community they had just briefed. At the forty people who had lost eight.

Because some of what the southern community is running -- I built part of it, he said. Not knowing fully what it would be for. I thought I was doing resilience consulting work. Well-funded, technically interesting, the kind of project you don't ask too many questions about because the work itself is good and the money is there and you have a daughter with a medical condition that costs what it costs.

He said the last part without emphasis. Just the fact of it.

He looked at the community's water connections.

I've been thinking about it since I understood what the optimization layer was doing, he said. And I've been thinking about what it means that I built infrastructure that could be used to do that. And I've been trying to figure out whether knowing now makes any difference to what it already cost.

Cole looked at him.

Tell me everything you remember about the installation, he said. Everything specific. Dimensions, specs, design decisions. The woman who commissioned it -- what she told you, what she didn't tell you, what you inferred.

Eddie told him.

It took forty-five minutes and Cole listened to all of it, asking questions at the points where specificity mattered. When Eddie finished, Cole had a picture of the southern installation that was more complete and more human than anything the Reader had given him.

I'm going to go talk to her after I fix the system, Cole said.

I figured, Eddie said.

I want you there.

I know, Eddie said. That's why I told you.

They drove on.

---

They reached the facility at two in the morning, as planned.

The facility road was unmarked except for an older utility access sign that Cole remembered from the installation project three years ago. He had driven this road once before, in a rental truck, with a team of four and a weekend's worth of integration work ahead of them. He had driven it thinking about the project schedule and the client's particular specifications and the specific technical challenge of retrofitting a modern distribution management platform onto aging physical infrastructure.

He had not been thinking about what the facility would mean to anyone when he was gone.

He parked and got out and stood in the dark outside the fence.

The Reader query came back in ten seconds. His credentials. Still valid.

Marcus was off the motorcycle.

The gate's electronic, Marcus said. The lock is powered.

I know. I set up the security parameters. Cole ran the credential query. The gate release came back as available.

He had a thought.

He did not release the gate. He queried the security system's access log instead.

Last access: six days ago. Credential: the unknown set. Time: four forty-three AM.

Someone had been here six days ago. Four forty-three in the morning -- before dawn, in the hours when surveillance was least likely.

They had been here recently.

He stood at the gate with this information for a moment.

Eddie came up beside him.

What is it? Eddie said.

Someone was here six days ago, Cole said. The same credential that modified the Bitterroot system. They were at this facility six days ago at four in the morning.

Maintaining it, Eddie said. Regular maintenance visits. That's what you'd do if you were managing the system and you needed to make sure the hardware was running correctly.

Yes, Cole said.

They're going to know you're here, Marcus said.

They already know. The camera query came back when we arrived. They're watching right now.

Marcus was very still for a moment.

Then he said: You're going in anyway.

Because the gate is your credential and you're going to use it.

Because the northern communities need water and I can fix this in a few hours and the person watching us on camera chose not to interfere at the substation and chose to maintain this facility for eighteen months, Cole said. Those choices tell me something about what they want.

Eddie looked at the gate.

What do they want? he said.

Someone who knows what they're looking at, Cole said.

He released the gate.

---

He left Eddie at the crossroads settlement.

Not abandoned -- left with a specific task. The settlement's water infrastructure was the same type Eddie had spent four years working with, and Eddie had identified two modifications that would significantly improve the distribution efficiency once the Bitterroot system was restored. Cole gave him the Reader access parameters he needed and left him there with the specific authority to do the work and the instruction to radio when it was done.

I'll be there when you go south, he said.

I know, Cole said. This first.

He drove to the facility with Marcus.

The road to the facility was a county road that turned onto a utility access track two miles before the building. He had been on this track once, three years ago, in a rental truck with a team and a weekend ahead of them. He remembered the quality of the approach: the building invisible from the main road, the track angling away from it into terrain that hid the destination until you were close.

The track was overgrown but passable. Two years of no maintenance had let the shoulders push in, and he drove carefully, watching for the drainage cuts that the maps showed and the actual track had confirmed.

Marcus was fifty yards behind.

At the facility perimeter, Cole stopped the truck and got out and stood at the fence.

He queried the security system.

His credentials. Valid.

He stood at the fence for a moment before releasing the gate.

He thought about the decision he had been making for four months, from the wrong side of a fence line on a ranch in central Montana. He thought about what it had taken to get here -- the Bitterroot query at six weeks, the fence line conversations with Nora, the insulin, the twenty-three people, the canyon crossing, the dark valley.

He thought about Iris at the substation, reading equipment, narrating what she saw.

He thought about what she had said: someone with a plan.

He went inside and got to work.

---

The third northern community was the smallest.

Fourteen people in a farmhouse two miles off the main road, down a track that Marcus had found because the logistics network's ghost delivery schedule showed a cache at this location and the cache was real -- a commercial feed delivery automated system, still cycling, the delivery bay stocked with two years of uncollected agricultural goods that the fourteen people had been drawing from without understanding why it kept appearing.

Marcus had brought Cole and Eddie here specifically because of this.

Look at the delivery manifest, he said.

Cole looked. The delivery schedule showed quarterly automated restocking from a regional distribution hub that was, as far as he could tell, still running. The hub was one of the systems that showed the credential's signature.

They're eating out of a ghost delivery system, Cole said.

For two years, Marcus said. They don't know it. They think it's something the previous owners left behind.

Cole thought about this.

The credential is managing the hub, he said slowly. Keeping its scheduling systems running. Directing it to continue restocking this location.

Why? Marcus said.

Because this community is in the delivery radius of the southern installation. Cole looked at the manifest, at the delivery schedule, at the fourteen people who were alive in part because someone had kept a logistics hub running and had kept this location on the delivery schedule. Whoever is running the credential isn't just taking. They're also giving. Selectively.

He thought about the optimization layer. The water redirection. The people in Stevensville who had gone without.

And the selective giving, he said, is not random. They're maintaining the communities that are close enough to be within their service area. The ones farther away, they let fail.

Marcus looked at him.

They're curating the valley, he said.

Yes, Cole said. That's exactly what they're doing.

He looked at the fourteen people in the farmhouse. At the delivery bay with its automated restocking. At the Reader showing the credential's presence in the hub's scheduling system.

Get them the water situation briefing, he said to Eddie. And tell them about the delivery system. They should know what's keeping them alive.

Will that be okay? For them to know?

Cole thought about it.

They've been surviving on information they don't have, he said. They deserve the information.

---

The facility road in daylight was different from the facility road in the dark.

At two in the morning he had driven it with the narrow-field attention that headlamps imposed -- forty feet ahead, the rest darkness. Now, in the morning's full light, he could see the terrain around the road: the hillside the facility was built into, the larger geographic context that explained why the building had been sited here.

The site made sense. He had not been thinking about site selection when he was building the integration three years ago -- he had been thinking about the technical work -- but looking at it now with different eyes he could read the decision. Southern exposure for solar. Hillside setting for thermal mass and concealment. Access road at an angle that kept the facility invisible from the main valley road while remaining accessible from it.

Someone had chosen this site deliberately. Not just for the practical advantages -- for the character of a building that could function without being found.

He had not built this. He had integrated software into a physical installation that someone else had chosen and built and sited.

He thought about all the decisions that had been made before he arrived. The site selection, the physical construction, the infrastructure design. He had been one node in a much larger project and had understood only his node.

He thought about Eddie. About building the water treatment and distribution systems without knowing what the full project was.

He thought about all the nodes. About the contractors and engineers who had each built their piece without the full picture. About the full picture that only Vera Solis had held.

He parked the truck and looked at the facility.

He thought about the kind of intelligence that kept the full picture to itself. The specific combination of capability and caution that built something this large by distributing the knowledge of its construction among people who each knew only their part.

He thought about whether that was the right way to build something.

He thought about what the full picture required from the person holding it.

He queried his credentials. The gate opened.

He went in to do what he had come to do.

---

The crossroads settlement had been a farm supply town called Corvallis before the collapse.

He knew this from the Reader's location database, which still held the pre-collapse mapping data. Corvallis, population 1,100. Agricultural services, light retail, a school. One of the hundred small towns that existed to serve the surrounding land.

Forty people now. In the buildings that had held eleven hundred.

He stood in the crossroads and thought about the ratio. 1,100 to 40. That was not an anomaly -- that was approximately the ratio he had seen everywhere he'd traveled in the last week. The collapse had not been uniform. Some people had left, some had died, some had not been here in the first place.

But mostly: the world had contracted.

The northern Bitterroot Valley had contracted from ten-plus thousand to several hundred across all its communities. The southern valley had apparently contracted less sharply -- if the installation held two hundred people, the ratio there was different.

He thought about why the ratio would be different.

He thought about someone who had planned for a population to survive in a specific location.
