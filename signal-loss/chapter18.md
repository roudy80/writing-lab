# Chapter 18

## The Backdoor

They drove north in the dark.

Cole told Marcus and Eddie what he had found inside the facility. Not the edited version -- the complete picture, including the secondary objective he had almost triggered.

The Reader didn't show you that remotely, Marcus said.

The Reader showed me what it could access remotely, which was the parameter layer and the optimization logic and the access log. The base code required physical interface. He paused. I was operating on an incomplete picture for three days and I knew the picture was incomplete and I still almost acted without checking for what I didn't know.

What would have happened? Eddie said.

The secondary objective would have routed the remaining supply to the most remote backup nodes in the system. The northern communities would have lost access entirely within hours.

He drove through that for a moment.

Because you would have triggered a failsafe, Marcus said.

A failsafe built into the original installation specification -- not by the person who installed the optimization layer, by the original design. A risk mitigation feature. If an unauthorized credential set modified the routing, the system would automatically reroute to backup nodes.

But you had authorized credentials, Eddie said.

Yes. My credentials wouldn't have triggered it. What would have triggered it was a generic override attempt -- which is how I would have approached the system if I hadn't had my specific credentials. He paused. And I would have made a generic override attempt if I'd moved faster and not taken ninety minutes to read the full architecture first.

The Reader almost got you, Marcus said.

The Reader gave me an incomplete picture that I acted on as if it were complete. That's my error, not the Reader's.

It's a distinction, Marcus said.

It's the only distinction that matters, Cole said.

They drove. The valley was dark on both sides and the road was empty and the stars were present in the way they were present in empty country -- not decorative, functional, the primary light source.

Marcus said: The person who installed the optimization layer knew about the secondary objective.

Yes.

So they built around it.

The optimization layer was structured specifically to avoid triggering the failsafe. The routing modification was implemented through the parameter set rather than the base code. It required knowing that the failsafe existed and knowing exactly how to stay below its trigger threshold.

A peer, Marcus said.

Cole looked at him.

That's exactly the word, he said.

---

He told Iris about the secondary objective the next morning.

She was in the grain barn eating breakfast with the group, and he sat down beside her and gave her the technical picture: the base code, the failsafe, what he had found and what it meant about the person who had installed the optimization layer.

She listened without interrupting. She had her bowl in both hands and she was looking at a middle distance while she listened, which was consistent with how she listened.

When he finished she said: Show me the failsafe trigger logic.

He showed her on the Reader.

She studied it for a long time. Longer than he expected.

This trigger logic, she said slowly. The threshold conditions. the conditions that would trigger the backup rerouting -- they're based on a parameter called authorization_level, and the value that triggers the failsafe is authorization_level less than two.

Correct, Cole said.

And the optimization layer was installed by a credential set with authorization_level three.

She looked at him.

Authorization levels are assigned during system installation, she said. They're set in the initial configuration and they require physical access to change. The person who installed the optimization layer had authorization level three because they were present during the original installation or because they knew someone who was.

Cole looked at the Reader.

The original installation team, he said slowly.

Yes. If there's someone on the original installation team who retained their authorization credentials and used them to install the optimization layer eight months ago -- She stopped.

Cole thought about the installation project three years ago. The local engineers. The company's installation team. The client's representatives. The range of people who had been present during installation and who would have received elevated authorization credentials.

He thought about the credential's format. Familiar but not his company's standard. Built on the same framework.

A peer who was present at the original installation, he said.

Or someone the original installation team trusted enough to give credentials to, Iris said.

He looked at the Reader for a long time.

She was waiting for someone to come, he said. She left the gate open. She left the note. She's been waiting for someone with the right credentials to come and fix what she built.

Iris looked at him.

Why would she want it fixed? she said. It was running exactly as she designed it to run.

Cole thought about the credential that had watched him work and chosen not to interfere. About the note on the printout. About what Nora had said: I think we're going to find that the people who made a wrong decision are not entirely wrong people.

Because she knows it was wrong, he said. And she's been waiting for someone who could fix it in a way that wouldn't make it worse.

Iris looked at him for a moment.

Go find out, she said.

He looked at her.

She was tired. He could see it in a way he hadn't been seeing it because he hadn't been looking. the tiredness of someone managing something that required more than day-to-day energy.

He wanted to say something about that.

He didn't know what to say about it that wouldn't make it more difficult for both of them.

He nodded and put the Reader away.

---

Iris was awake when they got back.

She was sitting near the barn entry in the pre-dawn dark, wrapped in her sleeping bag, with a cup of something hot in both hands. She looked at them when they came in and did not ask about the facility because she had been waiting for them and had already decided they had done what they went to do.

The northern communities have water, Cole said. It'll be at normal pressure by morning.

She nodded.

And the person who modified it? she said.

Watched us work and didn't interfere.

She looked at her cup.

That's interesting, she said.

She looked up at him.

Tell me about the secondary objective, she said. The one you almost triggered.

He told her. He walked her through the base code, the failsafe trigger logic, the way the optimization layer had been built around it.

She listened with the focused attention she gave technical explanations.

The authorization level parameter, she said when he finished. The trigger threshold of less than two. That's a very specific threshold. It's not a standard value -- it's been set deliberately for a specific purpose.

To filter out generic override attempts, Cole said.

To filter out anyone who wasn't on the original installation team, Iris said. Authorization level two or above was only assigned to people with deep system access. That's a small group. She paused. Whoever installed the optimization layer was designing a system that could only be undone by someone who had been part of building it in the first place. Or someone that person trusted.

She was building a door that only certain people could unlock, he said.

She was building a test, Iris said. And you passed it.

He thought about the note. More thorough than we expected.

She expected someone less thorough, he said.

She expected someone who would come with credentials and move fast and trigger the failsafe and make the situation irreversible, Iris said. She was prepared for that outcome. She had not planned for the outcome where the person who came took ninety minutes to read the full architecture first.

Cole thought about this for a long time.

She wanted someone to fix it, he said. But she needed to know the person was capable enough to fix it correctly.

Yes, Iris said.

And the optimization layer -- the water redirection -- was it always meant to be temporary?

Iris looked at her cup.

I don't know, she said. I think you're going to have to ask her that directly.

He looked at the dawn starting through the barn's ventilation gaps.

Rest, she said. You have a conversation to have today and you should have it when you've slept.

You should rest too, he said.

She looked at him with the expression of someone who had already had a specific conversation with herself about this and had made a decision.

I will, she said.

He went to find his section of floor.

---

He slept four hours and was awake again before the rest of the group.

He sat at the barn entry in the dark and ran the morning queries. The Bitterroot system: clean, running correctly, the water distribution normalized. The valley communities: he could see the pressure readings at the connection points, all of them rising toward design specification. The valley was getting its water back.

He thought about the conversation ahead.

He had more of the picture now than he'd had twenty-four hours ago. He had the technical picture, the physical picture, the credential set, the modification timeline. He had Iris's framework from the substation: someone with a plan. He had Eddie's account of the construction: a woman who knew exactly what she was building and kept the full picture to herself.

He had the note: more thorough than we expected.

He thought about what he was missing.

The why.

He had the what and the how. He had a hypothesis about the who. He did not have the why -- the specific reasoning that had led to the optimization layer, to the eighteen months of water redirection, to the decision to deprive the northern communities of a resource they needed in order to establish viability for a southern one.

He had a hypothesis about the why too. But hypotheses needed testing.

The testing required a conversation.

He thought about how to have the conversation correctly.

The wrong way: arrive with the accounting and demand explanation. That would produce defensiveness and selective information.

The right way: arrive with what he knew and ask what he didn't know. Make it clear that he had the technical picture and that he could verify what she told him, which meant she had no incentive to be less than accurate. Make it clear that he was not there to condemn but to understand.

And then, once he understood, to decide.

He could verify her technical claims. He could not verify her moral ones. He was going to have to make a judgment about the moral ones based on what he observed -- her behavior, her record, the quality of how she responded to the accounting he was going to lay in front of her.

He had been making judgments about people for two years. He was better at it than he had been at the start. He was not certain he was good enough at it for this.

He looked at the Reader.

He thought about Nora saying: hold both things simultaneously.

He thought about what both things were.

He thought about the eight people in Victor.

He went to wake Marcus.

---

He stood in the facility for a long moment after the analysis was complete, looking at the system he had just read completely.

He thought about the credential. About the eighteen months of maintenance visits, the access logs, the professional care of someone who knew this system and attended to it.

He pulled up the facility's maintenance log and read it in sequence.

The visits had started in month three. The first three months had been chaos, then the visits had become regular. Every three to four weeks, on different days, no detectable pattern in the timing that would suggest a schedule someone else could predict.

HVAC filter checks. Water quality sensor calibration. Software parameter reviews. The log of someone who understood both the physical and digital layers of the system and had been maintaining both with equal attention.

The software parameter reviews were the most interesting. In month four, the visitor had identified a potential failure mode in the distribution system's load balancing algorithm -- the same kind of edge case vulnerability that Cole had spent his career writing diagnostic tools to catch. The visitor had written a patch.

Cole read the patch.

It was good. Not flawless -- there was an efficiency issue in the secondary handling routine that a more senior engineer would have caught -- but good. The logic was correct. The vulnerability was genuinely addressed.

He thought about what it meant to maintain someone else's system carefully enough to identify and fix its edge cases.

He thought about the optimization layer sitting on top of all this careful maintenance, the water flowing south for eight months.

Both of these things were the same person's work.

He saved the log to the Reader and went to find Iris.

---

He woke Nora before he left.

Not because he needed to, logistically -- Marcus was ready, Eddie was ready, the plan was set. He woke her because he wanted to.

She was already half-awake when he touched her shoulder. She had that quality -- the light, functional sleep of someone who had been managing emergencies for long enough that full unconsciousness was a luxury she didn't always allow herself.

I'm going south, he said.

I know. She sat up. Tell me something before you go.

He waited.

Tell me what you expect to find, she said. Not what you know. What you expect.

He thought about this.

I expect to find someone who built the right thing the wrong way, he said. Someone who had accurate models and wrong implementations. Someone who knew what was coming and prepared for it and in the preparation made choices that cost other people things they couldn't afford to lose.

And?

And is still making choices. And needs someone to tell her when the choices are wrong, in terms she can verify. He paused. I'm the person who can do that.

Because you built the same systems she built, Nora said.

Because I understand what she's built well enough to know when it's doing what she says it's doing and when it isn't.

Nora looked at him.

That's a good reason, she said. That's not all of it.

No, he said. It's not all of it.

Tell me the rest, she said.

He thought about the fence line. About four months of watching from the wrong side of it. About the propane gauge and the cistern pump and the fence line on the east pasture and the careful, maintained distance.

I've been choosing not to be involved for two years, he said. I've been doing it carefully, thoughtfully, with good justifications. And Briggs left Nora his supply inventory. And Rosa Garza needed insulin. And there are eight names in James Kirby's memory that are now in my accounting.

He paused.

I'm done choosing not to be involved, he said.

She looked at him for a long moment.

Go, she said.

He went.

---

The facility in the morning light was different from the facility at two in the morning.

At night it had been operational and empty -- the hum of systems, the glow of indicator lights, the character of a building that was alive in the way systems were alive: continuously, without rest, without awareness. In the morning light it was just a building. Concrete and metal and glass, the mountain behind it, the October sky above it.

He had spent the night thinking about the note and the open gate and what came next. He was more certain of what came next than he had been at two in the morning.

He went in.

---

He drove north from the facility in the early morning and watched the valley wake up.

Not metaphorically -- literally. The lights coming on in the communities as the water returned and people understood that something had changed. A woman in Stevensville who had been carrying water from a cistern every morning for three weeks opened a tap and stood at it and did not move for a long time.

He did not see this. He imagined it. He had enough data about Stevensville and about what water access meant and about what three weeks without it had produced to construct the image accurately.

He thought about what it meant to fix something from a distance. To correct a variable in a system and have the correction ripple through to real people in real kitchens turning real taps.

He had built systems for four years and had understood this in the abstract. The abstract was becoming specific this morning.

He thought about the note on the printout. About the person who had been maintaining this facility for eighteen months and who had opened the gate and left the note.

He thought about what it would feel like to have been doing this alone.

He drove north. The valley was waking up around him.
