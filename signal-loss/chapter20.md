# Chapter 20

## The Maintenance Problem

He was doing his second pass of the Bitterroot system verification when Nora came back to the grain barn.

Not the medical rounds -- she had done those. This was something else. She came in and sat down across from him and looked at the Reader's display with the expression she had when she was about to say something she had been thinking about for a while.

He kept working. He had learned that she did not need him to stop what he was doing in order to be heard.

She said: I want to ask you something about the maintenance philosophy.

He looked up.

The maintenance philosophy, he said.

The way you talk about systems. Fix what's broken, restore the original parameters, keep things running the way they were designed to run. She looked at him. Is that the right framework for what we're dealing with?

He set the Reader down.

Tell me what you're actually asking, he said.

She looked at the Reader display -- the Bitterroot system, clean, running on its original parameters.

You fixed the system to its original design, she said. The system was designed pre-collapse for a valley of eight thousand people. The valley now has maybe eight hundred viable residents across the northern communities. The system's original distribution calculations were based on eight thousand.

He had thought about this. He had not finished thinking about it.

The original parameters distribute based on registered service connections, he said. Fewer active connections means the available supply is distributed across fewer points. The per-connection supply goes up.

So the system adapts automatically.

Within its existing parameters, yes.

But the parameters themselves were set for a different world, she said. A world where there were agricultural operations and commercial users and a population density the valley doesn't have anymore. She paused. What if the original parameters aren't the right ones for what the valley actually is now?

He looked at the Reader.

The alternative is someone writing new parameters, he said. Parameters that account for what the valley is now rather than what it was. That's -- He stopped.

That's what Vera Solis did, Nora said.

The silence had a specific quality.

Yes, Cole said.

She didn't redirect the system to serve only her population because she was malicious, Nora said. She redirected it because the original parameters weren't designed for her situation. She wrote new objectives. She made decisions about what the system should optimize for. She made those decisions badly, in ways that cost people their lives. But the underlying move -- she was doing the same thing you're going to have to do eventually.

Cole looked at her.

You think I should have written new parameters instead of restoring the originals.

I think restoring the originals was the right move for right now, she said. It was the fastest path to getting water to people who needed it. But right now is not the same as going forward. She looked at him steadily. Going forward, someone is going to have to look at the valley as it actually is and write parameters for that valley. Someone with the technical capacity to do it correctly and the judgment to do it without making Vera Solis's mistake.

The mistake being -- he said.

Deciding that your population is the one worth optimizing for, Nora said. Making the definition first and then designing the system to serve the definition. She paused. The mistake isn't the technical work. The mistake is who gets to say whose needs count.

Cole was quiet for a moment.

You've been thinking about this since the substation, he said.

Since the medical list, she said. Since I handed you twelve names and you handed it back. Since I watched you spend two weeks doing the math about who was worth going south for.

I went south for a water system, he said.

You went south for the eight hundred ghost addresses, she said. For the people in those addresses who weren't there. She met his eyes. The math you've been doing isn't about water distribution. It's about which people count. You've been trying to find a framework where everybody counts and the system still works.

He sat with this for a long time.

That's -- he said. And stopped.

That's the actual problem, she said. Not the Bitterroot system. Not Vera Solis's optimization layer. The problem you're actually trying to solve.

He looked at the Reader's display. The clean parameters. The restored distribution.

I don't know if I can solve it, he said.

I know, she said. That's why I'm telling you. So that when you go talk to Vera Solis tomorrow you're not just going to assess her infrastructure. You're going to that conversation with the actual question.

Which is.

Whether what she's building is designed to answer that question or designed to avoid it.

He looked at her.

When did you figure this out? he said.

She looked at the fire.

I've been doing triage for twelve years, she said. Triage is the professional practice of deciding whose needs count given available resources. I've been thinking about this problem since before the collapse.

He was quiet for a while.

You should have been in the room when we were designing the systems, he said.

She looked at him.

You wouldn't have listened then, she said.

He thought about that.

Probably not, he said.

---

He didn't sleep much that night.

He lay in the dark with the Reader in his vest pocket and thought about what she had said and about the discomfort of a correct analysis that he didn't have an answer to.

He had been, for four months at the ranch, a person who maintained things. He had told himself this was practical -- the ranch needed maintaining, the systems needed maintaining, the fence line needed maintaining. He had been correct about this. What he had not been correct about was the implication that maintenance was a sufficient orientation toward a world that was not maintaining itself.

Maintenance was backward-looking. It worked from the original design. It restored what had been.

The original designs had been for a world that no longer existed.

He thought about Rosa's notebook. About what her father had said: write everything down so the knowledge doesn't fracture. That was documentation. Documentation was maintenance of a different kind -- not of systems but of understanding. You maintained the understanding so that the next thing could be built on it.

He thought about Vera Solis, who had looked forward rather than backward. Who had written new parameters for a new world. Who had been right about the technical work and wrong about the human part.

He thought about what the human part required that the technical part didn't.

He was still thinking about it when the radio crackled and Marcus said: Eight vehicles on the north valley road.

He put the Reader in his pocket and got up.

He would come back to the question.

---

He worked through the afternoon in the grain barn, running the full infrastructure query set for the valley region.

He had been doing this in increments since they arrived -- the Bitterroot system status, the substation feed, the distribution management platform at the facility. Now he was doing it systematically, pulling everything he could access from his current position and building the complete picture.

Fourteen systems in southwestern Montana showing the unknown credential's signature.

He mapped them. A distribution management platform for the valley's water supply -- that was the Bitterroot system, now restored. The substation power management -- modified, running on private generation. Three irrigation scheduling systems serving agricultural operations to the south of the main installation. A regional weather monitoring platform that was now reporting to a private receiver rather than the public network. Two logistics scheduling systems that had been redirected toward a private routing protocol.

And more.

Communications relay modified to extend coverage over a specific geographic area. Emergency management platform -- the same type that had been broadcasting stale data at Stevensville, but this one had been updated. Its status data was current. It was providing accurate shelter and resource information for the southern installation's service area.

An operational emergency management platform. Someone was updating it regularly.

He sat with the complete picture.

Fourteen systems, managed for eighteen months, all oriented toward a specific service area in the valley's south. The technical scope of this was significant -- it was not one person maintaining a generator and calling it infrastructure. This was a systematic, professional-grade infrastructure management operation conducted in parallel with whatever else the installation was doing.

He thought about Iris's question at the substation: What's the size of the generation capacity relative to the distribution load?

He queried the distribution management platform for its load calculations.

The numbers came back: the generation source was sized for four hundred residential connections at full load, with thirty percent additional capacity for expansion.

Four hundred households. At two people per household -- conservative -- eight hundred people.

The installation that Eddie had described, designed for three to five hundred residents at full capacity.

The generation capacity sized for eight hundred.

The installation was planning to grow.

He sat at the equipment crate with the Reader and looked at the numbers and thought about someone who had modeled the collapse three years before it happened and had spent the intervening time building a destination for people who survived it.

He thought about what it cost to make that destination viable. About the water that had been routed south for eight months. About the eight people in Victor who had died.

He thought about what Nora would say.

He thought about what he needed to say when he went south tomorrow.

---

Purcell found him in the afternoon.

Not looking for him specifically -- moving through the valley community in the systematic way he moved through everything, learning the landscape. He stopped at the grain barn where Cole was working through the infrastructure data and looked at the Reader display and said: That's the infrastructure map.

Partial, Cole said.

You've been building it since before we left the ranch.

Yes.

Cole looked at him.

What do you want? he said.

Purcell sat down with the ease of someone who sat down uninvited because they had decided the calculus justified it.

I want to understand what you understand, he said. About the valley's infrastructure situation. What's running, what's not, what's going to matter in the next year.

Cole looked at the Reader.

Why do you want to understand it?

Because the valley is going to be organized by whoever understands it best, Purcell said. That's a fact about how communities work. I'd rather understand it and be involved in the organization than not understand it and be managed by whoever does.

That's honest, Cole said.

I try to be, when honesty is the best available tool.

The valley's infrastructure is being managed by someone I have not yet fully characterized, he said. The systems that are running correctly are running because of their management. The systems that weren't running correctly -- the Bitterroot water supply -- I've restored. But the person managing the rest of it is still managing it and I don't know yet whether their management is something the valley can work with or not.

But you're going to find out.

And the decision you make about whether to work with them or against them is going to determine the valley's trajectory, Purcell said.

Cole looked at him steadily.

Yes, he said.

I want to be in that conversation, Purcell said.

You'll know what I decide, Cole said. Whether you're in the conversation is a different question.

Purcell looked at him with the look he had when he found someone genuinely interesting.

Fair enough, he said.

He stood and looked at the valley through the barn's open door.

For what it's worth, he said, the northern communities trust you. I've been talking to people for three days. The name they keep saying is yours. He paused. I don't say this to threaten you with it. I say it because trust is a resource and resources should be understood accurately.

I know, he said.

Purcell walked out into the valley.

Cole watched him go and thought about the quality of a person who gave you accurate information about your own position.

It was a form of respect, or a form of leverage, or both.

With Purcell it was probably both.

---

He took the second watch rotation himself, at two in the morning.

The valley was quiet in the way that large spaces were quiet at night -- not silent, but the sounds were the sounds of the valley itself rather than human sounds. Wind in the grass. A creek somewhere to the east. The occasional vehicle sound from the distant road, which meant other people were still moving at night, which was information.

He ran the Reader through its queries. Bitterroot system: nominal. The northern communities' pressure readings: stable. The credential: quiet, no activity since the camera query at the facility.

He thought about what quiet meant in this context. Not absence -- the credential was there, the systems were running, the installation to the south was active. The quiet was the quiet of someone who was waiting. Who had made contact and was waiting for the next contact.

He thought about the note again.

More thorough than we expected.

He had been thorough about the system. He had been less thorough about himself -- about the process of understanding what he was walking toward before he walked toward it. He had the technical picture. He had the infrastructure map. He had Eddie's construction details and Iris's physical analysis and Marcus's ghost trail knowledge.

What he didn't have was any sense of what the person at the end of all this wanted. Not from the fix -- they had apparently wanted the fix, or at least accepted it. From Cole specifically. The note had not been left for a generic technically competent person. The note had been left for someone who had taken ninety minutes to read a system before modifying it.

They had been watching for someone specific.

He thought about that until the watch ended. Then he handed off and went to sleep, and his sleep was lighter than usual, and in the morning he felt the alertness of someone who has been thinking about a problem at the edge of consciousness all night and has woken up with more of it resolved than they had when they fell asleep.

---

The night was cold and still and very large.

He had grown up in suburban Illinois, where nights were never fully dark and seldom fully quiet. The Montana nights had taken two years to feel natural -- the adjustment of a nervous system that had been calibrated for urban ambient noise recalibrating for the sound of wind in grass and the occasional animal and nothing else.

He was calibrated now. The quiet felt like the correct state of things rather than a gap where noise should be.

He thought about the group sleeping in the barn behind him. About what each of them had given up to be here. Not the abstract giving-up of departure but the things: Eddie's workshop with its tools, Rosa's school and her hexagon notebooks, the Dillon residents and their town. Nora's clinic in whatever context she had run it before the camp.

None of them had asked for this.

He thought about what he owed people who had come with him because he had told them the valley was viable and the water would be fixed.

He owed them the valley being viable and the water being fixed.

He ran the Bitterroot query one more time.

Nominal.

---

He thought about Vera Solis without knowing her name yet.

He had been building a picture of her for a week from evidence. The evidence said: technically sophisticated, organized, capable of sustained long-term planning, willing to make hard decisions, possibly wrong about some of the hard decisions. The evidence said: someone who had been alone with the weight of the plan for a long time.

He thought about what it was like to carry a plan alone. He had some data on that. The ranch had been a plan he had been carrying alone for two years -- smaller in scope, less morally complicated, but structurally similar in the sense of: a thing you had decided and were executing without being able to share the full picture with anyone.

He thought about what it would be like to have carried the Bitterroot optimization alone for eight months. To have made the decision and lived inside the consequences and kept maintaining everything else with professional care.

He thought: that is a specific kind of discipline.

He thought: I want to talk to whoever has that discipline.
