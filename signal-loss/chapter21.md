# Chapter 21

## Nora's Call

He told Nora everything that evening.

They were in a corner of the grain barn that had become, over three days, the operational center of the camp -- a space marked out not by walls but by habit, where the medical supplies were stored, where the radio lived when Cole wasn't carrying it, where the decisions got made. The group understood this without being told. Physical space organized itself around function in camps the way it organized itself around status in more structured environments.

Twenty-three people were doing the things twenty-three people did in the evening in a barn in October: eating, talking in low voices, sleeping when they could, watching the road through the gaps in the siding. A community that had been in motion for four days and had not stopped being a community.

He had noticed this and had not said so to anyone.

Not the edited version he had been giving the group in increments -- the full picture. The credential set, the eighteen months of maintenance visits, the note on the printout, the facility's connection to the same installation that Eddie had helped build. The substation, the private grid, the deliberate redirection of the valley's water supply for eight months.

And the secondary objective I almost triggered, he said.

She looked at him.

What would that have done?

Made the water situation irreversible. The northern communities would have lost all access, not just the reduced access they had.

And you found it because you took ninety minutes to read the system before you modified it.

Yes.

What would you have done if you hadn't taken that time?

He thought about it honestly.

I would have moved faster, he said. I had a timeline in my head and a deadline and I would have prioritized the fix over the full diagnostic.

She looked at him for a long moment.

He had learned, over four days of watching her work, that the long look was the tell. Nora managed her reactions with the precision of someone who had spent twelve years in emergency medicine learning not to transmit alarm to patients who needed calm. The tells were small and she was not always aware she was producing them.

The long look meant: I have an opinion about this that I'm deciding whether to share.

Nora's call, she had said to him at the camp. He had not understood what she meant at the time. He thought he understood it better now.

She was quiet.

The fire between them moved in a draft from somewhere. Outside the grain barn, the valley was settling into night, the communities with their returned water and their temporary relief and their knowledge that something had been wrong and had been fixed and they still didn't fully understand either.

How bad is it? she said. The northern communities. What they lost during those eight months.

Cole had been doing this math since Victor. Since the extension agent who had told him eight people.

People died, he said. Not all from the water. The pandemic wave, other factors. But compromised water access compounded everything else -- dehydration, waterborne illness, reduced capacity to treat people who were already sick.

And now you want to go talk to them. The people who made that decision.

I want to understand who they are and what they're building. The water routing was wrong. But the infrastructure management -- maintaining the substation, keeping the facility operational, the private grid -- that's capacity the valley needs to survive the next two years.

You're doing the utilitarian math, she said.

He had done utilitarian math all his professional life. Triage at the system level -- which infrastructure to prioritize, which failure to address first, where the available resources would do the most good. The math he had done in the field had been math about systems. The math that Nora did was math about people, and the outputs were different.

He had been trying to import her math into his framework for four days and had not quite managed it.

What's the non-utilitarian version? he said.

She looked at the fire.

The non-utilitarian version doesn't start with the math, she said. It starts with the question of what we owe each other. Not as a calculation -- as a premise. What's the baseline we're working from.

He thought about this.

And what baseline are you working from?

That nobody gets written out of the equation without their knowledge, she said. That's all. Not that everyone gets equal resources -- I know that's not possible. But that nobody disappears from the accounting because it was convenient to exclude them.

He thought about the eight hundred ghost addresses. About the northern communities running dry while the southern installation established viability.

Vera Solis wrote them out, he said.

Yes. And the system she built let her do it cleanly, without having to watch what happened to them. She paused. The distance that infrastructure creates between decisions and consequences is a feature, not a bug. Systems people know this.

He looked at his hands.

Yes, he said. We do.

I'm doing the only math available.

She looked at him. The fire moved again.

Rosa Garza, she said. Past tense now, because the water is back and she's better. You fixed that. You made that decision to go toward the problem instead of around it, knowing you were going to alert whoever was watching. You made the decision even though you didn't have complete information about what you were alerting.

He waited.

Tell me what you're actually deciding, she said. Because it's not a calculation.

He looked at the fire for a moment.

I'm going to go talk to them, he said. Because the credential didn't stop me and the note and the open gate are the beginning of something. And because I need to know whether what they're building is something I can work with or something I have to work against. He paused. And because I'm one of the few people who can read the difference between what they say they're doing and what they're actually doing. That's a reason to go rather than a reason to stay out of it.

I know you are, she said. I just wanted you to say it like that instead of the other way.

What's the other way?

Like it's tactical, she said. Like it's just the right move.

A silence.

Is it not the right move? he said.

It's the right move, she said. It's also more than the right move. She looked at him steadily. You came out here because of a water system and forty-one people on your fence line. You're still here because of something else.

He had been thinking about the ranch as the default -- the place he would return to when this was resolved. He was not sure anymore that return was the right direction.

I'll need you to stay with the group, he said.

I know. She looked at the fire. Come back by tomorrow evening. I don't like running extended medical operations without someone in range who can make decisions I can't.

In case you need backup for a decision you'd want to discuss first?

In case, she said. Take Marcus. Take Eddie. Take the radio.

He looked at her.

What do you think we're going to find? he said.

She thought about it.

I think we're going to find that the people who made a wrong decision are not entirely wrong people, she said. And I think that's going to be harder for you than finding someone straightforwardly bad.

He held that.

Yes, he said.

---

She said: I need to tell you something before you go.

They were in the grain barn's medical corner, the only private space the barn offered -- not genuinely private, but set off enough that conversations could be had at a normal volume without being general information.

Cole sat on the equipment crate.

She sat across from him and looked at him with the direct attention she gave things that mattered.

You're going to go south tomorrow and have a conversation with someone who has done something wrong and something right, she said. And you're going to come back and tell me whether the right outweighs the wrong and what you think we should do about it. And I'm going to have an opinion about your answer.

I know, he said.

I want you to know before you go what my opinion is already, so you have it in the room with you. She folded her hands. The people who died in Victor and Stevensville and the other northern communities during the eight months of the water redirection -- those deaths are in the accounting. Whatever this person built, whatever their reasons were, those deaths are in the accounting. There is no framework in which they're not in the accounting.

I agree with that, he said.

And there is also the fact that the infrastructure she built -- if it works the way you think it works, if the capacity is what you think it is -- is going to keep people alive who would otherwise die. Both things are true. I'm not telling you one cancels the other. I'm telling you that when you're in the room with her, I need you to hold both of them simultaneously.

Cole looked at her.

You're worried I'll go utilitarian, he said.

I'm worried you'll go analytical, she said. Which is different. Analytical is: the infrastructure has value, the deaths are a cost, is the value greater than the cost. That's not a sufficient framework for this. The deaths are not an abstraction. The eight people in Victor had names. Curtis Hall knows their names.

He was quiet.

I think you actually do, she said. I'm saying it anyway because I want it said before you're in the room with someone who is going to make a very compelling case for a very analytic framework, and I want you to have heard a different framing before you hear hers.

How do you know she's going to make a compelling analytic case?

Because she built fourteen infrastructure systems across southwestern Montana while managing a post-collapse population, she said. That's not someone who's operating on feeling. She's going to have numbers and arguments and a framework that is internally consistent and that is going to make a kind of sense. Your job is to engage with it accurately and also to hold the thing that doesn't fit the framework.

The eight people in Victor, he said.

The eight people in Victor, she said. And the people in the other communities we don't have numbers for yet.

He looked at the medical supplies on the shelf.

She said: Come back. And when you come back, tell me the truth about what she says. Not the version that makes the decision easier. The actual truth.

Always, he said.

She looked at him with the expression that said she was deciding whether that was sufficient.

Good, she said.

---

She found him before dawn.

He had not slept after their conversation -- not from anxiety, from an alert state of someone who had made a decision and was running it forward to test its implications. He was at the barn entry with the Reader when she appeared.

She sat down beside him.

I want to tell you something else, she said.

When you come back, she said. Whatever you find, whatever you decide -- I'll support the decision. Not unconditionally. If I think the decision is wrong, I'll tell you it's wrong. But I'll support the process. She paused. You've built something here that I didn't expect you to build. She looked at the valley. You don't think you have. But from where I'm standing, watching the conversations you had yesterday with Elena and Robert and the others -- you've been building something.

Cole looked at the Reader.

I've been doing what needed doing, he said.

Yes, she said. That's what building looks like from the inside.

The valley outside was starting to lighten. The mountains to the west still dark, the sky above the eastern hills moving from black to the deep blue that preceded dawn.

She said: When this is all done. The valley, the conversation with whoever is running the southern installation, the Purcell situation. What do you want?

The same thing I wanted at the ranch, he said. Systems that work. People who have what they need.

And what do you want for yourself? she said.

He thought about this for a long time.

I want to be doing the right work, he said. I've been doing defensible work for two years. Work I could justify. Maintaining my property, staying out of other people's problems, not creating obligations I couldn't fulfill. He paused. That's not the same as doing the right work.

She looked at the Reader in his lap.

You're doing the right work now, she said.

She stood.

Come back, she said. That's the first requirement. Everything else gets figured out after.

I'll come back, he said.

She went back to the medical supplies. He sat at the barn entry until full dawn, watching the valley receive its light.

---

After the conversation, he sat for a while.

The barn was settling into its night rhythms. The cook fire banked to coals. the breathing of twenty-three people at rest. Marcus on watch at the door, a shadow against the slightly less dark of the outside.

He thought about what Nora had said. About therapeutic presence. About practicing the opposite.

He had been very good at being in his room.

He thought about the fence line policy, which had been his room in a literal sense. The clean separation of the ranch from the camp, the maintained distance, the availability at the fence and not inside the fence. He had told himself the policy was about resource management and the clarity of obligations.

The policy had been about not having to be present for things he did not know how to be present for.

He looked at the people sleeping in the barn.

Twenty-three people who had made the decision to come south. Eddie and Rosa because the alternative was Purcell. Nora because she had looked at the situation and decided that going was the right action. The others for their own specific reasons, each one a person with a full history of deciding things in a world that had been requiring decisions at a pace that no one had been built for.

He was responsible for them in a way that the fence line policy had been designed to prevent him from being responsible for anyone.

He thought about whether he had made a mistake.

He sat with the thought until it resolved.

No, he decided. He had not made a mistake. He had made a decision that had consequences he was still learning how to carry. That was different from a mistake.

He put the Reader in his pocket and went to check on Rosa's medical status, which was his excuse for going to sit with Nora for twenty minutes before the watch changed.

---

He went to find Marcus before he left and found him sitting outside the barn on an overturned feed bucket, not doing anything.

This was unusual. Marcus was always doing something -- the motorcycle, his gear, a scout of the perimeter. The stillness had a quality Cole registered and categorized: down time, recovery. He left him to it.

He told Nora he was heading out. She looked past him toward where Marcus was sitting.

Did you talk to him? she said.

I'm about to.

She looked at Cole.

Not about the route, she said.

He looked at her.

About Iris, she said. He's been sitting out there since the burial. That's not recovery time. That's someone who doesn't know what to do with something.

Cole thought about Marcus. Nineteen. Moving alone for two years. Attached to groups long enough to be useful and then detaching. He had classified this as operational independence -- a survival strategy, a practiced mobility.

He's fine, Cole said. He processes things differently.

Nora looked at him with the expression she had when he had made an error and she was deciding how to say so.

How do you know how he processes things? she said.

I've been watching him for a week.

From a tactical perspective.

He didn't answer.

She said: Iris was the first person who explained something to him since the collapse. Not information. Something. He asked her about the substation at the grain barn and she spent forty minutes on it. He listened the whole time. He asked follow-up questions. She looked at Cole. He was learning from her. That's different from being trained or being useful. He was learning from someone who wanted to teach him.

Cole stood in the barn doorway looking at Marcus on the bucket.

He hadn't seen that. He had seen Marcus ask the questions and he had noted: Marcus is gathering information. He had not seen what Nora was describing, which was a nineteen-year-old sitting across from a retired electrical engineer and feeling, briefly, like a student rather than a survivor.

He had missed it entirely.

He walked out to Marcus.

He sat down on the ground beside the bucket because there was nothing else to sit on. Marcus looked at him.

I'm okay, Marcus said.

I know, Cole said. He paused. I didn't see it.

Marcus looked at him.

What Iris was doing when she talked to you, Cole said. What it was. I filed it as -- He stopped. I categorized it wrong.

A silence.

She explained things like they were worth explaining, Marcus said. Not like she was being useful. Like it mattered that I understood it.

Cole sat with that.

Most people who explain things to you are explaining to demonstrate something about themselves, Marcus said. She was explaining because she wanted you to know the thing. He paused. That's rare.

Yes, Cole said.

Another silence. A raven crossed the field in front of them, low and purposeful.

I wrote down what she told me, Cole said. In the Reader. Everything I could remember. It's not complete -- there are gaps. But it's there.

Marcus looked at the field.

Good, he said. That's the right thing to do.

They sat in the cold for another minute.

Then Cole stood and put his hand briefly on Marcus's shoulder -- not long, not performed, just present -- and went to get the truck.

He had made the error and he had not fixed it, exactly. Some errors didn't fix. But he had gone out there and sat in the cold and said the true thing, which was that he had categorized it wrong, and that was the thing available to do.

He drove south.

The fire had burned down to a bed of coals by the time they were done talking.

The coals threw just enough light to see each other's faces -- the quality of firelight that made expressions visible without making them confrontable, the light of conversations that needed to happen in a context that allowed both people to look away.

He had not looked away.

She had not looked away either. She had looked at him with the steady attention she gave everything that mattered and had said the things she'd said and had not softened any of them.

He thought about what it cost to be looked at like that. Not threateningly -- with full attention. With the expectation that he would be seen accurately and respond accurately.

He had been living behind the fence line for two years. Not because he was afraid of the world. Because he was afraid of being seen in it.

The fence line was down.

He put another piece of wood on the coals and watched it catch.
