# Chapter 22

## The Fix and the Cost

He set the watch that night himself and found he didn't want to hand it off.

The watch was two hours. He sat at the barn's entry in the October cold and looked at the valley and thought about the Reader's display showing the Bitterroot system running clean, and thought about Curtis Hall in Stevensville standing at a tap listening to the pressure change, and thought about eight people in the northern communities who had been in the accounting when the optimization layer was running and were not in the accounting anymore.

He thought about Iris, who was also no longer in the accounting.

He thought about the notes he had written down from her explanation of the substation. The specific technical vocabulary she had used to describe what someone else had built. The respect in her voice when she said: it's good work. Technical respect was not moral endorsement. She had made that distinction clearly and he had not missed it. Good work could be used for bad ends. Knowing the work was good was still worth knowing.

He sat in the cold and let the night work through him.

The valley had a particular quality in the dark that he had not been expecting: it was not silent. The creek systems that fed it were audible from the grain barn's position, a constant low presence of moving water. Water he had restored to the northern communities' pipes six hours ago. Water moving through a system that he had first touched in a professional context three years before the collapse, in a life that felt like someone else's.

He had come back to fix it.

He sat with the strangeness of that.

The night was clear, the temperature in the low twenties, the valley floor quiet with the quiet of a place that had been loud and was no longer. He sat at the grain barn's entry with the Reader in his lap and looked at the valley.

Water was moving in the pipes.

He knew this from the system's distribution status on the Reader's display -- the pressures normalizing, the flow rates climbing toward design specifications, the northern communities' connection points registering active supply for the first time in three weeks. He had fixed a thing. The thing he had come to fix. The thing he had told himself was the reason to leave the ranch.

It was not the only reason, he was starting to understand.

He thought about the note on the printout. About the open gate. About the credential that had watched him work and chosen not to interfere, and what that choice said about the person making it.

He thought about what Nora had said: I think we're going to find that the people who made a wrong decision are not entirely wrong people.

He had been avoiding thinking about what that meant for what came after.

At three in the morning, Marcus's voice on the radio: Eight vehicles on the north valley road. Moving south. Purcell's column flag on the lead truck.

Cole stood and looked north.

Five days, Nora had said. It had been four.

He woke the group.

He woke them efficiently, the same way he had been waking them for four days when something required attention before dawn -- a touch on the shoulder for the light sleepers, a quiet word for the others, a specific instruction to each person about what they were waking up to do. He had learned, over four days, who woke fast and who needed a moment, who could receive technical information immediately and who needed thirty seconds of orientation first.

He had learned this the way he learned most things about people: by watching what they actually did in the situations they were actually in, rather than by asking them what they would do.

Rosa was up before he reached her. She was looking at the road north with the alertness of someone who had woken to uncertain situations often enough to know the posture.

Purcell? she said.

Yes.

She nodded and began preparing her pack with the economical movements of someone who had done this before.

He looked at her for a moment.

You're not scared, he said.

She looked up at him.

I was scared the first year, she said. Now I just figure out what to do.

He thought about that for the rest of the morning.

---

The next six hours required management of a specific kind.

Not combat preparation -- that would have been simpler. What the situation required was the management of twenty-three people who had varying levels of understanding of the threat, varying levels of capacity to respond to it, and varying levels of trust in Cole's judgment about what the right response was.

He gave them the information and the instructions in order of priority.

First: Purcell was coming because the valley was the only viable option within range and he had been tracking their route. He was going to arrive and assess. The group's job was to be visibly present and clearly organized and not provide him with any indication of disarray.

Second: they were not going to fight Purcell's column. The numbers were not right and the situation was not right and most importantly Purcell was not the problem he had come here to solve. The goal was to manage the confrontation toward an outcome where Purcell had less leverage than he arrived with.

Third: the people of Stevensville and the other northern communities were going to be in this conversation whether they chose to be or not, and their presence -- two hundred people who had clean water this morning because of Cole's group -- was the most important element of the leverage calculation.

He briefed Curtis Hall. He positioned the group across the approaches to the central block. He told Marcus to watch Purcell's column from the roof and radio updates.

Then he waited.

The wait was forty minutes. Long enough for Purcell's column to approach at the pace it was moving, long enough for Curtis Hall to organize the Stevensville community into a visible and coherent presence, long enough for the group to take their positions and settle into them.

Cole stood in the road.

Purcell arrived in the lead vehicle, which Cole noted and respected. He got out and looked at the assembled group and at the Stevensville residents and at the grain barn and at the trucks and at Cole, and he did the calculation that Cole had been watching him do since month one.

He got the same answer as before. And he had the same look he'd had when he signed the insulin agreement -- the look of a man who had lost a position and was already past it.

They talked for twenty minutes in the road.

Cole gave him the honest picture of the valley: the water restored, the northern communities fragile, the infrastructure issues that would take months to address. He told him about the organized southern community with the technical capacity and the resources and the decision that had cost eight people their lives. He did not tell him everything about the southern community.

Purcell listened with the focused attention he brought to everything useful.

You're telling me this valley needs management, he said.

I'm telling you this valley has communities that have been surviving for two years without management and have gotten reasonably good at it. What they need is people and resources. Not management.

Purcell looked at the assembled group. At the Stevensville residents watching from the block's edge. At the position Cole had put everyone in.

What's your interest? Purcell said. You could have fixed the water and gone home.

Cole looked at the valley. At the mountains west. At the Reader in his vest pocket.

I have an interest in what happens here, he said. I built some of the infrastructure that's running in this valley. What it gets used for is not something I'm indifferent to.

Purcell looked at him with something that was almost admiration and entirely calculation.

You're going to be here, he said. After this.

Then we have an arrangement to negotiate.

The negotiation took another thirty minutes. It was specific and professional and at the end of it Purcell had agreed to terms that were better for the valley than the alternative. Not ideal -- Purcell's motivations were his motivations and they did not suddenly become different motivations because he had agreed to terms. But manageable.

Purcell's column drove into Stevensville and began the process of becoming part of something rather than the top of something, which was going to be difficult for Purcell and Cole was not going to pretend otherwise.

Marcus appeared beside him.

He'll be trouble, Marcus said.

Yes. In specific ways I can predict and manage around. Cole looked at the column. Being predictable is his best quality from my perspective.

Marcus looked at him.

You're going to stay in the valley, he said. It wasn't a question.

I'm going to do what needs doing, Cole said. Right now that's south. After that -- He paused. I don't know yet what after that looks like.

Marcus nodded. He seemed satisfied with this.

Cole looked south.

---

He did two things before he slept.

He talked to Curtis Hall.

Curtis was awake, because Curtis was always awake in the hours when things could change. Cole found him at the edge of the Stevensville central block, standing with a cup of something hot and looking north at the dark road.

Purcell is two days behind us, Cole said.

I know. Word travels, Curtis said.

He's going to arrive and assess. He's capable and organized and he's going to try to make himself essential. Cole paused. I'm not asking you to oppose him. I'm asking you to know that his first move is always to find the thing nobody else is managing and manage it.

And if we keep managing our own things, he has fewer vectors, Curtis said.

Curtis looked at the road.

We've been managing our own things for two years, he said. We're reasonably good at it.

I know. Cole looked at him. I also know that two years of managing things under impossible conditions has a cost, and the cost is that sometimes you need someone who isn't exhausted to do the work. He paused. That's what Purcell offers. Not maliciously. He offers energy and capability when the people who have been here are tired.

And?

And the price is the authority he takes in exchange. Cole looked at the valley. I'm not telling you not to use him. I'm telling you to know the price before you accept the offer.

Curtis nodded.

You're going to be here, he said. After the conversation with the southern people.

Then we have options, Curtis said. Having options is better than not having options.

Yes, Cole said.

He went back to the barn.

He talked to Rosa.

She was awake, which surprised him, and she was at the barn's window looking at the valley.

He said: What are you looking at?

The southern lights, she said. They've been on the whole time we've been here. Everything else in the valley goes dark at some point. Those don't.

They have consistent power generation, he said.

She nodded.

Is that where we're going? she said.

I'm going there tomorrow. You're staying here with the group.

She looked at him.

But after? she said.

Cole thought about what the accounting with Vera Solis would produce. About what the valley's future looked like if that accounting went the way he hoped it would.

Probably, he said.

She nodded and went back to looking at the southern lights.

Cole stood at the barn's edge and looked at them too for a moment.

Then he went to find a place to sleep.

---

After Purcell's column drove into Stevensville, Curtis Hall came to stand beside Cole.

Who is he? Curtis said.

Dale Purcell. Former city councilman. He ran a camp of forty-one people in central Montana for four months after the person who actually ran it died.

How'd he do?

Well enough to still have forty people and vehicles, Cole said.

Curtis looked at the column.

Is he going to be a problem?

Cole thought about the agreement. About the terms and the leverage calculation and the fact that Purcell had agreed because agreeing was the right move, not because he had changed his mind about what he wanted.

Probably, Cole said. Not right away. He'll be useful first.

Curtis looked at him.

You sound like you've managed this kind of person before.

Not this exactly. The same operational logic.

What do you do about the operational logic?

Cole thought about what he had told Marcus: make sure there are other people who can manage the things he's managing. Make sure he's not the only person who understands any critical system.

You build redundancy, he said. Not just in the infrastructure. In the people.

Curtis was quiet for a moment.

You've been thinking about this for a while, he said.

I've been thinking about the valley for a week, Cole said. This is part of the valley.

Are you going to stay? he said. After you do whatever you're going south to do.

Cole looked at the valley. At the mountains going gold in the late afternoon light. At Stevensville with its returned water and its people and its new hundred and some residents who had arrived this morning in eight vehicles.

I think so, he said.

Curtis nodded once.

Good, he said.

He walked back toward the central block. Cole stood for another moment looking at the valley.

Then he went to find Nora.

---

He did the morning Reader queries at four AM.

The Bitterroot system: clean. The valley distribution: normalized, the pressure readings at every connection point stable and at design specification. The northern communities had water. The fix was holding.

He had fixed a water system.

He had also, in the three days since, done something he had not planned to do: he had become part of what the valley was building. He had talked to its people. He had learned its specific problems. He had made commitments, implicit and explicit, about what he was going to do next.

He thought about the ranch.

The ranch was there. The propane gauge was at sixty-two percent. The east pasture fence was in the condition he had left it. The chickens, if Daniel had been feeding them as Cole had asked, were managing. The equipment shed was locked. The main house was locked. The spare key was under the third stone of the east foundation wall.

It would be there when he came back.

He thought about whether he was coming back.

He was going to have to make a decision about this at some point. Not today -- today was the conversation with Vera Solis and everything that came from it. But at some point the question of what the ranch was going to be -- an endpoint or an asset in a larger picture -- was going to require an answer.

He thought about what Briggs had used the ranch for in his assessment of the situation.

He thought about what the ranch could be in the valley's future. A staging point. A resource base. A place where someone who understood the ranch's infrastructure could continue to manage it in service of something larger than itself.

He thought about the seventeen people who had stayed. About Purcell managing them now, which was an uncomfortable fact that was going to require attention eventually.

He looked at the Reader.

He looked at the valley in the early dark.

He thought about what the next version of his life looked like.

He did not have the answer. He had the direction.

That was enough for now.

---

The fix ran clean.

Three hours at the keyboard, the verification at the end, the water pressure climbing in the distribution system as the corrected parameters propagated through the network. He had run the verification twice, which was the protocol, and both times it had come back nominal.

He sat back from the interface.

The facility was quiet around him. Marcus on exterior watch, Iris doing one more circuit of the physical hardware. The only sound was the system's operational hum, the background frequency of infrastructure doing its job.

He thought about the four years he had spent building things like this. the satisfaction of a system running correctly -- not the heroic satisfaction of a dramatic intervention, the quieter satisfaction of something working the way it was designed to work. He had spent four years chasing that satisfaction and two years without it and now it was back and it felt like coming home to a house he had missed without knowing he was missing it.

He thought about what came next.

The northern communities would have water by morning. Purcell was two days away at his stated timeline, possibly one day given what Marcus had said about his operational tempo. The southern installation was forty miles south and the credential had opened the gate and left him a note and was waiting for him to come back.

He had fixed one thing.

He had the feeling of someone who has fixed one thing and can see six others waiting.

He closed the interface. Picked up the Reader. Ran one final verification of the system's status.

Nominal.

He put the Reader away.

Time to go, he said to the empty room.

---

He stood at the facility's gate after the fix and ran the full system status one more time.

Nominal in every parameter. The distribution running correctly. The northern communities' connections active. The optimization layer isolated. The secondary objective intact and correctly sequenced for future non-interference.

He looked at the gate -- the electronic lock released, the hinges oiled, the mechanism maintained.

Someone had been here this morning before him. Had opened this gate for him. Had been here and gone without making contact.

He thought about what it took to do that -- to be here, to open the gate, to leave the note, to go without making contact. To trust that the person who came would do what they had come to do and that the result would be acceptable.

He thought: that's a specific kind of faith in a specific kind of person.

He thought: they were right about what I would do.

He drove north.
