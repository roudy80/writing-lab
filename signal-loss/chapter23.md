# Chapter 23

## Purcell Arrives

Purcell agreed to the terms the next morning.

The terms had taken shape over two conversations -- the road confrontation the previous morning, and a second conversation that evening in the Stevensville central block, where Curtis Hall and Cole and Purcell sat at the same folding table and worked through what an integration actually meant in practical terms.

Curtis Hall was good at this. He had the quality of someone who had spent a career in official capacity negotiating between parties with competing interests, and he had retained the skill after the collapse and applied it to a different context. He was fair in a specific way -- not splitting the difference but identifying the interests underneath the positions and finding the arrangements that addressed the interests even when they didn't match the positions.

Cole had watched him work and thought: this is the thing that Purcell isn't.

Purcell could find the deal. Curtis could find the arrangement that people could actually live in.

The practical terms: Purcell's group would integrate into the Stevensville community structure, not establish a parallel one. Resources pooled. Decision-making shared, with a council structure that Cole had insisted on before Purcell arrived and that Curtis had been building for two weeks. Purcell could stand for the council. He could not chair it unilaterally.

Purcell had agreed to all of it.

Cole had watched him agree and had thought: he's filing this. He's agreeing now and filing the terms as provisional while he builds his position. He will agree to the terms until he has the standing to renegotiate them.

This was not the same as betrayal. It was how Purcell operated. Cole had known how Purcell operated since month two.

The question was whether the community structure they were building could absorb Purcell's operating style without being deformed by it.

He had told Curtis Hall this, privately, the previous evening.

Curtis had looked at him.

You think I haven't been building around this for two years? Curtis said. You think I don't know what Purcell is?

Cole had thought about this.

I think you know, he said. I'm telling you what I see so you know I know.

Curtis had nodded once. That's worth something, he said.

Not because Cole had persuaded him -- Cole knew better than to think persuasion was the mechanism. Because overnight Purcell had done his own assessment of the valley's situation and had arrived at the same conclusion Cole had been waiting for him to arrive at: the valley was a better long-term bet than authority over forty people in transit, and Cole had positioned things so that the valley's resources were only accessible on Cole's terms.

It was a calculation. Cole accepted it as a calculation and moved on.

The conversation was brief and professional. Purcell had the quality of someone who executed agreements he had decided to honor with the same efficiency he executed agreements he hadn't decided to honor yet -- you couldn't tell from his behavior which category you were in, which was either a strength or a warning depending on how you looked at it.

Near the end, Purcell looked at the assembled valley communities -- the Stevensville residents, Cole's group, the people from Victor and the crossroads settlement who had come into town that morning when word spread that something had changed.

Two hundred and some people, Purcell said. Counting mine.

Approximately, Cole said.

That's a real community, Purcell said. Not a camp.

It's been a real community since month one, Cole said. You're joining it. Not running it.

Purcell smiled. The perfectly calibrated smile of a man who had been managing rooms for thirty years.

That's what someone who's running it would say, he said.

Cole looked at him.

I'm not running it, he said. No one person runs it. That's not how this works.

Purcell's smile stayed exactly as it was.

I understand, he said. He turned back to his column. I'll have my people integrate with the Stevensville housing situation. We should discuss resource inventory by end of day.

He walked back to his vehicles. Cole watched him go.

Marcus appeared beside him.

He'll consolidate, Marcus said.

Yes.

In what direction?

Cole thought about Purcell's specific capabilities. The resource allocation framing. The fairness language. The council proposal that put him in the coordinator role. He had been building toward this in the camp for two years and he would continue building toward it here.

He'll find the thing nobody else is managing and make himself essential to it, Cole said. Then he'll use that essentialness as leverage for the next thing. He paused. It's a slow process. It takes months. And it's not necessarily bad -- he's actually competent at logistics and group management. The valley needs that. The question is whether he does it in service of the community or in service of his own position.

How do you tell the difference? Marcus said.

Cole looked at Purcell's vehicles, at the organized column being directed toward the integration point by two of Purcell's people who were already, this morning, beginning to be useful.

Watch what he does when he has to choose between the community and his position, Cole said. The choice will come. It always comes. And how he makes it is the answer.

What if the answer is wrong?

Then we deal with it, Cole said. But we deal with a known quantity, which is better than dealing with someone we don't understand yet.

Marcus looked at him.

You've thought about this a lot, he said.

I've had two years, Cole said. Same as you.

Marcus, beside him: He'll be back.

He'll be here, Cole said. He agreed to stay. That's different from back.

Marcus looked at Purcell's retreating figure. Is it though?

Cole thought about it honestly.

For now, he said. It's different for now. He'll make a move eventually. When he does, it'll be through process rather than force -- he's too smart for force. Process is manageable.

What kind of process?

Cole thought about Purcell's specific skills. The resource allocation framing. The fairness language. The council that determined what fairness meant.

He'll try to make himself essential, Cole said. Find the thing nobody else is managing and manage it so well that the thing and Purcell become the same thing. Then he controls the thing.

So what do you do about that?

Make sure there are other people who can manage the things he's managing. Cole looked at the valley. Make sure he's not the only person who understands any critical system.

I've worked with Purcell before, Cole said. Different name. Same operational logic.

He turned toward the grain barn. There was work to do before they could go south.

---

The integration took three days.

Purcell was efficient about it, which Cole had expected. He was also strategic about it in ways that Cole had also expected but watched for anyway. the way Purcell's efficiency was strategic: he identified the valley's critical functions and positioned his people to be involved in them. Not to control them -- to be involved. To be useful. To be associated with what was working.

Cole watched this from a professional distance.

He talked to Curtis Hall on the evening of the first day.

Purcell, Cole said.

He's capable, Curtis said. And he knows it and you know it and he knows you know it.

What's his read of the situation?

His read is that there are two people who understand what's happening here -- you and him -- and that the valley is going to be run by whoever those two people can work with, and he is positioning himself to be the one who can work with more people.

Cole thought about this.

He's not wrong about the situation, Cole said.

No, Curtis said. He's right about the situation. He's optimizing for himself within it. That's not the same as being wrong about it.

They looked at the valley community going about its evening.

Will you watch him? Cole said.

I've been watching people my whole adult life, Curtis said. I'll watch him.

Cole nodded.

He went to find Marcus.

Marcus was on the grain barn's roof, which was his default position when he wasn't actively needed elsewhere -- the high point gave him sightlines and the sightlines gave him information and information was what Marcus ran on.

Purcell's people, Cole said.

Seven of the forty have been talking to the valley residents more than Purcell has, Marcus said. Learning the operational picture. Not hostile -- curious. They're building a map of what's here and how it works.

Intelligence operation, Cole said.

Voluntary one. They're not asking secrets. They're asking how things work. Marcus paused. The valley residents are telling them everything because nobody's told them not to.

Is that a problem?

Marcus considered.

Not immediately, he said. It becomes a problem if the information is used to identify leverage points. Right now it's just information.

Cole looked at the valley in the early dark.

Keep watching, he said.

Always, Marcus said.

---

He talked to Nora that evening.

The Purcell integration was three days in and the initial structure was holding. Cole had been watching it with the attention he gave things that required ongoing monitoring rather than one-time resolution.

Purcell had made two moves in three days.

First move: he had positioned three of his people in the Stevensville supply management operation, which was the most visible and most important logistical function in the northern community. They were genuinely helpful -- organized, efficient, bringing skills the northern communities needed. They were also positioning.

Second move: he had started a regular morning briefing with the valley's community representatives. Not commanded -- proposed. Offered as a coordination mechanism. All the community leaders had accepted because coordination was genuinely valuable.

Both moves were reasonable. Both moves advanced Purcell's position. Both things were true.

He told Nora this.

She listened and said: And?

And I need to decide how to respond to the morning briefing specifically, he said. If I attend, I'm in Purcell's structure. If I don't attend, I'm outside the valley's coordination mechanism. Neither position is comfortable.

Attend, she said. And make sure the structure isn't Purcell's.

How?

Bring Curtis Hall, she said. Bring the person from Victor whose name you got. Bring the other community representatives. Make it a valley briefing that Purcell is participating in, not a Purcell briefing that the valley is attending.

Cole looked at her.

That's the move, he said.

Yes, she said.

He went and made it happen.

The morning briefing the next day had twelve people and Purcell was the twelfth. He managed it smoothly and without apparent friction, which was the characteristic quality of his adjustments -- he found the new parameter set and operated within it and did not make the adjustment visible.

Cole watched him do this and thought about it.

He thought about what it would require to maintain this over months and years. To keep being the person who made the framing rather than the person in someone else's framing.

It would require exactly what he had been avoiding for two years: sustained presence, sustained relationship, sustained attention to the human situation rather than just the technical one.

He thought about the ranch. About the fence line policy. About the maintenance of a distance.

He had given that up when he crossed the fence to talk to Nora about the Bitterroot system.

He had not missed it.

---

Rosa came to find him an hour after Eddie did.

She had her notebook. She sat down across from him without asking and put the notebook on the crate between them.

I know what you're going to do tomorrow, she said.

Go south.

To the installation Dad built. To talk to whoever built it.

She looked at him steadily. The evaluating look, the one that was older than fourteen.

Is my dad going to be okay? she said.

Cole thought about what okay meant in this context. About the conversation Eddie had just had. About what it cost a person to go back to the site of the thing they had built without knowing what they were building.

I don't know, he said. I think so. It's going to be hard.

She nodded.

He carries things, she said. He doesn't put them down easily.

I know the feeling, Cole said.

She looked at him.

I've noticed, she said.

He looked at her.

You've noticed a lot of things, he said.

She picked up the notebook.

I write things down, she said. It helps me see what's actually there instead of what I think is there.

He thought about the Reader. About the difference between the remote query picture and the full physical diagnostic. About the ninety minutes he had taken to read the system before modifying it.

That's a good practice, he said.

You do the same thing, she said. With the Reader. You read everything before you touch anything.

Yes, he said.

She closed the notebook.

Then we'll both be okay, she said. Dad too.

She got up and went back to Eddie's corner of the barn.

Cole sat for a while after, with the Reader in his lap and the barn around him and the weight of tomorrow already in his chest, and felt, for the first time in a long time, something that was not quite optimism but was in the same neighborhood.

---

He talked to Nora about Purcell on the evening of the third day.

She had been watching Purcell the way she watched everything -- with the clinical eye of someone who was assessing a situation rather than reacting to it. She had her own read and he wanted it.

He's going to be useful, she said. She said it the way she said things she found complicated: straightforwardly, because the complexity was in the content, not in the saying. He's organized and he has genuine skills for the organizational phase. The valley needs organizing and he can help with that.

And? Cole said.

And you need to decide what organizing means. She looked at the valley. Organizing can mean: building the shared frameworks and systems that let people coordinate. Or it can mean: building the frameworks and systems that let one person control the coordination. Purcell does both, and the difference between the two versions isn't always visible in the early stages.

I know, Cole said.

I know you know, she said. I'm saying it because knowing it and managing for it are different things and I want to be sure you're managing for it and not just knowing it.

He thought about this.

The morning briefing, he said. The structure I set up with Curtis Hall. That was managing for it.

Yes. That was a good move. She looked at him. You're going to need to keep making those moves. He's going to keep creating structures that subtly serve his interests, and you're going to need to keep redirecting them before they become load-bearing.

Cole thought about the work of that.

It was relational work. The kind he was less practiced at than the technical kind. The kind that required him to know people well enough to understand which structures they were already inclined toward and which they would resist.

He thought about the conversations he had been having. About Elena and Robert and Curtis Hall and the thirty other names he was accumulating.

He was building toward being able to do this work.

He was not there yet.

He thought about Briggs, who had done this work for thirty years and had made it look like not work at all.

He thought about what it meant to learn this at forty, in the middle of a collapse, with Purcell already in the room.

It meant: do it imperfectly and correct as you went.

He could do that.

---

After the meeting with Purcell, Cole found Curtis Hall.

He wanted to make sure the Stevensville council understood the terms of the agreement -- not the ones Purcell had agreed to, which Hall had witnessed, but the ones Cole hadn't said out loud.

He said: Purcell is going to be useful in the next six months. He has organizational capacity and resources and he understands how to manage a complex community. Use all of that. And watch the mechanism by which he uses it. If you see him making himself the only person who understands something critical, that's the signal.

Hall looked at him.

What do we do about the signal?

Build redundancy, Cole said. Find the people in his structure who are competent and develop their competence independently of Purcell's sponsorship.

That's a long game, Hall said.

Yes, Cole said.

Hall looked at Purcell's column, still settling into Stevensville.

You've been thinking about this valley for a week and you're already thinking about the long game, he said.

Cole thought about the ranch. About the fence line policy. About the chickens.

I'm thinking about staying, he said.

Then you should stay, he said. We could use someone who thinks about the long game.
