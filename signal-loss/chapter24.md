# Chapter 24

## What Eddie Knows

Eddie found him that afternoon.

The afternoon light in the valley had a quality he had not expected -- a specific soft sharpness that came from the combination of low angle and dry October air and the mountains on three sides. The kind of light that made things look more precise than they usually did. He had noticed it the previous afternoon and had thought: this is why people used to want to live here. Not the agricultural potential or the water rights or the proximity to services. The light.

He was aware that noticing the light was a change from how he had been orienting his attention for the last week.

Cole was at the grain barn working through the Reader's accumulated data. Since arriving in the valley he had been running queries when the situation allowed, building a broader picture of the infrastructure landscape: what was active, what was drifted, what showed the credential signatures he had come to associate with the southern installation.

The picture was larger than he had estimated.

The credential had accessed fourteen systems across southwestern Montana in the last eight months. Not just the Bitterroot system and the substation. Irrigation networks, a regional communications relay, two weather monitoring stations that were now reporting to a private receiver rather than the public network, a logistics distribution hub whose scheduling platform had been redirected toward a private routing protocol.

All of it modified in the same window. All of it pointing south.

Eddie sat down across the equipment crate without preamble.

I need to tell you something, he said.

Cole put the Reader down.

Eddie told him about the contract work. The company -- he named it, a name that meant nothing to Cole but that he noted for later research. The woman who had commissioned it. The work itself: a self-contained municipal infrastructure installation designed for isolated operation -- water treatment, power generation, distribution management, storage. Designed to run without external supply chains for an extended period.

I thought it was resilience consulting, Eddie said. The kind of work companies were doing after COVID. But the scale was wrong for resilience consulting. A corporate resilience installation serves a campus, a facility. What I built served a small city.

How many people? Cole said.

At design capacity -- Eddie thought about it -- three to five hundred people, indefinitely. Twelve hundred at reduced service levels for a shorter period.

And the woman who commissioned it. What do you remember about her?

She was precise about specifications, Eddie said. Not in the way of someone who had been given specifications to communicate -- in the way of someone who had generated them. She knew why every parameter was what it was. She pushed back when I suggested alternatives based on cost efficiency and her pushback was always technical. It was always: this parameter exists because of this constraint that you don't have full visibility into.

She had visibility you didn't have, Cole said.

Into the whole system, yes. I built the water treatment and distribution components. I knew there were other contractors working on other components but I didn't know who they were or what they were building. She kept the full picture to herself.

Cole looked at the Reader. At the fourteen systems. At the credential signatures.

She built something comprehensive, he said.

She built a plan, Eddie said. Not a plan to survive a collapse. A plan to rebuild after one. The infrastructure isn't just for surviving -- it's for providing services to a growing community. The treatment capacity scales. The distribution network has expansion nodes. The power system has a load profile that accounts for more users than the initial installation needed.

Cole sat with this for a long moment.

She expected people to come, he said.

She expected to be a destination, Eddie said. Yes.

But she redirected the valley's water supply for eight months.

I know.

Eight people died in Stevensville while she was running that optimization.

I know, Eddie said. His voice was flat and even in a way that Cole recognized as the sound of someone who had already had the internal argument and resolved it without resolution. I keep thinking about what I knew when I was building it. I knew it was a large installation. I knew it was designed for isolation. I asked enough questions to know what it was for and not enough to understand what it would cost.

Cole looked at him.

I want to come when you go south, Eddie said.

You said.

I mean I need to come. Not just because I built it. Because I need to stand in that room and say that out loud. What I built and why and what it cost. He looked at the Reader display. I don't think I can move forward from it without doing that.

Cole looked at him for a long moment.

Okay, he said.

---

Cole asked him: What was she like?

Eddie looked at him. The question caught him off-guard, which was not easy to do.

Vera, Cole said. What was she like to work with?

Eddie thought about it for a long time.

She was precise, he said. About everything. When she told you something it was the right information in the right amount. No more, no less. She didn't explain herself unless she decided you needed the explanation, and when she decided you needed it, the explanation was complete.

Did she trust you?

She trusted my technical work. He paused. I don't know if she trusted me personally. I don't think she distinguished between those two things the way most people do. If your work was accurate, you were someone she could work with. If it wasn't, you weren't. That was the whole classification.

Did she tell you why?

Why what?

Why she was building it. What it was for.

She said: I've run the models. The current infrastructure trajectory has failure modes that are going to manifest in the next five to seven years. I'm building ahead of that. I want to be part of what comes after, not just what survives the failure.

She said this before the collapse.

Three years before the collapse, Eddie said. Yes.

And you believed her?

I thought she was pessimistic. He looked at his hands. I thought she was the kind of person who built bunkers and sold them to people who were afraid. The scale was different from what I expected of that type of person, and the sophistication was different, but I filed it as a category I knew.

And now?

Now I think she was the most accurate person in the room and nobody gave her the credibility her accuracy earned because the accuracy was uncomfortable. He paused. That's a thing that happens. I've seen it happen. I've been part of it happening. He looked at Cole. Have you?

Cole thought about the reports he had filed. The concern documentation. The internal memos that had been acknowledged and not acted on.

Yes, he said.

So you know, Eddie said. What it costs when the accurate person in the room doesn't get heard.

Cole looked at the Reader in his hand.

I know, he said.

They were quiet for a moment.

She was also wrong about something important, Eddie said. I don't know exactly what. But you don't route a valley's water supply away from the people who need it for eight months without being wrong about something important. Even if the numbers justified it. The numbers don't justify everything.

No, Cole said.

Eddie stood.

When you talk to her, he said, tell her I'd like to know what the something important was. Not in accusation. I'd just like to know what she was wrong about. It seems like the kind of thing a person should be told directly.

I'll tell her, Cole said.

---

He spent the evening with Eddie.

Not talking about infrastructure -- they had covered the infrastructure. Cole asked about Rosa.

Eddie looked at him.

Why? he said.

Because she's been tracking the group's resources since day one and she approached me about integrating her data with the Reader and she's been building a skills inventory of the group in her notebook. Cole paused. She's fourteen and she's running analytical operations that most adults don't run.

Eddie was quiet for a moment.

She was twelve when it started, he said. She had been doing a school project on supply chain logistics -- it was a unit in her economics class, something about how goods moved from production to consumer. She had been working on it when the collapse started. He paused. She kept working on it. The world changed and she adapted the framework to the world that was actually there.

She didn't stop, Cole said.

She couldn't stop, Eddie said. She needed to understand where things were and where they were going and what the constraints were. The notebook was how she understood it. He looked at his hands. She's also been tracking my diabetes management -- the dosing adjustments Nora has been making, the relationship between activity level and insulin requirement, the storage conditions and their effect on potency. She built a model of it.

She built a model of her own medical management, he said.

Because she didn't want to be dependent on me or Nora understanding it correctly every time, Eddie said. She wanted to understand it herself. He paused. She's been right twice when I was wrong. Both times about the timing of doses based on activity patterns I hadn't been tracking accurately.

Cole thought about this.

She's going to want the Reader, he said.

I know, Eddie said.

Not to use it the way I use it. She doesn't have the credentials. But to understand what it's reading and why. She's going to want to integrate it with her own analytical framework.

Are you going to let her? he said.

Cole thought about Rosa's three questions. About the column notebook. About the skills inventory she had been building since day one.

Eddie nodded.

Good, he said.

---

He drove Eddie to the southern installation the morning after Iris died.

Not to the meeting with Vera Solis -- that was the next day. He drove Eddie to the installation's access road and stopped and let Eddie look at what he had built.

Eddie looked for a long time.

The scale was visible from here: the solar array, the wind turbines, the organized buildings, the covered walkways. A community rather than a facility. The infrastructure he had designed for water treatment and distribution was integrated into a larger whole that he had not known was being built.

He looked at it for a long time.

Cole said nothing.

After a while Eddie said: The water treatment building is there. He pointed at the second structure from the left. That's the distribution manifold building beside it. The buried lines run north, I can see where the excavation was filled in, it matches the route I specified.

It's been maintained, Cole said.

Yes. He looked at the maintenance evidence -- the weathering patterns, the access marks, the signs of regular use and upkeep. The way you maintain infrastructure that matters.

He was quiet again.

I used to drive past projects I had worked on, he said. After they were built. Just to see them. To see whether they were doing what they were supposed to do. He paused. I used to find it satisfying.

And now? Cole said.

Now I find it -- He stopped.

Now I find it complicated, he said. The building is good. The work is good. Everything I can see from here is exactly what it was supposed to be. He looked at the solar array. The purpose it's serving is not what I was told it was serving.

Cole looked at the installation.

It's serving both, he said. The community it was designed for. And, eventually, the valley it's in.

Eventually, Eddie said.

Eventually is better than never, Cole said.

Is that enough? he said.

Cole thought about the eight names in James Kirby's memory.

No, he said. But it's a beginning.

Eddie nodded and they drove back to the grain barn.

---

He found Nora after the conversation with Eddie.

She was in the medical corner of the barn, doing her evening inventory. He sat down on a supply crate and watched her work for a moment.

She looked up.

Eddie, she said.

He talked to me tonight.

I know. I've been watching him decide whether to for two days.

How did you know he was deciding?

She put a medication bottle back in its place.

He picks things up and puts them down, she said. Tools, cups, Rosa's notebook when she leaves it out. When someone is working through something they're not saying, their hands work through it too.

Cole looked at his own hands.

He's going to be okay, he said.

Yes, she said. He's a good person who built something for someone he trusted without asking enough questions. That's a recoverable situation. She paused. He built it well, too. That matters to him. The quality of the work, even if the purpose turned out to be something he didn't intend.

It matters to me, Cole said.

I know, she said. That's why you two get along.

He looked at her.

We get along because we both care about the work, he said.

You get along because you both built things you didn't fully understand and are trying to figure out what that means, she said. The work is what you talk about. It's not the only thing you have in common.

She went back to the inventory.

Cole sat with that for a while.

---

He thought about what Eddie's notebook would show, if Rosa's practice had passed to him.

Eighteen months ago: civil engineer, contract work, routine morning. Then: the slow development of the attentiveness of someone who has lost the context they were embedded in and has to build a new one from scratch. The contract that had been resilience consulting and had been something else. The construction that he had done well and had not understood.

He thought about writing and what it was for -- the act of making experience visible enough to examine, the way Rosa had said: it helps me see what's actually there instead of what I think is there.

He had the Reader for that. The Reader showed him what systems were actually doing rather than what he assumed they were doing.

He thought: the Reader is his notebook.

He thought about what Nora's equivalent would be.

He thought he might ask her.
