# Chapter 25

## The Loss

Iris died that night.

Not dramatically. Nora had been monitoring the cardiac situation -- she had identified it in the second day of travel, flagged it, discussed it with Iris directly and quietly. Iris had known about it. She had been managing it for three years before the collapse with medication she no longer had and with a deliberate calm in someone who had decided that the situation was manageable and that managing it was preferable to being managed by it.

The stress of the last week had done what stress did to managed cardiac conditions.

Nora was there for all of it. She did what she could with what she had, which was not enough, because what she had was not calibrated for this and she had known for two days that it might not be. Cole was there for the last twenty minutes. He stayed after Nora had done everything.

Iris was gone in forty minutes from the first indication.

He sat in the barn after.

He had known Iris for six days. He had known her in the way of people who work alongside each other under conditions that compress acquaintance into something that felt longer -- the substation, the facility, the two hours in the equipment barn where she had explained power system architecture with the clarity of someone who had spent thirty years developing that clarity. He had known her as a professional, which was the category he knew most people in, and professionals who were genuinely good at what they did had a quality he recognized and respected immediately.

She had been genuinely good at what she did.

She had said: Someone with a plan.

She had said it at the substation, looking at the primary feed rerouting, reading the physical signatures of someone else's professional work. She had given him the framework that had turned the Bitterroot problem from an isolated system failure into a pattern. Without that framework he would have fixed the water system and gone home without understanding what he was walking into when he went south.

He would have gone south without understanding it anyway. He was going to go south. But he would have gone without the right questions, which was the same as going blind.

He sat in the dark with the Reader in his lap and felt the weight of a loss that didn't fit the categories he had been using.

He had categories. Two years of collapse had given him categories for loss -- the people before the collapse, the faces he'd seen and then not seen again, the abstract millions that he processed as a fact about the world rather than a collection of individual losses. The categories held. Most of the time the categories held.

Iris didn't fit the categories. She was too specific. Six days was not enough time to build the structure that would process her correctly, and the absence of that structure left the loss without a place to go.

He sat in the dark and let it not have a place to go.

---

Marcus found him at midnight.

He sat down without asking. He had the quality, which Cole had been cataloguing since the fuel depot, of someone who read situations correctly and responded to what they actually were rather than what they appeared to be.

He was quiet for a while.

Then he said: She knew the grid better than anyone I've ever met.

Yes, Cole said.

We should write it down. What she knew. The substation analysis, the feed rerouting technique, the load profile reading. Before it's gone.

Cole looked at the Reader.

Yes, he said.

He opened the Reader's document function and started writing. Not the emotional content -- he was not ready to write the emotional content and he was not sure he would ever be ready to write it in a document. He wrote the technical content: the substation analysis, the terms Iris had used, the framework she had given him. Emergency grid segmentation. The weathering timestamps. The load profile diagnostics.

He wrote it all down with the precision she had used to deliver it.

When he finished it was past one in the morning. Marcus had fallen asleep against the barn wall, which was characteristic -- Marcus could fall asleep anywhere, which Cole had concluded was either a genuine talent or a form of practiced trust in whoever was watching.

Cole saved the document.

He sat in the dark for a long time after, with the Reader's screen the only light.

He thought about what she had said at the substation, which he kept coming back to: someone with a plan. He thought about the plan itself -- the fourteen systems, the credential, the installation that Eddie had helped build. He thought about the note on the printout: More thorough than we expected.

He thought about Iris expecting the maintenance on the substation to be good work, and it being good work, and what she had said about that: whoever did this understood power systems at a professional level.

He thought about what it meant that he was going to go south tomorrow and have a conversation with whoever that was.

Nora found him before dawn.

She sat down beside him. She didn't say anything consoling, which was consistent with her, and which he appreciated more than he had expected to appreciate it.

She said: How is Iris's death going to change what you do tomorrow?

He thought about it honestly.

It isn't, he said.

That's not true.

No, he said. It isn't true.

She waited.

She found the framework I needed, he said. The substation analysis -- without her I had the digital picture and I was missing the physical one. She put them together and gave me a way of understanding what the southern installation is doing that's specific enough to be useful. He paused. I'm going to use it. I'm going to go into that conversation better prepared because she spent two hours at a fence line reading equipment the way she had been reading equipment for thirty years.

She would have found that satisfying, Nora said.

Yes, Cole said. I think she would have.

They sat in the early dark for a while. The valley outside was beginning to lighten, the sky moving from black toward the deep blue that preceded dawn.

Go, Nora said. Come back.

Cole looked at her.

Thank you, he said.

Don't thank me, she said. Come back. That's how you thank me.

He stood.

He looked at the barn -- at the group still sleeping, at the medical supplies organized on their designated shelf, at the space where Iris had been sleeping for three nights and was not now.

He went to find Marcus and Eddie.

---

He told the group in the morning.

He told them the way he told them everything that mattered -- directly, without softening, with enough information for them to understand what had happened. Iris Cho had died. The cause was cardiac. Nora had been with her. She had not been in pain.

He watched the group receive this.

Iris had been with them for six days. Six days was long enough for most people to have a specific memory of her -- something she had said, something she had done, the quality of a person who had contributed to a situation they were all in together. Elena, the teacher, had talked with her for an hour about the pre-collapse curriculum she had developed. Robert, the veterinarian, had sat with her at the barn entry one evening trading technical vocabulary across their two domains. Rosa had asked her about the substation and Iris had explained it with the same complete precision she gave everything.

He watched these memories land differently in each person.

After, Nora came to him.

She had been awake since three in the morning managing the last hours and the aftermath. She had the quality of someone who had done this before and who knew how to be functional during and after and who also knew the cost of the functional.

She said: I need an hour.

Take it, he said.

She went to the back of the barn and he managed the morning without her.

He managed it by doing the things that needed doing: the watch change, the water filtering, the morning Reader queries. He organized the group's preparations for the day's movement with the efficiency of someone who had learned to let work be the treatment for the kind of thinking that needed a treatment.

At the one-hour mark, Nora came back.

She looked at what had been done and what was being done and said: Good.

He said: What did she need?

Nora looked at him.

She needed someone to be with her, Nora said. That's all. She didn't need anything fixed. She knew she was managing a situation that wasn't fixable. She needed someone to be there.

Cole looked at the barn.

I should have been there, he said.

You didn't know her well enough yet, Nora said. That's not blame. That's just the shape of it. She came with us six days ago. You didn't have the kind of relationship that means your presence specifically was what she needed. I did, from the medical relationship. He paused. You'll have it with other people. You're building it now.

Cole thought about Elena and Robert and the names he now had.

He thought about six days and what could be built in six days and what couldn't.

He thought about the notebook he had opened on the Reader -- the document where Iris's technical knowledge was now written down, preserved in the vocabulary she had spent thirty years developing.

He stood in the barn for a moment.

Then he went to find Marcus, because Marcus needed to know the plan for the day, and because moving was the thing they had left to do.

---

He held the watch that night himself.

The group was asleep, or trying to sleep, in the unsettled way of a group that had lost someone that day. The grief had specific textures at this scale -- not the communal grief of a long-known person, not the personal grief of a family, but something in between: the loss of someone who had been present and useful and whose presence and usefulness had been real even if the relationship had been brief.

Elena had cried, quietly, in a corner of the barn.

Robert had gone outside and stood in the dark for a long time.

Rosa had written something in her notebook that she had not shown to anyone.

He sat at the entry and thought about what Iris had been in six days.

She had been a professional -- one of the kind he recognized and respected, the ones who were genuinely interested in the problem rather than their performance on it. She had been a person who asked good questions and let him think through the answers. She had been someone who told him things he needed to know in the correct order and did not oversimplify them.

She had been, briefly, someone he had been learning from.

He thought about the document on the Reader. Everything she had told him about the substation, written down in the vocabulary she had used to explain it. The load profile diagnostics, the feed rerouting technique, the emergency grid segmentation method.

He thought about what it meant to write down what someone knew before the knowledge disappeared.

He thought about what else had disappeared in two years that had not been written down.

He thought about all the Irises.

The people who had known things that had not been written down, who had been maintaining expertise in specific domains that the world needed and that had died with them or dispersed when the communities dispersed. The institutional knowledge that had lived in people rather than systems, that had depended on the people being present and the communities being intact.

He sat with this.

The Reader gave him access to systems. It did not give him access to the knowledge of the people who had built and maintained those systems. The credential set was one kind of access. What Iris had was a different kind, and it was not recoverable from a database.

He looked at the document he had written.

It was incomplete. He knew it was incomplete. He had written what he could remember of what she had said, which was most of it, but there were gaps -- technical vocabulary he had not written down accurately, implications she had drawn that he had understood at the time and was less certain of now.

He thought about what a world that preserved this kind of knowledge looked like.

He thought about the installation to the south, built by someone who had understood that the collapse would cost the world knowledge, and had tried to preserve some of it.

He sat in the barn entry and watched the dark valley and thought about what was worth preserving and how.

---

He dug the grave in the morning.

Not alone. Marcus helped, and Robert, and two others. The ground east of the grain barn was harder than it would have been in summer -- October had been coming down toward freeze and the upper layer had the specific resistance of soil that was beginning its winter hardening.

They worked in the morning cold in near silence.

He thought about the work of it. The physical fact of digging a grave in hard Montana ground was different from the abstracted idea of a death. It required something from the body and the attention that kept the event from being only conceptual.

He was glad for the work.

Elena said words over the grave. She did it in the way she did most things -- thoughtfully, specifically, with attention to the concrete rather than the general. She talked about what Iris had been in six days. She talked about the substation and the two hours she had spent explaining grid architecture to anyone who would listen. She talked about a specific thing Iris had said at dinner two nights ago about the way Montana hills held the light at the end of the day, which Iris had found beautiful and had said so clearly.

Cole had not known about the conversation at dinner.

He thought about the things he had not known about Iris in six days and thought about the things he would not know now.

After, Rosa came and stood beside him.

She said: She told me about the substation. What the modifications meant. What someone had to know to make them.

I know, Cole said.

She said the person who did it was the same kind of person as you, Rosa said. Someone who understood the systems from the inside and built something from that understanding.

He looked at Rosa.

She said that?

She said: the technical quality tells you something about the person. Someone who does good technical work under pressure, with limited resources, for a purpose they believe in -- that's a specific kind of person. She paused. She said she thought you were going to be surprised by what you found when you went south. Not negatively. Surprised because it was going to be harder than a simple bad person.

Cole stood at the grave.

She was right, he said.

Rosa nodded.

She usually was, she said.

He looked at the grave for a moment longer.

Then he went to help Marcus with the morning's preparations.

---

He was awake at four in the morning with the barn around him and twenty-three people asleep and the Reader in his lap running the Bitterroot query for the last time before the move south.

The system was nominal. The northern communities' pressure readings were stable. The valley was a different place than it had been forty-eight hours ago.

He thought about Iris.

He had been thinking about her in pieces, the way you thought about recent losses before the full weight of them arrived. The pieces were specific: the tilted-head attention at the substation. The wrench in her hand at the roadside at three in the morning. The walk back from the facility, the raven working the field, her voice saying it's good work, technically -- not qualified, not hedged. Just: it's good work. Recognizing it as good work. Saying so.

He had written down what she knew. He had saved the document. He would use it.

That was not the same as her being there to tell him what he had gotten wrong.

He sat in the dark and let himself feel the weight of that for a few minutes.

Then he put it in the category of things that were real and that he was carrying and that he was going to carry and that were not incompatible with moving forward.

He ran the Reader one more time.

Nominal.

He put it in his pocket and went to wake Marcus.

---

The barn smelled like the barn had smelled for four nights now -- the base smell of old hay and diesel exhaust from the trucks, and on top of that the accumulated smell of twenty-three people and their cooking and their sleep.

He had been aware of the smell since the first night and had filed it as: this is what the group smells like. The way you filed the smell of a ranch or a kitchen or an office -- the ambient environment that told you where you were without having to think about it.

This was where he was.

He sat in the dark with the Reader and thought about that.
