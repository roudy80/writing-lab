# Chapter 26

## South

They drove south in the early morning.

Cole and Marcus and Eddie. The valley waking around them, the October light coming at a low angle that made the mountains west look closer than they were, the frost on the fields catching it and returning it as a pale glitter that would be gone by midmorning.

The water was running. He knew this from the Reader's status display and he knew it from the communities they drove through -- the quality of places that had gotten something back. A woman filling a bucket at a roadside tap, unhurried. Two men at a pipe connection, standing close, talking quietly in the way of people who have been through something together and are on the other side of it.

He drove through these scenes and filed them.

The southern end of the valley opened into broader terrain as they descended from the mountain section. The valley widened, the hills pulling back, the agricultural land extending in longer reaches. Better land, historically -- more water rights, the kind of property that commanded premiums before the collapse and would command them again afterward if there was an afterward worth having.

The installation appeared as they came around a long curve.

He had been building an image of it from Eddie's description and the Reader's infrastructure map and Iris's physical analysis of the substation, and the image he had built was approximately right in terms of function and completely wrong in terms of scale. He had pictured a compound. A facility. Something defensive.

What he saw was organized.

Six buildings, connected by covered walkways. Solar panels on every south-facing roof surface, tilted at the correct angle for this latitude, maintained -- not the ad-hoc installations he had seen across two years of surviving, but systems that had been planned and sized and installed with attention to the long-term. Wind turbines on the ridge behind, three of them, turning in the morning air. A water tower, not the original agricultural tank but a new one, sized for the installation's capacity.

And people. A community. Two hundred at minimum, probably more. Moving with the organized efficiency of people who had been running this operation for a long time and had developed the rhythms of shared work. Not frantic. Not desperate. Working.

Marcus had stopped his motorcycle beside the truck.

That's not a prepper compound, he said.

No, Cole said.

That's a community that's been running for two years.

Yes.

They have more people than the northern communities combined.

Cole looked at the installation. At the scale of the solar installation, sized for the current population and room to grow. At the water tower, holding treated water for distribution. At the covered walkways between buildings -- a detail that spoke of planning for weather, for long-term habitation, for the kind of investment you only made if you believed you were going to be here for a long time.

They were planning for this before it happened, Marcus said.

He paused.

Cole. How long before?

Cole thought about the construction timeline Eddie had described. The contract work that had taken eighteen months. The infrastructure modifications that had begun eight months after the collapse, once the original installation was running and needed to be integrated with the valley's systems.

The planning, he said, was happening before the collapse. Maybe years before. The implementation started immediately after.

They knew, Marcus said.

They believed it was coming, Cole said. There's a difference. And they prepared.

He drove toward the gate.

---

The gate was open. A woman was standing at it.

Late forties, early fifties, with the look of someone who had spent two years working outdoors and had adapted completely. She watched Cole's truck approach with the attention of someone who had been expecting this, or something close to this.

Cole got out.

She looked at him, then at Marcus on the motorcycle, then at Eddie in the passenger seat. When she saw Eddie, something moved across her face -- complex, brief, an emotion that resolved into something neutral before it finished.

You built the water system, Cole said.

Parts of it, she said. I had help.

She looked at Eddie.

You did good work, she said to him.

Eddie got out of the truck. He stood in the road and looked at her.

I didn't know what it was for, he said.

She held his gaze.

I know, she said. I'm sorry for that.

A long silence. The morning wind moved through the installation behind her. Someone called out from inside -- a question, work-related -- and she answered over her shoulder without looking away from Eddie.

Then she looked at Cole.

You fixed the Bitterroot system, she said.

We needed that water.

So did Stevensville.

I know. She held his gaze. We made decisions under constraints that were real and that cost more than I anticipated. I'm aware of what they cost. She paused. Come in. I'll tell you everything -- what we're doing, why, what we've learned about what it costs to build something in the middle of everything falling apart. You can decide what to do with it after you hear it.

Cole looked at the open gate. At the installation behind her. At the Reader in his vest pocket.

All right, he said.

He walked through the gate.

---

They stopped at Victor on the way.

Cole had not planned to stop. He had been planning to drive straight to the installation and have the conversation he needed to have. But as they passed through Victor he looked at the community -- the thirty people in the building that had held four hundred, the water now running in their pipes, the man at the road's edge who had told him about the eight people -- and he stopped the truck.

He got out and walked to the man.

The man was named James Kirby. Cole had not known his name the day before.

He said: I'm going to talk to the people who redirected the water. Today. He paused. I wanted you to know that before I go.

James Kirby looked at him.

What are you going to say? he said.

I'm going to ask them to account for what they did, Cole said. And then I'm going to figure out whether what they're building is something the valley can work with or something it needs to work against.

And the accounting,Kirby said. The eight people. How do you hold someone accountable for that in the world we're in?

Cole thought about this honestly.

I don't know yet, he said. I know what the accounting is. I don't know what the accountability looks like. That's part of what I'm going to find out today.

Kirby looked at the road south.

Tell them their names, he said. The eight people. Tell them we know their names and we'd like them to know their names too. He paused. If they don't already.

I'll tell them, Cole said.

He drove south.

---

Marcus rode alongside him for the last stretch.

He had moved the motorcycle up from its usual fifty-yard position -- not breaking the protocol, just adjusting it for the approach, where they were moving toward something rather than away from it.

He said: When this is done. Whatever it is we're going to. What do you want?

Cole looked at the road.

The same thing I wanted at the ranch, he said. Things working. Systems maintained. People with what they need.

But bigger, Marcus said.

Bigger, Cole said.

Marcus was quiet for a moment.

I want to stay, he said. After. I know I said I'd decide. I've decided.

Cole looked at him.

Okay, he said.

Marcus dropped back to fifty yards.

Cole drove south toward the installation and thought about what Nora had said: You'll have it with other people. You're building it now.

He thought about Elena and Robert and Curtis Hall and James Kirby and Eddie and Rosa and Marcus behind him on the motorcycle, and he thought about what it meant to be driving toward a conversation that was going to determine what came next for all of them.

He thought about the Reader in his vest pocket and the fourteen systems and the credential he was about to meet in person.

He thought about what Iris had said at the substation: someone with a plan.

He was driving toward the plan.

He wanted to know if it was the right one.

---

Marcus rode alongside him in the last stretch.

The valley was fully light now, the October sun past the mountain crests to the east, the installation visible ahead in the broader southern terrain. Cole could see the scale of it clearly in daylight -- the solar array, the wind turbines, the organized buildings and covered walkways.

He thought about everything he knew about it.

He thought about Eddie's account: a woman who was precise about specifications, who kept the full picture to herself, who had known why every parameter was what it was.

He thought about Iris's read of the substation: someone who had planned for this before it happened, who had used emergency grid segmentation correctly, who had done the technical work well.

He thought about the credential that had watched him work at the facility and chosen not to interfere.

He thought about the names James Kirby had given him.

He thought about what he was going to say.

He was going to say: I know what you built and why. I know what the optimization layer cost. I know what the maintenance visits have done for the valley's infrastructure. I know that you were waiting for someone to come who would read the full architecture before acting.

He was going to say: I read the full architecture. I fixed the water. I'm here to understand the rest.

He was going to say: I have the names of the people in Victor. I have the accounting. I want to know what your accounting says.

And then he was going to listen.

Marcus said: Last chance to not do this.

Is that what you want? he said.

No. Marcus paused. I just say it. Last chance to not do a thing, before you do the thing. It makes the thing feel chosen rather than fallen into.

Cole thought about the fence line on the ranch. About the morning he had finally gone over it.

It's chosen, he said.

Good, Marcus said.

He dropped back to fifty yards.

Cole drove toward the gate.

---

The installation in October morning light looked like a place where people had been doing serious work for a long time.

That was the word he settled on: serious. Not grave -- the people moving through the covered walkways and around the solar installation and across the open central space were not moving heavily. They were moving with the purposeful ease of people who had developed efficient routines and were executing them. The ease was not carelessness. It was competence.

He thought about what it took to build this.

Not technically -- he knew the technical requirements, had understood them from Eddie's description and Iris's physical analysis and his own remote queries. He was thinking about the human requirements. the quality of leadership that could take two hundred people through the collapse and the first two years after it and produce a community that moved like this.

He thought about Curtis Hall and the forty-one people who had held Stevensville together.

He thought about Harlan Briggs and the forty-one people he had managed until he died.

He thought about the ways that communities held together or didn't.

The woman at the gate was watching him think.

He stopped the truck and got out and walked to her.

She was in her early fifties, wearing work clothes that were actually worked in. She had the quality of someone who had been doing hard physical and cognitive work in parallel for a long time and had adapted to both demands rather than choosing between them.

She looked at him and then at Eddie and then at Marcus.

When she looked at Eddie there was the complex brief thing again -- not guilt exactly, but the quality of someone encountering evidence of a decision they had lived with.

Cole said: You built the water system.

She said it simply. No performance of humility, no defensive qualification. Just: parts of it, I had help.

He thought about that.

---

The valley south of Stevensville was different from the northern valley in ways he was cataloguing as he drove.

Better infrastructure. Not better condition -- better original design. The roads were wider, the drainage structures more sophisticated, the agricultural installations more ambitious. Someone had put more money into the southern valley historically, which meant the southern valley had more to work with when everything stopped.

He thought about what that meant for the people who had survived here versus the people who had survived in the north. Resources were not destiny, but they were odds. The southern valley had started with better odds.

And then someone had moved to the southern valley with a plan.

He thought about Vera Solis without yet knowing her name. About the person who had been managing fourteen systems from an installation that Eddie had built for a company that had funded it with money that had arrived from somewhere before everything stopped.

He thought about the question Eddie had asked: I want to know what I built it for.

He was forty minutes from an answer.

Marcus came on the radio: Installation visible on the ridge. I'm counting six buildings. He paused. There are people. A lot of people.

Cole looked at the ridge.

He had been building toward this for a week. The substation, the facility, the credential, the printout, the open gate. Each piece had pointed here. He was at the here now.

He took the road south.

---

He pulled over once, ten miles north of the installation.

He needed a minute.

Not for the technical preparation -- he had the picture, he had the Reader, he had Eddie and Marcus and every piece of information he had been assembling for a week. He was technically ready.

He needed a minute for the other thing.

He got out and stood at the truck's side and looked at the valley. The mountains west, the October light, the irrigation infrastructure standing in the fields doing its ghost work. The road south running through it toward something he had been approaching for a week.

He thought about what Nora had said. You get along because you both built things you didn't fully understand and are trying to figure out what that means.

He was about to have a conversation with a version of that. With someone who had built more than he had and had understood it better and had still produced a decision that was wrong.

He thought about whether he could do better.

He thought about the ninety minutes at the facility. About reading before acting. About the difference between the information the Reader gave him remotely and the information the physical system gave him when he was present with it.

He thought: be present with it.

He got back in the truck.

---

Eddie was quiet for most of the drive south.

Not withdrawn -- he talked when Cole talked, answered questions, contributed to the navigation. But the quality of his quiet had a different texture than his usual quiet, which was the quiet of someone thinking about a specific thing.

Cole let him be quiet.

Twenty minutes from the installation, Eddie said: I want to ask her something.

What?

Whether the design held up. Whether the infrastructure I built actually performed the way the specifications said it would.

Cole thought about this.

Why does that matter? he said.

Eddie was quiet for a moment.

Because if it failed to perform, then the thing I built was bad in two ways -- the purpose and the execution. He paused. If it performed correctly, then the thing I built was only bad in one way. The purpose. He paused again. I can live with one way.

Cole drove.

It performed, he said. The system has been running continuously for two years under field conditions. Iris analyzed the physical work and said it was good. He paused. Your execution was not the problem.

Eddie looked out the window.

That's all he said about it.
