# Chapter 27

## Signal Loss

Her name was Dr. Vera Solis.

She had been a systems theorist at a university in Colorado -- computational modeling, complex systems, the mathematics of how large interconnected networks fail. Before that, contractor work for infrastructure companies, including three of the companies whose systems Cole had worked with. The credential set had looked like a peer's because she was one, operating in the same domain from a different direction.

She gave him a tour first.

Not a performed tour -- a working one, walking him through the installation the way you walked someone through a system you understood and wanted them to understand. Water treatment building, distribution manifold, the buried lines running north under the valley floor that Eddie had designed and that were doing exactly what they had been designed to do. Power generation: the solar array, the three wind turbines on the ridge, the battery bank in the third building that could carry the installation through four days of zero generation. The community spaces: the common areas, the workshop, the medical facility that had more equipment than most hospitals had had in month three.

Cole walked through it and asked questions and she answered them.

He was looking for the gap between what she said it was and what it was. He didn't find one.

That was the first thing that cost him something.

They sat down in the room she used as an office -- a converted equipment space with a long table and maps on the wall, the kind of room where decisions got made rather than presented. She poured water from a pitcher. The water was cold and clean and came from the treatment building he had just walked through.

He thought about Stevensville. He thought about Curtis Hall and the forty-three people who had been drinking whatever they could get for three weeks while this pitcher sat here, full.

He didn't say that yet.

She told him the technical history first. The modeling work, the probability distributions, the decision to build. He listened without interrupting. She was giving him the version she had prepared, which was accurate and organized and told him everything except the things she hadn't prepared to say.

He let her finish.

Then he said: The water redirection. Walk me through the decision.

She walked him through it the way she walked through everything -- logically, with the appropriate acknowledgments of what the decision had cost. The installation had needed time to reach operational capacity. The water margin had been necessary. She had known the northern communities would be affected. She had run the numbers on the impact. She had made the decision.

He had heard this already, in the credential's behavior and the substation and the note on the printout. He had been building toward this conversation for days.

He said: When did you know it was wrong.

She looked at him.

The question wasn't what she expected. He could see that.

Not whether it was wrong, he said. When. The day you made the decision, did you know it was wrong, or did that come later.

She was quiet for long enough that the quiet was an answer.

I knew the cost, she said. I had modeled the cost. I knew people would be affected and I made the decision anyway. She paused. Whether I knew it was wrong -- He watched her work with the word. I knew the numbers. I told myself the numbers were the decision.

Cole looked at her.

But, he said.

She set down her water glass.

There was a morning in month four, she said. Before I had the full installation operational, before the water margin was secure. I had run the query on the northern communities and seen the drawdown and I sat with it for a long time. She looked at the table. I knew then. Not from the numbers. I just knew. And I made the decision anyway because the alternative was dismantling what I had built, and I had been building toward this for three years.

She looked up at him.

I told myself I would fix it when we were stable. That was the plan I was living inside. And then we were stable and I had been living inside the plan for so long that changing it felt like -- She stopped.

Like losing, Cole said.

She looked at him with an expression he hadn't seen on her face yet.

Yes, she said quietly. Like losing.

A silence. Through the window behind her, the valley was visible -- the northern end of it, the communities with their pipes that had water in them again because Cole had driven here and fixed a system.

Cole thought about the memos he had written before the collapse. The three internal documents about the architectural vulnerability in the distribution platform framework. The gap between acknowledged and acted on. He had written the reports and attended the meetings and accepted the responses and kept doing the work.

He thought about what he would have done with three years of lead time and independent funding and no company oversight and the certainty that the collapse was coming.

He was not certain he would have done differently.

That was the second thing that cost him something.

Eight people, he said. In Stevensville. The water was a contributing factor in at least three of the deaths.

I know their names, she said. She said it flatly, not as performance. The flatness of someone who had been carrying something for a long time and had stopped trying to make it sound like less than it was. James Kirby's wife. An elderly man named Peterson. A child, two years old, from a waterborne illness that presented in month seven.

Cole absorbed this.

I know the names, she said again. That doesn't fix it. I'm not offering it as mitigation. I'm telling you so you understand what I've been doing with it.

What have you been doing with it? he said.

Carrying it, she said. And building something that justifies carrying it. She looked at him. That's not enough. I know it's not enough. The question I've been living with is whether not enough is still better than stopping.

Is it? he said.

She looked at him for a long moment.

I don't know, she said. That's the first honest answer I've given you.

He looked at the table.

No, he said. It's the second.

She held his gaze. Something shifted in her face -- not relief, something more complicated. The adjustment of a person who has been holding a weight in a specific way and has just been asked to hold it differently.

He was not ready to tell her whether what she had built justified what it had cost. He was not certain he had the standing to make that determination. He had built the architecture she had used. He had filed the reports that hadn't been acted on. He had maintained his property for two years while the gap between acknowledged and acted on ran its course.

What comes next, he said.

The infrastructure I've built is larger than this installation, she said. Fourteen systems across southwestern Montana, most of them running on my credential set alone. I've been managing them without backup for two years. If something happens to me, they go unmanaged.

She looked at him directly.

I need someone who understands what these systems are doing and why. Someone who can verify that I'm doing what I say I'm doing, challenge the decisions I'm making. She paused. And someone who won't let me make the same mistake twice. She held his gaze. I don't think you'll let me.

Cole thought about the ranch. About forty-one people on the south fence. About the fence line policy and the insulin and the twenty-three people who had driven south with him.

I'll need to think about it, he said.

I know, she said. Take time.

He stood.

One more thing, he said. Eddie Garza wanted you to know what he was wrong about. He built part of this without understanding what it was for. He wants to know what the something important was -- the thing you were wrong about. He said to tell you directly.

She looked at the window for a moment.

Tell him: I was wrong about whether the numbers were enough, she said. I had the right numbers and the wrong understanding of what they were measuring. The numbers measured outcomes. They didn't measure what it cost to make people into variables in someone else's model.

Cole nodded.

He walked out through the installation. Past the water treatment building. Past the distribution manifold. Past the covered walkways full of people going about the business of a community that worked.

He got in the truck.

He sat there for a moment before starting it.

The installation was real. The work was real. The eight names were real. All of it was real simultaneously, without resolving into something simpler.

He had come here expecting a problem he could categorize. He had found something he was going to have to carry instead, the way she was carrying it, the way Eddie was carrying it -- not as a resolved thing but as a fact about what the world required from people who built things in it.

He started the truck and drove north.

Marcus kept pace on the motorcycle, not pushing. Eddie rode with his elbow on the windowsill looking at the valley. Neither of them spoke.

This was the right response and Cole noted it without filing it. Some things didn't need filing.

The valley in late afternoon had the quality October light got at this elevation -- low, gold, the shadows long and the edges sharp. The communities they passed through had a different look than they had had four days ago. Not restored, not fixed. Just different. Water did something to a place that was hard to name precisely. It meant the place was still in the category of places that could continue.

---

Marcus on the radio: You've got that look.

What look.

The one where you've already decided something and you're waiting for the rest of your head to catch up.

Cole drove.

I haven't decided, he said.

Okay, Marcus said. But you're going to.

He wasn't wrong.

---

Nora was in the barn when he came in. She looked at him the way she looked at things she was reading rather than just seeing.

He sat down and told her what had happened.

She listened without interrupting. When he finished she was quiet for a moment.

Are you going back? she said.

He looked at the Reader in his hand.

Yes, he said.

She looked at him.

Because of the infrastructure, she said.

Because of the infrastructure, he said. And because she's managing fourteen systems that affect everyone in this region and she needs someone who can verify what she's doing and challenge the decisions she's making. He paused. And because there are northern communities that have been excluded from resources they needed and there's an installation with the capacity to change that, and someone needs to be in the conversation about how that happens. He stopped.

And? Nora said.

He thought about what she had said: not the other way. Not like it's tactical.

And because I'm not going back to the ranch, he said. I've been thinking about going back since we left. But the ranch was -- He looked for the word. The ranch was a waiting position. I was waiting for the situation that would make staying out of things impossible. The situation arrived. He looked at her. I don't want to go back to waiting.

She held his gaze for a long moment.

Okay, she said.

That's all?

You've already decided, she said. I'm not going to argue with a decision that's right.

She looked at the medical supplies on their shelf. At the group settled around the barn. At the valley through the open door.

What does staying look like? she said.

I don't know yet, Cole said. I know what needs doing. I don't know the full shape of the thing it adds up to.

She nodded.

That's honest, she said.

He picked up the Reader and ran the routine query. The Bitterroot system, clean. The valley distribution, normalized. The northern communities' pressure readings, stable and consistent.

He stood up and looked through the barn's open door at the valley.

The Bitterroot Valley in October, late afternoon, the mountains to the west going gold-orange with the dropping sun. The valley floor with its water running in the pipes again, the communities with their lights, the grain barn and the twenty-three people and the grain elevator visible in Stevensville's center, still standing.

Two years ago he had been here for three weeks building something he had thought was a retrofit project. He had done good work. He had driven away and not thought about it much after.

He was not driving away this time.

Rosa came and sat on a nearby crate.

She had her notebook. She looked at the valley through the barn's open door for a moment.

Where are we going next? she said.

He looked at the valley. At the Reader. At the note in his vest pocket.

South, he said.

Back to the installation?

Back to the installation.

She nodded like this was acceptable and went back to her notebook.

The Reader pinged.

A new system, forty miles south. Clean status. Sophisticated parameter set. Post-collapse timestamp. In the system's public-facing log field, a message that had been placed there deliberately:

WE SAW WHAT YOU DID IN THE VALLEY. WE SHOULD TALK.

He read it twice.

It was not from Vera Solis. The credential was different -- a new one, from a different framework, originating from a different direction entirely. Someone else was out there. Someone who had been watching the valley's infrastructure situation and had seen what he did and wanted to make contact.

He looked south.

Marcus appeared in the barn doorway.

You've got that look again, he said.

Cole put the Reader in his vest pocket.

Get some sleep, he said. We move in the morning.

Marcus looked at him.

The good kind of problem or the bad kind? he said.

Cole thought about it.

I don't know yet, he said.

Marcus considered this.

I'll get some sleep, he said.

He went to find his corner of the barn. Cole stood in the doorway for a moment, looking south at the valley, at the mountains, at the dark that was coming down from the west.

Nora appeared beside him.

She didn't ask about the ping. She looked at whatever he was looking at.

Two problems again, she said.

You didn't go looking for either of them.

No.

You're going to be all right with that, she said. Eventually.

He looked at the valley.

I'm already all right with it, he said.

She stood with him for a moment in the doorway.

Then she went back to the medical supplies and he stood alone in the door, and the Reader was in his vest pocket, and the valley was below him, and whatever was forty miles south that wanted to talk had been patient enough to send a message rather than show up uninvited.

That was, he thought, a reasonable first impression.

He went inside.

---

The conversation with Vera Solis lasted four hours.

Not four hours of confrontation -- four hours of the kind of conversation that two people had when they were both carrying complete pictures that the other person needed, and they were deciding together how much of each picture to share.

She showed him the models.

The collapse models -- the ones she had run three years before the collapse, the ones that had shown the failure trajectory and the probability distributions and the range of outcomes. She had printed them, the printouts weathered now with two years of storage, and she spread them on the table between them and walked him through them the way she walked him through everything: precisely, in the correct order, with the patience of someone who had been explaining complex systems to people for thirty years.

He read the models.

They were correct. Not correct in the sense of exactly predicting what happened -- the pathways of the collapse had their own contingent factors, the timing had been affected by variables her models had as ranges rather than points. But the fundamental trajectory was there, the accumulation of fragility that had been building for years before the acute failure, the interaction effects that had turned a manageable crisis into a cascade.

He recognized the architecture of the failure. He had been working in it.

She watched him read.

When he finished she said: You saw some of this.

I saw pieces of it, he said. I filed reports. They were acknowledged.

I filed reports too, she said. For two years before I decided that filing reports was insufficient.

And then you started building, he said.

And then I started building.

The silence between them had the quality of two people who had tried the legitimate channel and had come out of it with different conclusions about what came next.

He thought about the reports. About the memos. About the gap between acknowledged and acted on.

He thought about what he had done with that gap: he had bought a ranch in Montana and maintained it and stayed on his side of the fence line.

She had built fourteen infrastructure systems and redirected a valley's water supply.

They had both been right that filing reports was insufficient.

They had made different decisions about what insufficient required.

He told her about the eight people in Victor. He told her their names, the ones he had gotten from James Kirby. He told her that Victor wanted her to know their names.

She was quiet for a long time.

Then she opened a notebook. She wrote the names down. She wrote them in the notebook next to other names -- more names, two pages of names, in the small precise handwriting of someone who had been keeping an accounting.

I know their names, she said. Most of them. I've been tracking the northern communities since month one. I know what happened and I know my decisions contributed.

And? Cole said.

And I made the decisions anyway, she said. Because I ran the math. Because the numbers told me that more people would survive if the installation achieved operational viability in the first year. She looked at the notebook. The math was right and the math was insufficient.

Insufficient, Cole said.

The math doesn't tell you what it costs the people in the math to be in the math, she said. It tells you outcomes. It doesn't tell you what the outcomes feel like from inside them.

He thought about Nora's framing: the analytic frame and what it left out.

He thought about the eight people in Victor and about Iris and about Rosa Garza's insulin and about what it felt like to be inside a situation you couldn't fully understand from the outside.

He looked at Vera Solis.

I'll stay, he said.

Not unconditionally, he said. I'll verify your work. I'll challenge your decisions. I'll tell you when the math is insufficient. And I'll need access to the full architecture of everything you're running.

She looked at him for a long moment.

Yes, she said.

He thought about the ranch. About the fence line and the four months he had spent on his side of it. About Briggs giving Nora his supply inventory.

The ranch, he said. There are seventeen people still there. Purcell has them now. They're going to need options.

We can accommodate, she said.

He looked at the notebook with the names in it.

Tell me what you're building, he said. Everything. Not the summary. The actual thing.

She told him.

It took another two hours.

---

The drive north was quiet.

Marcus on the motorcycle at his standard fifty yards, Eddie looking at the valley with an expression Cole could not fully read -- not simple, not resolved, the expression of someone processing something that did not resolve cleanly.

Cole drove and thought about what Vera Solis had told him.

The full story had taken six hours. Not because she was slow or evasive -- she was neither -- but because the full story was large. The model development. The building phase. the decisions made under real constraints. The optimization layer -- she had explained it in technical language first and then in moral language and both explanations had been complete and both had been true and neither had made the decision acceptable.

But she had known the names.

That mattered to him. It was not sufficient. It was not nothing.

He thought about what the valley looked like now, from where he was in it.

Twenty-three people he had brought from the ranch, plus the seventeen still there who would need options. The northern communities -- Stevensville and Victor and the others, several hundred people who had been managing impossible conditions with limited resources and who now had water and who were cautiously beginning to understand that something had changed.

Purcell and his forty people, integrating with varying degrees of genuine participation.

Curtis Hall and James Kirby and Elena and Robert and the dozens of others whose names he had been gathering.

And now, two hundred and some people at the southern installation with infrastructure capacity the valley needed and with the debt of eight months of wrong decisions that had to be part of how the future was built.

He thought about what building the future looked like from here.

It looked like: stay. Verify Vera Solis's decisions. Challenge the ones that were wrong. Build relationships with the northern communities that were substantive enough to withstand Purcell's organizational skill. Help Rosa Garza understand the Reader when there was time. Keep doing the work that needed doing.

It looked like: give up the ranch as an endpoint.

He had already given it up. He had given it up when he crossed the fence line three weeks ago. He just hadn't named it yet.

He named it now, on the drive north, watching the valley pass.

He felt, not relief exactly, but the settling of something that had been held in uncertain position for a long time.

He checked it while driving. A new system, forty miles south of the installation. Different credential from Vera Solis's. Different architecture. Post-collapse timestamp, sophisticated parameter set, and in the public-facing log field, a message:

Then he looked at the road ahead, and the road north, and the valley around him, and thought about what the message meant -- that someone else was out there, watching, and had decided what he had done was worth making contact over.

Someone who had seen him fix the Bitterroot system and decide to stay.

Someone who was building something and wanted to talk to someone else who was building something.

He put the Reader away.

He drove north toward the grain barn where twenty-three people were waiting.

He thought: this is the work. Not the fix. Not the confrontation with Vera Solis. Not the management of Purcell. The work is this -- staying in it, being present, doing the next thing that needs doing and the thing after that.

He thought about the ranch. About the fence line. About the morning two years ago when he had bought forty acres in central Montana and told himself that the distance was the point.

He thought about what Nora had said: You're already all right with it.

Yes, he said, to no one in particular.

---

The valley at the end of the signal loss chapter was the valley he had been driving toward for a week. He had arrived. The water was running. The conversation had been had.

He looked at the Reader one final time before he put it away.

Fourteen systems, nominal. Bitterroot, nominal. Northern communities, stable. The new signal, forty miles south, waiting.

He put the Reader in his vest pocket.

He had arrived at the thing he had driven toward. The thing he had driven toward was the beginning of the next thing.

This, he thought, was how it worked.

---

He sat with Marcus at the barn door the night before they went south.

Not for any particular reason. The watch was Marcus's, he didn't need to be there. He was there because the night was clear and the valley was visible and he had been inside his own head for three days and Marcus was someone who was good at not requiring anything from the people around him.

They sat for a while in silence.

Marcus said: Are you sure about staying?

Cole thought about the question.

No, he said.

But you're staying anyway.

Why?

He thought about how to answer this in a way that was actually an answer.

Because the ranch was a position, he said. And positions have a purpose. The purpose of the ranch was to be somewhere safe while I figured out what came next. He paused. I figured out what came next.

Marcus looked at the valley.

What if it doesn't work out? he said. The valley. The infrastructure thing. Vera Solis.

Then I'll have something to fix, Cole said.

Marcus was quiet for a moment.

You're pretty okay with things not working out, he said.

Two years of practice, Cole said.

Marcus smiled. It was the first time Cole had seen him smile -- not the operational ease he wore as a default expression, but an actual smile, brief and genuine.

Yeah, Marcus said. Same.

They sat for another hour. The valley was dark and the stars were very bright and somewhere in the northern communities the water was running clean through pipes that had been dry for three weeks.

Cole thought: this is worth being here for.

He went inside and went to sleep.
