# Chapter 7

## Marcus

The motorcycle appeared in the rearview mirror about an hour south of the ranch.

He was two hours south of the ranch when it appeared, which meant it had either been following them since departure and had maintained perfect pace and distance, or it had joined the route at a point he hadn't identified. Both possibilities were worth understanding. The first required knowledge of their departure time and route; the second required knowing which road they would take and getting ahead of them.

The third possibility -- random traveler going the same direction -- he filed as possible but unlikely. Random travelers in this environment did not maintain fifty yards of following distance for fifteen minutes. They either caught up or gave way or stopped.

Cole clocked it and didn't say anything. He filed it: single rider, pace matching the truck's, not gaining and not falling back. Keeping the gap at roughly fifty yards as though fifty yards were a considered distance rather than a coincidence.

He watched it in the rearview for fifteen minutes while the road unspooled through October rangeland -- the grass already winter-yellow, the sky a flat pale blue that would mean cold tonight. Nora was reading the medical inventory in the passenger seat with the focused attention she gave everything that mattered. Behind them, in the truck bed, twenty-three people were arranged among their packs and gear in configurations that were approximately comfortable.

He pulled over at a wide spot where the road shoulders widened against a gravel pit.

The motorcycle caught up and stopped twenty feet back. The rider sat on it for a moment with the engine running, which was information -- a person with hostile intentions would not have stopped twenty feet back and sat. Then the engine cut and the rider got off.

Young. Nineteen, twenty, the outer edge of that range. He moved with the ease of someone who had been on a motorcycle long enough to wear it as a second skin, the way experienced riders had a specific kind of comfort with their own bodies that came from thousands of hours of balance work. Canvas jacket, weathered. Boots that fit well. Dark hair that needed cutting but had been cut at some point in the recent past rather than just grown out.

A face that managed to look simultaneously alert and at rest. Cole had seen this quality before, in people who had been in difficult situations long enough to be comfortable in them.

Cole got out.

He stood at the front of the truck and looked at the kid and the kid looked back at him.

You've been behind us since the Dillon junction, Cole said.

I know, the kid said. I was trying to decide if I wanted to catch up or not.

What decided you?

He shrugged. The gesture was loose and genuine rather than affected. You went south. South is the right direction.

Who are you? Cole said.

Marcus. I was with the Briggs camp for a while. I left before you did. He looked at the truck, at Nora visible through the windshield, at the people in the truck bed who were watching this interaction with varying degrees of attention. You're going to the valley.

I haven't said where I'm going.

You don't have to. He said it without attitude -- just a fact, the way someone stated something they were confident about. I know this road. I know the fuel situation between here and the valley. I also know that what you have in the truck is enough to get twelve people there and you have twenty-three.

Cole looked at him. Assessed.

The kid was either exactly what he said he was or something else. Both possibilities were manageable. A resource or a liability -- he didn't have enough information yet to determine which, but he had enough to determine that the kid was observant, direct, and had chosen to approach rather than continue following, which suggested he wanted something he thought was obtainable through legitimate means.

He thought about what exactly what he said he was would look like: a nineteen-year-old who had been traveling alone or in loose groups for two years and had developed a specific skill at locating resources. This was not improbable. The collapse had produced people adapted to it in specific ways, and the adaptation depended on what the person's attention was oriented toward. Some people had adapted to social dynamics, learning to read groups and navigate them. Some had adapted to threat assessment. Marcus appeared to have adapted to something more logistical -- the landscape as a resource map, movement as optimization.

What he was, Cole thought, was someone who had figured out how to survive by being useful to people who needed what he could find. And then had figured out, at some point, that being useful was not enough -- that you needed to be attached to the right group to turn usefulness into something durable.

He was trying to attach to the right group.

Cole was not certain his group was the right group. He was certain enough to let Marcus stay fifty yards back and see.

How do you know about the fuel situation? Cole said.

Marcus looked at the road south. The pause before his answer was a real pause -- the kind that came from someone thinking about how to explain something they hadn't fully explained before.

I just know where things are, he said. I don't have a good explanation for it. I've been useful to a lot of groups because of it and asked to leave a few groups because of it too, depending on how much they liked having someone around who knew things they didn't.

Cole filed this. He would come back to it.

Stay back fifty yards, Cole said. Don't approach the truck without telling me first. Useful information shared immediately. Information you judge not useful kept to yourself -- if you're wrong about what's useful, that's a problem.

Marcus nodded. The nod of someone who had heard similar terms before and found them reasonable.

What are you useful at besides finding fuel? Cole said.

Scouting. Running ahead, coming back. I'm fast. He paused. I'm also good at not being seen when I don't want to be seen. Most people telegraph where they're looking. I don't go where people are looking.

You're good at a lot of things.

I'm nineteen, Marcus said. I've had two years to practice. He said it without self-pity or performance. Just: that's the situation. The situation produced these skills. Here we are.

Cole got back in the truck. He watched in the rearview as Marcus returned to the motorcycle, started it, and fell into position exactly fifty yards back.

Nora put down the inventory.

Who is he? she said.

Someone who was following us and decided to stop following us and start following us more officially, Cole said.

She looked in the side mirror.

Is that a problem?

Not yet. He knew about the fuel depot before we did. I want to know how.

She looked at the mirror for another moment.

He's young, she said.

Yes.

Young people who've been alone for two years are either very capable or very damaged, she said. Sometimes both.

Cole looked at the rearview. Fifty yards back, exactly.

We'll find out, he said.

---

They stopped at the fuel cache at a Forest Service road turnoff four hours south.

Marcus had told him about it over the radio during the third hour -- not with urgency, with the matter-of-fact quality of someone reporting information they expected to be useful. A warehouse distribution depot that the road didn't pass but was visible from a slight elevation. Cole had run the Reader. Nothing in his credential set, which meant nothing he could query remotely. But Marcus's description had the quality of accurate information.

The depot was exactly where Marcus said. A commercial fueling station for agricultural equipment, the above-ground tanks weathered and accessed before, but with fuel still in two of the three tanks. Cole ran the numbers. Enough to fill the truck's reserve cans and make the valley with margin.

He organized the fueling. The process took forty minutes -- the hand pump from the truck, the transfer cans, the filter Cole always used for salvage fuel. Marcus helped without being asked, working the pump in steady strokes while Cole managed the transfer. He had the quality of someone who had done physical work long enough that it didn't require thought, which freed attention for other things.

While they worked, Cole asked: Have you been through this road before?

Twice, Marcus said. Once going north, once going south.

When?

Eight months ago going north. Three months ago going south.

And the depot was here both times.

Yes. Different tanks each time -- the first time I used the east tank, it was almost full. Three months ago it was down to about what's in it now. He nodded at the tank. Something restocked it between my first and second pass.

Cole looked at the tank. At the Reader on the truck's dash, showing nothing for this location.

You noticed that, Cole said. That it had been restocked.

I notice most things, Marcus said.

Cole filed it.

Nora was standing by the truck watching the road. She had the focused alertness of someone running a continuous background assessment, the same quality Cole had developed and that he recognized in her as a different version of the same thing -- hers oriented toward people and medical states, his toward systems and failure modes.

He knew it was here, she said quietly to Cole when Marcus moved away to check his motorcycle's fuel.

How?

Cole looked at the depot. At the tank levels. At the route from the main road -- the slight elevation, the visibility, the access track that was just wide enough for a delivery vehicle.

I have a theory, he said. I want to talk to him about it tonight.

She looked at Marcus.

He's going to be useful, she said. Not a question.

Probably, Cole said. I want to know the mechanism before I decide how useful.

That's very you, she said.

He looked at her.

No, she said. It's just very you.

She went back to the truck. Cole loaded the last transfer can and watched Marcus strap down his own small fuel reserve with the efficiency of someone who had been traveling alone for long enough to have a system for everything.

He was nineteen and he had a system for everything.

Cole thought about what nineteen meant in this context. Not in the pre-collapse context -- in the context of a person who had been seventeen when the collapse started, who had been making adult decisions since then, who had been traveling alone or in groups for two years and had developed systems for everything because systems were how you survived when the people who would otherwise have been teaching you things were gone or had other priorities.

The systems were good. Cole could evaluate that. The motorcycle was properly maintained. The fuel management was professional. The disappearing skill Marcus had mentioned was real -- he had already done it once since the fuel stop, sliding out of the group's social geometry for a few minutes and reappearing without anyone appearing to have noticed he'd been absent.

He was not seventeen anymore in any meaningful sense.

Cole got back in the truck and they drove south into the afternoon.

---

The first night, Marcus told him about the groups he had left.

Not volunteering it -- Cole had asked, the direct way he asked things that he needed to understand about the people he was responsible for. Marcus told him in the economy of someone who had described a thing enough times to have the description reduced to its essential elements.

The first group: he had been with them for three months, had found resources for them, had left when one of the group's senior members had decided his resource-finding was either supernatural or deceptive and had advocated loudly for asking him to leave. The group had voted. Narrow margin. He had left.

The second group: six months. He had been useful to them and they had been decent to him and then a situation had arisen where the group had needed to make a decision that affected him and they had made the decision without him and the decision had been wrong and they had apologized but the apology had not changed what the decision had been. He had left.

The third group: the briefest. Two weeks. He had identified a resource that would have benefited the group significantly, told the group about it, and the group's leader had taken the resource without acknowledging where the information came from. He had left.

The fourth group: the Briggs camp. Six weeks. He had left before Cole's group left, for reasons he did not specify.

Cole listened to all four without filling the silences.

What are you looking for? he said. In a group.

Marcus thought about it.

Someone who uses information correctly, he said. Not efficiently -- correctly. Someone who takes information that's useful and uses it for the thing it's useful for, not for the thing that's convenient.

Cole looked at the road ahead.

That's a high bar, he said.

I know, Marcus said. It's why I've been with four groups in two years.

Cole drove.

He thought about the distinction Marcus had drawn: efficient versus correct. He thought about the groups Marcus had left and the failures in each case. He thought about what kind of group used information correctly.

He thought about whether his group was that kind.

He did not have the answer yet. He was aware that not having the answer was the correct epistemic state.

---

They drove through the afternoon and the road south of Dillon opened into the broad ranch country of the lower valley, the mountains to the west going blue in the middle distance, the October grass standing dry and gold on the flats. Marcus rode fifty yards ahead, exactly, and Cole watched him in the rearview with the attention he gave unfamiliar systems in their first hours of operation.

He was working well. The fuel depot, the correct assessment of Purcell's followers, the accuracy of his distance estimate on the motorcycle -- all of it was running at the level of someone who had been doing this for two years and had arrived at competence through the specific pressure of consequence. You either got good at this or you didn't survive to be here. Marcus had gotten good.

Nora was reading the topo map, which she had been doing for the last hour. She had the habit of understanding terrain before she needed to navigate it.

He's been alone a long time, she said, without looking up from the map.

Some of the time.

The kind of alone I'm talking about doesn't go away just because you're in groups. She traced a contour line with her finger. You can be in a group and still be the kind of alone he is.

Cole thought about this.

He understood the condition she was describing. He had been practicing it for two years. The fence line policy was one of its formal expressions.

What do you want to do about it? he said.

She looked up from the map.

I'm not asking you to do anything about it, she said. I'm telling you something about the person who's going to be operating fifty yards ahead of your truck for the next four days.

He looked at the rearview. Marcus, on the motorcycle, running the road ahead of them the way he ran everything: with easy competence and complete independence.

He's trying to attach, Cole said.

Yes. She went back to the map. Try to be worth attaching to.

---

He thought about what fifty yards meant as a following distance.

On a highway at speed, fifty yards was nothing -- a gap that closed in seconds. On a two-lane county road at thirty miles an hour, it was a workable interval: enough distance to stop independently, close enough to maintain communication, the distance of someone who wanted to be associated but not attached.

Marcus had arrived at exactly the distance that communicated his intent. Cole had not specified fifty yards. Marcus had chosen it.

He thought about the level of situational awareness that required. The reading of the social geometry of a convoy -- the lead truck, the following truck, the motorcycle that wanted to be part of the group without being part of the group -- and the calculation of the correct distance for the role.

He thought: this kid has been navigating adult situations since he was seventeen.
