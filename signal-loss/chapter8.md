# Chapter 8

## What Marcus Knows

They sheltered the first night in an agricultural equipment barn off a county road.

Marcus had found it by going ahead and coming back with a field report that was, as his field reports always were, specific and accurate: structure sound, space adequate for the trucks and the group, a stock tank fed by a creek that Cole could filter for drinking water. Cole ran the Reader, found three harmless drifted systems in the area, logged them. He set up the watch rotation and took the first shift himself.

The barn smelled of old machinery and dried grain and the sweetness of hay that had been absent for two seasons. Twenty-three people arranged themselves among the equipment with the pragmatic efficiency of people who had been making do in difficult spaces long enough to be good at it. They found the flat surfaces, the sheltered corners, the positions near the south wall where the concrete's thermal mass would hold warmth into the early hours.

Cole sat at the barn entry with the Reader and listened to the group settle.

The sounds of this: the rustling of sleeping bag arrangements, the quiet voices finishing conversations before sleep, the quality of a group's collective exhale when motion stopped and rest became possible. He had been treating these sounds as status indicators -- everything quiet meant no emergency, no conflict, no security concern. Green status. No action required.

He was aware, listening to it now, that this was a reductive translation.

Nora did her medical rounds at two hours -- a quiet circuit through the sleeping group, stopping at specific people, checking, moving on. She had the quality of someone whose professional attention was so precisely calibrated that it did not disturb what it was monitoring. She was managing twelve people with specific medical needs in a barn in Montana with a kit that had been assembled from what was available, and she was doing it with the focused competence of someone who had been doing difficult things well for a long time.

Cole watched her work and thought about the twelve names on the list.

Marcus found him at the entry at the three-hour mark.

He sat down nearby without asking. He had the quality of someone who could read accurately when asking was necessary and when it wasn't.

Outside, the Montana night was very dark and very cold and very large.

Cole said: The fuel depot today. Tell me how you knew it was there.

Marcus thought about it. Actually thinking -- the real pause rather than the performed one.

When I move through a region I get a sense of where things are, he said. Not things in general. Specific things -- fuel, food, medicine. Anything that was in distribution before. I'll be on a road and I know that if I go a specific direction a specific distance, there's going to be something worth finding. I'm right sixty, seventy percent of the time. Sometimes more.

That's a high hit rate for intuition.

I know. I've been trying to understand what I'm actually reading. He looked at the barn's dark middle distance. I thought at first it was pattern recognition -- distribution logic, commercial supply chain positioning. Fuel depots near agricultural operations and route intersections. But the depot today was off the main route, down a track you couldn't see from the road. I knew it was there anyway.

What does it feel like? Cole said. When you know something is there.

Like a pull, Marcus said. Like I'm downstream of something moving and I can feel the current.

Cole turned the Reader to face him.

What you're reading is infrastructure, he said. Specifically the residual outputs of a logistics distribution network that went offline during the collapse but didn't fully stop. Parts of it are still running on automated schedules -- restocking cycles, delivery routes, distribution targets. The data is two years stale. The sender is gone. The system is still executing its last standing orders.

Marcus looked at the Reader display.

Ghost deliveries, he said.

Partially. Some of the physical infrastructure is still filling -- automated dispensing hardware, remote-managed caches. The system keeps scheduling the fill, the hardware keeps executing the schedule, nobody's picking up the output. The depot today has been restocked automatically twice since the collapse. A regional scheduling system running on backup power somewhere, with no way of knowing the supply chain it was built to serve no longer exists.

So I've been finding fuel caches by accident for two years, Marcus said.

Not accident, Cole said. You've been reading the signal. You don't have the vocabulary for it, but you're picking up the same outputs the Reader detects -- the scheduling cycles, the distribution patterns, the physical signatures of active supply infrastructure. You've been navigating by dead infrastructure without knowing it.

Marcus was quiet for a long time.

That's a lot to process, he said.

Yes.

Does it change anything practical?

It means your hit rate isn't luck, Cole said. It means what you're doing is learnable. It also means other people are probably doing what you've been doing -- following the same ghost trails. The caches you've been finding aren't necessarily uncontested.

Marcus looked at the barn. Eddie sitting with Rosa, his hand on her shoulder without seeming to know it was there.

The world has a hidden layer, Marcus said.

Parts of it. Different people can read different parts. I can read the parts the Reader covers. You can read something else. Neither of us has the full picture.

A long pause.

Worse than I thought and better than I thought, Marcus said.

Most things are, Cole said.

He handed the watch to Marcus and found a flat section of floor near the south wall and lay down. He thought about the Bitterroot system. About Marcus reading ghost trails across the intermountain west without knowing what he was reading. About the gap between what the Reader could show him and what Marcus could find.

He thought about what it meant to be working with incomplete information that was more complete when you added someone else's incomplete information to it.

He closed his eyes.

---

He woke at four in the morning to the sound of rain starting on the barn roof.

Montana rain in October had a specific quality: it meant the temperature was going to drop further and the roads were going to become more complicated and the window before snow was closing. He lay in the dark and did the calculations that the rain introduced into the planning and then got up and went to the barn entry and stood with his hands in his pockets looking at the dark field.

Marcus was at the entry already.

The watch, Cole said.

Twenty minutes left, Marcus said. I wasn't going to sleep anyway.

They stood in the rain sound.

After a while Marcus said: When you built the Reader, what were you trying to solve?

Cole looked at the rain coming down in the headlamp's edge light.

The gap between what we could query remotely and what we needed to know on site, he said. We'd go to a facility to do integration work and we'd spend the first two days running diagnostics that should have been available before we got there. The Reader was supposed to close that gap.

Did it?

Partially. It gave us better remote visibility. It didn't give us complete visibility. There's always a gap between what you can see from a distance and what you can see when you're there.

Marcus was quiet for a moment.

Is that what the collapse was? he said. A gap between what people could see from a distance and what was actually happening?

Cole thought about this.

That's one way to describe it, he said. The people who were supposed to be monitoring the systems were looking at the summary data, the dashboards, the reports. The reports were accurate. They just didn't show the accumulation of small failures until the accumulation reached the threshold where it produced a large failure.

Like the system was lying to them.

Like the system was optimizing for the metrics that were being measured, and the things that were actually failing weren't being measured. He paused. The Reader was supposed to help with that. It was supposed to get below the dashboard to the underlying system states. It helped. It wasn't enough.

Marcus looked at the dark field.

You feel responsible, he said. For the collapse.

Cole was quiet.

I built infrastructure that contributed to it, he said. the pathways by which the collapse happened ran through systems I had worked on. I had filed reports about vulnerabilities in some of those systems. The reports were acknowledged.

But not acted on.

But not acted on, Cole said.

Marcus turned to look at him.

So the Reader, he said. Is that you trying to fix what you couldn't fix before.

Cole looked at the field.

It's a tool, he said. Tools don't fix what the people using them didn't fix.

But you built it, Marcus said. You brought it. You're here.

Cole didn't answer that.

The rain came down on the barn roof.

Marcus said: For what it's worth. The Bitterroot system. The credential that modified it. That's not something you caused.

I know.

Someone else made that decision.

But you feel responsible for the infrastructure that made it possible for them to make it.

Cole looked at the field for a long time.

The infrastructure did what it was designed to do, he said. The design had a gap in it. The gap was exploitable. He paused. Yes.

Marcus nodded.

At least you know what you're fixing, he said.

Cole looked at him.

Yes, he said. I know exactly what I'm fixing.

The watch ended. The rain continued.

---

He woke at four to rain and lay in the dark thinking about the systems.

The Reader gave him a specific kind of visibility and he had been working to expand that visibility since the collapse: adding credential sets where he could, querying everything in range, building a mental map of what was running and what wasn't in the regions he moved through. The map was partial. It would always be partial.

Marcus's read gave him a different kind of visibility. Overlapping in some places, extending further in others.

He thought about what a complete map would look like. What it would require to build one.

He thought about the people who could read different layers of it. Marcus and the ghost trails. The valley communities who knew the physical infrastructure from years of living with it. Eddie with his specific knowledge of what he had built and for whom. Iris with her thirty years of reading physical electrical systems.

None of them had the complete picture. All of them had pieces.

He thought about what it would mean to put the pieces together deliberately.

He had been operating as a single person with a tool. He had been treating the Reader as a complete window rather than as one window among several.

This was a revision to his operating model.

He thought about Cole Duren, six months ago, sitting in the ranch's equipment shed looking at the Bitterroot alert and thinking about the gap between acknowledged and acted on. He thought about the version of himself who had sat on the tailgate in the east pasture and told himself he was maintaining his property.

He had been a single person with a tool for two years.

He was starting to understand that a single person with a tool was not the adequate response to the situation he was in.

He lay in the dark in the barn and listened to Montana rain on the metal roof and thought about what the adequate response looked like.

It looked like this. Twenty-three people in a barn with specific, complementary knowledge and two years of hard-won survival skills and a set of problems that required exactly that combination.

He had not built this. It had assembled itself around a series of decisions he had made, some of which he had made for the right reasons and some of which he had made for the wrong reasons and all of which had produced this.

He fell asleep thinking about that.

---

In the morning, Nora found him at the Reader before the group was awake.

She crouched beside him in the barn entry and looked at the display. The ghost trail data -- the logistics distribution network's scheduling cycles, the physical cache locations Marcus had been navigating by instinct.

You've been thinking about this all night, she said.

Some of it.

What part?

He thought about how to answer this in a way that was actually an answer rather than a technical summary.

He said: The infrastructure that's still running is still running because it was built to run without people. The systems kept going after we stopped showing up. And now there are people who have learned to live inside the systems that kept going, and they don't necessarily understand the systems, they just navigate by them.

She was quiet for a moment.

Like gravity, she said. You don't have to understand gravity to fall.

Or to use it, he said.

She looked at him.

You're thinking about the systems as the substrate, she said. The thing people are living on top of without knowing it's there.

Yes. And someone modified one of those systems. And I don't know yet whether the modification was designed to serve the people living on the substrate or to serve the modifier.

She considered this.

Can it be both? she said.

He thought about it.

That's probably the most likely answer, he said.

She stood.

Get some food before we move, she said. You think better with food.

He looked at her.

How do you know how I think? he said.

She looked back at him over her shoulder.

Four months of fence line, she said.

She went to start the cook fire.

---

He ran the watch for two more hours after Marcus fell asleep.

Not because the protocol required it. Because the barn's quiet had a quality he wanted to sit in for a while -- the quiet of twenty-three people at rest, the temperature dropping outside, the occasional sound of the horses in whatever field they'd found, a coyote working the ridgeline to the west.

He thought about the last two years. About the choices that had produced the forty acres and the fence line policy and the Reader on the workbench. He had made each of those choices for reasons he had thought were good reasons at the time.

The reasons had been good reasons.

They had also been a story. A story about what was sustainable and what wasn't, about what he owed people and what he didn't, about what kind of person he was going to be in the world that had arrived.

The story was not false. It was incomplete.

He sat in the dark and felt the incompleteness.

---

He woke at five-thirty with the barn still dark and Marcus on watch at the door.

the quality of waking in a place you have been sleeping for only a few nights: the brief disorientation, the rapid calibration of the surroundings, the arrival at: barn, Montana, twenty-three people, day five. He had calibrated quickly for two years. He calibrated in about four seconds now.

Marcus looked over his shoulder from the door.

Nothing, he said. Quiet night.

Cole sat up and pulled on his jacket and looked at the barn. The group sleeping, the arrangement they had settled into over four nights, the positions of the people he had come to know by their sleeping locations the way he had come to know the camp by its routines.

He thought about what four days of travel together produced. The compressed familiarity of shared difficulty. The way you learned things about people in four days of hard road that you might not learn in months of normal proximity.

He went to look at the Reader.
