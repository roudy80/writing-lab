# Chapter 9

## Road Logic

The second morning of travel, Rosa Garza asked him what the Reader did.

He was running the morning queries -- Bitterroot system status, general area scan, the log of what had changed overnight -- when she appeared beside the truck and waited with the patient attention of someone who had decided to wait for the right moment rather than interrupt. He finished the query and looked at her.

The Reader, she said. You check it every morning and sometimes during the day. What does it tell you?

He thought about how to answer this.

She had a composition notebook and she had been making lists in it since before the ranch. She had organized four people at the canyon crossing without drawing attention to herself. She had asked three questions at the canyon that went directly to what mattered. He thought about what level of answer she would actually use.

It tells me what the infrastructure is doing, he said.

What infrastructure?

The systems that were running before the collapse -- water treatment, power distribution, logistics networks. Most of them are still running. They don't know anything stopped. He paused. The Reader can query the ones I have credentials for and show me their status.

She thought about this.

So they're still doing their jobs even though there's nobody there.

Most of them. They were built to run without human supervision. Some are running on backup power that will eventually run out. Some are running on sources that will last a long time.

Can you change what they do?

Some of them. The ones I have write access for. Usually I can only read what they're doing, not modify it. The ones I can modify, I can adjust their parameters. What they're optimizing for. What they prioritize.

She was quiet for a moment. Then: That's what's wrong with the valley water. Someone adjusted the parameters.

Yes.

She nodded and looked at the Reader.

Can I see what it looks like?

He showed her the Bitterroot display. He walked her through the parameter set -- the optimization layer, the routing logic, the drawdown calculation -- adjusting the vocabulary for what he could read of her background.

She asked three questions.

First: Who set it up wrong?

I don't know yet. Someone with technical knowledge of the system. Someone who understood what they were changing.

Second: Do they know you're coming to fix it?

Probably, by now. The system logged my remote query when I first checked it. Someone monitoring the system would have seen it.

Third: Are they going to try to stop you?

He thought about that one honestly.

I don't know, he said. I'll find out when I get there.

She nodded -- the filing nod, something going into its place in whatever organizational system she was running -- and went back to her notebook.

---

He thought about her questions for most of that morning.

Not because they were sophisticated, though they were. He was thinking about the sequence. She had gone directly to the three questions that mattered: who, do they know, will they interfere. In the correct order. Most adults asked about the technical mechanism first. She had asked about the human situation.

He thought about what two years had done to a twelve-year-old to produce that instinct.

The road ran through open rangeland, the Pioneers visible to the east in the clear October air. He drove and watched the terrain and thought about what he was driving twenty-three people toward.

He was driving them toward a technical problem he understood and a human problem he didn't yet understand completely. He could read the Bitterroot system's parameter set. He could not yet read the intentions of whoever had installed the optimization layer -- whether they would accept what he was about to do, whether they had the capacity to stop him.

He had been treating this as a secondary variable. Rosa's questions suggested it wasn't.

At midday, Marcus pulled up alongside.

The column to the east, Marcus said. Three people now, down from seven. One vehicle dropped back last night. I think they sent a report.

Purcell getting a status update.

That's my read.

Cole looked east through the tree line at the road he knew was there.

What do you know about this road? he said. Between here and the valley.

Marcus thought with the attention he gave geographic memory.

Two problem sections. The canyon we're approaching -- I've been through it twice and know both routes. And a section near the valley's north approach where the road has been failing since before the collapse. Drainage culverts were already compromised. They're worse now.

We take the canyon route and assess the north approach when we're close enough to evaluate, Cole said.

He looked at the Reader. The Bitterroot query. Stable -- the drawdown running its course, not accelerating, four and a half weeks at this rate.

He started the truck.

Rosa was in the truck bed writing in her notebook. He watched her in the rearview as they pulled back onto the road -- her pen moving steadily, whatever system she was tracking proceeding without interruption.

He did not ask what she was tracking.

He thought that someday, if they got to the other side of this, he might.

---

The notebook.

He had been watching it since the first day, when he'd noticed her carrying it. Not obviously watching -- the way he watched most things, as part of the ambient attention he paid to the group. But he had been noting what she did with it.

She made lists. He had confirmed this when she was filling a water container near the truck and had set the notebook down open. Two columns, each with entries in a compact hand. He had not read it. He had registered that it was a list and moved on.

At the midday halt on the third day she came to him with it.

I have been tracking our resource consumption, she said. She held the notebook so he could see the columns. Here is what we used from the time we left the ranch to today. Here is what I think we'll need to get to the valley and have something left when we arrive. The numbers were specific: fuel consumption, food estimates, water usage, medical supplies drawn down against Nora's inventory.

He looked at the numbers.

How did you get the fuel consumption figure? he said.

I watched the gauge each time you checked it and noted the hours between checks and the distance we traveled. She looked at the numbers. I know the estimate is rough because road conditions affect it. I put a margin on it.

He looked at the numbers for a long moment.

The margin is conservative, he said. Which is correct for planning purposes.

She nodded.

The food estimate, he said. How did you derive this?

I counted what everyone brought and estimated daily consumption from what I've been watching people eat since we left. She paused. It's less precise than the fuel estimate. People eat differently under different stress levels and I don't have good data on stress-level variation.

He looked at her.

You've been doing this since we left the ranch, he said.

I've been doing it since month three of the collapse, she said. My dad said knowing where things stood was better than not knowing. She looked at the notebook. He was right.

Cole looked at the two-column notebook.

Eddie had taught her to track resources at twelve years old, in the middle of everything falling apart, because the information was better than the absence of information. And she had been doing it ever since, in the same notebook, apparently, or a series of notebooks, maintaining her own version of the Reader in a composition book with columns.

He thought about what Nora had said at the fence line: He thought you were the kind of person who would help and was choosing not to. He thought about what he had been tracking from the other side of his fence line for four months.

He took the notebook. He looked at her numbers. He made two small corrections -- a fuel figure that needed adjusting for the terrain they'd encountered, and a water margin that needed tightening.

This is good work, he said. He handed it back.

She took it with the matter-of-fact receipt of someone who expected to do good work and had been told so.

Can I see the Reader data? she said. The Bitterroot numbers. I want to put them in context with what we have.

He showed her.

They sat beside the truck for twenty minutes while she worked the numbers into her column system and he watched the road north for Purcell's column and south for whatever was ahead, and the afternoon light came in at the low October angle that made the mountains look close.

When she was done she showed him the integrated picture: their resources against the timeline, the margin they had, the places where the margin was thin.

The fuel, she said. If the road is bad enough to drop our efficiency below this number, we run short before the valley.

Yes, he said.

We need a second cache.

Marcus knows where there might be one, he said.

She nodded and wrote this down.

Cole looked at the notebook and thought about Harlan Briggs's medical list and about Eddie's infrastructure knowledge and about Marcus's ghost trails and about Nora's clinical precision.

He thought about the twenty-three people who had decided the road was better than the alternative.

---

The valley appeared below them in the late afternoon of the third day.

They had come through the mountain approach and the road had dropped, and the Bitterroot Valley opened below them with the quality of a place that had been seen on maps and in data and was now visible as an actual geography. Mountains to the west, the valley floor running north to south, the creek visible as a silver line, the agricultural land laid out in the patterns of decades of use.

Cole stopped the truck at the viewpoint.

He could see from here, in the valley's northern section, the absence of lights that he had been tracking on the Reader. Small communities still there -- the rooftops visible, the smoke from cook fires, the shapes of occupied places. But dark in the early-evening light where there should have been power.

He ran the Bitterroot query.

The drawdown was accelerating. He had known this from the morning check but it was worse now -- the numbers had jumped in the six hours since. He stared at the display for a moment.

Six days, he said to Nora.

She looked at the valley and then at the Reader and then at him.

We go tonight, she said.

Behind them, the group had climbed out of the trucks to see. He heard the quiet of people seeing a destination after a long movement -- the quality of arrival even before arrival, the place becoming real.

He heard Rosa's voice saying something to her father.

He looked in the rearview. She was pointing at something in the valley -- not the dark section, the southern end, where there was a faint glow of consistent power. Light where there was no light everywhere else.

He looked at the southern end of the valley.

At the consistent power.

He ran the Reader query for the southern end.

The distribution management platform came back. Running. Clean. Well-maintained.

He looked at the glow and thought about the credential set and the eight months of water redirection and the fourteen systems.

Someone down there had been keeping the lights on. For themselves and for the community they had defined.

He was going to go talk to them.

But first he was going to fix the water.

He started the truck and drove down into the valley.

---

They were four hours south of the ranch when Marcus came back on the radio with a different kind of report.

Automated truck stop, he said. Lot A off the county road. It's still running.

Running how? Cole said.

Lights on. Pumps active. Dispensing to nothing.

Cole pulled off and drove to the location Marcus described. It was exactly what Marcus had said: a rural truck stop complex, the parking lot lights running on their photocell timer, the fuel pumps displaying active status, the building's interior lit through the windows.

And empty.

He ran the Reader.

The system managing the truck stop was a commercial property management platform -- the kind of integrated facility management software that had been running in gas stations and truck stops and convenience stores across the country before the collapse. It was doing what it had been told to do: maintaining the facility in a ready state for customers. The customers were gone. The system did not know that.

He queried the fuel status. The underground tanks were approximately forty percent full.

He thought about that.

Marcus, beside him: We're going in?

We're going in.

The fuel transfer took an hour. The system registered them as customers and ran through its standard transaction protocol -- except there was no payment component active, the payment servers having been unreachable for two years. The system had adapted by removing the payment requirement and continuing the transaction. It had been doing this for two years, dispensing fuel to anyone who pulled up to the pump, faithfully serving a customer base that had contracted to zero and then to occasional passersby who had learned the stop was still running.

Cole looked at the Reader while the transfer ran. The system had a maintenance log. It had been scheduling service calls for twenty-two months and none of them had been answered. It had been sending the calls into a void and logging the non-responses and adjusting its scheduling interval upward as it ran the maintenance escalation protocol.

He read the log for a while.

This is what most of them are doing, Marcus said. The systems. Running their jobs. Adjusting when they can. Not knowing that the context is gone.

Cole put the Reader away.

Not all of them, he said.

---

The automated truck stop sat in the landscape like a memory of a different world. The lights on their photocell timers, the fuel pumps waiting, the building lit for customers who had stopped coming two years ago and were not coming back.

He had been in three automated locations like this in two years. All of them running. All of them serving nobody. He had found each of them useful and each of them unsettling in a specific way that he had not fully articulated to himself until Marcus had named it: they don't know the context is gone.

They were doing exactly what they had been told to do in a world where being told that thing made sense.

He thought about that on the drive south.

---

South of the automated truck stop the road ran through a section of old highway that had been bypassed in the 1990s when the interstate went in. The old highway ran closer to the land, through small towns and farm intersections and the peripheral infrastructure of agriculture.

He preferred the old highway. The interstate moved faster and felt less like moving through a place.

This was not a preference that had been useful in the pre-collapse world. It was a preference that made sense now. The old highway passed through things. The things were still there -- the grain elevators and the farm supply buildings and the crossroads with their four-way stops still standing. The things were empty of their pre-collapse function and still present as structures, as geography, as the physical memory of how people had organized themselves here.

He drove through it and attended to it.

Marcus came on the radio: There's a fuel situation at the next junction. I want to show you something.

He followed Marcus off the main road and down a county track and found what Marcus had found: another automated depot, smaller this time, a single tank above ground with a hand pump still operational. The logistics network's ghost delivery cycle had been maintaining it.

They filled what they needed and moved on.

The road south kept going.
