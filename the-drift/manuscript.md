# The Drift
## Book One of The Signal Series

Memphis to Kingman · 1,500 Miles


## Chapter 1

Six Feet Under Flood

The water was at her knees by the time she reached the junction.

Maren had been down here four hours. The storm drain ran south from the old gasworks site and branched twice before it opened into the combined sewer main, and she'd mapped both branches twice already but the survey wasn't finished because the junction itself had flooded after the rain two days ago and what should have been a two-foot crawl had turned into four feet of standing water black with sediment and she'd had to wait, come back, wait again. Now it was down to eighteen inches at the deepest point and she could get through if she moved along the east wall where the grade was higher.

She did.

The headlamp threw a cone of yellow light across the tunnel face. Brick from the 1920s, relined in the 1970s with gunite that had started to spall in patches. She noted the spalling on the field sheet clipped to her wrist — not structural concern, cosmetic — and pressed on. The water pulled at her boots with each step, the particular resistance of cold shallow flood that makes every movement deliberate. She didn't mind it. The deliberateness was the point.

Above her, Memphis was doing whatever Memphis did now. She wasn't sure anymore. She'd been spending more time down here than up there, which was not a conscious decision so much as a series of unconscious ones, each individually defensible. The tunnels needed mapping. The infrastructure assessment mattered for water sourcing. No one else was doing it. All of that was true, and none of it was the whole truth, and she'd gotten good at not examining the gap.

The junction opened ahead. She waded into it.

It was bigger than the approach tunnel — maybe twelve feet across, the ceiling vaulting up to seven — and even with the flood sediment she could see the original brickwork running in clean courses above the waterline. Someone had built this in 1924 and it had been moving water under the city for a hundred years and it would outlast whatever was happening up top by a hundred more. She appreciated that about infrastructure. It had no opinion about the situation. It simply continued.

She measured the junction diameter with the laser, logged it, checked her compass heading, and noted the depth of standing water at three points: inlet, center, outlet. Then she stood still for a moment in the yellow circle of her headlamp and listened to the tunnel breathe.

Not breathe. She knew it didn't breathe. But it had a sound — the distant hydraulic murmur of water finding its level somewhere in the system, the drip of seepage through a mortar crack twenty feet up, the particular acoustic quality of a large enclosed space with hard surfaces and a little bit of water. She knew that sound the way some people knew music. She'd been listening to it for eleven years.

She pulled out the field notebook and sketched the junction. The sketch was fast and accurate — she'd had a drafting instructor in college who said the fastest way to truly see something was to draw it — and she added her measurements in the margins and noted the spalling locations and marked the inlet and outlet tunnels with their compass headings. Then she looked at what she'd drawn and felt the particular quiet that came from a completed observation. Everything in its place. Everything accounted for.

It was the only quiet she'd found in eighteen months. She stayed in it longer than the survey required.


---

She surfaced through the access hatch on South Front Street at four in the afternoon, pulling herself up the steel ladder with the survey pack dragging on her back, and Dex was there.

He was sitting on an overturned plastic crate about ten feet from the hatch, which meant he'd been waiting long enough to find seating. He had his forearms on his knees and he was looking at the street and he didn't turn when she came up, which also meant he'd heard her coming and had decided to wait. He did things like that. He let moments develop.

She climbed out, pushed the hatch closed, sat on the edge of it to pull off her waders. Her hair was damp against the back of her neck from the tunnel humidity. She didn't notice it anymore.

"You heard it too," he said. "I know you heard it."

She hadn't looked at him yet. She was working the right boot buckle. It had a tendency to jam when the rubber was cold.

"The broadcast," he said.

"I know what you mean."

"Then stop pretending you're thinking about your boot."

She got the buckle free and pulled off the wader and set it beside the hatch and started on the left one. Down the block, South Front was empty. It had been empty for most of a year. The river was visible two blocks west, the Mississippi going about its business, indifferent to the change in management.

"I heard it," she said.

"And?"

"And I'm thinking about it."

"That's what I figured." He stood up from the crate, stretching his back, a deliberate unfolding. He was a big man — not tall exactly, wide through the shoulders, the kind of build that came from thirty years of actual work rather than a gym. He turned to look at her. "What's there to think about?"

She got the second wader off, stuffed both into the mesh bag on the outside of her pack, and stood. She was aware she smelled like the tunnel — wet concrete and old water and the particular mineral note of sediment disturbed after a long rest. She'd stopped minding that too.

"A lot," she said. "There's a lot to think about."

He didn't argue with that. He just waited, which was something Dex did better than almost anyone she'd met — he could wait with his whole body, without impatience, without the fidgeting that most people used to signal that they were being patient. He was simply there, present, occupying the specific space he was in.

It was, she had decided, either a skill or a character trait, and she hadn't yet determined which.

"Come tell us what you thought of," he said. "Me and Luz. Come tell us tonight."

She picked up her pack.

"Where?"

"Coffee shop on Monroe. The one with the generator."

She slung the pack over one shoulder and looked at him for the first time.

"I'll be there," she said.

He nodded and walked south. She watched him for a moment, then looked back at the access hatch, then at the river. She had the feeling she always had at the end of a survey day: the world above was a problem she didn't know how to solve, and the world below was a problem she did, and sooner or later she was going to have to pick.

She had heard the broadcast. She'd been at the junction on the combined sewer main, sitting on a ledge two feet above the waterline in the pitch dark with her headlamp off — she did that sometimes, turned it off, let her eyes adjust to real dark, which they never fully did, which was the point — and the radio in her vest pocket had come to life. She always kept it on. Background noise. Proof that somewhere above the city there were still radio waves moving.

But this wasn't background noise.

The voice was measured. Technical. A man who chose his words the way engineers chose materials: for load-bearing capacity, not decoration. He had said: facility. He had said: transmitter. He had said: if you can get here.

She had sat in the dark of the junction for twenty minutes after the broadcast ended, listening to the tunnel breathe, and she had been thinking about a map. Not just about the route. About what it meant that someone had built a route worth taking, and that she might be the only person left who could read it.


## Chapter 2

The Signal

The coffee shop had power two hours a day from a salvaged generator running on whatever Dex had managed to find that burned. Tonight it was something that smelled faintly of cooking oil and made the lights run a yellowish amber, which was fine. Amber light was still light.

Maren arrived last. She'd gone back to her place first — a second-floor apartment on Jefferson, chosen for the fire escape access and the water tank on the roof that she'd fitted with a gravity feed — to drop the survey pack, change her shirt, and eat something. Crackers and peanut butter from the case she'd found in a deli storeroom two months ago. She was down to eleven jars. She'd made a note.

Luz was at a table near the window, which Maren didn't like — visible from the street, and the street was never entirely predictable — but the window faced east and east was away from the river and the river corridor was where most of the Drift moved, following old patterns, following the water. The window was probably fine. She sat down anyway in the chair that faced it, so she could watch.

"You smell better," Luz said.

"I changed my shirt."

"I know. That's what I said."

Dex came in from the back carrying three mugs. Real coffee — or close enough, some chicory blend they'd found at a warehouse on Summer Avenue. He set them down and sat and wrapped both hands around his mug and looked at the two of them.

"Six o'clock," he said.

They had forty minutes. Maren drank her coffee and watched the street and didn't say anything, because the broadcast would say it better than she could.


---

The radio was Dex's, a hand-crank emergency unit he'd had in his truck. He set it on the table at five fifty-five and they sat around it like it was something that needed tending.

Static. The generator hum. Somewhere outside, a dog that had been running feral for six months barked twice and stopped.

At six o'clock exactly, the voice came through.

It was the same as the night before. Same cadence, same register — a man in his fifties, Maren guessed, educated, with the particular flatness of someone who had been speaking clearly and carefully for a long time and had learned to keep the feeling out of it so the information could get through.

He said: My name doesn't matter. What matters is what I know.

He said: The installation I'm transmitting from is a decommissioned Air Force facility near Kingman, Arizona. During the first weeks of the collapse, an automated system came online. It should not have. It is broadcasting a low-frequency electromagnetic signal that is interacting with the pathogen — the fungal agent — in ways I can now describe with reasonable confidence.

He said: The signal is why the Drifts aggregate. The signal is why they move the way they move. Remove the signal, and the aggregation principle collapses. The Drifted begin to disperse. Some of them — those in early enough stages — may begin to recover. I have the data. I have spent seven months confirming it.

He said: I cannot leave. The Drift around this facility is too dense. I am broadcasting because I am waiting for someone who can find a route. The infrastructure between here and wherever you are still exists. It is underground and overland and it goes all the way through. Someone who knows how to read it can get here.

He said: Shut down the transmitter. I can walk you through it when you arrive. It is not complicated once you're inside.

A pause. Then: I broadcast at six AM and six PM. I will continue as long as I have power. If you're coming, come.

Static.

Dex reached over and turned the volume down.

No one said anything for a moment. The generator hummed. The amber light made everything look warmer than it was.

Then, from somewhere near the back of the shop, a voice said: "Is there more coffee?"

Maren turned.

A boy was sitting on the counter, legs dangling, holding an empty mug. He was maybe sixteen, maybe less — he had the angular look of someone who'd been eating inconsistently for a while, which could compress age in either direction. He was wearing a canvas jacket two sizes too large and boots that fit, which meant he'd found the boots more recently than the jacket. His hair was dark and needed cutting. He was looking at them with the specific alertness of someone who had learned that attention was a survival tool, and he had the quality -- rare in anyone, very rare in someone his age -- of not needing to fill the silence first.

"Who are you?" she said.

"Rabbit," he said, as if this were an answer.

"That's not a name."

"It's the one I use." He looked at the radio. "The guy on the radio — Kingman's in Arizona."

"Yes," Dex said.

"That's far."

"Yes."

Rabbit hopped off the counter and set his mug down on the bar top with a small click. He said: "There's a route. Through the old rail right-of-way until you hit the river, then south and then —" He stopped. Looked at Maren. "You already know, don't you. You've been looking at maps."

She had been looking at maps for the last eighteen months because she looked at maps, it was what she did, it didn't mean anything. Except that tonight it meant something.

She turned back to the table and put her finger on the wood where a map would be if she'd brought one, and moved it west.

"I know a way," she said.

Luz set down her mug. "Through what?"

"The infrastructure. Storm drains, combined sewers, the civil defense tunnels under midtown. We can move under most of the city without surface exposure. Out past the bridge district we go to ground level but the Drift is thinner out there. After Memphis —" She paused. She was improvising now, which she didn't like. After Memphis was inference and general engineering logic and a good guess. "— after Memphis I know the systems in outline. Not in detail. But enough."

"Enough for what?" Luz said.

"Enough to find a way."

The three of them looked at her. Rabbit had not been invited and was not technically part of any group that existed, but he was looking at her too, with the same focused attention. She had the feeling this was how he'd survived — paying attention to whoever in the room looked like they knew what they were doing.

She was not sure she knew what she was doing. She knew what the map looked like.

"Kingman to Memphis is approximately 1,500 miles," Dex said. "Give or take."

"Give or take," Maren said.

"That's a long way to give or take."

"Yes."

He drank his coffee. He looked at the radio. He looked at his hands.

"When do we go?" he said.

She hadn't expected that. Or she had expected it and hadn't let herself expect it. One or the other.

"I need to finish the survey of the south grid. Two more days."

"Two days," he said. He looked at Luz.

Luz looked at the window. The street was still empty. The dog had stopped barking. The amber light made the glass look like old film.

"I'll need to pull supplies from the clinic," she said. "Medical kit. Antibiotics. Whatever's left."

Maren nodded. She looked at Rabbit.

He shrugged. "I don't have supplies to pull. I travel light."

She looked at him for a moment. She should tell him this wasn't his decision, that he hadn't been asked, that whatever he thought was happening wasn't a group invitation. She should say that.

She turned back to her coffee.

"Two days," she said. "We leave on the third morning."


## Chapter 3

Drift Mechanics

They didn't go underground for the first encounter. That was the thing. She'd planned to, had routed them through the storm drain on Walnut that connected to the midtown grid, but the drain entrance was blocked — a vehicle had come off the overpass sometime in the last month and landed nose-first on the access cover, and moving it wasn't possible with what they had. So they went around, above ground, through the old residential blocks east of the drain, and that's where they walked into the edge of it.

The Drift was moving south.

Maren heard it before she saw it — not sound exactly, more a pressure change, the way the air in a tunnel felt different when another passage opened nearby. Then she saw movement at the far end of the block, and she put up her fist and they stopped.

Dex stopped immediately. Luz stopped. Rabbit, two steps behind and looking at something on the building to his left, bumped into Luz and Luz caught him by the sleeve without looking and he stopped.

Good, Maren thought. They can take instructions.

She looked at the Drift.

There were hundreds of them. Maybe more — the block beyond was also moving, which meant the mass extended further than she could see. They were moving south in the particular way of the Drifted: not walking exactly, more a slow continuous lean, like a field of grass in a steady wind. Each individual moved at human walking pace or slower, and the effect of many bodies moving together in the same direction at the same pace created something that looked like intention even though it wasn't intention, it was just physics — aggregation, momentum, the unconscious calibration of bodies in proximity to each other.

They were sixty feet away. The east side of the street.

She moved them into the doorway of an old insurance office on the west side, pressing flat against the glass. The glass was intact. She checked — no reflection angle that would catch the light and draw attention. Good.

"Don't move," she said, very quietly. "Don't make noise. Watch their heads."

She watched their heads.

The Drifted moved with their faces slightly down and forward, which was characteristic — the gaze oriented toward ground level, not horizon, as though they were following a track they could smell rather than see. They didn't scan. Individual Drifted had a narrow field of active response: sound, motion, and bright light within roughly forty-five degrees of their forward orientation. Outside that cone, you were invisible to them. What made the mass dangerous was simple probability: forty-five degrees times a thousand bodies in different orientations covered the whole compass.

But in a mass moving in one direction, the forward orientation of most individuals was aligned. Which meant the sides of the mass — the flanks — had gaps. If you were still and quiet and off the line of movement, you were furniture.

She had learned this the way she learned most things: by paying attention to the evidence.

They stood in the doorway and the Drift moved past.

It took eleven minutes. She counted.

The Drifted nearest to them came within twenty feet. A woman in scrubs — she'd been in a hospital or clinic when it happened, or she'd found the scrubs afterward, no way to know. The scrubs had a name tag. MEMORIAL BAPTIST, it said, and below that a name in smaller letters. Maren read the name. She didn't mean to. Her eyes moved to it before she could decide not to.

Luz was looking at the name tag too. Her jaw was tight. She had worked at Memorial Baptist.

Maren put her hand lightly on Luz's arm — not gripping, just contact — and felt Luz pull one slow breath in through her nose and hold it and let it out through her mouth and refocus on the middle distance. Good. She'd done it before. Maren hadn't. She kept her eyes on the Drift and let the name on the tag do whatever it was going to do to her, which it did, quietly, in the part of her chest she'd gotten good at ignoring.

Maren put her hand lightly on Luz's arm — not gripping, just contact — and felt Luz pull one slow breath in through her nose and hold it and let it out through her mouth and refocus on the middle distance. Good. She'd done it before.

The woman in scrubs passed. The Drift moved south.

Eleven minutes, and then it was gone, and the street was empty again, and the silence after a Drift was its own particular quality — the absence of all those footsteps, which you hadn't consciously heard as individual sounds but which together had made a kind of white noise, and now was gone.


---

They stood in the doorway for two more minutes. Maren counted those too.

Then she stepped out and looked both ways and said: "Come on."

They went north half a block and east one block and found the alternate drain access she'd noted as a backup — a sidewalk grate on a residential street, the kind that required a drain hook to open. She had a drain hook. She kept it on the outside of the pack, elastic-looped, available in three seconds.

She got it open. They went down.

Underground, she exhaled.

Not audibly — she was quiet about most things — but internally, the way a building settles in cooler air. The close walls and the headlamp beam and the smell of old concrete and standing water. Her environment. She knew exactly where she was in relation to the street grid above without having to think about it.

She set the pace and they followed.

Rabbit, coming down last and pulling the grate shut above them, dropped into the drain and said, quietly, into the dark: "I counted eleven minutes."

She glanced back at him. His headlamp was a smaller one, clipped to the strap of his jacket, the beam narrower than hers.

"So did I," she said.

He nodded, like this was a confirmation of something. Then he reached into the canvas jacket and pulled out a can of peaches and held it up. "There's a bodega two blocks back. I stopped while you were watching the street."

She stared at him.

"You left the doorway," she said.

"For like thirty seconds. While the Drift was past."

"You left the doorway."

"I was hungry." He held out the can. "Anyone want peaches?"

Luz took the can. Dex looked at the ceiling of the drain and then back at Rabbit with the expression of a man recalibrating his assumptions.

Maren turned and kept walking.

They were going to have to establish some rules.


## Chapter 4

The Map She Doesn't Have

She worked the problem at the kitchen table while the others slept.

The apartment she'd been using had a kitchen table that was the right height and a window that faced north, which meant the night sky came through it without obstruction — no other buildings close enough to block it, just sky and the ambient nothing that Memphis had become. She spread the maps out and weighted the corners with whatever was at hand. A coffee mug. A battery. A pocketknife she'd found in the drain three weeks ago and kept because a found knife was a found knife.

The Memphis maps were hers — not literally, not originally, but she'd been the one to survey them, so they were hers in the way that understood things are yours. She knew every tunnel on these pages. She knew which measurements were precise and which were estimated and why each estimate had been made that way. She knew where the brick was good and where it was failing and where the 1970s relined sections met the original 1920s construction and created a slightly different acoustic. She could close her eyes and walk the system.

West of Memphis was different.

She had USGS topographic maps for the region, downloaded before the collapse and printed at the office on the big plotter, rolled and stored in her map tube, carried with her since then because she'd always carried her maps and stopping made no sense. The topos were good for elevation and drainage and road networks. They were not good for what she needed, which was subsurface infrastructure: the buried water mains and sewer lines and old utility corridors and civil defense tunnels that might or might not exist under the towns between here and the Texas border.

She knew what was likely. She'd been doing this long enough to know how cities and towns built their underground systems — the patterns were consistent because the engineering problems were consistent. A town of twenty thousand built its water system the same general way a town of fifty thousand did, just smaller. The collector lines ran to a treatment facility, which ran to a river or reservoir, which was downstream and lower in elevation. Gravity was always the governing principle. Find the low point, find the water, find the route that follows the water underground.

She traced the route with her finger without touching the map.

West on 40, then dropping south to pick up the infrastructure corridor along the old rail line — rail right-of-ways almost always had utility easements buried alongside them, because the land was already cleared and the grade was already managed. She could follow those easements for a hundred miles into Arkansas and come up for air in Little Rock, where there was definitely an old civil defense tunnel network because she'd read a journal article about it in 2019 and the article cited the original 1951 construction documents. After Little Rock: Oklahoma City was the prize. A WWII-era civil defense system she'd read about years ago, not fully mapped since the 1970s. That system could carry them under a major urban Drift without surface exposure.

After Oklahoma City: the infrastructure thinned. The towns got smaller. The subsurface systems got simpler and more improvised and less documented. West Texas and New Mexico were largely above-ground. Arizona was above-ground almost entirely.

She marked the known sections in pencil. Solid lines.

She marked the inferred sections with dashes. The dashes were longer than the solid lines by a factor of three.

She marked the unknown sections — the parts where she was guessing from first principles, working from topography and elevation and the logic of what engineers would have built — with a lighter pencil, barely visible, more suggestion than line.

Then she sat back and looked at it.

The unknown was larger than the known. This was not a surprise. But seeing it laid out geometrically, in the specific visual language of maps she'd been reading her whole adult life, made it concrete in a way that the abstract idea of a long journey had not been. The distance between here and Kingman was not a number. It was a map. And the map showed her very clearly where she was going blind.

She picked up the pencil and wrote, in her careful field-notebook printing, at the western edge of the paper: Kingman.

She underlined it once.

Then she got up, went to the window, and looked at the river. The Mississippi was dark and wide and moving, the way it always moved, and somewhere south of here the Drift was moving along the river corridor the way it always moved, and above her the stars were out in a way they hadn't been before the collapse — no light pollution, now, to wash them out — and she could see the Milky Way as a genuine smear across the sky, which she'd never seen from a city, and which was beautiful in a way that she noted and then set aside because she was thinking about the map.

She stayed at the window a long time.

Then she went back to the table and added one more mark — a small question mark, faint, in the blank space that was west Texas — and put the pencil down and folded her hands on the map and sat.

She was afraid. She recognized fear by its practical effects: the narrowing of focus, the tendency to enumerate obstacles rather than routes. She knew what to do with fear. You mapped around it. You examined the path, not the fear, because the fear was always simpler than the path and the path was the only thing that actually needed solving.

The path needed solving.

She put the maps in order and rolled them into the tube and put the tube beside her pack and went to sleep on the couch because Luz was using the bedroom and she hadn't asked for it back.


## Chapter 5

The Vote Nobody Takes

The basement of the coffee shop had a table and chairs and a camp lantern that ran on batteries they were being careful with. Dex had found it — the basement — on their second day of meeting here, when the upper floor had gotten crowded with other people doing other things and he'd gone looking for quiet. He had a reliable instinct for quiet.

The four of them sat around the table. The lantern was turned low. Outside, above them, the city made its present sounds: distant, intermittent, nothing you could identify.

Maren spread the maps out.

She went through it the way she would have presented a survey to a client: here is what I know, here is what I've inferred, here is where the inference gets thin, here is the route. She kept it flat and specific. She pointed to the solid lines and said: these I'm confident in. She pointed to the dashes and said: these I'm working from documented sources. She pointed to the light pencil marks and said: these are engineering logic.

She did not say: I don't know if we can do this. She did not say that because it wasn't a useful thing to say — not untrue, but not useful, and she tried not to say things that weren't useful.

When she finished, Dex leaned back in his chair and crossed his arms and looked at the map.

"How long?" he said.

She'd done the math. "Twelve to sixteen weeks. Depending on what we run into between here and the New Mexico line. After that, I don't have good numbers because I don't have good data on the terrain."

"Three to four months."

"Yes."

He nodded slowly, the way he did everything: not reluctantly, just thoroughly. "And the tunnel network you're describing — these are functional tunnels? We can walk through them?"

"Most of them. Some sections will require wading. One section in Little Rock might require a short crawl — I won't know the clearance until I see it."

"And if the clearance is wrong?"

"Then we find a different way through Little Rock."

He looked at her. "Do you have a different way through Little Rock?"

A beat. "Not yet."

Luz, who had been watching the map the way she watched patients — with a particular focused calm that absorbed detail without reacting to it — said: "What's the medical risk? Not the Drift. The conditions."

"Flooding, cold water exposure, limited care for injuries underground. Air quality in the older sections — H2S, particulate. I have a meter. We move if it reads over ten parts per million."

Luz nodded once and wrote something in her notebook. She knew what ten parts per million meant. She didn't need it explained.

"I'll add a rescue breathing protocol to the kit," she said.

Maren nodded.

Rabbit, who had been sitting at the end of the table eating crackers from a sleeve he'd produced from somewhere, said: "What about above ground? How much of the route is above ground?"

She looked at him. He was listening more carefully than he appeared to be. She'd started to notice that.

"Best case, thirty percent of the total distance. Worst case, fifty."

"That's a lot of open."

"Yes."

He ate a cracker and thought about this with the particular quality of someone who actually thought before speaking, which was rarer than it should be.

"I'm good above ground," he said finally. Not bragging. Filing it as a fact that might be useful. Like: I carry a knife. Like: I don't sleep heavy.

Dex uncrossed his arms and leaned forward and put both palms flat on the table. It was a gesture Maren had come to recognize: he was about to say the thing that needed saying.

"Maren," he said. "You've been planning this since you heard the broadcast."

She hadn't planned to admit that. She also hadn't planned to deny it.

"Yes," she said.

"And you've presented it to us as though the decision has already been made."

The room was quiet for a moment. The lantern ticked.

"I didn't mean to do that," she said.

"I know you didn't mean to." He said it without accusation. Just: I know. "But I want to say it, so we all know what's happening. You've been planning. We're being asked to agree to a plan that's already been made. That's different from making a decision together."

She looked at the map. He was right. She knew he was right. She'd laid out the route the way she laid out a survey report — here is the situation, here is what I've determined, here are the conclusions — and a survey report didn't have a vote.

"What do you want to do?" she said.

"I want to go," he said. "I've wanted to go since I heard the broadcast. But I want us to say it out loud, all four of us, so it's a decision and not just a plan someone made and we went along with."

He looked at Luz.

"I'm going," Luz said. "I'd decided before she finished talking. I want to know what the road looks like between here and Little Rock, because I've got opinions about the route, but yes. I'm going."

He looked at Rabbit.

Rabbit ate his last cracker and considered the wrapper. "I was going anyway," he said. "I've been going since I got here. I just didn't have a direction."

Dex looked at Maren.

She understood what was being offered. Not permission — she didn't need permission. Something more like recognition. The difference between a plan and a shared decision was that in a shared decision, the plan belonged to everyone, and the failure would belong to everyone, and the making-it would belong to everyone. She had been treating it as her survey. It was not her survey.

"I'm going," she said. And then, because it was true and because Dex had done her the courtesy of making the space for true things: "I don't know if we can do it. I think we can. I don't know."

Dex nodded.

"Good," he said. "Let's go."


## Chapter 6

Below the River

They entered the main at 4 AM on a Tuesday, which meant nothing now except that Maren had calculated the Drift on the surface would be at its most dispersed in the pre-dawn hours, the aggregate mass having followed the warmth of the previous day west and north. She was probably right. She couldn't be certain. She moved as if she were certain because the alternative was to move as if she weren't, which would not help.

The main access was off Beale Street, a double-wide iron hatch set in a concrete surround, and she'd oiled the hinges three days ago in preparation. They went down: Maren first, then Dex, then Luz, then Rabbit — pulling the hatch shut above them and dogging the latch from below.

The sound of the hatch closing was very final.

Not actually final — she'd come back through that hatch seventeen times in the past three months — but the weight of it, at four in the morning, with four packs and a 1,500-mile route chalked out in her head, felt different than it had before. She stood on the ladder for a moment with her hand on the latch and catalogued that feeling and set it aside.

The tunnel smelled of old water and the particular vegetative note that came from the sections where surface runoff carried organic matter into the system. The floor was dry — she'd checked the forecast, no rain for five days, the combined sewer main was running at normal capacity, nothing that would push flood levels. The width was generous here at the start: eight feet, brick arch, seven feet of headroom at the apex. Plenty. Good start.

She turned on her headlamp and set the pace.


---

The system worked the way she'd told them it would. The main ran west-northwest for half a mile before the first branch junction, and at the junction she went left — northwest — into a smaller tunnel, five feet wide and six high, older, the brick more varied in color because it had been patched in more eras. The patches told a story if you knew how to read brick: 1940s repair here, the mortar formulation distinct. 1980s relined section there, smooth gunite. Original 1890s work at the upstream end, handmade brick that didn't match and hadn't matched for a hundred and thirty years.

She narrated as she moved. Not constantly — she wasn't going to run a tour — but when something needed knowing, she said it.

"Watch your head at the junction ahead. The arch is lower on the east side, it was never repaired correctly after the 1940 flood."

"The floor has a false surface here — looks solid, it's about an inch of silt over standing water. Step normally and it'll hold."

"This section has H2S history. I've run it clean for three weeks but we go on the meter."

She held up the meter. Green. They went through.

Dex, behind her, moved with a contractor's attention to structure — she could hear him looking, the pauses when he registered something, the small sounds of professional assessment. He didn't ask questions. He observed and filed.

Luz moved quietly. A medical person's quiet — attentive without announcing the attention. She was the one Maren was least worried about underground.

Rabbit was the surprise.

She'd expected him to be difficult — too fast, or too slow, or unable to resist touching things. He was none of those. He moved through the tunnel with an ease that suggested he'd spent time in confined spaces before, and not unhappily. He watched his footing without being told. He kept the correct distance behind Luz — close enough to react, far enough for clearance. He did not shine his headlamp where she'd asked him not to shine it, which was forward of Maren's position, because two headlamp beams in the same direction created a washed-out zone where detail got lost.

She didn't know what to do with the fact that he was good at this.


---

Three hours in, the tunnel changed.

She stopped the group with her fist and swept her headlamp across the tunnel face. The brick ended in a rough break — not a construction joint, a failure — and beyond it the passage was blocked by a concrete slump, a section of the tunnel ceiling that had come down and filled the channel completely. Not recent. The slump had been there long enough to develop its own surface coating, sediment layering over the concrete in thin depositional bands she could read like tree rings. Years. Maybe ten years.

She stood and looked at it.

The problem with a blockage you didn't know about was not the blockage. The problem was the map — her map, the survey in her head — was wrong. This section should be clear. It had been clear when the last documented inspection was done, which was 2011 per the records, but that was thirteen years ago and apparently at some point in thirteen years the ceiling had come down and no one had been down here to record it.

She sat in the tunnel in the water and looked at the slump and did not say anything for a moment.

"So," Rabbit said, from behind Luz.

"So," Maren said.

She closed her eyes and ran the map. The branch she was in ran northwest. There was a parallel branch two blocks south that also ran northwest, rejoining the main further up. She'd marked it as secondary because it was narrower — four feet, which meant single file and probably some sections that required a stoop. But it was clear. She'd surveyed it eight months ago and it was clear then and it was smaller and therefore less likely to have experienced ceiling failure because smaller tunnels distributed load differently.

She opened her eyes.

"Two blocks back," she said. "There's a lateral junction I passed. We go south through it and take the secondary channel."

"How much longer does that add?"

"An hour. Maybe less." She stood. "It's narrower. Single file, some ducking."

Dex looked at the slump, then at her. He didn't say: you said this passage was clear. He didn't say: the map was wrong. He said: "Lead the way," and turned around in the tunnel — it took him a moment, his pack catching on the wall — and started back.

She followed.

Behind her, she heard Rabbit say to no one in particular: "I kind of like it down here."

She didn't say anything to that. But she noted it, the way she noted everything useful, in the part of her mind that was always running inventory.

She didn't say anything to that. But she noted it.


## Chapter 7

Surface

They came up in West Memphis on a street that had once been residential and was now, like most residential streets, simply empty.

The houses were still standing. That was the thing about the Drift — it didn't destroy the built environment, just the human one. The lawns had gone to knee-high grass and the grass had gone to seed and the seed had scattered and in some yards there were already small trees, volunteers from the neighbors' oaks and maples, two years of unchecked growth making something that looked post-human even when the humans were only recently gone. The cars in driveways were already showing rust at the wheel wells. The street itself had a crack running its full length that hadn't been there before, or had been small and was now larger, now that no one was filling it.

Maren stood at the access hatch and looked at all of it. Three days underground. She'd surfaced briefly twice for air quality checks, but those were thirty seconds at an angle, crouching at the hatch, not standing in the open light. This was different. This was standing in the open light with the sky above her and the street around her and absolutely no walls.

She didn't like it. That was new. She'd never liked the surface less than underground — she'd always just preferred underground, which was different from disliking the alternative. But standing here in the morning light of West Memphis with her pack and her maps and three other people, she felt exposed in a way she didn't underground, even though underground was objectively more dangerous in the ways that didn't involve the Drift.

She catalogued the exposure and kept moving.


---

The crossing was two miles. She'd mapped it from the topographic charts: east to west across the city, avoiding the river corridor where the main Drift moved, cutting north of the bridge approaches where the aggregation was densest. The safest route went through a commercial district that had some remaining structure and cover, then through a light industrial area along the rail line, then out to the western edge where the city dissolved into highway and open country.

They moved in loose formation. Not tight — too tight was noise, one person's stumble became everyone's stumble — but not scattered. She kept them close enough to communicate in low voices and far enough apart that a threat to one wasn't immediately a threat to all.

The commercial district was quiet. Most Drift moved at predictable patterns — along corridors, following the paths of greatest aggregation, which tended to be the same paths humans had used: major roads, river corridors, the routes between population centers. Side streets and abandoned commercial blocks were relatively sparse. She'd chosen the route on this basis and the route was good.

She was always reassured when the route was good.

They were past the commercial district and into the rail corridor when Rabbit stopped.

She noticed the absence of his footsteps before she noticed anything else, and turned to find him standing half a block back, looking at a building. A second-floor window. An old apartment building, brick, the kind of building that had been subdivided into small units sometime in the 1970s.

She retraced her steps to him. "What?"

"Someone's up there." He was still looking at the window. "Second floor, right side. That window."

She looked. The window was covered by a curtain — not closed, but hanging in a specific way that suggested someone had positioned it from behind. An almost-closed curtain. The kind of window a person might stand at if they were watching the street and didn't want to be seen watching the street.

"It's a Drift pattern," she said. "They'll stand at windows sometimes. Heat and light from outside."

"I know what that looks like." He didn't lower his voice, but he spoke quietly, the way she'd noticed he spoke when he was being careful. "This is different."

She looked at the window for another five seconds. The curtain shifted slightly — could be a draft through a broken seal, could be contact. She couldn't tell.

"We keep moving," she said.

He looked at the window for another moment. Then he looked at her. Something moved behind his eyes that she didn't have time to read.

"Okay," he said, and turned west.

She stayed two steps behind him for the next hundred yards, watching him. He moved normally. He didn't look back.

She looked back.

The curtain was still.


---

The incident happened in the last block before the rail corridor.

A cluster of Drifted — seven or eight, separated from a larger mass, moving in a loose group east through the industrial block. Not looking for anything. Just moving. But their line of movement would intersect the group's line of movement in about thirty seconds if everyone kept their current heading.

She stopped them. Pressed them against the wall of a warehouse. Metal siding, cold. She watched the cluster's angle.

They would pass. Twelve feet of clearance if everyone held position.

Eleven feet. Ten.

She heard Rabbit shift his weight — a boot scuff, very small — and reached out without looking and put her hand flat on his chest. Still. He stilled.

The cluster moved past.

She watched the last one go. A man, heavyset, wearing a Grizzlies jersey — the team had been good the year it ended, she remembered that for no reason — moving with his face slightly down, following something she couldn't perceive.

She held the position for twenty more seconds.

Then she moved them forward and they reached the rail corridor and went north along it to the western edge of the city and there it was: open country. Flat. The land going pale and wide all the way to a horizon that was just a line, just the edge of seeing, with no structure between here and there.

She stood at the edge of it.

She was aware, standing here, of a feeling she would not have expected: reluctance. Not to go on — she was going on. But to leave behind the last city infrastructure for a hundred miles. Out there, she was working from inference and logic. In here, she'd been working from knowledge. The difference was the difference between a solid line on the map and a dashed one.

She adjusted her pack and stepped into the open.

Behind her, Rabbit wiped his right hand on his jeans.

She didn't see it. She was looking at the road.


## Chapter 8

The Radio, Again

They sheltered in a church on the western edge of the city — the last building before the commercial strip dissolved into highway approach and open sky. It was a small congregation, or had been: a Baptist church with white siding and a parking lot that held eight cars, all of them present, none of them going anywhere. The front door was unlocked. They didn't discuss whether to go in.

Inside: pews, hymnal racks, a baptismal pool in the chancel that still held water. Clean water — the pool had been sealed and the seal had held and eighteen months later it was still good, which was luck and also the specific theology of people who maintained their facilities. Maren filled the water bottles while the others set up for the night.

The church was quiet in the way of buildings that had been built for quiet. Thick walls. High ceiling. The particular acoustic of a space designed to hold large collective silence. She found she didn't mind it.

They ate. Crackers and canned black beans and the last of the peaches from Rabbit's bodega score, divided four ways without ceremony. Dex produced a small camp stove and heated water and made something from a packet that was technically coffee in the way that a canal was technically a river. It was hot. Nobody complained.

At five fifty-five Dex put the radio on the pew between them.

They sat in a rough circle around it — not intentionally, it just happened, the way things happen when four people run out of seating configurations. The church candles Luz had found in a closet and lit made the stained glass glow faintly from inside, a reversal of their intended function, and the light was strange and not unpleasant.

The signal came in cleaner than it had in the city. The church was high ground — barely, a six-foot rise above the surrounding grid, but enough to improve the antenna angle.

The Cartographer said: I want to be clear about what recovery looks like, because I think the word recovery is doing more work than it should.

He said: The fungal colonization is not reversible in every case. It depends on how long the exposure, how deeply the pathogen has established itself in the frontal cortex, and the individual's immune response. For those in the early stages — what I'd call the first weeks of behavioral change — there is meaningful probability of partial recovery if the electromagnetic stimulus is removed. For those in later stages, the probability decreases. I don't know the exact threshold. I don't have the data. I'm telling you this because if you're coming here expecting to cure the world, I don't want to be responsible for that expectation.

He said: What I can tell you is that removing the signal will stop the aggregation. That alone will change everything. A dispersed infection is a manageable one. It is not a manageable one now.

He said: I'm still here. Facility is stable. Come.

Static.

Dex reached over and turned the volume down.

Nobody said anything for a moment. The candles moved in a draft from somewhere.

Then Dex said: "Do you trust it?"

She'd been waiting for this question. Not from Dex specifically, but from someone. She'd been running her response in parallel with the broadcast, checking it.

"I trust the engineering," she said.

He looked at her. "That's not what I asked."

He was asking: do you believe the man. That question was not in her skill set, and she knew it, and she was tired of knowing it without being able to fix it.

"No," she said. "That's not what you asked."

Luz was looking at the candle nearest to her. "The mechanism is plausible," she said. "I'm not an immunologist, but fungal behavioral modification is documented. Ophiocordyceps does it to insects. The jump to humans is — was — considered theoretical. Clearly it happened." She paused. "The question I'd want answered is how he identified the electromagnetic component. That's a specific claim. It's either very well evidenced or it's wrong."

"He could be wrong," Dex said.

"Yes," Luz said. "He could be wrong. And we'd walk 1,500 miles to find that out."

Rabbit, who had been lying on his back on a pew with his arms folded behind his head, staring at the ceiling, said: "Or he could be right and we don't go."

Nobody argued with that.

Maren looked at the radio. The broadcast was over. The static was white and even. She listened to it for a moment and thought about the man on the other end — the flatness of his voice, the specific quality of someone who had been alone for a long time and had reorganized around the experience, the way a tree reorganizes around a fence post it's grown against.

She thought about what it would take to broadcast into the void every twelve hours for seven months on the chance that someone out there had both the skills and the reason to listen.

"I think he's there," she said. "I think what he's describing is real. I think the science is probably right." She picked up the radio and turned it off to save the battery. "Whether what we find at the end is what he described — I don't know. I can't know. But the route exists. And someone should take it."

Dex looked at her for a long moment.

"Okay," he said. Just: okay. Not satisfied or reassured — just: received. Processed. Moving on.

They banked the candles and settled in their pews with what padding they had, which wasn't much, and the church was quiet around them with its thick walls and its high ceiling and its eight cars in the parking lot that weren't going anywhere.

Maren lay on her back and looked at the ceiling and thought about the route. The Arkansas section. The grade of the land west of here, and the likely path of the storm drains following it, and where the first documented sewer access would be. She fell asleep calculating.

In the morning they crossed into Arkansas. The city ended. The open started.


## Chapter 9

The Flatlands

Arkansas opened up like a held breath finally released. The land went flat and wide and pale under a sky that had no building in it, no structure, nothing for the eye to catch except the road and the fields and the far tree line that marked where the drainage ditches ran.

She had known this would happen — she'd looked at the topographic maps, she knew the elevation profile — but knowing it and walking in it were different things. Underground you always had the walls. The walls were the point: they told you where you were, they bounded the space, they gave the compass bearing meaning because north in a tunnel is a specific north and north in a field is just a direction in which things continue. She adjusted. She was good at adjusting. She didn't enjoy it.

They walked US 70. Not on the road itself — the road was exposed and the road noise carried and she didn't trust the sightlines — but alongside it, in the treeline when there was one and in the drainage ditch when there wasn't, which was most of the time. The drainage ditch was three feet deep and two feet wide and ran the length of the road corridor, and she spent the first morning in it reading the ditch: where it narrowed, where it had been maintained and where it hadn't, the silt deposits that told her the flow direction and the volume and the history of what had come down from the fields.

She was mapping. She couldn't help it. She also understood, for the first time, that the mapping was not only professional. It was the thing she did when she was frightened. She filed this understanding and kept moving.

The maps she were making weren't formal — no laser, no field notebook, just her eyes and her mental model, which worked in terrain the way sonar worked in water: send attention out, receive the return. The return told her things. This drainage ditch connected to a larger collector half a mile ahead, she could see the grade change that meant the junction. The collector would run south toward a creek, and the creek would have a culvert under the road that was probably concrete pipe, probably forty-eight inch diameter, standard for a road of this class, passable.

She noted it. Not on paper. In the running inventory she kept of possible shelter and movement options.

Three days of open road. They slept in vehicles. Dex had found a pickup with a covered bed on the first night and they'd rolled into it like it was a bunk room, which it approximately was: four people in a truck bed, sleeping in shifts, two on and two off, the off ones folded around their packs in whatever configuration allowed their legs to extend. It was not comfortable. It was shelter, which was the operative category.

On the second night they found a farmhouse set back from the road with a water pump in the yard that still moved water from the well — Dex worked the handle for ten minutes before it caught, then let it run until the rust cleared, then they filled every container they had — and a first floor that was intact and a second floor that had a roof issue but was structurally sound. They slept inside, which was better.

Maren sat at the kitchen table with the maps while the others slept and tried to fill in the blank spaces by inference. The blank spaces didn't fill. She knew that. But the act of attending to them, of sitting with what she didn't know and running her pencil over it without marking it — there was something in that. It wasn't solving. It was a form of respect for the problem. And tonight it was also, if she was honest, a way of not thinking about Rabbit asleep in the next room with the thing she hadn't told the others ticking quietly in the back of her mind.


---

On the third morning, Dex talked.

She had been walking beside him for most of an hour in companionable silence — she had found that Dex's silences were companionable, they had the quality of being with someone rather than the absence of being with someone, which not everyone managed — when he said, without preamble:

"I did a job in Tulsa in 2019. Renovation, old craftsman house, the kind with the built-in bookcases and the high baseboards. The homeowner wanted to take it down to the studs and modernize the whole thing."

She waited.

"He changed his mind four times. First he wanted modern, then he wanted to keep the original character, then he wanted a hybrid, then he wanted to go back to the original plan." Dex adjusted his pack. "We were three weeks in before he settled. Three weeks of work that we kept having to adjust, redo, re-approach. The foundation was worse than anyone told us — I found a footer that had moved maybe two inches over sixty years. Not a structural concern, but it needed addressing, and addressing it meant pulling up the subfloor in two rooms, which the homeowner had not budgeted for."

"What happened?" she said.

"We fixed the footer. He paid for it. The house turned out beautiful." He paused. "I'm telling you this because I've been thinking about what you said last night. About trusting the engineering but not knowing about the man."

She waited.

"I've worked on a lot of structures. Renovations, new builds, a few things that were technically not legal that I won't get into. And in my experience, you can trust the engineering and not trust the client and still do good work. The work doesn't require you to believe in the person commissioning it. It requires you to understand the structure."

She thought about that for a while.

"You're saying the Cartographer is the client," she said.

"I'm saying the building is the building. Whether the person who hired you is trustworthy — you find out at the end. What matters now is whether the structure holds."

Ahead, Rabbit had stopped at the edge of the drainage ditch and was looking at something in the water. Luz crouched beside him. Some small event — a living thing in the drainage water, something that had survived. She watched them crouch over it and felt something she didn't examine too closely.

"What's in Tulsa now?" she said.

Dex was quiet for a moment. "I don't know. Last time I went through was eight months ago. It was —" He stopped. "It's Tulsa."

She didn't ask what that meant. She could hear what it meant. She could also hear what he wasn't saying, which was: I have a daughter somewhere in this world and I am walking away from her direction because I believe this is the right thing to do, and some days that belief is enough and some days it is not.

They walked on. Arkansas kept opening up around them, that held-breath flatness, and she kept reading the drainage ditch and the road cut and the sky for what they had to tell her, and the maps filled in slowly in her head with things she hadn't known a day ago.


## Chapter 10

What Rabbit Knows

She noticed it first in the way he turned his head.

They were in the drainage ditch on the second day in Arkansas, moving single file through a section where the ditch had deepened to four feet and there wasn't room to walk side by side, and she was ahead of him and she heard him and in the small sounds of his movement — boot on packed earth, pack settling, the specific rhythm of someone walking in a confined channel — there was something that registered without registering, the way a slightly wrong note registers in a piece of music you know well.

She filed it. She was good at filing things.

That evening, they made camp in the machine shed of an abandoned farm operation — metal building, insulated, the smell of oil and old seed still in it, a stack of fertilizer bags in the corner that she told them not to sleep near because of the ammonia off-gassing risk from degraded ammonium nitrate, which was real even if the risk was minor. They set up on the far side. Rabbit was the last one in.

She watched him cross the shed.

He moved normally. He moved the way he always moved — efficient, a little loose, the kind of young-body ease that doesn't think about walking because it doesn't need to. But when he set down his pack and straightened up, he reached back and scratched the base of his skull. A slow, absent scratch. Not the scratch of an itch. The scratch of a sensation that was looking for a name.

She had seen that scratch before. She had seen it eighteen times in the last five months, on eighteen different people, at various points in the first weeks of Drift progression. It was consistent enough to note. She had noted it.

She looked at him. He looked at her.

"I'm fine," he said.

She hadn't said anything. She hadn't moved.

"Okay," she said.

He unzipped his pack and started sorting through it with the focused efficiency he used for tasks he wanted to appear to be concentrating on.

She turned away and started on the H2S check for the shed. Negative. Good. She checked the corners. Checked the air flow. Noted the exit points. She always noted the exit points.


---

The next morning he found her at it.

She was up an hour before the others, as usual, working the map by the early light that came through the shed's ventilation slats. Specifically the drainage network between here and Little Rock, which she was reconstructing from the watershed indicators she'd been reading in the landscape for two days. If her reading was right, there was a collector line under the old highway alignment that ran due west for twelve miles before turning north to its outfall. Twelve miles of covered movement. Good.

Rabbit dropped down next to her without ceremony and looked at the map.

"You're going to figure out the drainage before we get to it," he said.

"I'm trying to."

He studied the map. He'd looked at her maps before — she'd noticed he always oriented himself immediately, found north, traced the route — and he did it again now, one finger hovering over the surface without touching it, following the road lines west.

"If you drop south here" — the hovering finger — "there's a creek that runs due west for about eight miles before it bends. I came through there six months ago. The creek bank is solid, no flooding issues, and there's a culvert under the county road at the eight-mile mark that's big enough to move through if you need to avoid the road crossing."

She looked at him. "You scouted that?"

"I was moving through it." He pulled his finger back. "I notice things."

She looked at the map. The creek alignment he'd described matched the drainage pattern she'd been inferring from the watershed indicators. He was right. Eight miles of creek bed was better than road exposure, and the culvert at the crossing was a detail she wouldn't have known without ground truth.

"How long have you been moving?" she said.

"Since about two weeks after it started."

"Alone?"

"Mostly."

She folded the map to the relevant section and noted the creek on it in pencil. He watched her.

"Maren," he said.

"Yeah."

"I know what you saw last night." He wasn't looking at the map anymore. He was looking at the ventilation slat, the line of pale early light. "I know what the scratch means."

She didn't answer.

"I've been watching for it for a while. In myself." He said it evenly, the way he said most things — not performing calm, just actually calm, which was either admirable or a symptom, and she wasn't sure which. "I'm not — it's not progressed. I know what progression looks like. I've seen it."

"When?"

"Friend of mine. About five months ago." A pause. "It took two weeks from the first scratch to —" He stopped. "Two weeks."

She sat with that.

"I don't know when mine started," he said. "I've been trying to figure that out. I think it might have been the cluster in the rail yard, back in Memphis. I got too close. I was moving fast and I cut it closer than I should have."

She should tell Luz. She should tell Dex. She should tell them this morning, before they packed up, before they went another step down the road. She knew this.

She looked at him. He was sixteen — maybe — and he had been alone for eighteen months or close to it, and he had been watching himself for symptoms the way she watched drainage systems, with careful attention and no illusions, and he had told her and not the others because he had understood, correctly, that she would not panic.

"I'm going anyway," he said. "I was going anyway."

"I know," she said.

"So tell me about the culvert. What size do you think it is?"

She looked at the map. "Forty-eight inch, probably. Concrete pipe. Standard for this road class."

Then we can move through it, he said. Like it was settled. Like they were just talking about a door.

"Yes," she said.

They sat in the pale light from the ventilation slat and she told him about the drainage network, and he listened, and neither of them said anything else about the other thing, and she filed it where she filed the rest of what she didn't know what to do with, in the space at the edge of the map where the lines ran out.


## Chapter 11

Salvage

The hardware store was on the main road through a town called Hazen, which had been a town of about 1,500 people and was now a town of buildings. The sign above the door still read HAZEN FARM & HOME in faded red letters. The glass in the front windows was intact. The door was locked.

Dex got it open in forty seconds with a pry bar and a technique he explained while he was doing it: the frame is the weak point, not the lock, always the frame. He spoke the way he did most things -- practically, without flourish, as though the information were too useful to waste on theatrics.

Inside: the smell of metal and rubber and mineral oil that was the universal smell of hardware stores, overlaid now with something organic, a slow decomposition of the seed stock in the back aisle. She moved through it with her headlamp and made the list.

Rope: yes, two spools of quarter-inch braided nylon, still good.

Lamp oil: four bottles in the camping section, lamp-oil grade, clean burn.

Hand pump: one, the well-pump type with the lever action, heavy, worth it.

She found the other things on her list -- cable ties, a coil of copper wire, a set of drill bits in a sealed case, two spare headlamp lenses that matched her model, which was luck -- and brought them back to where Dex was working through the fastener bins with the systematic calm of a man who had been in hardware stores his whole life and knew that the fastener bins were always the most valuable part.

He didn't ask what she needed fasteners for. He was collecting them on general principle.

Luz was in the back of the store with the first-aid section, which was sparse -- the good stuff had been taken already, and the good stuff was always taken already, you were never the first person to clear a pharmacy or a first-aid section. But she was doing what she did: working what remained, sorting what was there, noting the deficits in the small notebook she kept.

Maren found her at it.

What do we need? she said.

Luz held up a package without looking up. Wound closure strips. She put them in the bag. I want better antibiotics before New Mexico. She paused, still not looking up. Also. Your left palm.

Maren looked at her left palm. There was a cut -- she'd caught it on a rusted gate fitting two days ago, a shallow thing, she'd cleaned it and forgotten it.

It's clean, she said.

Keep it that way. Luz looked at her now, direct, the expression she had when she was being professionally exact. You're the navigator. Your hands matter more than you're treating them.

Maren put the cut hand in her pocket.

Noted, she said.

Rabbit appeared from the far aisle carrying a twelve-pack of emergency candles and a box of waterproof matches and an expression that said he had been in there longer than he'd needed to be for these items and had explored.

What else is back there? Maren said.

Camping section has two sleeping pads, partially inflated, both have patches. Generator fuel cans, empty. A hand-crank lantern that works. He set the candles and matches on the counter. And there's a door that goes to a back room that has a cot in it and about two months of canned food. Somebody was using this place.

Silence.

Recently? she said.

The cot has a blanket on it that isn't moldy. The cans are stacked. It's not recent but it's not a year ago either.

She looked at the front windows. The street was empty. The Drift here was thin -- Hazen was small and the small towns had less to aggregate around, fewer bodies to start the mass. But thin didn't mean absent. And whoever had been here wasn't here anymore, which meant something had found them or they had left, and either answer was a reason to move.

We take what we need and we go, she said. We don't sleep here.

Nobody argued.

They were loaded and moving within twenty minutes. The hand pump was the problem -- it weighed eleven pounds and they were already at pack limit. Dex rigged it across his pack with the rope, balanced across the top, and said he could carry it through Oklahoma if the pack frame held. She watched him rig it and thought about the engineering of the load distribution and said nothing, because the engineering was sound.

They were back on the road when the Drift found them.

## Chapter 12

Oklahoma Line

They crossed into Oklahoma on a clear morning with the temperature at fifty-two degrees and the wind out of the southwest carrying the particular smell of the plains -- dry grass, turned earth, something mineral and old -- and the state line itself was just a sign on a pole at the edge of the road, white letters on green, the way it had always been.

Oklahoma. Rabbit read the sign as they passed it. He didn't say anything. He just read it, the way he read everything, with full attention.

She'd been watching him for three days now since the machine shed. He was the same. The scratch at the back of his skull had happened twice more -- she'd seen both -- and both times he'd caught himself and stopped and gone on. He was monitoring himself. She was monitoring him monitoring himself. It was a strange arrangement and neither of them mentioned it.

The Drift in Arkansas had been manageable -- the agricultural flatlands had it in slow continental spreads that were visible from distance and navigable with patience. Oklahoma was different on the map: Oklahoma City was two days out and it was the largest Drift concentration they'd face between Memphis and the Texas border, which meant they needed to be in the tunnel system before they hit the city's outer neighborhoods, which meant she needed to find the access.

She reviewed what she knew about the tunnel system for the hundredth time.

The Oklahoma City civil defense tunnels dated to 1943, part of the wartime infrastructure program that had put similar systems under a dozen mid-sized American cities. The original construction documents were in the National Archives. She hadn't seen them, but she'd read the journal article that had cited them, and the article described a network of six primary corridors radiating from a central hub beneath the old Biltmore Hotel, each corridor approximately four feet wide and five feet high, the hub itself considerably larger. The corridors ran roughly to the major compass points and connected to several secondary systems that had been added in the 1950s.

The Biltmore Hotel was now the Devon Tower parking structure. She knew this from an architectural survey she'd read in 2021. The building had been demolished in 2015, but the basement had been incorporated into the new construction -- parking structures often incorporated existing basements because the excavation was expensive and the structure was usually sound. Which meant the tunnel hub was likely still there, three levels below the parking deck.

Likely was doing a lot of work in that sentence.

What's in Oklahoma City? Dex said. He'd asked this before, days ago. He was asking again because they were close enough that the answer had changed from theoretical to operational.

She told him about the tunnels. The 1943 system, the Biltmore, the Devon Tower parking structure, the four-foot clearance.

Fair questions.

We'll meter going in. She paused. If the Devon Tower access is blocked, I go to my secondary. There's a 1951 addition to the system that accessed from a different building -- the old First National Bank, which is now -- was -- a parking garage on Harvey Avenue. Two access points, different owners, different construction eras. The probability that both are blocked is low.

Low is not zero, he said.

No. It's not zero.

He nodded. That was the end of the question. He'd established the contingency and filed it.

The sign for Oklahoma City said 107 miles.

Luz did the math on their water. Dex did the math on their food. Rabbit did the math on nothing, sitting on a concrete barrier at the edge of the road, looking west with his boots hanging over the edge.

Maren did the math on time. Seven days to get through Oklahoma City if everything worked. Ten if it didn't. After Oklahoma City: the Texas Panhandle, which was above-ground and exposed and the section she was least confident about. After Texas: New Mexico, which was mountains and thin infrastructure and the part of the route she'd been avoiding thinking about in detail because the detail was discouraging.

One section at a time.

She put the Texas Panhandle math away and focused on Oklahoma City.

Just that. Hurry.

Yes, she said.

He looked at her. But you don't think it was static.

## Chapter 13

City of Doors

Oklahoma City rose out of the plains the way all plains cities did -- slowly, incrementally, the skyline appearing as a smear on the horizon and growing taller over hours of walking until it became buildings and then became streets and then became the fact of the city itself, which was different from its appearance from a distance.

From a distance it had looked intact. From the outskirts it looked inhabited, which was a different thing entirely.

She stopped them on the roof of a grain elevator at the city's eastern edge -- twelve stories, the tallest structure for two miles, reached by an interior ladder that had been terrifying in the dark but was fine in the abstract -- and they looked at Oklahoma City from above.

The Drift was visible from here.

Not as individuals -- too far -- but as movement, the city's streets animated in a way that streets were not supposed to be animated, a slow directional flow visible in the way shadows moved on the grid below them. The flow was organized. It moved primarily north-to-south through the major corridors, pooling at intersections and then continuing, the Drift following the street grid the way water follows drainage channels because the street grid was a drainage channel, in its way -- it directed movement from distributed points to central collection points, and the Drift had learned this the way water learned topography: without intention, purely through physics.

That's more than I thought, Dex said.

She'd thought the same thing. The pre-collapse population of Oklahoma City proper was about 650,000. If the conversion rate held -- and she'd been estimating it at roughly 70 percent, based on what she'd seen in Memphis -- there were 450,000 Drifted in this city. Which was consistent with what she was seeing from here. A mass of that size moving through a constrained grid had behavioral properties that a smaller mass didn't. It organized. Not intelligently -- never intelligently -- but structurally, the way any large system organized around the constraints of its environment.

She watched it for ten minutes.

The center of the city was densest. She pulled out the map.

Here's what I see, she said. The transition zone, east of downtown, is navigable if we time the movement correctly. Yes.

## Chapter 14

The Biltmore

They moved at first light, which in late November was late enough that the cold had settled into the streets and the Drift had slowed to something closer to a shuffle. She'd been right about that -- temperature affected it, the aggregate mass becoming sluggish in cold the way any biological system became sluggish in cold, and the sluggishness bought them time.

The storm drain access was where she'd seen it from the elevator roof: a sidewalk grate on East California Avenue, the kind with the four-bolt frame. She got it open in forty seconds while Dex watched the street and Luz watched north and Rabbit watched everything.

The junction ahead had water -- not flooding, but standing water at two feet, a residual pool from the last rain event, still and dark in the headlamp beam. She checked the ceiling. We wade, she said, and went through.

Cold. The water came to her mid-thigh and soaked through immediately because waders were in the pack, not on her legs, and she'd made a judgment call about pack weight versus water exposure. Wrong call. She made a note of it for the next time she had to make it, and kept moving.

Secondary access. It worked.

Civil defense system, she said. This is what we came for. We're going through.

They did.

Come on, she said.


## Chapter 15

Below Oklahoma City

The west corridor ran straight for nine hundred feet, then jogged south for thirty, then continued west. She tracked it on compass and counted paces and built the map in her head with the precision she'd learned to maintain underground: every measurement mattered, every bearing was recorded, every junction was noted with its compass heading and approximate distance. If she had to come back through this in the dark without a headlamp, she could do it.

She always planned for the headlamp to fail. Eleven years in the field had taught her: plan for the headlamp to fail.

The group moved well. Better than the Memphis tunnels, which had been unfamiliar and the group had been untested. Luz moved with a nurse's control. Rabbit moved --

I know, he said. It's the backup. I'll swap at the next stop.

The corridor was better than she'd hoped. The 1943 construction had been conservative -- thick walls, careful pour, the kind of work that got done when material was expensive and failure was unacceptable. She was good at that. Calling what was coming before it arrived. It was the same skill as reading topography -- you looked at what the environment was doing and you extrapolated, and the extrapolation was usually right if you'd read enough environments.

The problem was that extrapolation was not memory. And memory was what she trusted.

Somebody had come in here in the 1970s and done work.

She moved on.

Rabbit, she said.

Stopping to swap, he said.

## Chapter 16

The Cost

## Chapter 17

Three Days

The three days between Oklahoma City and the Texas line were walking days, which was what the open country demanded: walking, with no tunnels to provide cover and no urban structure to navigate, just the road and the sky and the particular relentlessness of distance accumulating under your feet.

You moved for an hour and you had an hour's worth of distance behind you, and you moved for another hour and you had two hours' worth, and the accumulation was only visible if you looked back -- and she didn't look back, she looked forward, which meant the distance she'd covered was always invisible and the distance remaining was always visible.

## Chapter 18

Luz Sees It

## Chapter 21

## Chapter 22

Infrastructure Prayer

## Chapter 24

## Chapter 25

The implications settled over the next day like weather -- slowly, pervasively, changing the quality of the air without a single identifiable moment of arrival.

That meant he was tracking the route in real time. Not from memory or from a pre-calculated probability -- actively tracking. Which meant he had current information about conditions along the route.

The only communications she'd seen functioning were line-of-sight radio, which decayed with distance, and the AM/FM broadcast band, which was what he was using.

And what he was using was a broadcast, not a two-way signal. He was transmitting. He wasn't receiving anything she could identify.

So either he was calculating probability from historical data -- which was possible, the panhandle drought was probably in the pre-collapse agricultural records -- or he had some other information source she couldn't see.

She put the problem in the back of her mind, which was where she put things that needed more data before they could be solved. It sat there with the rest of the unsolvables and she moved on.

He knows about the infrastructure, she said. He has access to the pre-collapse data. He was looking for someone like me -- someone who reads the underground systems.

She paused.

He was looking for me specifically.

Dex leaned forward. What do you mean?

I mean I'm the only person in the municipal engineering department who did field survey work. I'm the only person who had current, detailed subsurface data for Memphis and the corridor west. If he was searching the pre-collapse employment records for someone with my specific skill set in a city with access to this route --

She stopped.

You think he found you before you found him, Luz said.

I think it's possible.

The three of them sat with that.

That's unsettling, Dex said.

Yes, she said.

Does it change anything?

She thought about it honestly.

No, she said. The route is the route. The transmitter is the transmitter. Whether he was looking for me specifically or broadcasting into the void and I happened to hear it -- the signal is the same. The mechanism is real. The mission is the same mission.

But the man might be different than you thought, Luz said.

The man was always different than I thought. I never thought about him very much. She looked at the map. I thought about the route.

Luz looked at her with the expression she had when she was assessing something.

That's going to have to change, she said. When we get there.

I know, Maren said.


---

That night she added the note to the map.

In the margin, in the very small printing she used for things she didn't want to lose: Who is waiting?

She looked at it for a while.

Then she looked at Kingman, at the end of the route, at the solid line she'd drawn there because it was a known point even if what she'd find there was not yet known.

She folded the map and put it away and lay down and listened to the panhandle wind against the building and thought about a man alone in a facility for eighteen months, broadcasting twice a day into the static, waiting for someone he might have known was coming.

What was that, she thought. What was the name for that.

She didn't find a name for it. She fell asleep without one.


## Chapter 26

The Drift Thins

Three days outside Amarillo, the Drift almost disappeared.

She'd been expecting a gradient -- denser near the city, thinner as you moved away from population centers, thinning further as you moved into the agricultural open -- but the thinning here was more pronounced than she'd predicted. She went half a day without seeing a single Drifted individual.

She didn't trust it.

The signal was weaker out here. She'd been thinking about what the Cartographer had told them -- the signal drove aggregation, the signal came from Kingman, Kingman was still west -- and the logic of it meant the thinning here, in the geographical dead zone between population centers, was consistent. The Drift aggregated where two conditions coincided: population density to provide bodies, and signal strength to drive the aggregation behavior. In the panhandle flatland, midway between Oklahoma City behind them and Amarillo ahead, both conditions were at their minimum.

Which meant the thinning was real. Which meant she still didn't trust it, because real things could be wrong.

They relaxed slightly. She told them not to.

The Drift can reconcentrate faster than it disperses, she said. A change in wind direction, a change in temperature gradient, anything that shifts the aggregation cue can bring a thin zone back to dense in hours. We treat this the same as a dense zone.

Luz and Dex received this and maintained their discipline.

She found the fuel depot from the road sign -- a commercial fueling station for agricultural equipment, the kind with the large-capacity above-ground tanks. The tanks were empty, predictably, someone had pumped them early in the collapse. But the structure was solid: a metal roof over the pump island, walls on three sides, a locked tool cabinet that Dex opened in two minutes, which contained standard agricultural repair supplies and, in the back corner, a case of bottled water that had been there since before and was still sealed.

They were doing inventory on the cabinet when the cluster found them.

Not a mass. A cluster, maybe forty people, moving east through the depot lot in the particular diagonal pattern she'd come to recognize as a response to a distant sound cue -- something had attracted them, something to the east, and they were moving toward it in the loose spread of early aggregation.

Forty, moving diagonally across their position.

She assessed the clearance. The cluster would pass through the lot in the next three minutes if their direction held. If it held. She watched their bearing.

It was Luz who moved.

She stepped out from behind the tool cabinet -- not into the cluster's path, but to its edge, to a position where she could watch the whole group -- and she watched the way the cluster moved.

Then she came back.

They're going to pass south of us, she said. Fifteen feet clearance if we hold here.

Maren had been calculating the same thing but from the north side. Luz had calculated it from the south and gotten the same number.

You read them, Maren said.

ER, Luz said. You watch bodies. You learn how they move when they're not choosing to.

They held. The cluster moved past, fifteen feet south, angled east, the particular forward lean and the unfocused gaze. One of them had a child's backpack -- a small purple thing with a cartoon on it, absurd against the Drift.

When they were past, Dex said: How long have you been able to do that?

I don't know, Luz said. Since Memphis, I think. I didn't think it was a thing until just now.

Maren looked at her. You never told me you could read them.

You never asked.

They looked at each other. The specific look of two people who had been operating beside each other without fully seeing each other.

From now on, Maren said. If you see something I don't see, you tell me.

From now on, Luz agreed.

They went back to the supply cabinet and finished the inventory.


## Chapter 27

Night Survey, Texas

The irrigation system was the biggest she'd ever documented.

She'd gone out after dark -- a habit now, the night surveys, the two AM trips that weren't about the route but were about something else she hadn't fully named. The system was visible from the road during the day, the center-pivot irrigation arms extending from the central pivot point in long metal spokes, six of them, each maybe a quarter mile long, rotating slowly around the pivot to cover the circular field.

Except they weren't rotating. Not anymore. The power was out. The pivots had stopped wherever they were when the grid went down, frozen in their last position, and now they stood in the dark like the spokes of an enormous compass rose, pointing to whatever direction they'd been pointing when everything stopped.

She walked out to the pivot point and shone her headlamp up at the mechanism.

It was beautiful engineering. Not in the decorative sense -- there was nothing decorative about agricultural irrigation equipment -- but in the functional sense, the kind of beauty that comes from a problem solved with exactly the right amount of material in exactly the right configuration. The pivot mechanism at the center: a water-sealed rotary joint, the main supply coming up from the well below, the distribution manifold routing water to each arm, the drive motors that had turned the whole assembly around its axis. Somebody had worked this problem out in the 1950s and the solution had been so good that it was still the solution sixty years later.

She sat down at the base of the pivot and took out the field notebook.

She sketched the whole thing. The pivot point, the six arms at their stopped bearings -- she measured the bearings with the compass, noted the length of each arm as best she could estimate in the dark, drew the circular field boundary, added the well head location and the pump house and the above-ground supply line that ran from the pivot back to the main irrigation canal.

The sketch took an hour. It was much more detailed than anything the route required.

She noticed this while she was drawing and kept drawing.

She had been doing this more often -- the unnecessary detail, the sketches that were about the thing itself rather than about getting past the thing. She'd done it with the culvert in Oklahoma, the dome in the civil defense hub, the hand pump at the farmhouse. Each time she'd recognized it as unnecessary and done it anyway.

She thought about what Rabbit had said: You've been making the drawings more detailed than they need to be. He'd seen it too, before she'd named it to herself.

She hadn't been making drawings for herself since Diego. Since before Diego, maybe. Since she'd decided that the drawings needed to be useful to justify the time they took, because her time was limited and she was a professional and professionals made drawings for purposes.

She looked at the sketch of the irrigation system.

It was a good sketch. The system was a good system. Nobody would see this sketch. It served no operational purpose. She'd made it anyway because the thing deserved to be drawn, because the thing was worth the attention of someone's eyes and hands, because paying attention to a thing was a form of -- she didn't know the word. Not respect exactly. Something adjacent to respect. The acknowledgment that the thing existed and was worth knowing.

She sat at the base of the pivot for a while after she'd finished.

Then she walked back to the shelter.


---

In the morning she showed it to Dex.

She put the notebook on the table in front of him -- they were eating at a kitchen table in the farmhouse they'd found a mile from the pivot -- and opened it to the sketch.

He looked at it for a long time without speaking. He turned the notebook slightly to read the bearing notations. He looked at the arm lengths. He looked at the pivot mechanism sketch.

You drew the whole thing, he said.

It's a good system, she said.

He looked at her over the notebook.

Yes, he said. It is.

He handed the notebook back and went back to his food. She put it away.

She thought he understood what she'd said, which was not about the system.


## Chapter 28

Luz and the Map

Luz asked to see the full map on the third day out of Amarillo.

Not a section -- the full map. All of it, from Memphis to Kingman, the solid and dashed and light-pencil sections, the notes in the margins, the word Kingman underlined at the western edge.

Maren spread it on the hood of an abandoned pickup they'd stopped beside for a water break. The afternoon light was flat and cold and perfect for reading maps -- no glare, even illumination, the world at its most legible.

Luz studied it.

She didn't touch it. She had the medical person's instinct for not disturbing things that belonged to someone else's professional domain. She looked at it the way she'd look at an x-ray: for the information, not the object.

What's your confidence level on the blank sections? she said.

Sixty percent. Maybe less through New Mexico.

Luz nodded slowly, tracing the route with her eyes. The solid sections -- Memphis to the Arkansas line, the Oklahoma City tunnel approach, the Amarillo corridor. The dashed sections -- the Arkansas flatlands, the Texas Panhandle, the New Mexico approach. The light-pencil sections -- the mountain terrain through central New Mexico, the Arizona desert approach, the final fifty miles to Kingman.

Okay, Luz said.

Just: okay. Maren had expected something -- a question, a challenge, a recalculation. She'd prepared for the conversation.

That's it? she said.

Luz looked up from the map.

You got us this far, she said. Show me what you need from me for the rest.

Maren looked at the map. The light-pencil sections. The fifty miles to Kingman that she'd never mapped and couldn't map from here.

The New Mexico section is the hardest, she said. The infrastructure is sparse and the terrain is variable. I'm going to be reading the landscape in real time more than I'm used to. I need -- she stopped. I need to not be managing other things while I'm doing that.

Luz waited.

I need you to watch the group, she said. The way you watch the Drift. The way you watched Rabbit. I'm not good at that kind of attention and you are and if I'm focusing on the terrain I won't be focusing on the people.

Okay, Luz said.

And I need you to tell me what you see. Even if I don't ask. Especially if I don't ask.

Luz looked at her.

Maren, she said. I need you to tell me something.

She waited.

What happened to your brother?

The question landed differently than she'd expected. She'd been braced for the clinical version -- symptoms, timeline, the factual record. Not the direct question.

He was Drifted in the first month, she said. I was underground when it happened. I finished my survey before I surfaced.

Luz was very still.

Did you know he was in trouble?

She had known. Not with certainty -- she'd had a feeling, the kind of feeling she'd learned to classify as data rather than emotion, because feelings that arrived like data usually were data. She'd had the feeling and she'd been in the middle of a complicated junction survey and she'd told herself she'd be up in forty minutes.

She'd been up in four hours.

I had a sense, she said. I chose the survey.

She had not said that to anyone. She had carried it as a fact, the way you carry a measurement you've taken and confirmed and don't need to take again. The survey had needed three more hours. She had known Diego was in trouble the way she knew when a tunnel was going to flood -- not with certainty, but with the particular pressure of accumulated evidence. She had overridden it. She had finished the survey. And when she surfaced he was already in the mass on Beale Street and she had stood at the hatch with the survey pack on her back and watched him until she couldn't see him anymore and then she had gone home and made coffee and sat at the kitchen table and looked at the map of the system she'd just finished.

Luz didn't respond immediately. She was looking at the map.

Is that why the tunnels? she said. Not just professional. Something else.

Maren folded her hands on the hood of the truck.

Maybe, she said. Both things can be true.

Yes, Luz said. They can.

She looked at the map.

I need you to tell me things I don't ask about, Maren said. I miss things about people. I know I miss them.

I know, Luz said. I will.

She rolled the map back up and put it in the tube.

New Mexico in three days, she said.

Three days, Luz said.

They got back on the road.


## Chapter 29

Amarillo on the Horizon

They saw Amarillo from twelve miles out.

The skyline was still standing -- the taller buildings, six or eight of them, visible against the west like a statement. She'd been watching for it and there it was, the familiar geometry of a mid-sized American city, the glass-and-steel downtown that looked like every other glass-and-steel downtown from this distance, which was both reassuring and disorienting.

They assessed it from the grain elevator she'd identified on the topo as the best vantage point -- a standard grain elevator on the eastern approach, twelve stories, accessed by the same interior ladder she'd come to think of as a standard feature of these structures. She was getting good at grain elevator ladders.

From the top: the city spread west and south, the downtown cluster at the center, the residential grid extending to the edges. She watched for twenty minutes.

The Drift pattern was different from Oklahoma City. Less organized -- the mass here was thinner, more dispersed, moving in smaller clusters rather than the coordinated corridor flow she'd seen in the larger city. A smaller city with a smaller pre-collapse population, and the thin zone they'd been moving through had reduced the density further.

It was navigable.

Probably navigable, she corrected herself. She was done with unqualified assertions about things she hadn't physically moved through.

The water treatment facility was on the north side. She'd identified it from the pre-collapse infrastructure maps -- a 1970s-era facility, large enough to serve the city's population, with storage tanks that might still hold treated water from before the grid went down. If the storage was intact and hadn't been contaminated, it was the best water source they'd find before New Mexico.

Dex, beside her, did the math she was already doing.

North of center, he said. The Drift concentration is lower there.

Yes. The north corridor is thinner. We come in from the northeast, move west along the utility corridor -- she pointed -- there's a buried water main that runs that route, I can see the marker posts from here. We stay on the north side, hit the facility, refill, and exit west on the Coulter route.

And if the facility is compromised?

There's a university on the west side. Texas A&M Amarillo. I want to look at the engineering building regardless -- instrument salvage. And the university campus will have potable water from the campus emergency supply if the facility doesn't work out.

Two targets, Luz said.

Two targets.

Luz looked at the city. She was doing the medical math -- what they needed, what they had, what the crossing would cost in energy and exposure.

What do we need medically from the city? she said. Not just water.

Maren looked at her.

You tell me.

Luz took out her notebook. Wound closure -- we're low. Ibuprofen -- almost out, and I've been rationing it. Betadine or equivalent. If we can find it, oral rehydration salts -- the Texas crossing was hard on everyone's electrolytes and I've been watching for signs.

Hospital or pharmacy?

University health center if the campus is intact. They stock differently than hospitals -- more general, less specialized. More likely to still have the basic supplies.

We add the health center to the university stop, Maren said.

Dex had been quiet for a while. Now he said: The plan is not a good plan.

Both of them looked at him.

He was looking at the city. Not with the expression he had when he was dismissing something -- he said it the way he said everything: precisely, with no more weight than the observation required.

It's not a good plan, he said again. It's the best available plan. I've worked on enough jobs to know the difference and I want us all to know we're working with the second, not the first.

Maren looked at the city.

Yes, she said. That's exactly right.

He nodded.

Okay. Then let's make the best available plan work.

They climbed back down the ladder.


---

The 6 AM broadcast the next morning was the clearest they'd received since Memphis. The distance was short enough now that the signal came in almost static-free, the Cartographer's voice with a presence it hadn't had from a thousand miles away.

The facility is stable, he said. I'm still here. His voice was the same: measured, flat, the information stripped of ornament. But something in it was different from the early broadcasts. Something that she would not call emotion but that was adjacent to it -- the quality of a person who had been waiting for a long time and believed, for the first time, that the wait might be ending. If you're in Texas, you're going to make it.

A pause.

I think you're going to make it.

Static.

The three of them sat in the silence after.

He thinks we're going to make it, Dex said.

He thinks someone is going to make it, Maren said. He may not know how many of us there are.

Does it matter? Luz said.

She thought about it.

No, she said. It doesn't matter.

She turned the radio off and put it in the pack and looked west at Amarillo.


## Chapter 30

West

They went through Amarillo in eleven hours.

The water treatment facility was intact. The storage tanks held 800,000 gallons of treated water from the last processing run before the grid went down, and the storage structure had maintained positive pressure, which meant the water quality was still within acceptable parameters by everything Luz could test with what they had. They filled every container. They drank as much as they could. They filled again.

The university health center had been cleared but not thoroughly -- the prescription medications were gone, taken in the first weeks, but the general supplies were still there, the things that weren't valuable enough to take first: wound closure strips, antiseptic, ibuprofen in the large-size bottles, the oral rehydration salts Luz had asked for. She went through the shelves with the systematic focus of someone who knew exactly what she needed and didn't take what she didn't.

Maren found the engineering building.

The instrument cabinet was locked and she didn't have the right pick for the lock and she stood in front of it for forty seconds and then Dex walked up behind her and took it off the wall with a pry bar, frame and all, and set it on the floor, and she opened it.

Inside: a Leica total station, which was too heavy to carry. A set of optical levels, also too heavy. But in the back: a quality hand level, a Brunton compass in better condition than hers, a pocket transit, a 100-foot steel tape, and a case of 0.5 millimeter H pencils, Japanese, the brand she'd been using before the collapse.

She picked up the pencil case and looked at it.

She put it in her pack.


---

They exited Amarillo on the Coulter route as planned and were in open country by four in the afternoon. The city fell behind them the way all cities fell behind -- gradually, and then completely, and then it was just a skyline in the east mirror-image of the skyline they'd approached.

She didn't look back. She looked at the route.

They found the grain elevator she'd identified on the topo for their shelter -- the right structure, the right position, the interior secured and the roof sound. She did the structural check while Dex set up the camp stove and Luz organized the new medical supplies.

When she finished the check she climbed to the top.

The elevator roof gave her a full 360 -- the flat panhandle in every direction, the Amarillo skyline to the east already fading into the dark, the New Mexico border to the west invisible but real, the stars beginning to come out in the clear November sky.

She looked west for a long time.

The route from here: three days to the New Mexico line, then the mountain approach, then the desert, then Kingman. The light-pencil sections. The inference. The first principles.

She had been thinking about what Dex had said. The best available plan. And about what Luz had said: you got us this far. And about what Rabbit had said, the last morning in the farmhouse, over the chicory coffee that was technically coffee in the way that a canal was technically a river.

You were worth it. That's what I wanted to tell you.

She had not been worth following in the way she'd originally understood the phrase -- she had not been competent enough, not infallible enough, not sufficient on her own. The map had been wrong about the Oklahoma tunnel blockage. She'd miscalculated the water situation. She'd kept Rabbit's secret too long and managed the information like a survey report and gotten the same thing wrong twice in different forms.

She had been worth following in a different way. Worth the risk. Worth the commitment of another person's judgment, the specific bet that she would not be sufficient but would be enough.

Worth it.

She looked at Kingman on the map she was holding in her head. The solid line at the western end, the point where inference became destination.

She could get there.

She was not sure she could get there on her own. She was sure she could get there.

The distinction was new.

Dex's voice from below: Come eat.

She climbed back down the ladder and sat with them in the yellow light of the camp stove and ate and didn't say anything about what she'd been thinking, because the thinking wasn't finished. But she was aware, in the way she was aware of grade changes in a tunnel, that something had shifted -- the angle of the thing she was doing had changed, very slightly, toward something she didn't have a name for yet.

She was aware of it the way she was aware of the people across from her.

She was aware of it the way she was aware of the people across from her. Not separately. Together.

She looked west. The sky was enormous and clear. Dex refilled her mug without asking. Luz was writing in her notebook, the medical record she kept, the running account of where they were and what they had and what they'd used and what remained.

There it is, Dex said. Quietly, the way you said something true that didn't need emphasis.

Yes, she said.

And something in her voice meant more than the map.

She held the mug and looked at the dark and thought about the route, and thought about the people on it, and for the first time in a long time thought about both at once without choosing between them.

Tomorrow: New Mexico. She was ready.

She was ready.
