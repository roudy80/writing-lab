# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter One Draft

ONE
First Call
The call came in at quarter to six, still dark enough that the security light at the Harlan property hadn't switched off yet. Chest pain, possible cardiac. Seventy-one-year-old male, wife on the line. Patrice had the rig moving before dispatch finished the address.
I worked the monitor while she drove. Route 9 to FM 116, five minutes out if the train wasn't sitting on the crossing. It wasn't. We made it in four.
His name was Arthur Harlan. He was sitting up in bed when we came through the door, which was a decent sign. His wife, Margaret, stood at the foot of the bed with her arms crossed tight across her chest, holding herself together. She'd been crying but had stopped. People stop crying when the paramedics arrive because crying feels like giving up and they aren't ready to give up.
"Started about an hour ago," she said. "I told him to call sooner. He said it was just gas."
"It might be," I said.
Arthur looked at me the way men his age sometimes look at me, like he was trying to decide if I was old enough to trust. I'm thirty-eight. I've been doing this six years. Before that I spent ten years keeping soldiers alive in places where the nearest hospital was an hour away by helicopter on a good day. I don't tell people that. It doesn't come up.
"Pressure or squeezing?" I asked.
"Squeezing," he said. "Like somebody's got a fist in there."
"On a scale of one to ten."
"Six." He paused. "Maybe seven."
I got the IV started on the first stick, which mattered less than it sounds because the vein was right there, a man who'd spent his life doing physical work, his arm resting across the bed rail like he'd done this before, which I found out later he had. Second cardiac event. The first one was eight years ago and he'd told nobody except Margaret because he didn't want the fuss.
That's a thing about men in Drayfield. They don't want the fuss. We had him loaded and moving in eleven minutes. Patrice called ahead to Harker City. I sat in back with Arthur and kept my hand on his wrist more than I needed to because sometimes that's the medicine, the knowing that someone is right there with their hand on you and not going anywhere.
"My tomatoes aren't going to water themselves," he said somewhere on 116.
"Margaret'll get them."
"She'll overwater them. She always overwater them."
"Then they'll have a good story to tell when you get back," I said.
He made a sound that was almost a laugh. His pressure was stabilizing. His color was better than it had been in the house. I've done enough of these to know when a man is going to make it and when he isn't, and Arthur Harlan was going to make it. He had too much to complain about. Men with that much to complain about don't go easily.
We pulled into Harker City Regional at six-forty. I handed him off to the ER team, gave my report, signed where I needed to sign. Patrice was already restocking the rig. This is the part of the job nobody thinks about, the refilling, the paperwork, the making sure everything is where it needs to be for the next call. It's not glamorous. It's how people don't die. The automatic doors into the main entrance were propped open. Someone had wedged a rubber doorstop under the left panel, the kind of permanent temporary fix you see in hospitals everywhere. A fan of cool air came through from inside, antiseptic and underneath it that older smell, the one that's always there no matter how hard they clean, the smell of what hospitals are actually for.
I was walking back to the rig. Patrice was around the far side. I had the clipboard in one hand and my radio on my hip, and I stepped through the open doorframe to sign one more form at the desk just inside, and then I was somewhere else.
Not dizzy. Not a blackout. Somewhere else. Saturday morning. The kitchen of the house I grew up in.
The light through the window over the sink was the particular yellow of early summer, the kind that means it's already warm outside and will be hot by ten. The floor was the same speckled linoleum it always was, the corner near the back door worn pale where three generations of boots had come and gone.
I was twelve years old. I could feel it, the specific lightness of a body that hasn't accumulated much yet, the way sound lands differently when you're that size. I was sitting at the table eating cereal, the kind with the marshmallows, and I had the box turned sideways to read the back because I'd already read the back a hundred times but I still did it.
My father was at the other end of the table.
He had a carburetor in front of him on a folded shop rag, an old Briggs & Stratton, I could tell from the housing, and he was breaking it down in the way he did everything, methodically, laying each part out to the right in the order it came off so he'd know the order to put it back. He had a cup of coffee at his elbow. He wasn't talking. The radio above the refrigerator was on low, something country, but I couldn't have told you what song.
I didn't look up from the cereal box. He didn't look up from the carburetor.
That was the whole thing. A man and a boy at a table on a Saturday morning, not talking, not needing to. The refrigerator hummed. Outside, a truck went by on the county road. My father's hands moved over the small parts with that particular certainty of someone who has done a thing so many times that his hands know the way without him.
I watched his hands.
I don't think I watched his hands the first time. I came back in the hospital entrance, standing in the doorframe with one hand on the metal jamb and the other gripping the clipboard hard enough that the edge of it had left a mark across my palm.
Patrice was coming around the front of the rig. She stopped when she saw me.
"What happened?"
"Got dizzy," I said.
She looked at me for a moment. Patrice has a way of looking at you that doesn't accuse or push, it just waits. She's been doing this job longer than I have and she knows the difference between a man who's dizzy and a man who is describing something in the least alarming way available to him.
"You eat this morning?"
"I'm fine," I said.
She looked at me one more second. Then she turned back to the rig.
I didn't follow right away. I stood in that doorframe for another minute, one hand still on the jamb, and looked down at my palm where the clipboard had pressed in. The mark was already fading.
I felt completely clearheaded. That was the strangest part. Not the way you feel after your body has done something strange to you, not the heavy cotton of a near-faint or the throb behind your eyes after you've stood up too fast. Just clear. Like I'd been somewhere real and had come back.
I went back to the rig. We drove to Drayfield. Patrice didn't bring it up again, which meant she was saving it, which meant she was going to bring it up later at a time of her choosing.
I signed in at the station, left my notes on the desk, walked to my truck. My house is on the east side of town, the rented single-story on Aldridge Road with the covered back porch and the screen door that sticks in summer. It was summer. The door stuck. I pulled it hard the way I always do and went inside.
The kitchen was the same as I'd left it. Coffee maker, cereal box on the counter, a glass from the night before still in the sink because I'd meant to wash it before the shift and hadn't. The morning light came in the window over the sink. A different window than the one in my memory, a different angle, but the same early color, that particular yellow.
I sat down at the table.
I didn't have a carburetor. I didn't have anything. I just sat.
My father had been dead two years. Heart attack, second one, the kind that doesn't give you the same leeway as the first. I was with him. He didn't suffer long. That's what people say and in his case it was true, and I've seen enough of both kinds to know the difference, and I am grateful for the difference, and I also know that fast doesn't mean you're ready, that fast doesn't mean there wasn't more left to say.
I'd been trying to figure out for two years what that was. What was left to say. I had the shape of it but not the words, the way you can sometimes feel a thing pressing on the inside of a closed door but you can't see what it is.
I sat at my kitchen table for a long time with my hands flat on the surface of it.
I'd watched his hands.
I didn't watch his hands the first time. Twenty-six years ago on a Saturday morning when I was twelve years old and my father was taking apart a carburetor and I was reading the back of a cereal box, I had not watched his hands. I'd been there. I hadn't been watching.
I got up, washed the glass from the night before, set it in the rack.
I stood at the sink for a minute looking out the window at the backyard, the dry grass and the fence line and the line of cedar elms along the east edge that block the wind in winter. Same trees every morning. I'd stopped seeing them.
I went to bed. I had a shift in fourteen hours.
I lay in the dark and stared at the ceiling and did not sleep for a long time.
