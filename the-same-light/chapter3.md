# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Three Draft

THREE
The Fence
From the notebook, Third slip. I was 17. Storm had come through two nights before and taken out three posts on the east fence, cedar posts, old ones, soft at the base where the ground had been holding moisture for years. Dad had been watching that fence. He knew those posts. He'd probably known they were coming down before the storm gave them an excuse.
We worked all morning. He didn't explain what we were doing, just started and expected me to follow, and I followed because there was nothing else to do on a Saturday in July in a town with one stoplight. I was annoyed the whole time. I had somewhere to be that afternoon, a girl, a plan, nothing serious, and I kept checking the angle of the sun like that would move it.
Watching myself be annoyed at a man who was teaching me everything. I don't know what to call what I felt standing there. Not guilt exactly. Something with more heat than guilt. Like being angry at yourself is too easy so you go looking for a better target and can't find one.

I'd been up since four-thirty. Shift started at five-forty-five, which meant the alarm went off at four-thirty and I lay there for ten minutes in the specific way of a person who already knows they're not going back to sleep, staring at the ceiling while the room came gray and then lighter gray and then the color of an ordinary Tuesday.
The drive to the station takes nine minutes. I know because I've driven it enough times that my body knows it without counting: two turns, the grain elevator going past on the left where the road bends, the water tower with the Drayfield Wildcats faded on the side, the station coming up on the right with the bay doors already open because Patrice is always there before me. She says she's a morning person. I believe her the way I believe people when they say things like that, which is to say I believe it's true but I don't understand it.
I had my coffee in a travel mug that leaks slightly at the seam and has for two years. I keep meaning to replace it. I don't.
The bay was lit up and smelled like diesel and the industrial soap we use on the rigs. Patrice was running inventory in the back of the ambulance, moving through it with the efficiency of someone who has done it so many times she could do it in the dark, which she has, which I've watched her do during a night shift when the interior light burned out and we didn't have a replacement bulb.
"Morning," she said without looking up.
"Morning."
I set my coffee on the bench by the door and pulled on my jacket and walked toward the bay. The big roll-up door was all the way up, the parking lot outside still dim, the sky just starting to go from black to something with more blue in it. I could smell the air coming in, dry grass and a little diesel from the road, the particular smell of early morning in a Texas summer before the heat takes over and burns everything else out.
I stepped through the bay door. The field was bright. Full morning, already hot at the edges, the sun a few hours up and working on the clay soil that had gone soft in the rain two days before and was now starting to crust at the surface. I could feel the heat coming off the ground even where I was standing, which was at the back corner of the east fence line on my father's property, holding two cedar posts upright against my chest because both my hands were occupied and my father needed them vertical while he tamped the base.
I was seventeen years old. I could feel it the same way I felt twelve in the kitchen, the body's particular weight and reach at a specific age, the way the posts felt heavy in a way that was more effort than I wanted to be making on a Saturday. The soil was dark where my father had dug the new holes, and the three bad posts were laid out in the grass behind us, their bases gone gray and punky with rot. I could see exactly where the storm had pushed them over. Two had snapped clean. One had just tilted and pulled free, the ground too soft to hold it.
My father was on his knees at the base of the first new hole, tamping packed soil around the post with a metal bar, working it in layers the way you're supposed to. He had a system. He always had a system. You packed a layer and tamped it and packed another and tamped it again, and you didn't move to the next layer until you were sure the previous one was right, and you didn't rush it because if you rushed it you'd be back in six months doing it again, and he wasn't going to be back in six months doing it again.
The seventeen-year-old version of me was not thinking about any of this.
What he was thinking about was a girl named Sarah Pruett who was waiting for him at the Dairy Queen in Meridian at two o'clock, and whether two o'clock was going to happen given that it was already nine-fifteen and there were two more posts to set and his father had not mentioned anything about stopping for lunch.
Standing in my thirty-eight-year-old body inside my seventeen-year-old body, watching my father's hands work the metal bar down into the packed clay, I understood something I didn't understand the first time: he wasn't going slow to frustrate me. He was going slow because slow was right. Because a post set right holds for twenty years and a post set fast holds for eighteen months, and my father had been maintaining this fence line since before I was born and he intended to maintain it after I was gone, and none of that had anything to do with Sarah Pruett or the Dairy Queen in Meridian.
He glanced up at me once. "Keep them vertical."
"They are vertical," I said.
He looked at the posts and then back at the hole without commenting, which was its own comment. I adjusted my grip and straightened the near post another half inch.
This is the thing about my father that took me most of my life to understand: he was almost never saying what he appeared to be saying. Keep them vertical was not a criticism. It was a correction, and a small one, and the reason he'd offered it instead of just taking the post from me and doing it himself was that he thought I was capable of it. The men in my father's life who he didn't think were capable of something, he just did the thing himself. He only corrected the people he believed in. I know that now. I spent most of my teens and twenties reading it backwards. He worked his way around the first post and then straightened and looked at it the way he looked at things he'd built or fixed, not with pride exactly, more like confirmation. Yes. That's what that should look like. Then he moved to the second hole.
The grass in the field beyond the fence was tall and going to seed, moving in a little wind that was doing almost nothing to cut the heat. The cattle were in the far pasture; I could see them as shapes at the distance, barely moving. A scissor-tailed flycatcher landed on the old fence wire ten feet away, looked at us, and left. My father watched it go with the same quality of attention he gave the post, unhurried, complete.
"You get to the Whitmore place yesterday?" he said. He meant the part of the county road where a tree had come down in the storm.
"Helped move it off the road. Yeah."
He nodded, tamping.
"Anybody's fence catch it?"
"No. Just the ditch."
He nodded again. This was how conversation worked with my father, not back and forth like a game, more like dispatches, each one landing and being received and then quiet, because quiet between those dispatches was not a problem that needed solving. My mother talked the way most people talk, in real time, working things out as she went. My father talked the way he did most things, after he'd already thought it through, and only then.
Standing there at seventeen I found this maddening in the way that only the people most like you can madden you, because I was exactly the same and I didn't know it yet.
He got the second post set. We moved to the third.
The third hole was the deepest because that corner post had to carry more load. It was where the fence line turned and ran back toward the barn, and the tension of the wire pulled on it from two directions. My father had dug it two feet deeper than the others. I know this because I helped dig it the day before and it had taken most of an afternoon and I'd complained about it and he'd said, "Corner posts need to go deep," and that was the whole explanation.
I understood the engineering of it now. A corner post set shallow fails first, and when it goes the whole line goes with it. But you can't see that from the surface. From the surface, all the posts look the same. The depth is invisible, and the depth is everything. We finished the third post a little past noon. My father walked the line and pushed on each post with his weight and they held and he seemed satisfied by this in the quiet way he was satisfied by things that worked the way they were supposed to work. He gathered the tamping bar and the extra soil and the bad posts and loaded them in the truck bed and then went to the cab and came back with two bottles of water, which had been there the whole time and which it had not occurred to me to ask about.
He handed me one. We stood in the shade of the truck and drank.
The field looked the same as it had before we started, except the fence line was right again. That's what a good repair looks like, not better than before, just correct. Invisible work. The kind that nobody notices until it fails, and when it's done right, it doesn't fail.
My father drank his water and looked at the fence and then looked out at the pasture where the cattle were still barely moving in the heat.
"You had somewhere to be today," he said.
It wasn't a question. He'd known.
"It's fine," I said, because it was one-fifteen and Sarah Pruett was not going to wait until one-fifteen and I'd understood that since about ten o'clock.
My father didn't say anything for a moment. He finished his water and capped the bottle and held it.
"Call ahead next time," he said. "If you've got somewhere."
That was it. That was the whole thing. Not an apology. He didn't think he'd done anything wrong and he hadn't, but an acknowledgment that I was a person with my own life, and he could factor that in, and next time I should say so. It was more generosity than I deserved given that I'd spent the morning being sullen about it instead of just telling him, and I didn't have the sense to recognize it as generosity at the time.
I said okay and went inside to call Sarah, who had not waited, which was also fair. I came back in the bay door of the station and sat down hard on the bumper of the rig. Not because my legs had given out, they hadn't, but because I needed to be on something solid.
Patrice's head appeared around the side of the ambulance. She looked at me the way she'd looked at me outside the hospital in Harker City, not alarmed, not moving toward me yet, just watching.
"You need something?"
"No." I looked at my hands. Same hands. Thirty-eight years of living in them. "I'm good."
She came around the front of the rig and stood with her arms crossed and looked at me for a moment without speaking. Patrice has children of her own, two in college now, and she has the particular patience of someone who has spent years waiting out the version of a thing that a person is willing to show before the real thing appears. She's used it on me before. I don't love it, but it works.
"That's the second time," she said.
"I know."
"Same thing as Harker City?"
I thought about how to answer that. The same in the sense that it happened. Different in the sense that this time I'd been somewhere real enough that I could still feel the weight of those posts against my chest, the soil working loose against my boots, the heat coming off clay that was done being wet. I could still feel the cold of the water bottle in my father's hand when he passed it to me. I could still hear the way he said it. You had somewhere to be today.
"Something like that," I said.
She looked at me another second. Then she uncrossed her arms and went back to her inventory and didn't push further, which is one of the things I respect most about her. She files things. She comes back to them. She doesn't make you have the conversation until you're ready to have it, but she doesn't let you pretend the conversation doesn't exist.
I sat on the bumper until I was ready to stand up. Then I stood up. I bought a composition notebook on the way home that night. Black cover, college-ruled. The kind that costs two dollars at the pharmacy on Main and has a Mead logo on the front and is no different from the ones I used in school except I'm not in school and the things I'm writing in it aren't homework.
I sat at the kitchen table for a long time before I wrote anything. The pen was there and the notebook was open to the first page and the kitchen was quiet and I just sat.
I'm not a journaling person. I've never been a journaling person. I don't process things by writing them down. I process them by doing something with my hands, by working, by running until my body is tired enough that my head gets quiet. Nora used to say I thought with my feet. She wasn't wrong. But whatever was happening to me wasn't going to get processed that way. I could feel that already. It was too interior for anything my hands could fix.
So I wrote.
I didn't try to explain it or theorize about it. I just wrote what had happened, the way I'd write a patient report: time, location, what I observed, how long it lasted, what I came back to. I wrote the hospital one first, then the station today. I wrote what I saw in both of them. I wrote what I noticed that I hadn't noticed the first time.
When I finished I looked at what I'd written. Two entries. Maybe four hundred words between them.
They'd both involved my father.
I hadn't thought about that consciously until I saw it on the page. Not the hospital, where I was twelve and my father was at the table, of course that one involved him. But the fence. The fence had been about the fence. It had been about a Saturday in July and three rotten posts and a girl who didn't wait. My father had been the one doing the work, yes, but the slip had felt like it was about the fence, about the work, about my own impatience, and yet when I wrote it out flat, there he was. Center of every frame. Saying things I hadn't heard right the first time.
I thought about that for a while.
Then I wrote: both times, my father.
Then I didn't know what else to write so I closed the notebook and put it in the drawer by the stove where I keep things I'm not ready to file anywhere else: batteries, extra keys, a birthday card from Cal that I haven't been able to put in the recycling and haven't been able to put anywhere more permanent either. Somewhere in the middle. A lot of things end up there.
I went to bed.
I didn't sleep well, but I slept.
In whatever I dreamed, I can tell you my father's hands were probably in it somewhere, moving over something that needed work, patient in the way they always were, doing the thing the right way and not another way, because that was just how he did things and always had been and I was only now, at thirty-eight years old, starting to understand that it had never been about the fence.
