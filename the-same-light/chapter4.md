# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Four Draft

FOUR
Cartography
From the notebook, I've been trying to find the shape of it. Not explain it. I don't think I can explain it, and I've stopped needing to. Just find the shape. What I know: three times. Hospital in Harker City, the bay door at the station, the bay door again. Thresholds, all of them. Doorways, frames, the place where inside becomes outside. I don't know if that matters or if I'm connecting dots that don't belong together. I don't know a lot of things. What I know is my father was in all three. Not incidentally. Centrally. And I was watching him in a way I didn't watch him the first time, and I came back each time feeling like I'd been somewhere more real than where I'd left from. That's the inventory. That's everything I've got.

Wednesday I worked a double. Eight in the morning until eight the next morning, which sounds worse than it is. The nights in Drayfield are usually quiet, the calls front-loaded into the afternoon and early evening, and by midnight you've either got a full night ahead of you or you don't, and mostly you don't. I spent the back half of the shift on the bunkroom cot reading a book I'd already read twice before, the kind of reading you do when you want your eyes occupied but you don't want to have to think.
We had a call at two in the morning. Elderly woman, chest tightness, turned out to be anxiety, her daughter had called her with bad news from three states away. She was embarrassed about calling us. I told her not to be, that chest tightness at two in the morning is always worth checking, and she made me coffee and Patrice drank two cups and I drank one and we sat in her kitchen for twenty minutes longer than we needed to because sometimes that's also the job.
Her kitchen smelled like the candles people light to make a house smell less like what it actually smells like when they live there alone. That particular sweetness that doesn't quite cover the underneath. I noticed it and then felt unkind for noticing it and then realized I wasn't being unkind, I was just noticing, and there is a difference.
We cleared the call at three-fifteen. I drove back in the quiet and went back to the bunkroom and lay on the cot and did not try to sleep because there was no point.
I thought about the notebook.
I'd written four entries now. Three slips and an observation I'd added the night before, not about a slip but about the pattern I was starting to see in them. I don't theorize well in writing. I'm better on my feet, thinking while I'm moving, but the act of putting it flat on a page had done something useful, had made the thing less large. When something lives only in your head it can fill whatever space is available to it. Writing it down gives it a fixed size. It's still the same thing, but now it has edges.
What I'd written the night before: Both slips at the station, one at the hospital. Frames. Doorways. The place where you step from one thing into another. Always him. Always something I wasn't watching the first time.
I lay on the cot and looked at the ceiling and let myself wonder, carefully, what that might mean. I'm not a mystical person. I don't reach for explanations that require me to set aside what I know about how the world works. But I also spent ten years keeping people alive in situations where the difference between life and death was sometimes a matter I could explain technically and sometimes a matter I could not, and I've learned to be careful about the word impossible. The world is larger than the part of it I understand. I've seen enough to know that much.
So I let myself wonder. And what I came to, lying there at three in the morning in a bunkroom in Drayfield while Patrice slept in the other cot with the particular efficiency of someone who can sleep anywhere, was this: I didn't need to understand it. I just needed to pay attention.
It was the same thing my father had been trying to teach me at the fence. I'd missed it then. I wasn't going to miss it now. Cal called Thursday evening, while I was eating dinner. I almost didn't answer because I had a mouthful of food and my phone was across the kitchen and I'd had a long day, and then I thought about the reading night, about how he'd found me in the room before he looked down at his page, and I got up and got the phone.
"Dad."
"Hey, bud."
"Are you eating?"
"Just finished," I said, which was close enough.
I could hear the television in the background at Nora's, something animated, and the sound of him walking to another room and closing a door, which meant he wanted to talk without the noise. He does that sometimes, seeks out the quiet for a conversation he's decided is serious. He's nine years old and he already knows that some conversations need a different room. I don't know where he learned that. Maybe he was born with it.
"I have a project," he said.
"What kind?"
"For school. We have to pick an animal and find out everything about it and make a poster. I picked hawks."
"Good choice."
"Did Grandpa Dale ever tell you about hawks?"
I set my fork down. Outside my kitchen window the sky was going orange at the edges, the sun getting low behind the tree line. A mockingbird was doing its thing in the cedar elms, running through its catalog.
"He did," I said. "Once or twice."
"What did he say?"
I tried to pull it up and found I could get the shape of it but not the details. I could remember him pointing once, standing at the fence line on the back of his property, tracking something moving in a slow circle high up. I could remember that he'd said something about the way they hunted, something about patience, about the waiting being the skill. But the specifics were gone, worn smooth the way things get when you don't tend to them.
"I don't remember everything he said," I told Cal. "But I remember he had a lot of respect for them. He used to watch them."
"They can see eight times better than us," Cal said, with the authority of a person who has recently read one fact and is deploying it. "Their eyes are bigger than their brain."
"That's something."
"Did he ever show you one up close?"
"Not up close. He'd point them out when we were working outside. He knew where they nested on the property."
A pause. I could hear Cal processing this the way he processes things, quietly, filing it somewhere.
"Do you know where?" he asked.
"I could probably find it," I said, which was either true or something I was telling myself. "I'll find out more for you, okay? For your project."
"Okay." Another pause. "Dad?"
"Yeah."
"Do you miss him?"
The question landed the way his questions sometimes do, sideways, from a direction I hadn't seen coming. He wasn't asking to be difficult. He was asking because he wanted to know, because he is a person who wants to know the real answer to things and has not yet learned to be afraid of asking.
"Yeah," I said. "I do."
"Me too," he said. Simple as that. And then: "Okay. I have to go do my reading."
"All right. Love you, bud."
"Love you." And he was gone.
I stood in my kitchen holding my phone for a minute after he hung up. The mockingbird had moved on. The orange in the sky had deepened to something closer to red. I thought about my father standing at a fence line pointing up at a slow circle in the sky, and I thought about what he'd said about patience, about the waiting being the skill, and I thought about how I didn't remember the exact words and now I never would.
I thought: I need to find out.
And then I thought, with a clarity that surprised me: I think I can. There were two calls Friday. A kitchen fire on the north side of town that was already out by the time we arrived, the homeowner having dealt with it himself with admirable if reckless efficiency, a grease fire, the right call being to cover it, which he'd done with a sheet pan, and he was proud of himself in the way people are proud when they've done the correct thing under pressure, which is a feeling I understand. The second call was a kid on a bicycle who'd gone over his handlebars on the county road shoulder and needed stitches above his eyebrow.
The kid was ten, maybe eleven, and he was trying extremely hard not to cry, which I respected, and when I told him so he relaxed slightly and let himself bleed a little more naturally, which made the wound easier to manage. His mother was there by the time we got there and she was cycling between fury at the bicycle and terror for her son in the specific rhythm mothers have, and his father stood behind her with his hands in his pockets saying nothing useful but being present, which was what was needed.
I thought about that on the drive back. The father standing there with his hands in his pockets, not saying anything useful, just being a body in the room. I used to think that was the passive option, the lesser thing, when I was young enough to believe that doing something was always more valuable than being something. I've revised that. There are moments where being present is the active choice, the harder one, and the people who understand that are usually the ones who've been through enough to know the difference.
My father was one of those people. It took me until he was two years gone to see it clearly. Saturday morning I woke early and lay in bed for a while and then got up and made coffee and took it to the back porch.
The yard was still and cool in the way that yards are in early morning before the heat reasserts itself, the grass holding a little moisture, the cedar elms at the back edge motionless. I drank my coffee and watched the light change on the east side of the fence and didn't think about anything in particular, which is its own kind of thinking, the kind that happens under the surface while the top of your mind is just watching light move.
I'd brought the notebook out with me. I set it on the rail of the porch and didn't open it yet. Just had it there.
I'd been trying, the last few days, to understand what I was waiting for. Not the slips specifically. I'd already accepted that those weren't mine to summon. But something larger. Some sense of what this was building toward, if it was building toward anything. I'm a person who likes to know where I'm going before I start walking, which has served me in some situations and cost me in others. Nora used to say I planned everything except the things that mattered most, and she wasn't wrong about that, and the fact that she was right about it didn't make it easier to hear.
I opened the notebook and read what I'd written. Four entries, maybe six hundred words total. More than I'd thought but not more than I needed.
I wrote a fifth entry: Cal asked about the hawks today. Dad used to watch them from the back fence line. I know where he'd have found them on the property, the stand of live oaks on the northeast corner. That was always the kind of place they'd use. I should take Cal out there sometime. The family from Abilene might not mind. I haven't been back. I keep not going back. I'm not sure what I'm waiting for there either.
I closed the notebook.
The sun was fully up now and the cool was going out of the morning. I finished my coffee and listened to the mockingbird start up again in the cedar elms, working through the same catalog it always worked through, tireless, committed to the repetition, covering everything it knew. That evening I stood in my front doorway.
I'd been thinking about it all day in the background, the way you think about something when you're not letting yourself think about it directly, and by evening I'd made up my mind to try. Not with any real expectation. Just to see.
I stood in the frame of the front door with my hand on each side of it, the way I'd stood at the back door a week ago, and I looked out at the street. A truck went past. The streetlight at the corner had a moth circling it, big lazy loops. The Hendersons across the street had their porch light on and their front curtains open, the blue flicker of a television inside.
I let myself be still. I stayed with it longer than felt comfortable, which was the point, or was at least the experiment. The air was warm and smelled like cut grass from somewhere down the street. A door closed somewhere. A dog, not the beagle, a different one, barked twice and stopped.
Nothing happened.
No narrowing at the edges. No change in the air. No sense of the world preparing to give way to some other version of itself. Just my front doorway on a Saturday evening in July, and the street outside it, and everything exactly where it was supposed to be.
I stood there for five minutes. Maybe a little more.
Then I stepped back inside and closed the door.
I didn't know whether to feel relieved or disappointed and when I checked I found I felt both, which told me more than I was quite ready for, that some part of me had been hoping for it, had wanted the slip the way you want the dream you're not sure you should be having. That wanting needed to be noted. Filed somewhere. I wasn't sure yet what to do with it but I knew it was the kind of thing that gets larger if you don't look at it.
I wrote it in the notebook: tried the front door. Nothing. But I wanted it to work. That's new. Should probably think about that.
I closed the notebook and put it in the drawer and stood in the kitchen for a moment in the quiet of the house.
Somewhere out in the dark, on a property eight miles down County Road 114 that no longer belonged to anyone named Morrow, there was a stand of live oaks in the northeast corner where the hawks used to nest. My father had known where to find them. He had stood at a fence line and pointed up at something moving in slow circles against a blue sky and said something about patience, about the waiting being the skill, and I had been there and I had not been watching.
I was watching now.
I just needed to give it time.
