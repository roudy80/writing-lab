# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Five Draft

FIVE
What He Recognizes
From the notebook, No slip this week. Five days since the last one. I've stopped standing in doorways. Not because I've given up on understanding it. I don't think I can give up on it, it's not that kind of thing, but because trying to force it felt like the wrong posture. Like the trying itself was the problem. My father never went looking for the hawks. He just knew where to be, and he'd be there, and eventually they'd come.
Cal is here this weekend. I want to be present for that. Really present. I'm not sure I know how to do that as well as I think I do.

Nora pulled up at nine on Saturday morning in the blue Expedition she'd kept in the separation, the one with the parking sticker from Cal's school still in the corner of the windshield. I was on the porch with my coffee. Cal was out of the truck before it stopped moving, the way he always exits vehicles, like staying inside one second longer than necessary is a kind of failure.
"Dad." He said it the way he says it when he hasn't seen me in a few days and doesn't want to make a production of that, which means he says my name and then immediately asks about something else. "Do we have cereal?"
"We have cereal."
"The good kind?"
"Define good."
He was already past me and through the screen door, which he hit with the flat of his hand in a way that would have torn it off its hinges if he were twenty pounds heavier. I heard the kitchen cabinet open. I heard him decide the cereal was acceptable.
Nora came up the porch steps with Cal's bag on her shoulder. She'd cut her hair since I'd last seen her, not dramatically shorter but enough that it sat differently, and she looked rested in the particular way she sometimes looks when she's been sleeping well, which she hadn't when we were together, which was partly the nature of shared life with a young child and partly, I know now, the particular restlessness of being married to someone who is not fully there.
"He's been excited since Thursday," she said, handing me the bag.
"About the weekend or about the cereal?"
The corner of her mouth moved. "Both, I think." She looked past me through the screen door toward the kitchen sounds. "He has a soccer thing Sunday at two. I can pick him up here after if that works."
"That works."
She nodded. A beat of quiet that wasn't uncomfortable but wasn't entirely comfortable either, the kind that happens when two people have run out of the logistical things to say and are standing in the space where other things used to go.
"He's good," she said. Not answering a question I'd asked, just offering it. "He had a good week."
"I could tell when he called," I said. "The hawk project."
"He's very serious about the hawk project." She said it with the warmth she always has when she talks about Cal being serious about something, which is a warmth she doesn't edit or measure, just allows. It was one of the first things I loved about her, that she let herself be moved by him fully and without self-consciousness. Some people hold back from their own children in small ways, guarding against something I've never quite understood. Nora never did that.
"Drive safe," I said.
"Always." She went back down the porch steps and got in the truck and backed down the drive and was gone, and I stood on the porch and finished my coffee and listened to Cal open and close the refrigerator twice in the way that means he's looking for something specific and not finding it. We spent the morning on nothing in particular, which is the right way to spend a Saturday morning with a nine-year-old. He watched something on his tablet for an hour while I did laundry and cleaned the bathroom, the useful maintenance of a life that doesn't take care of itself. Around eleven he got restless and I suggested the yard and we went out and threw a ball back and forth for a while, not really playing anything, just the rhythm of throwing and catching, which has its own kind of quiet to it the same way working side by side has a quiet.
He threw hard for his size. He'd gotten that from somewhere. Not from me. I was never a natural athlete, I had to work for everything physical I could do, and not from Nora, who is coordinated but gentle with it. Maybe from Dale, who had been a decent baseball player in high school according to my mother, though I'd never seen evidence of it except once when he picked up a rock at the fence line and threw it at a fence post forty feet away to make a point about something and hit it dead center without appearing to think about it.
"Tell me about hawks," Cal said.
I caught the ball. "What do you want to know?"
"Anything Grandpa Dale said."
I thought about it for a real moment, not stalling but actually trying to excavate something useful from the place where those conversations lived, which was a place I didn't visit as often as I should have and now couldn't visit as easily as I'd like.
"He said they were patient," I told him. "He said the waiting was most of the skill. That they could hold a position in the air for a long time without moving, just watching, and that most animals can't do that. Most things that hunt have to keep moving. Hawks figured out how to be still."
Cal caught the ball and held it. He was thinking about this with the full weight of himself, the way he thinks about things that interest him, not performing interest but actually having it.
"That's kind of like fishing," he said.
"It's exactly like fishing."
He threw the ball back. "Did he fish?"
"Some. He wasn't obsessive about it. He liked being at the water more than he cared about catching anything."
"That's what I like too," Cal said, which surprised me, because I didn't know he'd fished enough to have a preference about it. I made a note to fix that. To take him and let him discover whether he was the catching kind or the being-there kind and not push him toward either.
We threw the ball until lunch. After lunch he wanted to play cards. We have a game we play called Rummy that I taught him last winter, real Rummy, not the kid version, and he has gotten good enough at it to beat me sometimes, which he is aware of and which gives him a quiet satisfaction he tries not to show too obviously, which is another thing that came from somewhere older than him.
We played three hands at the kitchen table. I won the first, he won the second, the third went long and complicated and ended in a disputed play that I ruled in my favor and which he contested with more legal precision than I expected from a nine-year-old.
"You can't do that," he said. "You already drew."
"I drew before I saw what it was."
"Doesn't matter. Once you draw the turn's over."
"That's not the rule."
"That's the rule." He said it with the certainty of someone who believes completely in what he's saying, which is the most convincing kind of wrong.
I looked at him across the table. He looked back at me with his cards held flat against his chest and his chin set in a way that I recognized, the particular angle of it, the stillness, and for a moment I couldn't speak because I was looking at my father's face in my son's face, the same expression Dale had used on me a hundred times when he'd made up his mind about something and was waiting for me to catch up.
"Fine," I said. "New rule going forward."
He nodded once, satisfied, and looked at his cards.
I lost the hand. The fourth hand is where it went wrong.
Not dramatically. Nothing broke. Nobody cried. But I watched it happen and I handled it badly and I knew I was handling it badly in real time and I handled it badly anyway, which is its own particular kind of failure.
Cal had a bad run of cards. Draw after draw of nothing useful, his hand getting larger and more unwieldy, and I could see him starting to tighten. He went quiet first, the specific quiet that means something is building rather than resolving, the kind I've learned to recognize in him if not always in time. He set his cards down carefully and looked at them.
"Do you want to deal again?" I asked.
"No."
"We can take a break."
"I don't want a break."
He picked his cards back up and stared at them. His jaw was set. His eyes were doing the thing they do when he's deciding whether to feel something or not, weighing the options, trying to figure out the cost of each.
What he needed was for me to come around the table and put a hand on his shoulder and say something small and true. Something like: bad run of cards, it happens, let's deal again. Physical first, then the words. Presence before language. Close the distance.
What I did was give him space.
I leaned back in my chair and let the quiet sit there and waited for him to come through it on his own because that was how I'd been taught to handle difficulty: work it out privately, don't make a production of it, find the ground under your feet without needing someone to help you find it. My father had done that for me and I'd done it and it had worked, mostly, in the sense that I'd survived it, and I'd carried that forward without ever stopping to ask whether survival and thriving were the same thing.
They're not the same thing.
Cal shut down. Not all the way, not a full withdrawal, but enough. He put his cards on the table face down and said he was going to watch something in his room and he went, and I sat at the kitchen table with both our hands of cards in front of me and the afternoon light coming in the window and did not call after him because giving him space was what I was doing, that was the plan, that was what I knew.
Nora was in the kitchen doorway.
I hadn't heard her car. She'd knocked and I hadn't heard that either. She must have let herself in, which she did occasionally when she was dropping something off and her hands were full, and I had no idea how long she'd been standing there.
Long enough.
She didn't say anything. That was the thing. She didn't shake her head or sigh or make any of the sounds that would have given me something to respond to. She just stood there with her arms at her sides, and after a moment she said, "I forgot his soccer cleats. I'll just get them from his bag."
And she went down the hall.
I heard her knock softly on Cal's door and him say come in and the door close behind her, and I sat at the kitchen table and listened to the murmur of their voices through the wall, too low to make out words, the particular sound of a mother talking a child back from the edge of something, easy and sure.
After a few minutes she came back down the hall with the cleats in her hand. She stopped at the kitchen doorway again.
"He's okay," she said.
"I know."
She looked at me for a moment. Not unkindly. Nora has never been unkind, not even at the end of things, which is one of the facts about her that I carry around like a stone in my pocket, heavy and specific. She looked at me the way she sometimes looks at me when she's decided not to say the thing she's thinking, not because it's cruel but because she's done having that particular conversation.
"Sunday at two," she said.
"I'll have him ready."
She left. I heard the front door close and her car start and the sound of it going down the street. I sat on the back porch that evening after Cal was in bed, in the dark, with the kind of quiet that only comes after a day that didn't go the way you wanted it to. The frogs were going in the drainage ditch two properties over. Somewhere a screen door opened and closed.
I kept coming back to the moment at the kitchen table. The way Cal's jaw had set. The way I'd leaned back instead of leaning in.
And then, slowly, the way recognitions come when you've stopped chasing them, I placed it.
I'd seen it before. Not Cal's jaw. My father's.
The year I was sixteen, my father went somewhere inside himself for about eight months. Not dramatically. He didn't stop working or stop coming to dinner or stop being in the room. But something in him pulled back from the surface and stayed back, and when you tried to reach him there was a slight delay, like speaking to someone in a different room, the words arriving but the response taking longer than it should. I didn't know until years later that he'd lost his closest friend that spring, a man named Roy Caldwell who'd been his friend since boyhood and who'd died of a fast cancer that gave nobody time to prepare. My father had not told us about the cancer until Roy was already gone. He'd processed it somewhere I didn't have access to, in the private interior that was his own, and by the time I understood what had happened the eight months were mostly over and he was coming back to the surface and nobody talked about it.
What I'd felt that year, at sixteen, was the same thing Cal had felt this afternoon. The space where closeness should have been. The slight delay. The waiting for someone to come back from somewhere you couldn't follow.
I hadn't gone to Cal. I'd given him what my father had given me, not out of cruelty, not out of neglect, but out of the honest belief that it was the right thing. That people need to find the ground under their own feet. That you don't help someone by always closing the distance for them.
But Cal is nine years old. And the ground shifts under nine-year-olds in ways they can't always navigate alone, and what they need in those moments isn't space, it's a hand. Just a hand. Just the weight of someone saying I'm still here, I see you, the cards don't matter.
My father knew this. I'd seen him do it with me when I was younger than Cal, before I got old enough to start reading his silences as something other than what they were. Before I inherited them. At some point the thing that had held me became the thing I deployed, and I hadn't noticed the transition, and the cost of it was sitting in the third bedroom right now probably not sleeping, staring at the ceiling the way Morrows stare at ceilings when something is unresolved.
I sat in the dark for a long time.
Then I got up and went inside and down the hall and knocked on Cal's door, soft.
"Yeah," he said. Awake, as I'd thought.
I opened the door. He was on his back looking at the ceiling. The nightlight cast everything in a dim orange. I came in and sat on the edge of the bed and put my hand on his shoulder and didn't say anything right away.
He didn't say anything either.
We stayed like that for a while. His breathing slowed. His shoulder under my hand lost some of its holding.
"Bad run of cards," I finally said.
"Yeah."
"Happens to everybody."
"I know."
"Deal again tomorrow if you want."
"Okay."
I sat there another minute. His eyes were getting heavy. I could feel it through my hand, the way a body decides to let go of the day.
"Dad?"
"Yeah."
"You came in."
He said it the way you'd state a fact you weren't sure would hold, testing the weight of it. I didn't say anything. I just left my hand where it was.
By the time I stood up he was asleep.
I stood in his doorway on the way out and looked at him in the dim orange light, the specific slack of a sleeping child, the complete surrender of it, the way they go somewhere utterly safe when they finally let themselves go, and I thought about my father, who had also stood in doorways, watching.
I understood something then that I hadn't understood before. Not everything. Not even most of it. But something.
I wrote it in the notebook before I went to bed: He didn't give me space because he didn't know how to close the distance. He gave me space because he thought that was the gift. He was wrong about that, or he was right about it for me and I took it too far, or both. I don't know. What I know is that I almost did to Cal tonight what was done to me, and I almost meant it the same way, and I caught it just barely and went back. I need to get faster at catching it. I need to get faster.
