# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Six Draft

SIX
The Hospital
From the notebook, Fourth slip. Longest one so far, closer to seven minutes, I think, though time is hard to measure in them. Drayfield clinic, front entrance, Wednesday morning. I went in to drop off paperwork and came out somewhere I wasn't prepared for.
I've been trying to think about how to write this one. The others I could put down plainly, here's what I saw, here's what I noticed, here's what I hadn't noticed the first time. This one is different. This one has my mother in it. And what I saw her doing, I don't know what to do with yet. I'm writing it down because that's the only way I know to keep a thing from getting too large to hold.

The clinic on Barton Street is where Drayfield sends the non-emergencies, the stuff that doesn't need a full hospital but needs more than a doctor's office, the workers' comp cases, the follow-ups, the people who've put something off long enough that putting it off further would be its own kind of decision. I'd been there a hundred times dropping paperwork, picking up records, waiting on sign-offs from the attending. It smelled like every clinic smells, floor wax and antiseptic and beneath both of those a human smell that cleaning can reduce but never quite reach.
Wednesday I had a stack of incident reports that needed a signature from Dr. Vasquez, who worked Wednesdays and Thursdays and was usually in by eight-thirty. I got there at eight-forty. The woman at the front desk knew me and waved me through and I went down the short hall toward the back offices and stepped through the interior door. The apartment was small. That was the first thing, the scale of it, the particular compression of a space designed to hold one person's life and doing it adequately but not generously. I knew the apartment. I'd helped move into it, had carried boxes up one flight of stairs on a August afternoon that was too hot for the work, sweating through my shirt while my roommate complained about the elevator being broken.
I was twenty-two years old. I was in Kandahar Province ten days from now, a fact that in this moment I didn't know yet, though my thirty-eight-year-old self inside this body knew it with the precision of someone reading a map they've already used.
It was evening. The lamp in the corner was on, the overhead off, which was how I always kept it in that apartment because the overhead was a single bare bulb that made everything look like an interrogation. Through the window the sky was going dark. The sounds of the base came in low through the glass, vehicles, distant voices, the specific ambient noise of a place that never fully slept.
My phone was in my hand. It was ringing.
I watched myself answer it.
"Hey, Mom."
Her voice came through clearly enough that I could hear it, a little thinned by the connection, the slight delay of international calls in 2007, but her voice, recognizably hers, and I noticed something in it immediately that my twenty-two-year-old self was not noticing, a particular quality of controlled steadiness, the voice a person uses when they have decided how they are going to deliver something before they pick up the phone.
"Hi, honey. Do you have a few minutes?"
"Yeah, just got off." My younger self sat down on the edge of the bed. He, I, had a certain looseness to the body that I'd forgotten about, the specific physicality of twenty-two, the way everything moves a little easier when you haven't yet spent sixteen years accumulating the small injuries and compensations of a working life. "What's up?"
A pause. Just a half-beat. Enough.
"Your dad had a little scare this week. Nothing serious, the doctors say he's fine, but I wanted to let you know."
I watched my younger self's body register this and then, almost immediately, begin to manage it. The shoulders settling. The jaw doing the thing where it tightens slightly and then releases, the physical equivalent of deciding not to make a production of something. He, I, had learned that from my father. The regulation. The not making a production.
"What kind of scare?"
"His heart. He had some chest pain and we went to the ER and they kept him overnight and ran some tests. But they say it looks like a warning, not a, they say he's going to be fine. He's home now. He's already annoyed about being told to rest, which is probably a good sign."
My younger self made a sound that was almost a laugh. "Yeah. That sounds right."
"I didn't want to call until I knew something," she said. "I didn't want you worrying over there without..."
"I know. It's okay."
Another pause. On the other end of the line, in a house in Drayfield I could picture with my eyes closed, the kitchen with the window over the sink, the living room with the lamp in the corner that had been there since before I was born, the hallway that went back to the bedrooms, my mother was choosing her next words.
"He'd want you to know he's fine," she said. "He told me not to call. I thought you should know anyway."
"Tell him I'll call this weekend."
"I will."
"And tell him..." My younger self stopped. On the edge of something. I could feel it from inside, the shape of the unsaid thing, and I knew what it was because I was thirty-eight and carrying the knowledge of every conversation I'd had and not had since then. Tell him I love him. Tell him I'm coming when I can get leave. Tell him I'm sorry I'm not there. Any of those. All of those.
"I'll call this weekend," I said.
"Okay, honey."
"Thanks for telling me."
"Of course. Be safe over there."
"Always."
The call ended. My younger self sat on the edge of the bed for a moment looking at the phone. Then he set it down and leaned forward with his elbows on his knees and looked at the floor, and I could see from inside the specific thing happening in him, not panic, not grief, but a kind of recalibration, the taking in of new information and the filing of it in the category of things to handle later, because there was nothing to handle now, because he was ten days from deployment and the machinery of that was already in motion and there was no version of the next two weeks that had him on a plane back to Texas.
He was right about that. The math was the math. I don't fault him for the math.
But then, and this is the thing I couldn't have seen the first time, this is the thing I needed the slip to find, I looked to the left. Through the doorway of the small apartment kitchen, past the counter and the two-burner stove, through the opening that led into the next room, that wasn't right, I was in the apartment, there was no next room, and then I understood that I wasn't in the apartment anymore, I was somewhere else, some seam in the slip, some doubling, and I was looking into my parents' kitchen in Drayfield, the actual kitchen, the one with the window over the sink and the table in the middle and the lamp light coming in from the living room.
My mother was sitting at the table.
She had a cup of tea in front of her. The tea had gone cold, I could tell from the way she wasn't drinking it, the way it just sat there, the steam long gone. She wasn't doing anything. She wasn't reading, wasn't looking out the window, wasn't on the phone. She was just sitting in the aftermath of a phone call where she had said everything was fine in the voice she'd rehearsed, where she had sent the steadiness through the line so her son wouldn't feel it pulling at him from eight thousand miles away, where she had been braver than the situation required and was now, alone in her kitchen, allowed to be exactly as scared as she actually was.
She looked small at that table. I don't remember my mother as a small person, she was always the largest presence in any room, the one who organized things, who knew where everything was, who held the shape of the family together through the force of her attention. But sitting alone in the kitchen that night with the cold tea and the lamp light from the other room, she looked small in the way people look small when no one is watching them be strong.
She put both hands around the cup. Not drinking, just holding.
Her eyes were dry. She wasn't crying, hadn't been crying. She had moved past the point where crying was available to her and into the further country of simple waiting, the place you get to when the fear has been present long enough that it stops being acute and becomes instead a weather, a climate, something you just live in. I know that country. I've been there professionally. I've sat with people in it.
I had never seen my mother there before.
I had never thought to look. I came back through the interior door of the clinic and walked directly to the single chair against the wall in the hallway and sat down.
A nurse I recognized came past pushing a cart and looked at me and slowed. "You okay?"
"Fine," I said. "Just, long morning."
She nodded and kept going, the way people do when the answer they get is close enough to acceptable. I sat in the chair and looked at the wall across from me, which had a framed print of a landscape that no one had chosen for any reason except that it was inoffensive, and I looked at it without seeing it for a while.
My mother.
I had thought about that phone call before, of course I had, it was the call where I first understood my father's heart was going to be a factor in how long I had him, and those calls mark you whether you want them to or not. But every time I'd thought about it I had thought about my father. His chest pain. His overnight in the ER. The image of him in a hospital bed being annoyed about being told to rest, which was so entirely him that it was almost funny even then.
I had never once thought about my mother sitting alone at the kitchen table after she hung up the phone. I had never put her in the picture at all. She was the one who called. She was the mechanism of the information. And then she was gone from the scene in my memory because the scene in my memory was about my father, and she had made it about my father, and she had done that on purpose so I wouldn't worry about her on top of everything else, and it had worked so completely that she had disappeared from her own story.
Sixteen years. She'd been sitting at that table, in the version of that night I carried around, for sixteen years, and I had never seen her.
Dr. Vasquez found me in the hall ten minutes later. I signed the incident reports. I thanked her. I walked back through the front entrance of the clinic, through the door I'd come in, and stood on the sidewalk in the flat morning light and looked at nothing in particular.
I needed to call my mother.
Not to tell her about the slips, I wasn't ready for that conversation and wasn't sure it was a conversation I'd ever be ready for, the one where I try to explain to a woman of sixty-four that her dead husband has been showing up in involuntary visions accessible through doorways. But I needed to hear her voice. I needed to hear the real version of it, not the rehearsed one, not the steady one she'd used on that phone call. I needed to call her the way I should have called her the weekend after that phone call, before the deployment, and said: Mom. How are you actually doing.
I hadn't done that. I'd called and talked to my father and that had been the call.
I couldn't fix that. I knew that already. The slip had made sure I knew that, the locked door, the mouth that forms only the words it formed the first time, the past that doesn't need my corrections. But I could call her now. I could call her tonight. I could ask the question I should have asked sixteen years ago and let whatever she said be enough and let it matter.
I got in the rig. Patrice was in the passenger seat eating an apple, which meant she'd been waiting long enough to get hungry.
"Vasquez make you wait?"
"Yeah," I said.
She looked at me with the same unhurried attention she'd given me outside the hospital in Harker City, outside the bay door at the station. Three times now she'd seen me come back from somewhere and land badly. She hadn't pushed once. But the file she was keeping was getting thick, and I could feel the weight of it.
"You want to talk about it," she said. It wasn't a question, she knew the answer, but it was an offer, laid flat on the seat between us, take it or leave it.
I looked out the windshield at the street. A woman was walking a dog past the clinic entrance. An old pickup was parallel parking across the way with the slightly too many corrections of a person who doesn't parallel park often. The ordinary morning world, going about itself.
"Not yet," I said.
She bit her apple. "Okay."
We drove back to the station. I called my mother that evening, after dinner, and asked her how she was actually doing, and she was quiet for a moment in a way that told me she'd noticed the word actually, and then she told me.
It took a while. We were on the phone for almost two hours.
It was the best conversation we'd had in years. Maybe ever. And when I got off I sat in my kitchen with my hands flat on the table and thought about all the time I'd spent trying to understand my father when the person who understood him best had been available to me the whole time, patient as a hawk, waiting on the same fence line, watching me circle.
