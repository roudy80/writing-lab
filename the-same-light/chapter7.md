# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Seven Draft

SEVEN
Chasing
From the notebook, Fifth slip. Station bay door, Tuesday morning. I was there on purpose. That's the part I keep writing around and need to just say: I was standing in that doorway because I wanted it to happen, and it did, and I don't know yet whether that means I've figured something out or made some kind of mistake.
Dad was in the background. Loading feed bags into the truck bed, the old white Ford he drove the last fifteen years. I was maybe twenty-five, twenty-six in the memory. I can't place it exactly, which is new. The early ones I could place precisely. This one felt like a photograph where the date stamp is worn off. He worked. I watched. He didn't know I was watching. Three minutes, maybe less. I came back and Patrice was right there and I told her I'd gotten dizzy and she looked at me the way she looks at me when I've said something we're both going to pretend we believe.
Sixth slip, same day. I went looking for it a second time. Diner entrance on Main, lunch break. He was in it again, sitting at someone's kitchen table I didn't recognize, talking to a man I've never seen. Laughing about something. I've never seen him laugh like that, that loose, that easy. The laugh of a man twenty years younger than the man I knew. I didn't know he had that in him. I came back in the diner doorway and sat down at the counter and ordered coffee I didn't want and couldn't stop thinking about that laugh.

It started the morning after I called my mother.
I'd gotten off the phone late, past ten, and sat in my kitchen for a while afterward with the particular stillness of someone who has just had a conversation that rearranged something. She'd told me things about my father I didn't know. Not secrets, nothing dramatic, no revelations that changed the shape of who he was. Just the texture of a life I'd only ever seen from one angle. The way he'd worried about me during both deployments, how he'd kept a map of Afghanistan on the wall of his shop and tracked the news every day even when he didn't say so to anyone. How he'd kept every card I sent him. How he'd cried once, alone, at the kitchen table on a Sunday morning, and she'd found him there and stood in the doorway and let him have it without going in.
I thought about that last one for a long time. My father crying alone at the kitchen table. I couldn't make the image sit right in my mind, not because I doubted her, but because the man she was describing was larger and more interior than the one I'd been carrying around for thirty-eight years, and making room for the larger one required a kind of renovation I didn't know how to do all at once.
I went to bed. I slept better than I had in weeks.
I woke up the next morning with a thought that was there before I was fully awake, fully formed the way some thoughts are when your sleeping mind has been working on something your waking mind was too busy to finish: I could go looking for it.
I lay there with that for a while.
Then I got up and drove to the station. The fifth slip was short. Less than three minutes, I think, though the time inside them is still something I measure imprecisely, by feel rather than count. My father loading feed bags, the way I described it in the notebook is accurate but doesn't capture the specific quality of watching him work when he didn't know he was being watched, which was different from watching him work when he did know. He was easier. More himself. There was a looseness to the way he moved, an efficiency without performance, the way people move when no one is measuring them and they're just doing the thing.
I came back in the bay door and Patrice was coming out of the small kitchen with her coffee and she stopped when she saw me.
"Morning," she said, watching.
"Morning."
She let a beat pass. "You just get here?"
"Few minutes ago." I moved past her toward the rig. "Anything overnight?"
"Single vehicle out on 116. Sheriff handled it, didn't need us." She followed me at a reasonable distance that was not quite following. "You eat?"
"I'm fine."
She accepted this with the same neutrality she always accepted it, which was not the same as believing it.
We had two calls that morning. Both routine. Between them I stood in the bay door once more, just to see, and got nothing, which was the same result I'd gotten the week before when I'd tried to force one. Whatever the rule was, and there was a rule, I was increasingly sure of that, it wasn't simply about the doorframe. The doorframe was necessary but not sufficient. Something else was operating.
I thought about that on the drive to the second call. Patrice drove. I looked out the window at the county road going past and turned the question over.
The first one had been involuntary. I hadn't been thinking about my father, hadn't been trying for anything. I'd walked through the hospital entrance on ordinary business and the day had split open. The second and third were the same way, the bay door on two separate mornings, both times before I'd fully woken up, before the day had organized itself around the regular machinery of attention.
The fourth, at the clinic, had been involuntary too. Or mostly. I'd been thinking about my father after the phone call with my mother, carrying him in the foreground of my mind, and maybe that had something to do with why it happened in that doorway at that particular moment. Maybe the thinking was part of it. Not the forcing but the holding, keeping him present without demanding anything of the presence.
I wrote that in the notebook that evening: maybe it's about holding, not forcing. The way you hold something fragile. Open hand, not closed. The sixth slip happened at the diner.
I'd gone for lunch on my break, the way I do two or three times a week. The diner on Main does a lunch plate that costs seven dollars and is better than anything I make at home, which is not a high bar but it clears it. I pushed through the front door and the bell above it rang the way it always does and I was somewhere else before I was fully inside.
A kitchen I didn't recognize. Older than mine, older than my father's, the kind of kitchen that had been added onto a house rather than built with it, the ceiling a little lower on one side where the roofline changed. A table with four chairs, only two of them occupied. My father on one side. A man across from him I'd never seen, heavyset, white-haired, laughing at something my father had just said.
And my father laughing back.
I'd seen my father laugh. He had a sense of humor, quiet and dry, the kind that arrives without announcement and is gone before you've fully registered it. But this was different. This was the laugh of a man in the middle of a long friendship, with someone who knew the whole story and didn't need anything explained, someone in whose company my father could be the version of himself that existed before he was anyone's father or husband or steady reliable presence. The laugh of a man being young for a minute.
I stood in that kitchen and watched my father be young for a minute and could not have told you what they were laughing about. It didn't matter. The laugh was the thing. The ease of it, the way his whole body participated, the way the years came off him temporarily the way they do when you're with the right person in the right room.
The man across the table. I know now who it was. It was Roy Caldwell. My father's closest friend, the one who died the spring I was sixteen, the fast cancer, the one my father had processed alone in the private interior I didn't have access to. I hadn't known what Roy Caldwell looked like. I'd only known his name and the fact of his absence in my father's life and the eight months that absence had cost us all. But looking at him across that table, watching him laugh with my father, I knew. The way you sometimes know a thing before you can explain it.
They'd been friends since boyhood. My father had a boyhood I knew almost nothing about. He'd had a whole long life before I appeared in it, and the man at that table was part of that life, the part that predated me and therefore existed in a country I could never fully visit even with the slips, because the slips only gave me what I'd been present for, the overlapping section of our lives.
What they were giving me instead was this: the knowledge that my father had been loved well, and long, by someone who knew him from before. That he hadn't always been alone in the way that stoic men can seem alone. That the quiet he moved through the world with was not the quiet of a man who had never been reached, but the quiet of a man who'd been reached deeply by a few specific people and carried it privately, the way he carried everything that mattered to him.
The slip ended. The diner bell was still ringing above my head.
I sat at the counter. I ordered coffee. I drank it looking at the surface of it while the lunch crowd moved around me and Lila behind the counter asked twice if I wanted to order and I said in a minute both times. Three slips in ten days after that. All of them deliberately sought, all of them at doorways I'd used before, the bay door at the station, the clinic entrance, the diner, and none of them carrying the weight of the earlier ones.
My father in them, but distantly. Loading the truck. Crossing a room I was only half inside. Sitting in his chair in the living room with a book open in his lap, not reading, just resting his eyes, and me passing through the doorway on my way somewhere else in the memory, not stopping. The slips I went looking for gave me my father the way bad cell reception gives you a voice, recognizable but incomplete, the signal dropping in and out, everything important cutting away at the wrong moment.
I started to understand that the mechanic had an opinion about being hunted.
The real ones, the ones that found me instead of the other way around, had given me things I couldn't have asked for because I hadn't known they were there to find. My mother at the kitchen table. The laugh in Roy Caldwell's kitchen. My father on his knees at the fence post, hands working in the way that was teaching me everything I knew about how to work. Those were gifts from something that knew what I needed better than I did. The ones I went looking for were what you got when you substituted your own judgment for that, which was a diminished version, an echo of the real thing.
I wrote that in the notebook and then sat with it for a while.
I was thirty-eight years old and I was chasing my dead father through doorways in a small Texas town and I was doing it badly and I knew I was doing it badly and I kept doing it anyway, which is the precise definition of a thing that has gotten larger than your judgment of it. Patrice asked me directly on a Thursday, ten days into the chasing. We were eating lunch in the rig, parked in the shade of the grain elevator on the east side of town because the station kitchen had a smell that week from something in the drain that maintenance hadn't gotten to yet. She had a sandwich. I had the leftover diner plate from two days ago that I'd been meaning to eat since two days ago.
"You running toward something or away from it?" she said.
She said it the way she says things she's been holding for a while, no preamble, just the thing itself, dropped into a silence that had been sitting there long enough to be ready for it.
I looked out the windshield. The grain elevator threw a hard shadow in the afternoon sun. A truck went past on the road, going somewhere with purpose.
"I don't know yet," I said.
She chewed her sandwich. Considered this.
"Those are different problems," she said. "Running toward something, you might get there. Running away from something, you just end up somewhere else with the same thing behind you."
"I know."
"Do you?"
I looked at her. She was looking out the windshield, not at me, which was its own kind of consideration, giving me the question without the weight of her attention on my face while I sat with it.
"I think I'm running toward," I said. "I think there's something I'm trying to see and I keep almost seeing it."
She nodded slowly. "Almost is a hard place to live."
"Yeah."
She finished her sandwich, folded the wrapper into a square the way she always does, tucked it in the door pocket.
"You'd tell me," she said, "if it was a health thing. If I needed to know."
It wasn't a question, but it needed an answer.
"It's not a health thing," I said. Which was true in the way that incomplete truths are true, technically accurate, addressing the surface of the concern without touching what was underneath it. She deserved better than that, and I knew it, and I wasn't ready yet to give it.
"Okay," she said. And let it go. Filed it. Would come back to it later at a time of her choosing.
We drove back to the station. I stood in the bay door when we got there, automatically, the way I'd been doing for ten days, and stood there for almost a full minute while Patrice went inside and I stayed in the frame and tried to be open rather than reaching, tried to hold rather than force.
Nothing came.
I went inside. I ate the diner plate standing over the sink. I did my paperwork. I drove home. Eleven o'clock. I was in my front doorway again.
I don't know how long I'd been standing there. Long enough that the streetlight at the corner had cycled through its moth-attendance and the moths had moved on or given up and the street was just a street in the middle of the night with nothing happening on it.
My hands were on either side of the frame. The wood was warm from the day's heat, still holding it at eleven the way Texas holds heat, reluctant to let it go. The neighborhood was quiet. The Hendersons' porch light was off. The dog two properties over wasn't barking. A car went past on the cross street a block away, headlights swinging briefly across the ceiling inside, and was gone.
Nothing.
No narrowing. No change in the air. No sense of the world preparing to become a different version of itself. Just the doorframe and the street and the particular loneliness of a man standing in the entrance of his own house at eleven o'clock at night, hoping for something he couldn't ask for directly and couldn't make come by wanting it.
I stood there and looked at myself in the dark window of the house across the street. Not clearly, just a shape, a man-shaped thing in a lit doorframe, hands out to either side like something between a posture of welcome and a posture of bracing. I couldn't see my face. I didn't need to.
I knew what I was doing. I'd known for days what I was doing and I'd been doing it anyway, which is the thing about a certain kind of loss: it makes reasonable people stand in doorways at eleven at night hoping for ghosts. It makes you willing to look foolish in the dark because the alternative is accepting that the door is just a door and the other side of it is just a room and your father is not anywhere that a doorframe can reach.
I wasn't ready to accept that yet.
But standing there, looking at the shape of myself in the dark window across the street, I understood that the chasing was over. Not because it had given me what I wanted but because it had shown me clearly what it couldn't give me, and there's a specific kind of useful information in that. You learn the limit of a thing by running at it until you find the edge. I'd found the edge.
The slips weren't mine to summon. They came when they came, from whatever source they came from, grief or memory, or something that lived in the overlap between those two things, and they gave what they gave, which was always more than I'd known to ask for. All I could do was be in the right posture to receive them. Open hand. Not closed.
I stepped back inside. Closed the door.
I wrote in the notebook before I went to bed: Done chasing. If it comes, it comes. I've been making this about what I need. I don't think it's about what I need.
Then I went to sleep, and this time I stayed there.
