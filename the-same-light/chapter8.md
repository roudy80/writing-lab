# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Eight Draft

EIGHT
The Real Emergency
From the notebook, Seventh slip. None. I sat in a hospital waiting room for two and a half hours surrounded by doorways and nothing came. Not even close. I think I understand now what the pattern wants from me, or maybe what I want from it, which is the same thing said from different sides. You can't chase it. You can't manufacture the conditions. All you can do is be entirely somewhere, and when you're entirely somewhere for a real reason, for a reason that has nothing to do with what you're looking for, the door stays closed. I don't know yet what that means. I'm writing it down.

The call came at 6:17 PM on a Thursday. I was in the kitchen, heat in the pan, thinking about nothing in particular. I saw Nora's name on the screen and picked up and she said his name first -- Cal -- and something moved through my chest before she finished the sentence.

"He's okay," she said. "He fell out of a tree in the backyard. His arm. I think it's broken."

I asked which hospital. She said Harker City, the one with the pediatric wing. I asked if he was scared. She said he'd cried in the car and then stopped and hadn't cried since, which meant yes, he was scared, and had decided not to show it.

I turned off the burner. I was in the truck in four minutes.

Forty miles to Harker City on a two-lane county road. The speed limit is sixty through most of it and I was doing seventy-five most of the way, not from panic but from the particular arithmetic of a parent who has done the math and decided the speed is worth it. The land out there is flat enough that you can see anything coming from a long way off. I could see a long way off. Nothing was coming.

I've been a medic for six years. Before that ten years as a combat medic, two deployments, a hundred situations where the appropriate response to emergency was to slow down and think clearly. I know how to do that. What I don't know how to do is apply that training to my own child. The professional calm is available everywhere except here. I've thought about that before, why it works that way, and the answer I keep arriving at is that detachment requires not mattering, and Cal matters in a way that doesn't leave room for detachment.

I know this about myself. I'm not ashamed of it.

I got to the Harker City Regional ER at 7:04 and found the waiting area. Nora was already there, sitting in a row of plastic chairs near the window with her coat folded in her lap. She stood when she saw me.

"They took him back," she said. "X-ray, then casting. The nurse said two hours, maybe a little less."

"How was he?"

"Brave," she said, and the word cost her something to say it. "He kept asking if I'd called you."

I sat down. She sat down. We were two chairs apart, which was the distance we'd arrived at over eighteen months of navigating the same space separately, close enough to be together, far enough to acknowledge we weren't. The waiting room was under fluorescent lights that turned everyone the same color. A television mounted near the ceiling was running local news with the sound off. A man across the room had a child in his lap, maybe three years old, asleep with his face turned in against the man's chest.

"He was in the hackberry," Nora said. "The one in the corner of the yard. He's been climbing it all week. I told him the third branch from the top was too thin."

"He heard you."

"He did," she said. "He went up there anyway." She was quiet. "I was watching through the kitchen window. I saw him start to come down and the branch move wrong and then he was on the ground and I was outside before I knew I was moving."

"He's okay," I said.

"I know he's okay."

She looked at the television. I looked at the entrance to the treatment wing, the double doors with the push-bar handles, the strip of light visible through the narrow window. My son was on the other side of those doors. Two hours, maybe a little less.

There is a particular helplessness to waiting. I know its face in every version: waiting for news on a patient, waiting for an extraction, waiting for orders that may or may not come in time. This version was the worst of them. Not because the situation was worse than the others -- Cal was going to be fine, his arm would heal, the worst case had already happened and already resolved itself -- but because I had no role here. There was nothing for my hands to do. There was no skill I could apply. I was exactly as useful to my son right now as any other man in that waiting room, which is to say not useful at all. That's the part the training doesn't prepare you for.

I sat with it.

The first hour I thought about Cal at nine, which is to say I thought about Cal specifically: the way he's been climbing things all summer, anything that will hold his weight, trees and fence posts and the side of the grain elevator he thinks I don't know about. The way he goes quiet when something is wrong, the way Dale went quiet, the way I go quiet, this particular inheritance passed down through the men in our family like a piece of land that transfers without being discussed. The way he asked if I'd called you. That detail.

Nora got coffee from the machine in the corner. She brought me one without asking. It was bad coffee, the kind that's been sitting on the element for too long, but she'd remembered I take it black and that was its own information.

"Tell me about the hackberry," I said.

She looked at me. Not surprised, just checking something.

"Big tree," she said. "It's been there as long as the house has. The previous tenants put a board across two branches about eight feet up. Cal found it the second day we were in the house. He's treated it like his personal property ever since."

"What was he doing up there?"

"I don't know. Looking at things. He takes things up there sometimes. A book once, which didn't make sense to me because you can't hold the book and hold on at the same time, but he figured it out." She wrapped her hands around her cup. "He took his grandfather's binoculars up there two weeks ago. He wanted to look at the fields from higher up."

"Those are good binoculars."

"He was careful with them," she said. "He's always careful with things that matter."

I drank the bad coffee. The man across the room shifted his sleeping boy to his other arm without waking him. The television moved through weather.

At some point in the second hour I noticed the doors. Not just the treatment wing doors but all of them: the entrance behind me, the fire exit to my left, the door to the family consultation room, the door to the family bathroom, the door to the small children's area in the corner with the toy bins and the bright plastic chairs. The waiting room was ringed with thresholds. I was sitting in the middle of them like a room made entirely of frames.

Nothing came.

Not even the narrowing, the first sign I'd come to recognize, the slight change in the light that preceded the slip. Just the fluorescent ceiling and the quiet television and Nora beside me and the doors standing open or closed, ordinary doors with ordinary rooms on the other side. I sat in the middle of them for two and a half hours and nothing reached for me and I didn't reach for anything either, because there was nowhere to go. There was only here. Cal was on the other side of those double doors and here was the only place I had any chance of being useful to that fact.

I thought, briefly, about the past three weeks. The bay door at the station, the diner, my own front doorway at eleven at night with my hands on the frame. All of it looking for something. And here, surrounded by the very architecture that carried me backward every other time, nothing. Because I was already somewhere. You can't be in two places at once, and this place had me completely.

My father would have understood that. My father, I think, lived entirely in the present the way some men do, not because he lacked an interior life but because the present was always asking him for something and he'd learned to answer the ask. The fence post. The barn beam. The boy beside him in the truck. He was a man who showed up, and showing up means you're here, which means you're not there.

I've been trying to get back to him. He would have told me to look forward.

The double doors opened at 9:41. A nurse came through first, and then Cal behind her, and Cal had his right arm in a fresh cast from below the elbow to the palm and he was moving with the careful dignity of a nine-year-old who has decided that whatever happened in that treatment room is private.

He saw me and his face did something.

"Dad," he said.

I got up. I crossed the room and crouched down in front of him and looked at the cast, which was white and clean, the plaster not quite set yet.

"Does it hurt?" I asked.

"Kind of," he said. "Not like before."

"Before was worse?"

"Before was really bad," he said. "When it happened. It kind of made a sound."

I looked at him. He was watching me, checking something, the same way he checks to see if I'm paying attention, and I was paying attention, every bit of it, there was nothing else in the room.

"Can you sign it?" he said. "The cast. Can you sign it first?"

I had to look away for a moment. Toward the entrance, the fluorescent exit sign above the door. Not long. Three seconds, maybe four.

"Yeah," I said. "I can sign it first."

He turned to Nora. "You can sign it second."

"That's generous," she said.

The nurse had a marker in her pocket, the kind people always seemed to have in hospitals, and she handed it to me and I signed my name on the cast just above his wrist. My handwriting looked strange on the white plaster, larger than I expected. Then Nora signed it below mine, and Cal looked at both signatures with the satisfaction of a boy who has acquired something that nobody else has.

The nurse went through the discharge instructions with Nora. I stood with my hand on Cal's good shoulder, which was not quite a hand on his shoulder the way my father's hand had been on mine -- that was a memory, not this -- but it was my hand on my son's shoulder in a hospital waiting room at 9:45 on a Thursday night, and that was what it was.

We walked out together through the entrance, through the automatic doors that slid apart for us, and Cal held his cast arm carefully at his side like something he was carrying. The night air was cool after the fluorescent hours inside.

"I'm hungry," Cal said.

"When did you eat last?" Nora asked.

"Lunch."

"Cal."

"I forgot to be hungry until now," he said, which was so precisely how he operated -- forgetting ordinary things in the middle of extraordinary ones -- that I almost laughed.

"Pancakes," I said.

He looked at me. "What?"

"If you want pancakes, there's a diner open on the way back through town."

He considered this with the gravity he brought to most decisions. "Is it good?"

"I've been eating there for six years."

"Okay," he said. "Pancakes."

Nora drove back separately. Cal rode with me, his cast on his lap, watching the county road go dark ahead of the headlights. He didn't talk for most of the drive and I didn't push it. The silence between us was easy, the kind of easy that takes years to develop, and I was aware of it as something worth noticing.

"Were you scared?" he asked, somewhere past the midpoint.

"Yes," I said.

He looked out his window. The fields were dark out there, the kind of dark that only exists in places where there's nothing to interrupt it.

"I was too," he said. "When I fell."

"That's the right response."

"I didn't cry in front of the doctor."

"I know. Your mom told me."

He looked at the cast. "She cried a little. In the car."

"She was scared too."

"Is it okay to be scared?"

I'd had this conversation before, or versions of it, but the version you've had before is not always the version in front of you, and the version in front of me was my son with a broken arm at ten o'clock on a Thursday night asking me something real.

"Yeah," I said. "It is. The only question is what you do while you're scared. And you did fine."

He was quiet for a minute.

"I didn't fall on purpose," he said.

"I know."

"I know the branch was too thin."

"Also know that."

He looked at me. "Are you mad?"

"No."

"You can be."

"I know I can be. I'm not." I kept my eyes on the road. "You're going to climb things for the rest of your life. I'd rather you know how to fall than never climb."

He sat with this for a while. The lights of town appeared ahead, the grain elevator lit up against the dark sky, the water tower with the faded town name. Cal straightened a little when he saw them, the unconscious posture adjustment of a kid recognizing home.

The diner was still open. Meredith was behind the counter, forty years in that job if she was a day, and she looked at Cal's cast when we came in and said, "Rough night?"

"I fell out of a tree," Cal said.

"Terrible place for a fall," she said, and handed us menus.

We sat in the back booth. Cal ordered the short stack with a side of bacon. I ordered coffee and the apple pie, which I didn't need but which was the right thing to order in a diner at ten at night when your son is across the table from you with his arm in a cast and everything is fine.

He ate with his left hand, which was slower and messier than he liked, and he accepted my help cutting the pancakes without making it into anything, just shifted the plate toward me and waited and pulled it back. The syrup got on his cast. He looked at it and decided not to care.

We didn't talk about the fall. We talked about the binoculars, what he'd seen from the hackberry with them. A redtail hawk working the field to the east. A man on a ladder two streets over painting something. The water tower, closer than it seemed from the ground. He'd been building a list in his head of things that look different from higher up, which was so specifically himself that I filed it away in the same place I filed all the Cal things, that mental inventory of who he was becoming.

At 10:40 I paid the check and we drove to Nora's. She was waiting on the porch, the light on. Cal hugged her and then turned and hugged me, the good arm tight, the cast arm careful.

"Thanks for coming," he said.

"Of course," I said.

"I knew you would," he said, and went inside.

Nora and I stood on the porch for a moment in the quiet.

"He did fine," she said.

"Yeah."

"You both did."

I looked at the street. The same houses at the same angles they always were, the same oak that had dropped acorns on this sidewalk every October for forty years. Small town with a long memory, everything in it carrying everything that had ever happened in it.

"He asked if he could sign it first," I said.

"I know." She was quiet for a moment. "He talked about you the whole ride to the hospital. Did you know he's been telling his friends about when you were in Afghanistan?"

"What does he tell them?"

"That you helped people." She pulled her coat closed against the evening. "That's the whole story. That you went somewhere hard and you helped people."

I didn't say anything.

"Goodnight, Eli," she said, and went inside.

I drove home. The house was the same house it always was, the porch light I'd left on, the yard keeping its shape in the dark. I stood at the front door with my key in the lock and went through the calculation that had become automatic -- the threshold, the possibility -- and felt nothing. No narrowing. No change in the light. Just a door, just my house, just a regular Thursday night that had been many other things first.

I went in. I sat at the kitchen table for a while. The pan was still on the burner, cold now. I'd been going to make something before the phone rang. I couldn't remember what.

I wrote in the notebook: He asked me to sign it first.

Then I went to bed and slept, and when I dreamed it was nothing I could hold onto in the morning, which was fine, which was exactly fine.
