# The Same Light


THE SAME LIGHT
Chapter Nine Draft

NINE
The Porch
From the notebook, Seventh slip. Station parking lot, back door, late afternoon. This one I wasn't looking for. Wasn't near a doorway I'd been using. Just walked out of the building and the world shifted and I was somewhere I didn't expect. He didn't know I was there. That's the part I keep returning to. He had no audience. He was just himself, in the dark, alone, and I saw what a man looks like when he's alone and doesn't know he's being watched. I have been thinking about it ever since. I think I will be thinking about it for a long time.

It was a Friday, mid-afternoon, sun still high. We'd had a slow week, two calls in four days, the kind of stretch that should feel restful but doesn't, that instead makes you aware of your hands and where to put them. I'd been doing paperwork, reorganizing the supply cabinet, the low-grade busywork of a slow station.

At around four I went to take the trash to the dumpster. The back door of the station opens into the rear lot, a small paved area with the dumpster on one side and the fence of the neighboring property on the other. I pushed through the door with both hands, the trash bag in my right, and it happened before I'd fully cleared the threshold.

I knew I was somewhere else before I understood where.

Nighttime. A porch. The particular dark of a night without cloud cover, the stars sharp, the yard below the porch a flat black. I knew the porch before I knew I knew it. The boards, the specific sag near the second step. The aluminum patio chair with the bent leg that my mother had kept meaning to fix. The smell of cedar and dry grass and the faint distant animal smell of the fields.

Home. The Morrow house on County Road 114, eight miles out of town.

I wasn't in the slip yet, not fully -- I mean that I wasn't inside my own younger body the way the slips usually worked, looking through my own eyes from the inside. I was there, present in the moment, but standing somewhere off to the side of it. An observer rather than a participant. I'd been that way once before, in the kitchen during the Roy Caldwell slip, where I'd watched my father at a table rather than been in the room with him. I don't know what the difference is. I didn't ask for either version.

My father was in the chair.

He was sitting the way he sat on evenings when the work was done and dinner had been cleared and there was nothing he had to do, which was differently than the way he sat at other times. The work-posture was forward, organized, already thinking about the next task. This posture was back and still. His arms were on the armrests. His coffee mug was on the floor beside the chair, empty or nearly so.

I knew from the light, the season, the particular quality of the dark that this was summer. Late enough in the evening that the heat had started to come off the ground. The crickets were going.

I had already left.

I understood this without being told it. The house behind him had the specific quality of a house with one fewer person in it than there had been before, something in the proportion of the light through the kitchen window, something in the way my father occupied the space. Two months gone, the notebook entry had said, and standing there in that yard looking up at that porch I knew this was exactly right. I'd been at Fort Benning for two months. I hadn't come home since the enlistment. I was eighteen and I was gone.

He didn't know I was watching.

That was the thing that hit first. He had no idea. There was no performance in this, no arranged posture, no face he was putting on for anyone. He was alone on his porch on a summer night after his boy had gone away and he was just -- himself. The version of himself that existed when no one was measuring him. I had seen that version once before, loading feed bags into the truck, and had understood it as ease. This was different.

He was still. Not the stillness of rest. The stillness of a man absorbing something.

His coffee mug was on the floor. He'd set it there and left it. He was looking at the yard, the dark fields beyond the fence, the same flat horizon I'd looked at my whole life until I'd stopped looking at it. His hands were on the armrests, not loose, not gripping, just resting with the particular weight of hands that have been doing hard work for thirty years and know how to be still.

I stood in that yard and looked at my father on his porch and tried to understand what I was seeing.

He was proud.

I don't know how else to say it. It wasn't contentment, it wasn't sorrow, though there was sorrow in it too -- the cost was real, I could see the cost in the set of his shoulders, in the way he was absorbing the quiet rather than simply sitting in it. But underneath the cost was this: his shoulders were back. His chin was up a degree. He had the posture of a man who has paid something and decided it was worth it and is sitting with the knowledge that it was worth it.

His boy went. His boy is out in the world. It cost him something and he is paying it and he is proud.

There were no words. There was no action. There was a man on a porch alone at night and the particular shape of him in the dark, the particular set of those big-knuckled hands on the armrests, and the way he looked when nobody was watching. That was the whole of it. Three minutes, maybe four. Then the slip ended and I was in the station parking lot with the trash bag still in my hand and the dumpster four feet away and the afternoon sun exactly where it had been.

I stood there for a long time.

I set the trash bag down next to the dumpster rather than putting it inside. I stood with my back against the rear wall of the station and looked at the fence of the neighboring property, a chain-link fence with a climbing vine going brown in the late summer heat, and I tried to hold what I'd just seen.

I had been eighteen years old and I had signed the papers and I had told my father I was going. He had looked at me for a long moment across the kitchen table and then he had said, all right. That was the whole of it. All right, and a nod, and he'd pushed back his chair and asked if I wanted coffee. We had not talked about it further. I had taken this as approval without warmth, the particular economy of a man who didn't produce more words than necessary, and for twenty years I had carried it that way -- a transaction, a handshake, a door closing quietly on something that might have been more.

He had been proud of me.

Not after, not when I'd come home. In the first days of my absence, alone on his porch, when there was no reason to perform it for anyone's benefit. He had been sitting there with the cost of my being gone and he had been proud. The pride was not something he'd built to manage the cost. It was underneath the cost. The cost sat on top of it the way a stone sits on good ground.

I have thought about this -- about how a man can be proud and missing someone at the same time and how those two things can coexist without canceling each other out. I think it is because pride is not the same as happiness. You can be proud of someone and gutshot by their absence simultaneously, which is what my father looked like on that porch, which is what I had not understood until I was standing in a parking lot behind the EMS station with a trash bag at my feet and the afternoon going gold around me.

He never told me. He wasn't going to tell me. That wasn't how he operated and I'd always known that, had accepted it as a characteristic of him the way you accept other things about people you love, but what I hadn't understood until now was that his not telling me wasn't the same as his not feeling it. The silence was not absence. The silence was its own language, fully loaded, saying everything it had to say. I had simply not known how to read it. I had been looking for the version I knew how to understand and missing the version he was actually speaking.

I stayed in that parking lot for twenty minutes. Maybe more. Patrice came to the back door at some point and looked out and saw me and didn't say anything and went back inside, which was the most Patrice thing she could have done and the thing I needed most at that moment.

Eventually I put the trash bag in the dumpster. I went back inside. I sat in the kitchen with a cup of coffee I didn't taste and looked at the wall and let the thing settle.

That night I wrote the notebook entry. Short, because the thing it was trying to describe resisted being described, and I had learned by now that trying to say more than the facts was how you lost the facts. He had a no audience. He was just himself. He was proud.

I thought about the summer I was eighteen, the table, the coffee, all right, the nod. I thought about what he'd been carrying when he said it, the specific weight of a man letting his son go out into the world while sitting with the knowledge that the going was right and the cost was real. Thirty-eight years old and I was only now seeing that moment clearly, seeing the face behind the economy of it, seeing my father in full for the first time.

I thought about Cal, who was nine, who was growing toward the same moment in his own time, and about what it would cost me when it arrived, and whether I'd have the sense to let it cost me without making it into something he had to manage.

I thought about the posture. The shoulders back. The chin up. A man on a porch in the dark, paying the price of a love that knew how to let go.

I wrote one more line in the notebook: I want to know how to do that.

Then I closed it and went to bed.
