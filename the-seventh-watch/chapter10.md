# CHAPTER TEN

## LOOP 5: GOOD WORKS

He arrived on the same street.

He stood there.

He breathed.

He had come from fixing a pump in the mud in Texas and the specific calm of that work, the ordinary competence of maintaining something that mattered to people he loved, was still in his body, and he stood in the Jerusalem street and let it stay there. He didn't immediately orient and assess and move to the next objective. He stood in the street in the morning light of the first-century city and he let the city come to him rather than going to it.

This was different from how he had been arriving.

He had been arriving with purpose. With the forward-pointed weight of a man who had somewhere to be and something to do when he got there. He had been treating each loop as an operation, arriving with the mission brief already in progress, moving toward the objective from the first second.

This loop he was not going to do that.

He walked to Marcus.

Marcus looked up when Grant came through the market and they looked at each other for a moment with the specific quality of two people who each time met as strangers and each time, within minutes, found themselves in something closer to the relationship of acquaintances, or something better than acquaintances, something that didn't have a precise name because the loops made it strange.

Marcus said: "Room."

Grant said: "Please."

He put no knife on the counter. Marcus looked at the empty counter and looked at Grant and said: "Room. No charge."

Grant looked at him.

Marcus said: "Some people you charge. Some people you don't." He went back to his work. "You've been here enough."

Grant went up. He sat on the bedroll. He looked at the ceiling.

Then Miriam came up with the lamp and she looked at him and said, in Aramaic: "You look different."

He said: "I'm trying something different."

She said: "Good." She handed him the lamp. She said: "My father wants to know what you need."

He said: "I want to help. This week. Not to prevent anything. Not to organize anything. Just, to do what is needed."

She said: "I'll tell him."

She came back in ten minutes and said: "My father says there is a man in the lower quarter who cannot walk. He has been there for years. He needs food."

He said: "Give me directions."

He found the man in the narrow space between two buildings in the lowest part of the city, in a spot that was covered by a stretch of cloth someone had stretched for shade and that functioned as a kind of permanent encampment of a person who had nowhere else to go. The man was maybe sixty and had been there long enough to have made a kind of peace with being there, not contentment, exactly, but the absence of the constant expectation of something better, which was its own kind of state.

He looked at Grant with the assessment of someone who had learned to be careful about strangers, particularly strangers bearing food, because strangers bearing food were usually running some kind of account and the account was usually more expensive than the food.

Grant sat down in the dust next to him.

He sat in the dust in the narrow space between the buildings, with his back against the wall and his legs stretched out in front of him, and he handed over the bread and the water and the dried figs and he didn't say anything because he didn't yet have the Aramaic for what he wanted to say and the man didn't appear to be waiting for words anyway.

The man ate slowly, with the deliberate attention of someone for whom food was never guaranteed and who had learned to be fully present for the eating of it. Grant watched without staring, the peripheral attention of a man who had learned to see without appearing to look.

He thought about his father.

His father had been an Air Force officer, not combat, logistics, but he'd had friends in combat and he'd done the things that officers in peacetime did, which included the duties that had nothing to do with warfare and everything to do with what you did when war had already broken its promises. His father had spent years going to the bedsides of men who were dying, not as a chaplain, not professionally, but as a friend, as a neighbor, as a man who understood that presence was not nothing and who had decided to offer what he had.

Grant had watched his father sit beside a dying pilot when Grant was eighteen years old and he had not understood what he was watching. He had understood that his father's presence mattered to the man in the bed, had understood it empirically from the change in the room when his father arrived, the specific settling that happened. But he hadn't understood why. He'd been eighteen and he'd understood things about action and effectiveness and outcome and he hadn't yet understood the specific value of just being there.

He was forty-four years old and he was beginning to understand.

He sat next to the man who couldn't walk for two hours.

He went back to find food twice. He brought water from a public cistern. He sat and was there, simply and without agenda, and by the end of the two hours the man's specific quality of wariness had changed into something else, not trust, exactly, which was too large a word for two hours, but the relaxation of the expectation of harm.

When Grant finally stood to go he said, in Aramaic: "Tomorrow. I'll come back."

The man said something that Grant's Aramaic caught most of: Why do you do this?

Grant thought about how to answer in Aramaic. He said: "Because someone showed me."

He walked back through the lower city toward the market. He passed the spot where, in Loop 4, he'd helped the merchant with the broken cart. He went by the stretch of market near the Temple courts where, in Loop 4, he'd defused the dispute between the vendor and the Roman soldier. He thought about the difference between doing those things as tactical side effects of being in the city and doing them as the actual point.

He thought about Marcus's description of being in a place versus moving through a place.

He thought: maybe being in a place means being available to what the place needs from you. Not what you need from the place. What the place needs from you.

The second Tuesday of the fifth loop, Simon was worse.

Grant had been coming for four days now, the fifth loop's version of the pattern he'd been developing, the reliability of showing up, and he had been reading Simon's condition the way he read anything he needed to understand, which was with his full attention and without the management of false hope. Simon was worse. The illness had progressed in the way that illnesses progressed in a first-century city without medicine, not dramatically, not in the kind of visible deterioration that would alarm a passerby, but in the specific incremental way of a person who was losing ground.

His appetite was less. He ate more slowly. He had a stillness that was different from the stillness of the previous days, the stillness that was not rest but the conservation of resources.

Grant sat in the dust next to him and did not say anything about this because there was nothing useful to say about it. He brought food and water and he sat and he was there. He had been learning, across five loops, the specific skill of presence without agenda, and he was bringing that skill to this specific situation, which required it completely.

Simon talked when he wanted to and didn't when he didn't. Today he wanted to.

He talked about his wife, who had died eight years ago, a fever, fast, the way fevers sometimes came in this city. He talked about her with the specific past-tense love of someone for whom the love was still present but the person was not, the specific quality of a grief that had settled into the body over years until it was part of the body rather than something happening to the body. He said her name, something Grant's ear rendered as Devorah, and he said it the way you said the name of someone whose name you had been saying your whole adult life and who was still present in the saying even when they were not present in the room.

Grant listened. He listened the way his father had sat beside the dying pilot, with the full weight of his attention, the complete presence, the specific quality of a person who was giving what they had which was their full self and their time.

He thought about the dying pilot. He had been eighteen and he had watched his father do this and had not understood it. He had understood the visiting, you visited the sick, you showed up for the dying, this was what you did. He had not understood the quality of the visiting. The difference between being present and being there, which was the difference his father had been demonstrating and which Grant had been learning across five loops in first-century Jerusalem.

He understood it now.

Simon said, at some point in the middle of the afternoon: "You listen the way my wife listened."

Grant said: "How did she listen?"

Simon said: "As if the thing you were saying was the most important thing in the world, not because she always agreed with it or always found it interesting, but because you were the one saying it and that made it worth hearing."

Grant sat with that.

He said: "My wife listens like that too."

Simon said: "You are a lucky man."

Grant said: "I know."

Simon was quiet for a while. Then he said: "What is her name?"

Grant said: "Sarah."

Simon said: "That is a good name. An old name. A name that knows something about waiting for things that seem impossible."

Grant looked at him.

Simon said, with the faint quality of a smile: "I know my scripture."

Grant said: "I'm beginning to believe you do."

He stayed until the afternoon light shifted toward evening and then he said goodbye the way he always said goodbye to Simon, which was without fuss, which was the way Simon seemed to prefer it, a hand briefly on the shoulder, the assurance that he would come back tomorrow.

Simon said: "I know you will."

He walked back through the lower city in the late afternoon light. The city was beautiful at this hour, the specific quality of Judean light at the end of a spring afternoon, the limestone catching the warm angle of it and glowing with a warmth that was not orange, not gold exactly, but something between them, a color that existed in this place at this hour and nowhere else he had ever been.

He walked slowly.

He had been walking quickly for four loops, the forward-pointed walk of a man with an objective. He walked slowly now, and the slowness changed what he saw. He saw the vendor at the corner who had been there every day across five loops, an old woman who sold honey cakes and who had the specific unhurried quality of someone who had found the exact right spot and intended to stay in it. He had passed her hundreds of times. He stopped now and bought a honey cake with a scrap of material from his shirt hem, the ongoing improvised economy of a man who had come into a first-century city with no applicable currency, and he ate it standing at her stall and she watched him eat with the assessment of a vendor who took quality control seriously.

She said something in Aramaic.

He said: "Good."

She said something else.

He said: "Very good." Because it was. The honey cake was extraordinary, the kind of food that had been perfected over generations by people who had no other options for sweetness and who had therefore given honey the full professional attention it deserved.

She looked pleased. She said something else.

He said: "I'll come back."

He walked on.

He thought about the honey cake and about the old woman's satisfaction and about what it meant that across five loops he had walked past her hundreds of times and not stopped. He thought about all the things he had walked past while he was moving through rather than being in.

He thought about the ranch. About the specific details of the place that he walked past every day, the limestone outcropping on the east edge of the south pasture that had a seam of calcite running through it at a beautiful angle, the thing he had noticed the first week they moved in and had meant to come back to and never had. The cedar tree at the creek bend that had been struck by lightning at some point before they bought the place and had grown around the scar in a way that was worth looking at. The specific view from the north fence line at dusk in October when the light came at the same angle as the Jerusalem afternoon light and turned the cedar gold.

He had been walking past things.

He thought: when I go home, I am going to stop walking past things.

He climbed to Marcus's roof in the evening and sat with the city and felt what there was to feel.

He thought about Simon saying: you listen the way my wife listened. He thought about what that meant for the Texas life, for Sarah and the boys and the specific quality of presence he had been giving them and had been keeping some portion in reserve, the presence of a man who was always also managing something else.

He thought: the presence I have been practicing here is the presence I want to bring home.

Not the tactical version. Not the management version. The actual version, the full weight of himself in the room with them, the complete attention of a person who understood that the people in front of you were worth the full attention and that giving it was not a cost but a practice, a skill, a thing you got better at by doing it.

He lay on the roof under the Jerusalem sky.

He thought about Sarah's hands in the cedar.

He fell asleep.

He went to the Sheep Gate on Thursday morning of the fifth loop.

He had seen it on his reconnaissance in previous loops, the gate in the northeast corner of the city wall, the one through which the sheep for the Temple sacrifices were brought, the one near the pool where the sick gathered. He had noted it tactically: a secondary egress point, moderate Roman presence, mostly used by agricultural traffic coming from the north.

He went to it this loop because something had been nagging at him for three loops.

The Sheep Gate was a working gate, the unglamorous gate, the gate for animals and farmers and the kinds of commerce that the city needed but preferred to keep separate from the Temple courts. It smelled of sheep and the specific complex smell of large animals being moved through a small space for purposes they didn't understand. The stone around the gate was worn in the specific pattern of a place that had been heavily trafficked, not the elegant wear of the Temple courts, the polish of a million sandaled feet, but the more functional wear of hooves and cart wheels and the specific scuffs of livestock.

He stood at the gate and looked at the door frame.

It was stone, massive, set in the city wall with the permanent commitment of construction that expected to outlast whoever had built it. The stone of the frame was a different color from the surrounding wall, cut from a lighter limestone and fitted with precision, the joints tight and clean even after whatever number of years the gate had been standing.

He looked at the door frame and he thought about the red gate at the end of his drive.

Not because they were similar. They were not similar. One was iron pipe and red paint and a chain latch, modern and practical and painted red so that people could find the place. The other was stone, ancient, a working gate in an occupied city, used for the traffic of sacrifice animals and agricultural commerce.

But they were both gates.

They were both the place where you crossed from one world into another. The place where what was outside became inside, where the road ended and the property began, where the specific distinction between here and not-here was made physical and permanent.

He stood at the Sheep Gate of first-century Jerusalem and thought about his own gate on the county road in Central Texas and he understood something.

The gate was the marker.

His gate was the marker between the world and home. This gate was the marker between the world and the city. Every gate was the marker between what was outside and what was inside, the specific physical acknowledgment that inside mattered, that the distinction between inside and outside was real and worth making permanent.

He had been standing at his gate every morning for six years and he had never asked what he was doing there. He had attributed it to the tactical habit, watching the exit, maintaining the perimeter. That was part of it.

But there was another part.

He stood at the gate every morning because standing at the gate was the act of a man who was claiming his inside. Who was saying: this is where I am. This is what I have. This is worth standing at the edge of and looking at and confirming that it is still here.

He stood at the Sheep Gate of Jerusalem and felt the specific weight of that recognition arrive.

He thought about the man who had come into this city through the crowds and the palm branches. The man who had ridden through the specific gate on the west side of the city, the one the crowds had lined for him, and who had entered the city with the full intention of the week that was coming and who had not looked away from any of it.

He thought about what it meant to enter a place completely. To go through the gate without reserving any part of yourself for the outside.

He had been standing at his own gate every morning for six years and he had been a man with one foot inside and one foot outside, the man who watched the exits because he hadn't fully come in yet, because some part of him was still managing the distance between himself and the thing he'd built.

He thought: when I go home, I am going to come through the gate all the way.

He turned and walked back into the city.

He had one more day before the garden and the arrest and the white.

He used it well.

He spent the week in that mode.

Monday: the merchant's cart. He found it again, or one like it, the wheel hub cracked under a heavy load of timber on the steep street below the Temple courts, and he stopped and helped without calculating whether he had time, because he had time, this loop was not organized around his having objectives, this loop was organized around his being available.

He showed the merchant's son the truckers' hitch. He showed it three times, slowly, and watched the boy's hands get it right on the third try, and he felt the specific satisfaction of something useful transferred. Not the satisfaction of completing a mission. The satisfaction of passing something to someone who would use it.

The merchant's son looked up at him with the expression of a kid who had just learned something that felt like power. Grant thought about his own boys. About the things he'd tried to pass to them. About whether he had been as patient with that passing as this moment required.

He thought: I need to be more deliberate about this. Not just doing it in front of them and hoping they notice. Actually teaching. Showing.

He made a note of it in his mind and held it.

Tuesday: the vendor and the soldier. He was not looking for the conflict, he was passing through the market district on his way back from the sick man's spot, and he heard the specific escalating quality of the dispute before he reached it. He inserted himself with the body language of a disinterested party and adjusted the temperature of the situation without either party having to back down publicly.

He did it in Aramaic, mostly, his fifth-loop fluency enough for the specific situation. He managed the sequence of de-escalation, acknowledge the soldier's legitimate authority, reframe the vendor's position as cooperation rather than resistance, give both parties an exit that cost them nothing, and the situation resolved in under three minutes.

He moved on.

Wednesday: he went back to the man in the lower quarter. The man was there, in his spot, and he had the specific quality of someone who has been waiting for a thing without fully believing the thing would happen. When Grant appeared he saw the quality change.

They ate together. Grant's Aramaic was good enough now for fragments of real exchange, not conversation yet, but the shape of one, the back and forth of two people trying to understand each other with the specific effort of a language barrier. He learned that the man's name was something that Grant couldn't quite render in his phonetic memory. He learned that the man had been a sandal maker before his legs stopped working. He learned that the man had a daughter who brought him food when she could, which was not always.

He learned, from the specific quality of the man's attention in the two hours they spent together, that company was what the man needed. Not the food, which Grant could provide and which was real and which mattered. The company was the thing.

On Wednesday night he climbed to Marcus's roof and watched the Passover fires and thought about presence.

He had been practicing presence for four loops without recognizing that was what he was practicing. Each loop he'd been present to the next objective, he'd been in the city physically while being oriented toward the mission mentally. This loop was different. This loop he was practicing actual presence, being in the place with the full weight of himself, and he was finding that the place had things to show him that the mission orientation had been occluding.

He thought about the man in the lower quarter. About the specific quality of having his company received as something of value. About what it meant that two hours of sitting in the dust and being there was the specific thing the man needed.

He thought about his boys. About sitting with them at the kitchen table and being fully there rather than there-and-elsewhere. About what that would give them that the half-present version didn't give them.

He thought about Sarah in the kitchen at night after the boys were in bed, the specific quality of quiet between them that was different when he was fully present rather than managing his own interior at the same time.

He thought: I have been so organized around the doing that I have been missing the being. And the being is more than I realized.

Thursday evening the garden.

He sat against the olive tree. He let the weight build in the lower grove without trying to do anything about it. He prayed, the directed attention toward something that was the only prayer he had access to, pointing his whole self at the thing in the lower grove and not asking anything of it, just attending to it.

He thought about Miller. He thought about Jesse Miller directly, the specific man, the laugh, the way he talked about Linda, the unborn baby. He thought about the fact that Jesse Miller's death was grief and not debt, that the grief was real and the debt was not, that carrying one and putting down the other was both possible and required.

He thought about the thing in the lower grove carrying something for him. For Miller. For everyone.

He sat with that.

The arrest came.

He watched it without moving.

The white came.

He sat on the porch steps in the full Texas dark.

He looked at the red gate. The county road light caught the top rail and made the red paint glow slightly in the dark. He looked at it for a while.

He had done good this week. He knew that clearly and without the complicated accounting he'd been doing in previous loops. The merchant's son knew a knot. The vendor had not been arrested. The man in the lower quarter had had company for two days while he suffered. Those things were real. They would persist.

And the week had ended the way it always ended.

He sat with the arithmetic of that.

Good works are real. Good works matter. Good works are not the currency that pays the debt.

He thought about that directly. He thought about what he'd been doing for twelve years, all the good work, the fence mending and the pump repair and the showing up and the being competent, all of it real and valuable and genuine, and he thought about what had been underneath it.

The debt. The debt under all of it, driving some of it, the specific fuel of a man who needed to be the man she could count on because not being that man was the same as admitting that Miller had died for nothing.

But Miller hadn't died for nothing. Miller had died for everything. Miller's death was part of the same account that the man in the lower grove was settling tonight, in the garden, in the weight that had been building all week.

He went inside.

Sarah was at the kitchen table, still up, reading something. She looked up when he came in. She looked at his face.

She said: "You're different."

He said: "I'm getting close."

She said: "To what?"

He said: "To being able to put down the ledger. To carrying Miller as grief instead of debt."

She came around the table and sat beside him. She said: "Tell me."

He told her. He told her about the man in the lower quarter and about the merchant's son and about sitting on the roof watching the Passover fires. He told her about the thought he'd had in the garden, that the thing in the lower grove was carrying something for him, specifically for him, specifically including the seat trade and the morning in Ramadi.

He said: "I've been trying to pay a debt that was settled before I picked it up."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "And the loops are showing me that every time I try to pay it myself, I can't, because it's not mine to pay."

She said: "Yes."

He said: "I think I almost believe it."

She said: "Almost."

He said: "Two more loops."

She held his hand.

He sat in the kitchen of his house in Central Texas and felt something that was adjacent to rest. Not rest itself, not yet. Adjacent to it, in the direction of it, the specific sensation of a man moving toward a thing rather than being stuck in a thing.

That was something.

That was, for this night, enough.

The test results from Ramos came back on a Monday and she read them in her workshop with the door closed and the music off.

Clean MRI. Normal blood work except the inflammatory markers, which were worse than last panel, the number climbing in the specific direction of someone whose body was being repeatedly asked to repair something without adequate recovery time. Sleep study: fragmented to a degree Ramos had now noted twice. His clinical phrase this visit: *sleep architecture consistent with an organism under sustained non-standard physiological stress. Etiology unknown.*

She read that phrase three times.

*Non-standard physiological stress.*

She thought about what it would look like on paper, the full picture. A man who had lost twenty-two pounds in eight weeks while eating adequately. A man whose sleep was being interrupted at a rate the machine could measure and the doctor could not explain. A man with palmar scarring consistent with repeated abrasion to the same surfaces and callousing on his feet beyond what his stated daily activity would account for. A man with ancient limestone dust in the weave of his clothing and a watch crystal cracked in a pattern a watchmaker had attributed to thermal shock and pressure differential.

She thought about what it would look like to a doctor who had never met Grant, who was seeing these results cold.

It would look like a man who was being injured somewhere he wasn't supposed to be.

She closed the laptop. She went to the window. She stood there for a while looking at the south pasture and the fence line.

She had been patient. She had been documenting and waiting and managing her fear with the specific discipline of a woman who had learned that fear not expressed was not fear eliminated but fear stored, and that stored fear had a way of becoming something worse than the original thing. She had been storing it for eight weeks.

She thought: I am going to tell him tonight. Not as a speech. Not as a confrontation. Just the truth of what I'm carrying and what I need from him.

She went to find him.

He was in the barn replacing a rotted board in the south wall. She leaned against the door frame and watched him work for a moment, the methodical competence of it, the specific ease of a man whose hands knew what to do, and she felt something she could not fully name, which was the combination of love and grief and fear and the specific ache of watching something good be used up.

She said: "The results came back."

He set down the hammer. He turned.

She said: "Clean MRI. Everything else is pointing in the wrong direction."

She told him about the inflammatory markers. She told him about *non-standard physiological stress.* She told him about the sleep study language and about what a doctor seeing these results cold would understand.

I watch you eat and I watch you sleep and I watch the marks accumulate on your hands and I see you losing ground, and I do not know what the limit is, and the not knowing is the thing that scares me most."

He said: "Sarah , "

She said: "Let me finish."

I am asking you to know, to actually carry the knowledge, that the cost of going is real and that I am the one watching the cost from the outside and that I need you to treat what is happening to your body as something that matters. Not as acceptable collateral damage. As something that matters."

He said: "I know."

She said: "Tell me you know."

He paused. "I believe the seventh loop is the one where it resolves. I believe after that the cost stops. But I don't know that. I believe it."

Because my boys are watching their father get used up by something and they need to see him here, present, not somewhere else in his head. And I need it too." She paused. "I need the man who comes back from Jerusalem to be the one who chose to come back. Not just the one who got sent back."

He went inside.

He called Kyle Miller.

He slept.

On Friday at 2:58 PM she was in the kitchen when the white came and she was right there, watching, and their eyes met for one second, her face was not afraid, or not only afraid, it was something harder than fear, it was the face of a person who has decided to hold on regardless of what holding on costs, and then he was gone.

The test results from Ramos came back on a Monday.

Sarah had been waiting for them for three weeks and she opened the email in her workshop with the door closed and the music off and she read through the report with the specific attention of someone who had been preparing to receive bad news and who was now recalibrating to a different kind of bad news, which was the kind that wasn't in the report.

The MRI was clean. No new lesions, no structural changes, no evidence of the progressive TBI pathology that Ramos had been checking for. The blood work was normal except for the vitamin D deficiency that was normal for anyone who spent Texas summers indoors, which Grant did not. The sleep study had shown significant fragmented sleep, multiple wake events per night, reduced REM, the sleep architecture of someone under sustained physiological stress, but nothing that explained the other things.

The other things were in Ramos's clinical notes, which she had read twice already because Ramos had given her a copy at her specific request, because she was the one who had sent him the geological report and she was the one who had been documenting the physical changes with the methodical attention of someone who understood that documentation was what you did when the situation was beyond your direct control.

Ramos had written: Patient presents with unexplained weight loss of twenty-two pounds over approximately six weeks, inconsistent with stated dietary intake. Bilateral palmar scarring in distribution inconsistent with stated occupational activities. Callousing of plantar surfaces exceeding that expected from stated daily physical activity. Watch crystal fracture pattern suggests repeated thermal shock, mechanism unknown. Soil sample analysis (external lab, submitted by patient's wife) indicates geological origin inconsistent with patient's region of residence or known travel history. Sleep architecture significantly disrupted. No pathological explanation identified for the combination of physical findings. Diagnosis deferred pending additional observation. Patient appears cognitively intact and demonstrates insight into the anomalous nature of his symptoms.

She had read the phrase anomalous nature of his symptoms three times.

She closed the laptop.

She went to the window of her workshop and looked out at the south pasture and the fence line and the cedar beyond it and she stood there for a while.

She was not a person who allowed herself extended periods of feeling before she moved toward function. She had learned this about herself over the years, that the feeling was real and necessary and that the way she honored it was by moving through it toward something useful rather than staying in it until it stopped on its own, which it rarely did. She allowed herself five minutes at the window. Then she went to the kitchen and put on coffee and sat at the table and opened her notebook and started writing.

She wrote: What I know.

She listed it. The dust, confirmed Levantine by independent geological analysis. The watch crystal, fractured in a pattern inconsistent with normal use, checked with a watchmaker who had said something about thermal shock and pressure differentials. The boots, she had taken the second destroyed pair to a leather worker in Fredericksburg who had looked at the wear pattern and the specific way the welt had failed and had said: this leather has been in desert heat. Not Hill Country heat. Different kind. The weight, twenty-two pounds in six weeks, and she had been feeding him, had been watching him eat, had been the one doing the cooking and she could account for the calories going in and they were not accounting for the weight going out. The sleep, she knew his sleep the way she knew all his physical patterns, and what she knew was that he was coming up multiple times a night and staying up for long stretches and that the quality of his waking was different from the quality of his waking before the loops started, more present but also more used, the quality of someone returning from somewhere rather than someone who had simply been asleep.

She wrote: What I don't know.

She listed that too. She didn't know how long this could continue before the physical cost became unsustainable. She didn't know what the upper limit of the weight loss was before it became a medical emergency. She didn't know what happened if a loop happened and Grant was not physically capable of managing the transition. She didn't know what the seventh loop would cost him.

She sat with the two lists and looked at them.

Then she wrote: What I can do.

She could document. She was already documenting. She could make sure he was eating the maximum amount possible and getting the best nutrition available, which she was doing. She could maintain the routine of the ranch and the family so that when he came back from each loop he came back to a stable environment, which she was doing. She could keep the boys informed enough to not be frightened and not so informed as to be burdened with adult weight they weren't ready for, which she was doing, barely.

She could trust him.

This was the hard one. Not because she didn't trust him, she trusted Grant the way she trusted the fence line she could see from her window, the way you trusted something that had been there and held through every kind of weather. She trusted him without reservation or condition.

But trusting him was not the same as not being afraid. And she was afraid.

She had been afraid since the second loop, when he'd come back with the second pair of destroyed boots and the split welt and another four pounds gone and the specific quality in his face that she'd been watching develop across six weeks, not haunted, exactly, because haunted implied being followed by something, and this was the opposite of that, this was a quality of a man who was being pulled toward something, a man in a current, and she didn't know where the current was taking him and whether the place it was taking him was a place he could come back from.

The boys had noticed.

Caleb had come to her three weeks ago and said, with the characteristic Caleb directness that was going to serve him well and occasionally cost him: "Mom. Is Dad okay?"

She had said: "He's working through something."

Caleb had said: "The thing from the porch chair."

She had said: "Yes."

Caleb had said: "He looks like he's been somewhere hard."

She had said: "He has. He keeps going somewhere hard and coming back."

Caleb had said: "Is he going to stop?"

She had said: "I think so. I think he's close."

Caleb had looked at her with the look that was his father's look, the one that ran inventory and filed the result without reacting to it. He had said: "Okay." He had gone back to whatever he'd been doing.

Noah had been more quiet about it, which was Noah's way, Noah processed privately and emerged with conclusions rather than questions, and his conclusion, delivered one evening while helping Grant with the truck, had been: you're coming back different every time and it seems like the right direction. Which was the most accurate thing anyone had said about the situation and which had come from a fifteen-year-old who communicated in calibrated understatements.

She closed her notebook.

She went to find Grant.

He was in the barn, replacing a board in the south wall that had been rotting since last winter. She watched him work for a moment from the doorway, the methodical competence of it, the way his body knew what to do and did it without consulting his mind for much. He was thinner than he should be and she could see it now in the way she could see the structure of a piece of wood when you'd stripped the surface finish, the underlying thing, more visible than it ought to be.

He looked up when he heard her. He set down the hammer.

She said: "The results came back."

He said: "And?"

She said: "Clean. Nothing pathological."

He said: "Ramos's phrase."

She said: "Yes." She came and leaned against the barn wall beside him. She said: "Grant."

He said: "Yeah."

She said: "I need to tell you something and I need you to hear it."

He waited.

She said: "I am frightened. I have been frightened since the third loop and I have not said this to you directly because you were working through something and I did not want to add to what you were carrying. But I am telling you now because you need to know what this has been like from where I am."

He said: "Okay."

She said: "You have lost twenty-two pounds. You come back every time with more damage and less of you. I watch you eat and I watch you sleep and I watch the marks accumulate on your hands and I know you are being used up by something and I don't know how much is left." She paused. She said: "I have been lying awake at night running through what happens if the next loop is the one that costs too much."

He said: "Sarah , "

She said: "Let me finish."

He let her finish.

She said: "I believe you. I have believed you since the dust. I know this is real and I know it matters and I know you need to see it through. I am not asking you to stop. I am telling you that the cost is real and that I am the one watching the cost accumulate from the outside and that I need you to know it, so that you are carrying this with the full weight of what it is, not just the Jerusalem part of it."

He was quiet for a moment.

He said: "I know."

She said: "Tell me you know."

He said: "I know that I am being used up. I know that every loop costs something I don't entirely come back with. I know that what I'm carrying into the loops is the physical consequence of the loops and that it compounds." He paused. He said: "I think there is one more loop. I think the seventh loop is the one where it resolves and I think after that the cost stops. But I don't know that. I believe it."

She said: "Okay."

He said: "I'm sorry I haven't been saying this to you directly."

She said: "You've been managing it."

He said: "I've been managing it because managing it is what I know how to do."

She said: "I know." She looked at him. She said: "After the seventh loop, whatever you come back with, we deal with it together. All of it. The Jerusalem part and the Miller part and whatever the loops have taken from you physically. We deal with it together."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "Not you managing it while I watch."

He said: "Not me managing it while you watch."

She said: "Together."

He said: "Together."

She put her hand on his arm. She said: "Eat everything I put in front of you between now and the next loop."

He said: "I will."

She said: "Sleep."

He said: "I'll try."

She said: "Don't try. Sleep."

He said: "Okay."

She said: "I love you."

He said: "I know."

She said: "Say it back."

He said: "I love you."

She nodded. She went back to the house.

He stood in the barn with the hammer in his hand and looked at the board he'd been replacing and then at the barn wall and then out through the barn door at the pasture and the cedar ridge and the sky moving over all of it.

He thought about the specific weight of being known by someone who was frightened for you. Not frightened of you, not frightened by something you'd done, frightened for you, which was the specific fear of a person who had put their life alongside yours and who understood that what happened to you happened to them.

He thought about the twenty-two pounds.

He thought about Sarah lying awake running the numbers on what was left.

He thought: she deserves the full resolution. She deserves to have this done and done completely and not managed.

He set the hammer down.

He went inside.

He sat at the kitchen table and she put food in front of him and he ate all of it.

He went to bed at nine and he lay in the dark beside her and he said, quietly, into the dark of their bedroom: "One more. And then I come back with all of it."

She said, from the edge of sleep: "I know."

He lay in the dark and thought about the seventh loop and about what it was going to take and about what he was going to be on the other side of it.

He slept.

The white came on Friday at 2:58 PM.

He was at the kitchen sink washing the lunch dishes and he grabbed the edge of the counter and she was right there, watching, and their eyes met for one second before the white took him, and what he saw in her face in that second was not fear, it was something harder and more permanent than fear, the specific quality of a person who has decided to hold on regardless of what holding on costs.

He went.

He held that with him into the seventh loop.
