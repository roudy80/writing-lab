# CHAPTER THREE

## THE TEXAS WEEKS

The first thing Sarah noticed was that he was awake before she was.

This was not, by itself, remarkable. Grant was an early riser, had been since the Army, which had trained the early-morning habit into him so thoroughly that even now, six years out of law enforcement and twelve years out of the military, his body considered 0500 a reasonable time to be operational. But there was a difference between the quality of his early-morning waking and what she was experiencing in the days after the first white-out, and the difference was this: he wasn't waking up early. He was waking up and not going back to sleep and not getting up either, just lying there in the rigid stillness of a man who was managing something.

She knew that stillness. She had been sleeping beside it for fifteen years.

She didn't say anything. She lay in the dark beside him and listened to him not sleep and thought about what she'd seen when she'd come to the porch door to call him for dinner on Friday evening: the quality of his face when he'd looked up, which was the quality of a face that had been somewhere and come back and was not yet fully back. She had seen that face before, after deployments, after bad shifts, after the three or four moments in their marriage that had required him to do things that cost something. She knew that face.

This was different from those times.

Those times the face had been tired in a way that was moving toward recovery. This face was not moving toward anything yet. It was processing something that was still too close to process.

She went back to sleep.

In the morning he was at the fence before she had her coffee made. She watched him from the kitchen window, the post driver going up and coming down, the patient repetition of a man doing work that needed doing, and she thought about the palms of his hands, which she had seen when he'd washed up at the sink, the raw abrasion of a fall.

He had not fallen on any hard surface she knew about.

She poured his coffee and took it to him.

He told her the fence needed mending. She said she knew. He drank the coffee and looked at the gate and she looked at him looking at it and said the thing that was true because that was what she did, she said the true thing: *you're the man I can count on.* She said it without performance, without agenda, as the statement of a fact she had arrived at through fifteen years of evidence.

He turned back to the wire.

She went inside and thought about his hands.

That evening she took the work shirt he'd been wearing on Friday out of the laundry hamper. Instead she held it up to the window the way she held pieces of wood when examining grain. The collar was dusty. Not the reddish-brown clay of the caliche road or the tan of the Hill Country soil. Pale dust, almost white, packed into the cotton weave with the density of something that had been there for hours rather than something that had blown on in a moment of wind.

She pinched a small amount of it between her fingers.

Fine. Very fine. Almost like chalk, but not chalk, she knew chalk, used it in the workshop for marking cuts on dark wood. This was something else. Something that had been ground down to this fineness by something other than industrial milling. The fineness of a thing worn down slowly by time and weight and the passage of many feet.

She put the shirt in a plastic bag instead of the wash.

She labeled the bag with the date and put it on the shelf in her workshop where she kept things that needed attention.

She went to bed and lay in the dark beside Grant, who was not sleeping, and she thought about pale dust and about the quality of his face when he'd come in from the porch.

She thought: something happened to him. Something real. The evidence of it is in his hands and on his collar.

She decided to wait for him to tell her.

She was patient about waiting. She had learned that the telling came, with him. It always came. The timeline was not always the one she'd choose. But he came to it.

She fell asleep.

---

On Monday he fixed the barn roof. On Tuesday he replaced the truck's brake caliper. On Wednesday he drove the boys to school and sat in the parking lot for a few minutes after they went in, and she watched him from the drive and said nothing.

On Wednesday afternoon she went to the university extension lab website and submitted the dust sample.

On Thursday he sat on the porch in the mid-afternoon and she brought him coffee and she looked at his hands, healing now, the raw skin closing, but the marks clear and distributed in the pattern of a fall on hard stone, and she thought about what surfaces were that hard.

Stone. Ancient stone. The kind that had been worn smooth by the passage of centuries.

She went back inside.

On Friday at 2:58 PM she was in the kitchen when he grabbed the edge of the counter.

She turned.

His knuckles were white on the counter edge. His eyes were open but not seeing the kitchen. His body was doing something that she had no framework for, not a seizure, not a faint, not the specific physical signatures of any medical event she had witnessed. His body was somewhere else while his hands held the counter.

She moved toward him.

The white-out lasted approximately forty seconds. She counted.

When he came back, and she understood it as a coming back, a return, because whatever had been in his eyes during those forty seconds was not him in the way she knew him, he took a long breath and let go of the counter and stood up straight.

He looked at her.

She said: "I'm going to make you an appointment with Dr. Ramos."

He said: "Okay."

She said: "Today. I'm calling today."

He said: "Okay."

She called Ramos's office from the workshop with the door closed.

She said: "My husband is a veteran, he has prior blast trauma history, and he is experiencing episodes that I don't have a good way to describe. They last approximately forty seconds. He's physically present but appears to be, elsewhere. He has unexplained abrasions on his palms and an unexplained weight loss and there's dust on his clothing that I've sent out for geological analysis because it looks wrong for where we live." She paused. She said: "I know that sounds strange."

The scheduler said: "We have a Thursday opening."

She took the Thursday opening.

She went back inside and made dinner.

She set a plate in front of Grant.

She said: "Eat."

He ate.
