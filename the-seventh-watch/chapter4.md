# CHAPTER FOUR

## LOOP 2: THE TRAITOR

The second time, he came with a plan.

He'd spent the week between loops in the operational mode that had served him across twenty years, not the anxious rehearsal of a man who didn't trust himself, but the clean methodical thinking of someone breaking a complex problem into solvable components. After-action review, honest assessment, revised approach.

Errors, Loop 1: No objective on arrival. No prepared cover. No local intelligence network. No language. Got distracted by the procession. Drew attention. Made a poor tactical withdrawal into a dead end. Result: arrested inside twenty minutes.

Corrections: Establish cover first. Go directly to the market district on arrival. Build local intelligence network before attempting any surveillance operations. Develop at least functional language capability.

Intelligence, Loop 1: General layout of the lower market district and the approach to the Temple courts. Roman patrol timing and pattern in the Temple area. Timing of the weekly loop, five days, Sunday through Friday afternoon. The name and behavioral profile of the target: Judas Iscariot, treasurer, logistics manager, distinguishable by operational separation from the main group.

The mission was clear. The crucifixion happened because of a betrayal. Interrupt the betrayal, the arrest doesn't happen. He had the target identified and profiled. He had the operational timeline. He had a base of operations, Marcus's room, established in principle if not yet in practice for this loop.

He'd spent six days doing four to six hours a day of Greek on the Rosetta Stone program, eating his meals while the program ran, drilling vocabulary in the truck between errands, writing words on index cards and testing himself against the kitchen window. He had approximately one hundred and twenty functional words in Greek. Not enough for a real conversation. Enough to start one.

He'd ordered the concordance. He'd spent two evenings with the UT professor's notes from the phone call, building his city map as completely as he could from her descriptions. He'd thought through every detail of the cover story, the clothing, the approach.

He'd kept the legal pad hidden in the nightstand drawer. He examined this instinct when he caught himself doing it, the operational security reflex, trained so deep it didn't ask for justification, and decided to let it stand. Whatever was happening had the quality of an operation and the habits of an operation applied.

Sarah had watched him do all of this with the patient observant attention she'd been giving him all week, and on Thursday evening she'd said: "You look like you're preparing for something."

He said: "I am."

She said: "For the next loop."

He said: "Yes."

She said: "What's the objective this time?"

He said: "Interrupt a specific transaction before Thursday night."

She said: "And if that doesn't work?"

He said: "Then I figure out what approach to take on Loop 3."

She said: "Okay." She looked at him for a moment. "You have the look you had before Ramadi."

He looked at her.

She said: "The look that means you've locked in on a mission and you're not fully present anymore. You're already there."

He said: "I'm here."

She said: "Part of you is."

He said: "Sarah , "

She said: "I'm not complaining. I'm just telling you what I see." She went back to the piece of cedar she'd been sanding. "Come back when you can."

He sat with that for a while.

---

He arrived Sunday on the same street, oriented in under twenty seconds, progress, and moved directly downhill to the market district. He went to Marcus's stall. He set the knife on the counter. Marcus took it and assessed it and they arranged the room in Greek in under two minutes.

He went up, set down his pack, and came back down.

He said to Marcus: "I need to find a man. A man in the group of the teacher who came in yesterday with the procession. He handles money for the group."

Marcus said: "Why?"

Grant said: "Because he is going to do something that I need to understand. And possibly something I need to prevent."

Marcus looked at him. He said: "You know what is going to happen this week."

Grant said: "Some of it."

Marcus said: "More than you're saying."

Grant said: "Yes."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. He said: "If you know what is going to happen this week, and you are trying to prevent it, you should know that this is not the first time someone has tried." He picked up the harness he'd been working on. "The prophets said this was coming. The teachers have said it. Many people have wanted to stop it. None of them could."

Grant said: "I need to try."

Marcus said: "I know." He pulled a stitch through. "That is something about you that I have noticed, even in the short time I have known you."

Grant said: "What's that?"

Marcus said: "You need to try everything before you can accept a thing."

He said it without judgment. As observation. As the specific pragmatism of a man who had spent fifty years watching people navigate the city and had learned to read the different varieties of human stubbornness.

Grant said: "Tell me where I can find the man."

Marcus told him.

---

The problem with one hundred and twenty words of Greek was not the number. The problem was the register.

He had drilled vocabulary for six days, the nouns and verbs and the basic grammatical connectors that the Rosetta Stone program considered foundational. He could name objects. He could ask where things were. He could express simple wants and simple refusals. What he could not do was navigate nuance, and nuance was what he needed.

In the market district on the first full day of the second loop, he understood this with the specific educational quality of experience, which was that it taught you things you hadn't known you needed to know.

He was trying to purchase food.

The vendor at the bread stall was a heavyset woman in her forties who had the specific impatient energy of someone who had been doing the same transaction ten thousand times and had no tolerance for inefficiency in the doing of it. She looked at him when he approached with the assessment of someone who had seen every kind of foreigner that pilgrimage season produced and who had developed a fast and accurate read on what category of transaction was about to happen.

He said, in Greek: "Bread. I want bread."

She said something in Aramaic.

He said: "Greek? Do you speak Greek?"

She switched to Greek, but it was a different Greek than Marcus's, faster, more idiomatic, contracted in ways his program hadn't prepared him for. He caught one word in three.

He said: "I don't understand. Slowly, please."

She said it again, not slowly.

He gathered that the question was about quantity or about payment or possibly both. He had no money. He had no trade goods. He had the pocket knife, but the pocket knife was the thing he traded for Marcus's room and he wasn't giving that up.

He thought about what he had.

He had his belt. Good leather, good buckle, constructed better than anything available in this market. He unbuckled it and held it out. The vendor looked at it. She took it and turned it in her hands and looked at the stitching and looked at the buckle mechanism, the specific ratchet design that had no equivalent in the first century, and she said something that he read as skeptical.

He demonstrated the buckle. He worked it three times. He could see the moment she understood what she was looking at.

She gave him bread and figs.

He ate standing at the edge of the market with his jeans held up by his hand, thinking about the logistics problem.

He had come into the first loop with nothing. He had come into the second loop with the knife, which had bought the room, and the belt, which had bought two meals. He had the watch on his wrist, which was not tradeable without a story he couldn't tell. He had his wallet, which contained currency that was worthless here and identification cards that would create more problems than they solved. He had the quarter, which he was not going to trade.

He did the math.

In the first century, an unskilled day laborer earned approximately one denarius per day, he knew this from his concordance research, the specific detail of the parable of the workers in the vineyard. He could not earn denarii. He had no relevant skills in the first-century economy, and the skills he did have, intelligence analysis, defensive tactics, firearms operation, were not useful here.

What he had was physical capacity and the ability to do hard work without complaint.

He went back to Marcus.

He said: "I need to work for food. I'll work in the stall. I'll carry things. I'll do what you need."

Marcus looked at him. He said: "You don't know leather work."

Grant said: "Teach me."

Marcus considered this. He said: "You can carry the deliveries."

He carried deliveries. He spent the rest of Monday and all of Tuesday morning moving finished leather goods from Marcus's stall to buyers in the upper market and in the Temple precincts and to a saddler near the Roman garrison who took delivery of a set of horse tack with the professional appreciation of a man who knew quality when he handled it. Grant carried the bundles and watched Marcus negotiate in rapid Aramaic that he followed at about forty percent, and he paid close attention to the city as he moved through it.

This was, he realized by the second delivery, better surveillance than anything he'd been doing.

When you moved through a city as a person with a purpose, a purpose that was visible, that read to observers as legitimate, you became part of the city rather than an observer of it. The stall holders who had noticed him on Sunday as a conspicuous foreigner acknowledged him on Tuesday as the leather merchant's carrier. The Roman soldiers whose patrol he'd been timing had no interest in a man moving bundled goods between stalls. He was visible and invisible simultaneously, which was exactly the cover he needed.

He also learned, by Tuesday afternoon, that his Greek was not the primary problem.

The primary problem was Aramaic.

Greek was the trade language, the language of commerce and administration, the language you used with foreigners. But the actual life of the city, the arguments between neighbors and the gossip between vendors and the things people said to each other when they thought no one was listening, that happened in Aramaic. And it happened fast, with the specific contracted speed of a language being spoken by people who had been speaking it their whole lives and who had no interest in being understood by people who hadn't.

He had maybe seventy words of Aramaic from his pre-loop drilling. Enough to buy things and express simple directions. Not enough to follow a conversation between two men at a market stall who were discussing, from what he could gather, either a business arrangement or a family problem or possibly both.

He went back to Marcus on Tuesday evening and said: "I need to learn more Aramaic."

Marcus said: "This will take years."

Grant said: "I have five days."

Marcus considered this. He called up the stairs: "Miriam."

Miriam came down and looked at Grant and looked at her father and said, in Greek: "You want me to teach him."

Marcus said: "He has five days."

Miriam said: "Five days is enough for maybe fifty words."

Grant said: "Then teach me the fifty most important ones."

She thought about it. She said: "Come up."

He followed her up to the room. She sat cross-legged on the floor with the lamp between them and she taught him Aramaic with the specific efficiency of someone who had thought about this before being asked. She didn't start with nouns. She started with verbs, the action words, the words that moved situations. Come. Go. Stop. Look. Wait. Danger. Help. She drilled them with the repetitive patience of someone who had been taught this way herself, making him say each word until the pronunciation satisfied her and then moving to the next.

She said: "Most foreigners learn the market words first. Where is. How much. Give me. These are useful for buying things but they don't help you understand what people are saying. The verbs help you understand what people are doing."

He said: "That's a smart distinction."

She said: "My mother taught me Greek this way. She said: learn what people want from you before you learn what you want from them."

He said: "Your mother was wise."

She said: "She was." She said the next verb: "Hear."

He said it back.

She said: "Good."

He spent two hours with her. He came away with fifty-two new words and the specific pedagogical gift of someone who understood the difference between vocabulary and comprehension. He lay on the bedroll afterward and ran through the words until they stopped slipping, the way you ran through a weapons check until the sequence was in your hands rather than your head.

He slept better that night than any previous loop.

Wednesday morning he went back to the surveillance work, but he went at it differently. He was moving through the city now with the cover of the deliveries and the beginnings of a functional Aramaic vocabulary and the accumulated knowledge of two loops' reconnaissance. He was not, he thought, doing it smoothly yet, he was still large and wrong-colored and dressed in clothes that no amount of wear was going to make look local. But he was better.

He was at the edge of the Temple courts on Wednesday morning when he saw the guard he'd been seeing since Loop 1.

Not Lucius, that was a different loop. This was a Temple guard, one of the Levitical temple police who managed security in the courts, a compact man in his thirties with the specific bearing of someone who had been doing security work long enough to have developed a professional relationship with his own instincts. He moved through the courts differently from the pilgrims, not browsing, not attending, just watching. Grant had noticed him in Loop 1 without quite registering him as significant. This loop he registered him.

The guard had noticed Grant.

Not aggressively. Not the accusatory attention of someone who was about to do something. The professional attention of someone who had added Grant to a list of things worth monitoring. Grant caught the flicker of recognition when the guard's eyes crossed his position, the specific quality of a gaze that was not encountering something for the first time.

He had been made.

He moved out of the courts with the unhurried purpose of a man with somewhere to be and three hours to get there, and he stood in the lower market and thought about the specific problem of being a person of interest in a city where he had no official identity and no plausible explanation for his presence.

In a modern environment he would have changed his pattern, varied his routes, used different surveillance positions. In Jerusalem in the first century his options were more limited because his cover, such as it was, was primarily physical. He was carrying Marcus's deliveries. He was a man with a job. He needed to keep looking like a man with a job while conducting surveillance on a specific target.

He went back to the market and picked up a delivery and moved.

By Thursday he had Judas's pattern mapped. By Thursday afternoon he had arranged the cistern meeting through Marcus. By Thursday evening he had sat with Judas and heard can you choose not to breathe and understood that the plan was not going to work the way the plan was supposed to work.

He went to the garden that night with the residue of all of it in him, the language work and the delivery routes and the guard's professional gaze and the conversation at the cistern, and he stood at fifteen feet and felt the weight stop him and watched the arrest proceed without him.

The city had been in him by the end of this loop in a way it hadn't been at the end of Loop 1. The dust in his boots was the dust of specific streets he could now navigate. The smell on his clothes was the specific smell of the lower market and the tanning district and the east side of the Temple courts where the sellers set up. The calluses developing on his hands were from two days of carrying leather goods across the uneven stone of the market district.

He had been in the city. Not just in the loop.

When the white came, he was ready for it.

He came back to Texas with Aramaic verbs in his head, come, go, stop, look, wait, danger, help, hear, and the specific understanding of a man who had learned something by earning it rather than by being given it.

He lay in the dark and thought about the fifty-two words and about the guard's professional gaze and about the weight at fifteen feet.

He thought about Miller.

He thought about the morning light in Ramadi and about the letter in the chest pocket and about a man who had wanted to see where he was every day, who had wanted to know what the city looked like today.

He thought: I am learning what this city looks like. Loop by loop, I am learning what it looks like.

He fell asleep before he understood why that felt important.

He spent Sunday afternoon and Monday watching. He identified his two candidates from Loop 1 on the first afternoon of observation, his body had retained the pattern-recognition from the previous loop, though his mind couldn't have articulated exactly why these two men read as more likely than the others. By Monday evening he had eliminated one of them. By Tuesday morning the remaining candidate had resolved the question by doing the thing Grant had been waiting for.

He tracked Judas to the secondary courtyard. He watched the conversation with the functionaries. He read the body language of negotiation and agreement.

He had his man.

Tuesday through Wednesday he tracked. He built the pattern of life with the accumulated intelligence of a man who had done this before in this specific place, even though this version of the week didn't know that. He knew when Judas went out in the mornings. He knew which routes he used. He knew where the meetings happened.

Wednesday afternoon he arranged the meeting at the cistern through Marcus's network.

Judas came. The conversation was brief, they had already said the essential things in their first meeting. Judas still believed the man would walk out of it. He had the evidence for it and he was holding to both. Grant still knew he wouldn't. There was nothing more to say between them.

He sat at the cistern after Judas left and felt the specific frustration of a man whose best professional approach has bounced off the situation without leaving a mark. The target was informed. The target had a fully formed understanding of the outcome and a fully formed decision to proceed anyway. The normal mechanisms of surveillance and disruption applied to this situation exactly the way normal tools applied to materials they weren't designed for: they touched the surface and had no purchase.

He went to the garden Thursday night.

He positioned in the olive trees.

He moved to intercept.

At fifteen feet, the weight stopped him again.

It was the same weight as Loop 1, the same quality, the same absolute quality of something immense occupying the space in the lower grove. But this time he was expecting it, and expecting it changed the experience. The first time it had been a wall he'd walked into. This time it was a wall he approached and stopped before.

He stood on the root in the olive grove and he felt the weight and he thought about what it meant that he kept stopping at fifteen feet.

The arrest proceeded.

He watched it.

When the white came, he was ready for it.

---

He came back on the porch. Evening. The orange light of late October on the pasture.

He sat in the porch chair and took off the left boot and looked at the split.

Sarah appeared.

"Needs conditioning," she said.

"Yeah," he said.

She went inside.

He looked at the boot and looked at the gate and thought about the second loop.

He had correctly executed the surveillance. He had correctly identified and tracked the target. He had correctly established a meeting and had the conversation he'd planned to have. He had correctly positioned himself for the intercept.

At fifteen feet, something had stopped him.

Not the first time. Not a coincidence. A thing that operated in the situation with the consistency of a rule.

He thought: the tactical approach to this situation is not the right approach. I don't know what the right approach is yet. But the tactical approach is not it.

He went inside.

He ate dinner.

He thought about what Marcus had said: you need to try everything before you can accept a thing. He thought about whether that was a weakness or just the shape of who he was.

He thought: probably both.
