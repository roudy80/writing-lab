# CHAPTER FIVE

## THE TEXAS WEEKS

The VA appointment was on Thursday.

She drove him. She had not planned to drive him, he was capable of driving, there was nothing physically preventing it, but she had woken up on Thursday morning and decided she was going to be in that room, and when she'd told him this he had said okay the way he said okay to the things she decided that were not negotiable. He recognized which things were not negotiable. He was good about that.

Dr. Ramos was thorough. Sarah had not met him before, Grant had been seeing him for routine follow-ups for three years and she had not accompanied him to those appointments because there was nothing in a routine follow-up that required her presence. She was there now.

She watched Ramos examine Grant's hands. She watched Ramos note the abrasion scarring and the distribution and write it down without comment. She watched him weigh Grant, eleven pounds below his last recorded weight, and write that down without comment. She watched him look at the watch crystal, the hairline crack from the two to the eight.

She said: "I submitted a dust sample to the university extension lab. The report said Levantine limestone geology. Inconsistent with Central Texas or any contiguous North American region."

Ramos looked at her.

She said: "I have the report if you'd like a copy."

Ramos said: "Please."

She handed him the printed report. She had brought it in her bag. She watched him read it twice.

He said: "This is from his clothing."

She said: "From his collar. After the first episode."

He said: "The first."

She said: "There have been two. The second was last Friday." She paused. She said: "He came back from the second one with the watch crystal cracked. It wasn't cracked when he, went."

Ramos wrote for a moment. He said, to Grant: "Tell me about the episodes."

Grant told him. Ramos listened. He wrote.

At the end he said: "I'm ordering an MRI, a sleep study, and a full blood panel. I want to see you back in six weeks with the results." He looked at Grant over the top of his glasses. "I'm documenting everything exactly as you've described it. I want you to know that." He paused. He said: "I've been doing this for twenty-two years. I've had patients describe events I couldn't explain medically. In those cases I documented carefully and followed the documentation. That's what I'm going to do here."

Grant said: "Thank you."

Sarah said: "What's your working hypothesis?"

Ramos said: "I don't have one. That's honest. The TBI history gives me a framework for the sensory profile of the onset, the tinnitus, the olfactory component, but it doesn't explain the physical sequelae. The dust sample is genuinely anomalous." He looked at her steadily. "I think you were right to come in. I think you were right to send the dust out. Keep documenting."

She said: "I am."

They drove home through the Hill Country. The late October light on the cedar. She drove and he looked at the hills and neither of them said much.

She said, eventually: "The boys."

He said: "I know."

She said: "Caleb asked me yesterday if you were okay."

He said: "What did you tell him?"

She said: "That you were working through something and that it was real and that he should leave you room." She paused. "He said you look like you've been somewhere."

Grant said nothing.

She said: "He's seventeen, Grant. He has your eyes. He sees things."

Grant said: "I know."

She said: "Noah is quieter about it but he's watching too."

He said: "I'll talk to them."

She said: "Not yet. Not until you have more of it." She looked at the road. "But soon."

He said: "Okay."

She said: "And I need you to eat. Ramos said eleven pounds. I need you to eat everything I put in front of you. I need you to sleep when you can. I need you to , " She stopped. She started again. "I need you to be as present here as you are there. Whatever there is."

He said: "I know."

She said: "Tell me you'll try."

He said: "I'll try."

She drove them home. She made dinner. She sat at the table with her family and watched her husband eat and thought about anomalous dust and a doctor who didn't have a working hypothesis and a crack in a watch crystal that hadn't been there before.

She thought: he is going somewhere. Something is taking him somewhere.

She thought: I have to trust that he'll come back.

She had been trusting that for fifteen years. The trust was not nothing. It was not easy and it was not naive and it was not the absence of fear. It was the specific and practiced trust of a woman who had looked at a man from enough angles to know what he was made of.

She set her fork down.

She reached across the table and put her hand on his.

He looked at her.

She said: "Come back."

He said: "Always."

*THE WRONG MAN IN THE RIGHT PLACE*

---

The geology report was still on the kitchen table when she found him in the porch chair.

She'd been in the workshop when he came back, the white-out happened while she was sanding a table leg, the specific focused work of a woman who had learned to keep her hands busy when she was waiting for something, and she'd heard the back door and known from the sound of his boots on the floor that he was back.

She'd gone to find him.

He was in the porch chair. Not the one he usually sat in, the one positioned to see three sides of the property, the tactical chair, the one he'd been unable to sit in since the first loop because sitting in it meant waiting to go back. He was in the regular chair. Both hands in his lap. Looking at the pasture.

She sat on the steps.

She said: "The extraction route."

He said: "Didn't work."

She said: "He wouldn't take it."

He said: "No."

She let the silence be for a while. The evening was doing its Hill Country thing, the light going sideways and the cedar going gold and the first coyote starting up in the middle distance. She had been sitting in Texas evenings like this for six years and she had not gotten bored of it and she did not expect to.

She said: "Tell me what he said."

So he told her about the upper room. About the man telling him directly: the thing you're carrying is not yours. She listened without moving. When he finished she looked at her hands on her knees and thought about that.

She said: "Miller."

He said: "Partly."

She said: "More than partly."

He said: "Yes. More than partly."

The coyote called again and a second one answered it from further south. She looked at the property, the barn, the fence line, the creek draw where the cedar was thick, the gate at the end of the drive with the evening light on the red paint.

She said: "I've been reading about the atonement."

He looked at her.

She said: "Since the second loop. I've been reading. Not devotionally, I've been reading the way I read anything I want to understand, which is systematically." She paused. "I grew up in the church the way you did. I know the framework. I wanted to understand the framework at a level that was actually adequate to what you're describing."

He waited.

She said: "If the framework is real, if what happened in Jerusalem that week is what it's supposed to be, then the account is settled. Not partially settled. Completely settled. Not by anyone who owed it. By the only one who could settle it." She looked at him. "And the settlement would include you specifically. Your specific debt. The seat trade and the morning and twelve years of the paying."

He said: "I know."

She said: "Do you believe it?"

He said: "Intellectually, yes."

She said: "That's not what I asked."

He said: "I'm working on believing it in the other way."

She said: "I know." She stood up from the steps. She brushed the cedar dust from her jeans. She said: "There's something else."

He looked at her.

She said: "Ramos called. The MRI is clean. No new lesions. But the sleep study , " She paused. "He said it's the most fragmented sleep architecture he's seen in a non-pathological brain. He said it looks like, his phrase, 'repeated traumatic interruptions occurring outside the patient's conscious experience.' He doesn't know what that means medically. He wanted me to know."

Grant said: "The white-outs."

She said: "That's what I told him. He said he'd documented it."

She went inside.

He sat on the porch and looked at the gate in the dark. He thought about the sleep study data, the specific objective record of what the loops were doing to his brain, the scientific documentation of a man being repeatedly interrupted in a way that a machine could measure and a doctor could not explain.

He thought: every loop leaves a mark. Some of the marks are on the outside and some of them are in the data.

He went inside. He ate dinner. He went to bed.

He did not sleep well. The sleep study had said so, was saying so, was recording it in its specific way. He lay in the dark and let the fact of the recording be a comfort. It was being documented. Something was paying attention.

He fell asleep eventually.

The weight was beginning to accumulate in his body the way it accumulated in his boots, the specific wearing down of repeated use on hard surfaces, the evidence of a place he'd been that didn't come off in the wash.

He was losing weight faster than he was eating.

Sarah had noticed and had not said anything yet because she was waiting until she had enough evidence to say it right. She was building the case. She was always building the case.
