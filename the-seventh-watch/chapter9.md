# CHAPTER NINE

## THE BREAKING POINT

The well went out on a Tuesday in November.

It was the east pasture pump, the secondary one that fed the stock tank in the far field, not the main well, not the one the house ran on, but important enough that the cattle in the east pasture were going to need water by the afternoon if it didn't get fixed.

Grant was on the porch in the pre-loop state, the building awareness he'd learned to recognize over the previous loops, the specific pressure of the other world beginning to press through, when Sarah appeared in the back doorway and said, with the tone she used for ranch problems that needed addressing: "East pump's out."

He came off the porch without hesitation. That was the thing about being the man she could count on: when something needed doing, his body moved toward it before his mind finished the calculation. He didn't resent this. It was who he was. He'd built his life on it.

But he was beginning to understand that it wasn't the whole story.

They worked it together, the way they worked all physical problems on the property, she held the flashlight and handed tools and communicated what she saw from her angle, and he ran the diagnostics. His hands knew the pump system from six years of maintaining it and from the specific attention he'd given it when they'd bought the place, the deliberate learning-by-examining of a man who had decided to know his property rather than just own it.

He found the corroded contact in the pressure switch housing within twenty minutes. He had the replacement part, he kept the basic service components for both wells in the barn, organized in a labeled plastic bin on the second shelf, the specific preparation of someone who had decided that running out of parts was not a problem he was willing to have.

He made the repair. The pump came on. The water ran.

He stood up and looked at Sarah. She was looking at him with the assessment.

He said: "I think I'm about to go."

She said: "I know. I can see it."

He said: "Twenty minutes, maybe less."

She said: "Is there anything you need?"

He said: "No." He paused. He said: "Tell the boys. If I'm gone when they get home."

She said: "I always do."

He said: "I know." He looked at her. "Sarah."

She said: "Grant."

He said: "I'm going to come back from this last set different. I think this is the one where I finally understand it."

She said: "I've been waiting twelve years for you to understand it."

He said: "I know." He looked at the property, the barn, the pasture, the creek draw where the fence ran, the red gate at the end of the drive. "I love this place."

She said: "I know."

He said: "I love what we built here."

She said: "I know." Her voice had something in it. "I'll be here when you get back."

He went to the barn to put his tools away.

The white came while he was cleaning the mud off the pump wrench at the barn spigot, the water running over his hands, the particular cold of groundwater in November.

He didn't grab anything.

He just went.
