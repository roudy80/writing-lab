CHAPTER 1

Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc was ruining his first impression.

He knew it even as the words came out. He knew he should stop, that Captain Durand had not asked about the stables, that nobody cared. But the silence after the question had stretched one beat too long and he had filled it the only way he knew how.

"I grew up with horses, sir. My family has a vineyard, and we"

"That is not what I asked."

Heat crawled up the back of his neck. Captain Durand was watching him with the patience of a man deciding whether to bother being angry. His jacket hung open, his desk covered in stacked paper, and he held his quill like a tool he had used ten thousand times.

"No formal cavalry experience, sir. But I rode extensively at home, and I have studied"

"You have studied."

Charles heard what the captain left out of that sentence. He stopped talking.

The quill scratched. Durand signed something, slid it aside, and finally looked up with the expression of a man who had processed a hundred new lieutenants and expected this one to be no different.

"You are assigned to Major Beaumont's squadron. Mess at six bells. Formation at dawn. Report to Sergeant Mercier at the stables to draw your mount." He pushed a paper across the desk. "Quartermaster. Building adjacent to the stables."

"Thank you, sir." Charles took the paper.

He turned for the door. Captain Durand's voice stopped him.

"Leblanc."

"Sir?"

"This is a working garrison. We train cavalry officers here, not vineyard inspectors. Keep that in mind."

"Yes, sir."

Charles closed the door behind him, perhaps a degree more firmly than necessary.


He had been in the garrison for perhaps forty minutes.

The carriage had left him at the gate that afternoon with his trunk and a sentry's indifferent gesture toward the main building. Nine days of travel: post-chaises smelling of other men's sweat, roads that rattled his teeth, water from a canteen that had been sitting in afternoon heat. His uniform was as pressed as he could manage, which was not very. He was eighteen years old and had never been more than thirty kilometers from Bordeaux before last week.

The parade ground had helped, briefly. He had stood at the gate taking it in: forty men drilling in the middle distance, an NCO's commands carrying clearly across open ground, horses and riders moving with a precision that was not yet natural but was becoming so. Real training. Men learning movements that would keep them alive.

His father's parting words had been practical, as they always were. "Five years, Charles. Make Captain and come home." Not a wish. An instruction, with the weight of the family estate and whatever was left of the Leblanc name sitting behind it. Charles had nodded and climbed into the carriage and spent nine days rehearsing how he would present himself when he arrived.

The rehearsal had not included interrupting a senior officer to discuss stable management.


Elise would have laughed at him. His sister had warned him about exactly this, about talking to fill silence, about saying the wrong thing at the wrong moment. "You do that thing where you keep talking," she had told him the last evening before he left. "The horses do not care, Charles, but people do." She had been right. She usually was.

He found the adjutant on the way back: a sergeant graying at the temples, efficient movements, the look of a man who checked things twice. Orders processed, room number confirmed, Sergeant Mercier confirmed, formation at dawn confirmed. Charles left before he could say anything else unnecessary.


His trunk was still at the gate. He hauled it up three flights of creaking stairs to room twelve. The corridor smelled of woodsmoke and wool. The room was small: two narrow beds, a shared wardrobe, a table under the window. One side was occupied, a jacket on a peg, boots underneath, small effects on the shelf. His roommate was on patrol. Back tomorrow evening.

Through the window: the stable complex. Men still moving below in the last of the afternoon light.

He unpacked quickly. Spare uniform into the wardrobe. Books and writing materials onto his shelf. His father's pocket watch on the ledge where he would see it. At the bottom of the trunk, wrapped carefully, his dress uniform, hung and left alone. Elise's going-away gift was the portable writing desk; he set it on the table.

He should write. Let them know he had arrived safely.

He sat on the edge of the bed with the desk across his knees and looked at blank paper. What would he say? He closed the desk. Later, when there was something worth telling.


The stable complex was stone and timber, larger up close than from the window. Wide doors at both ends for air circulation. Inside: thirty stalls each side, most occupied, the dense familiar smell of hay and horses and worked leather. Charles felt his shoulders drop slightly as he walked through the doors.

The horses were good quality, lighter cavalry mounts, bays and chestnuts, well-kept. He could see it without looking closely.

A corporal directed him to the third stall on the right, where a sergeant was working through an inspection of a bay gelding. Methodical, precise: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, then hooves in the same order, then a hand along the spine. The sergeant moved with the economy of someone who did this without thinking.

Charles watched without announcing himself. He recognized the pattern.

The sergeant finished, patted the horse's neck, straightened.

"Sergeant Mercier?"

Mercier turned. One quick look: new uniform, eighteen years old, trying to seem older. The left hand, Charles noticed, was missing the pinky at the knuckle.

"You would be Leblanc."

"Yes, Sergeant. I was told to report here to draw my mount."

Mercier gestured down the row. "Adjutant assigned you the bay gelding. Fourth stall. Go have a look."

The horse watched Charles approach with calm, dark eyes. Bay, black points, fifteen hands or so, well-proportioned. Charles opened the stall and stepped in without rushing, let the horse take his scent, ran a hand down its neck before checking anything else. Good muscle tone. The horse shifted weight and he noted it: sound, no favoring.

He checked the way his father's stableman had taught him. Hands down each leg in sequence, feeling for heat or swelling. The legs were clean. Hooves: good shoes, properly fitted, no problems. Teeth put the horse at around six years. He ran a hand along the back, checking how a saddle would sit.

The horse turned and investigated his jacket with patient curiosity.

"You know horses, sir."

Sergeant Mercier was in the stall doorway, arms crossed. Not a question.

"Yes, Sergeant. Vineyard outside Bordeaux. Mostly working horses, fields, wagons." Charles gave the bay a last pat. "He is in excellent condition."

"They all are. A cavalryman is only as good as his mount."

Charles nodded. Then: "You check horses the old way, Sergeant. My father's stableman did exactly that sequence. He served in the cavalry in the nineties."

Something shifted in Mercier's expression.

"Did he. What unit?"

"I am not certain. He did not talk about it much."

Sergeant Mercier stepped into the stall and looked at the bay for a moment. "Old cavalry school. Too slow for Napoleon's army now, everything is faster. But thorough. Catches problems before they become injuries."

Charles took a breath. "I studied the 1796 Italian campaign. Rivoli, specifically. The accounts say cavalry covered seventy kilometers in two days over mountain roads. How did you keep the horses sound?"

The sergeant's eyebrows moved. He studied Charles for a moment, recalibrating.

"We walked them. Every meter we could afford, we walked. Saved the galloping for when it mattered. Fed the horses before we fed ourselves: horse breaks down, you are useless. Infantry can limp on bad feet. Cavalry cannot." A pause. "Remounts we requisitioned when we could. Took them when we had to. That is war."

"And reading about it is different from doing it."

"At least you have bothered to read. That is something." Sergeant Mercier straightened. "That horse is yours. Name is Tonnerre. Previous officer transferred to the dragoons last month. He is trained and steady. Whether he holds steady under fire you will not know until it happens. Treat him well and he will keep you alive."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier turned to leave, paused in the doorway.

"You know horses, Lieutenant. Tomorrow you will learn the rest."

His boots were quiet on the stable floor going out.

Charles stood with one hand on Tonnerre's neck for a moment. The horse went back to the hay net.

"Well," Charles said, quietly. "At least that went right."


The mess hall was noisy, officers from various companies eating dinner and talking over each other. Charles stood in the doorway long enough to feel conspicuous, then got his bowl and found a seat at the least crowded end of a table. Two lieutenants at the far end were discussing patrol routes and did not notice him.

The stew was decent. He ate and listened.

"Told him the requisition was approved three weeks ago."

"Rousseau tried to jump that fence, horse refused and he went right over its head."

"Stable duty for showing up drunk, and that is lenient."

This was military life. Not campaigns but the daily architecture of a garrison: paperwork, patrol routes, men jumping fences badly. He had known it would be like this. It was different to sit inside it.

A lieutenant at the next table stood and raised his tin cup. The room quieted.

"A cavalry officer is born to fight, ride fast, drink hard, and die young!"

"Die young!" came back across the room, every cup raised.

Charles raised his with the rest. They drank.

Die young. He had read that toast in books, heard it described. Said here by men who meant it the way you mean a thing you have decided not to think too hard about, it landed differently. Like the difference between reading about a river and standing in the current.

He returned his bowl and cup to the serving area and left without anyone noticing.


Room twelve was dark when he got back. He lit the lamp on the table and sat on the bed to pull off his boots. His father's watch on the shelf. The portable writing desk Elise had given him.

Through the window: the parade ground in full dark, lanterns at the gate, the stable lights still burning. He could hear the garrison settling, boots in the corridor below, distant voices, the watch calling the hour. He would learn those rhythms. In a week they would be automatic.

Five years. Make Captain and go home.

He pulled out the writing desk, set a sheet of paper on the table, and dipped a quill.

Dear Father and Elise,

I have arrived. The garrison is larger than I imagined. I report to my squadron tomorrow.

He stopped. Considered the rest of what he might say and did not write it. He sealed the short letter anyway: they would want to know he had arrived. He set it aside to post in the morning.

He put out the lamp and lay back on the bare mattress, still mostly dressed, looking at the ceiling in the dark.

Five years suddenly felt like a very long time.
