CHAPTER 1

The hired carriage lurched to a stop at the garrison gate, and Charles Leblanc felt his stomach lurch with it. Through the dusty window, he could see the guard post - a simple wooden structure with two sentries who barely glanced at the approaching vehicle. Beyond it, the garrison spread out across a broad expanse of trampled grass and worn stone.
This was it. This was real.
He’d been traveling for nine days, rattling across France in a succession of post-chaises that smelled of other people’s sweat and cheap tobacco. His uniform was rumpled despite his best efforts to keep it pressed between stops. His boots were dusty. He probably looked exactly like what he was: an eighteen-year-old from a Bordeaux vineyard who’d never been more than thirty kilometers from home before last week.
The driver opened the door. Charles climbed out, legs stiff from the long day’s ride, and stood blinking in the afternoon sun.
The parade ground stretched before him, a broad rectangle of grass worn bare in patches from constant use. Men were drilling there now - maybe forty of them, arranged in loose formations. Cavalry, he could tell from the way they moved, from the controlled chaos of it. They were practicing… something. Wheeling movements? He couldn’t quite tell from this distance. An NCO’s voice carried across the field, sharp commands Charles couldn’t make out over the general noise of the garrison.
They weren’t parade-ground perfect. This was training, real training, men learning or relearning movements that would keep them alive in combat. But there was a professionalism to it, a seriousness. These men knew what they were doing.
I’m going to do that, Charles thought. In a few days, maybe tomorrow, I’ll be out there with them.
His chest felt tight. Excitement or fear? Both, probably.
“Your trunk, monsieur?”
Charles turned. The driver was already unloading his belongings - one battered trunk that held everything he owned. His father’s parting words echoed in his head: “Five years, Charles. Make Captain and come home.”
“Yes, thank you.” Charles fumbled for coins, counting out the fare with fingers that felt clumsy. The driver took the money, touched his hat, and climbed back onto his perch without ceremony. The carriage pulled away, leaving Charles standing beside his trunk at the garrison gate with no clear idea of what to do next.
One of the sentries was watching him now. Charles straightened, tried to look like he belonged here.
“Second Lieutenant Leblanc,” he said. “Reporting for—”
“Adjutant’s inside, sir.” The sentry gestured vaguely toward the main building. “He’ll process your orders.”
“Right. Yes. Thank you.”
The sentry had already lost interest.
Charles looked at his trunk, then at the main building - a solid stone structure three stories high, windows reflecting the late afternoon sun. Other buildings clustered around it: long, low barracks, what looked like storehouses, and off to the left, a large stable complex that seemed to be the busiest part of the entire garrison. Men and horses moving in and out, the sounds of work carrying clearly across the parade ground.
He’d have to leave the trunk here for now. He couldn’t very well drag it with him to report.
The main building’s entrance was impressive - heavy wooden doors, worn stone steps. Charles climbed them, straightened his uniform jacket one more time, and stepped inside.
The interior was cooler, dim after the bright sunlight. A corridor stretched ahead of him, doors opening off to either side. Somewhere deeper in the building, he could hear voices, boots on wooden floors, the ordinary sounds of a military post at work.
An officer emerged from one of the side rooms - older, maybe thirty, wearing a lieutenant’s uniform that had seen better days. He had a sheaf of papers in one hand and the distracted look of someone dealing with too much administrative work.
“Excuse me,” Charles said. “I’m looking for the adjutant? I’m—”
“New posting?” The officer didn’t slow down. “Second door on the right. He’s expecting you.”
“Yes, thank you, I—”
But the officer was already gone, disappearing around a corner.
Charles found the second door on the right. It stood open, revealing a cramped office lined with wooden filing cabinets. A sergeant sat at a desk covered in paperwork, scratching away at a ledger with a quill that needed trimming.
Charles knocked on the doorframe.
The sergeant looked up. He was perhaps forty, graying at the temples, with the weathered face of a man who’d spent years outdoors before being assigned to administrative duty. His eyes swept over Charles in one quick, assessing glance - taking in the new uniform, the nervous posture, the dust from nine days of travel.
“Second Lieutenant Leblanc,” Charles said. “Reporting from—”
“Papers.”
Charles fumbled for the sealed orders he’d been carrying since leaving Paris. His hands felt clumsy as he extracted them from his jacket pocket. One of the seals had cracked during the journey, leaving a smudge of red wax on his fingers.
The sergeant broke the remaining seal without ceremony and scanned the contents. His face showed nothing - not approval, not disapproval, just the blank efficiency of a man who’d processed a hundred such orders before.
“You’re assigned to Captain Beaumont’s company,” the sergeant said. “Second squadron. Your quarters are in the junior officers’ wing, third floor, room twelve. Your roommates are on patrol, won’t be back until tomorrow evening.”
Charles nodded, trying to absorb all of this at once. Third floor. Room twelve. Captain Beaumont. Second squadron.
“You’ll need to draw your mount from the stables. Report there after you’ve settled in. The stable sergeant is Mercier - he’ll see you’re properly equipped.”
“Yes, thank you. Should I—”
“Morning formation is at six, on the parade ground. Don’t be late.” The sergeant was already turning back to his ledger. “Captain Durand will see you now to complete your processing. Down the corridor, last door on the left.”
“Captain Durand?”
“To process your orders,” the sergeant said without looking up. “Last door on the left.”
Charles stood there for a moment longer, waiting for… something. Further instruction? Welcome to the garrison? But the sergeant had returned to his work, quill scratching steadily across the page.
Right. Last door on the left.
Charles retreated into the corridor, turned left, and made his way deeper into the building. The sounds of the garrison felt muffled here, distant. His boots echoed on the wooden floor.
The last door on the left was closed. Charles knocked.
“Enter.”
The office beyond was larger than the adjutant’s, with a window overlooking the parade ground. A captain sat behind a desk - forty-something, broad-shouldered, with the look of a man who’d rather be anywhere but behind a desk. His uniform jacket hung open, his desk was covered with papers arranged in haphazard stacks, and he was frowning at something he’d just written as Charles entered.
“Second Lieutenant Leblanc, sir,” Charles said. “Reporting for—”
The captain held up one hand without looking at him.
Charles stopped mid-sentence and stood there, suddenly very aware of every small sound - the scratch of the captain’s quill, the distant voices from the parade ground, his own breathing.
The captain finished whatever he was writing, signed it with a flourish, and set it aside. Only then did he look up.
“Leblanc. From Bordeaux.”
“Yes, sir.”
“Cavalry experience?”
“I grew up with horses, sir. My family has a vineyard, and we—”
“That’s not what I asked.”
Charles felt heat creep up his neck. “No formal cavalry experience, sir. But I rode extensively at home, and I’ve studied the—”
“You’ve studied.” The captain’s tone made it clear what he thought of studying. “The stables here are magnificent, Lieutenant. The horses are in excellent condition, and the stable management is—”
“I didn’t mean to imply—” Charles started, then caught the look on the captain’s face.
The captain was staring at him. Not angry, exactly. Just… staring. Waiting.
Charles’s mind raced. What had he done wrong? He’d just been trying to—
Oh.
Report and shut up. That was the protocol. Report your presence, answer direct questions, don’t volunteer commentary about the garrison’s facilities like you’re inspecting the place.
“I apologize, sir,” Charles said.
The captain studied him for another long moment, then returned his attention to the papers on his desk. He shuffled through a stack, found what he was looking for, and made a notation.
“You’re assigned to Captain Beaumont’s company, second squadron. Your quarters are on the third floor - the adjutant gave you the room number?”
“Yes, sir. Room twelve.”
“Mess is at six bells. Formation at dawn. You’ll report to Sergeant Mercier at the stables to draw your mount - he’ll assess your riding and assign you appropriately.” The captain signed another paper, slid it across the desk. “Take this to the quartermaster for your kit issue. Building adjacent to the stables. Dismissed.”
Charles took the paper. “Thank you, sir.”
He turned toward the door, relieved to be leaving, but the captain’s voice stopped him.
“Leblanc.”
“Sir?”
“This is a working garrison. We train cavalry officers here, not vineyard inspectors. Keep that in mind.”
“Yes, sir.”
Charles left, closing the door behind him perhaps a bit more firmly than necessary.
The corridor felt longer on the way back. He passed the adjutant’s office - the sergeant was still scratching away at his ledger - and emerged into the main entrance hall.
Through the open doors, he could see the parade ground. The drilling had stopped. Men were leading horses back toward the stables, the day’s training complete. The sun was lower now, casting long shadows across the grass.
Vineyard inspector.
His face still burned.
He’d fumbled it. First conversation with a senior officer and he’d fumbled it like a green recruit. Tried to make conversation when he should have just reported and shut his mouth. The captain wasn’t even cavalry - probably infantry or artillery, stuck on administrative duty - and Charles had started babbling about stables like…
Like an idiot.
He took a breath, squared his shoulders. Fine. He’d learn. That’s what he was here for, wasn’t it? To learn how to be an officer.
His trunk was still sitting by the gate where the carriage had left it. Charles walked over, hefted it - not heavy, but awkward - and started looking for the junior officers’ quarters.
The third floor was reached by a narrow staircase that creaked under his boots. The corridor was dim, lit by windows at either end, and smelled faintly of woodsmoke and unwashed wool. Room twelve was halfway down, the door standing slightly ajar.
Charles pushed it open.
The room was small - perhaps three meters by four - with two narrow beds on opposite walls, a single shared wardrobe, and a small table beneath the window. One bed was clearly occupied: personal effects on the shelf above it, a jacket hanging from a peg, boots tucked underneath. The other bed was bare, waiting.
His bed, he supposed.
Charles set his trunk down and looked around. This would be home for… how long? Months? Years? Until he made Captain and could go back to Bordeaux with his head high and the family name restored.
Five years, his father had said. Maybe six.
Through the window, he could see the stable complex. Men were still moving around down there, tending to horses, putting away equipment. The busiest part of the garrison, even now as the day wound down.
He should go there next. Report to this Sergeant Mercier, draw his mount. But first…
Charles opened his trunk and began unpacking. His spare uniform went into the wardrobe - he’d have to share the space with his roommate, whoever that was. His personal effects - a few books, writing materials, his father’s old pocket watch - went onto the shelf above his bed. At the bottom of the trunk, carefully wrapped, was his dress uniform. He’d wear that for the first time at… when? Some formal occasion? He wasn’t sure.
He pulled out his writing desk - a small portable one, a gift from Élise before he’d left. Inside were paper, ink, quills, sealing wax. Everything he’d need to write home.
He should write. Let them know he’d arrived safely.
Charles sat on the edge of his bed, opened the writing desk across his knees, and stared at a blank sheet of paper.
Dear Father and Élise,
I have arrived safely at the garrison near Strasbourg. The journey was long but uneventful. The garrison is…
Was what? Impressive? Professional? He’d barely seen it. And what would he say about today? That he’d already embarrassed himself with a senior officer? That he didn’t know the proper way to report? That he’d fumbled the simplest interaction like a schoolboy?
Charles set down the quill.
He’d finish it later. After he had something worth reporting. After he’d drawn his mount, met his squadron, done something that didn’t make him look like a fool.
He closed the writing desk and stood, straightening his uniform. The stables. Sergeant Mercier. His assigned mount. That was next.
At least with horses, he knew what he was doing.
The stable complex was larger up close than it had seemed from a distance. The main building was stone and timber, solidly built, with a peaked roof and wide doors at either end to allow air circulation. Charles could hear horses inside - the stamp of hooves, the occasional whinny - and smell them too. That familiar scent of hay and horse and leather that reminded him of home.
The yard around the stables was packed earth, worn smooth by countless boots and hooves. Several soldiers were working there - some grooming horses, others cleaning tack, one repairing a saddle under the fading sunlight. They glanced at Charles as he approached but didn’t stop their work.
A corporal looked up from the bridle he was mending. “Help you, sir?”
“I’m looking for Sergeant Mercier. I was told to report here to draw my mount.”
“Inside, sir. Third stall on the right.”
Charles nodded his thanks and stepped through the open doors into the stable’s dim interior.
The building stretched back further than he’d expected - maybe thirty stalls on each side, most of them occupied. The horses were good quality. He could see that immediately. Not the heavy warhorses used by cuirassiers, but lighter cavalry mounts - built for speed and endurance rather than shock charges. Chasseurs’ horses. Most were bays or chestnuts, a few grays, all in good condition despite the work they must be doing daily.
He walked down the center aisle, boots quiet on the packed earth floor. Third stall on the right.
A man was there, examining a horse - running practiced hands down its forelegs, checking for heat or swelling. The horse stood patiently, clearly accustomed to the inspection.
The man was older - mid-forties, Charles guessed - with the weathered face and calloused hands of someone who’d spent decades working with horses. His uniform jacket was worn but clean, sergeant’s stripes visible on the sleeve. He moved with the efficient economy of long practice, checking the horse’s legs, then moving to examine its hooves with a hoof pick.
Charles watched him work. The sergeant had a specific pattern to his inspection - left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Then hooves in the same order. Then he ran his hand along the horse’s spine, checking for saddle pressure points. It was methodical, thorough, old-fashioned. The kind of inspection Charles’s father’s stableman used to do, before the war, before everything changed.
The sergeant finished his inspection and straightened, patting the horse’s neck.
“Sergeant Mercier?” Charles said.
The sergeant turned. His eyes swept over Charles in one quick glance - new uniform, nervous posture, eighteen years old and trying to look older. He’d seen a hundred young officers just like this.
“You’d be Leblanc.”
“Yes, Sergeant. I was told to report here to draw my mount.”
Mercier gestured to a stall further down the row. “Adjutant assigned you the bay gelding, fourth stall. Go have a look.”
Charles walked to the indicated stall. The horse inside was a bay - dark brown with black points - maybe fifteen hands high, well-proportioned. It watched him approach with calm, intelligent eyes.
Good confirmation, Charles thought automatically. Clean legs. Good chest depth. The horse shifted its weight, and Charles noted the movement - sound, no favoring. He opened the stall door and stepped inside.
The horse didn’t shy, just turned its head to investigate this newcomer.
“Easy,” Charles murmured. He ran his hand down the horse’s neck, feeling the muscle tone beneath the coat. Good condition. Well cared for.
He did what his father’s stableman had taught him years ago - checked the horse thoroughly. Hands down each leg, feeling for heat or swelling that might indicate old injuries. The legs were clean. He picked up each hoof in turn, checking for stones, examining the shoes, looking for any sign of thrush in the frogs. The shoes were in good condition, properly fitted. He checked the horse’s teeth - maybe six years old, good age for a cavalry mount - then ran his hands along the back, checking how the saddle would sit.
The horse stood patiently through all of this, occasionally turning to sniff at Charles’s jacket.
“You know horses, sir?”
Charles looked up. Mercier was standing in the stall doorway, arms crossed, watching.
“Yes, Sergeant. I grew up on a vineyard outside Bordeaux. Working horses for the fields and wagons.” Charles finished his inspection, patted the bay’s shoulder. “He’s in excellent condition.”
“They all are.” Mercier’s tone was matter-of-fact. “We work them hard but we don’t break them. A cavalryman’s only as good as his mount.”
Charles nodded, still running his hand along the horse’s neck. The bay leaned into the touch slightly - enjoying the attention or just a friendly temperament. Good either way.
He looked at Mercier more carefully now. Mid-forties. Twenty-plus years of service, probably. The way he’d inspected that other horse - that old-fashioned, methodical sequence…
“You check horses the old way, Sergeant,” Charles said. “My father’s stableman did it like that - served in the cavalry in the ’90s.”
Something shifted in Mercier’s expression. Not quite surprise, but… interest.
“Did he now.” Mercier stepped into the stall, examined Charles more carefully. “What unit?”
“I’m not sure. He didn’t talk about it much. But he always checked the horses exactly the way you just did - that specific pattern, foreleg to hindleg, then hooves, then spine.”
Mercier grunted. “Old cavalry school. They don’t teach it that way anymore - too slow for Napoleon’s army. Everything’s faster now.” He patted the bay’s shoulder. “But it’s thorough. Catches problems before they become injuries.”
Charles took a breath. This was either going to work or make him look like a fool. “Italy, Sergeant? I studied the ‘96 campaign. Rivoli, specifically. The accounts say cavalry covered seventy kilometers in two days over mountain roads.” He gestured to the bay. “How’d you manage that without breaking half your mounts?”
Mercier’s eyebrows rose slightly. He studied Charles for a long moment - reassessing.
“You’ve studied, at least,” he said finally. “More than most who show up here.” He stepped back, leaned against the stall door. “Rivoli, we walked them. Walked them whenever we could, saved the galloping for when it mattered. Fed them better than we fed ourselves - horse breaks down, you’re useless. Infantry can limp along on bad feet. Cavalry can’t.”
“And remounts?”
“Requisitioned when we could, stole when we had to.” Mercier’s tone was dry. “Not proud of it, but that’s war. You use what’s available.” He straightened. “Reading about it and doing it are different things, Lieutenant. But at least you’ve bothered to read. That’s something.”
It wasn’t warm. It wasn’t quite approval. But it was acknowledgment - more than Charles had gotten from anyone else since arriving.
“Thank you, Sergeant.”
Mercier nodded toward the bay. “That’s yours now. Name’s Tonnerre - Thunder. Previous owner was a lieutenant who transferred to dragoons last month. Horse is trained, responsive, won’t panic under fire - though you won’t know that until it happens. Treat him well and he’ll keep you alive.”
“I will.”
“Equipment’s in the tack room - I’ll have one of the corporals show you. Formation’s at dawn tomorrow on the parade ground. You’ll meet your squadron then. Captain Beaumont will assign you to a section.”
“Yes, Sergeant.”
Mercier turned to leave, then paused in the doorway. “Lieutenant. You know horses - that’s good. But cavalry’s not just about horses. Tomorrow you’ll learn the rest.”
“I understand, Sergeant.”
“We’ll see.”
Mercier left, his boots quiet on the stable floor.
Charles stood there in the stall for a moment, one hand still on Tonnerre’s neck. The horse turned to look at him with those calm, intelligent eyes.
“Well,” Charles said quietly. “At least one thing went right today.”
Tonnerre snorted softly, then returned to investigating the hay net in the corner.
The mess hall was on the ground floor of the main building - a long room with wooden tables arranged in rows and a serving area at one end where two cooks were ladling stew into tin bowls. The room was noisy, full of officers from various companies eating dinner and talking over each other.
Charles stood in the doorway, uncertain. Where should he sit? With the other lieutenants? But he didn’t know any of them. Would it be presumptuous to just… join a conversation?
Most of the tables were already occupied. At the far end, a group of captains sat together, talking in low voices. Closer, several lieutenants were laughing about something, one of them gesturing animatedly with his spoon. Another table held what looked like artillery officers - their uniforms slightly different, gold trim instead of silver.
No one was looking at Charles. No one had even noticed him arrive.
He got his dinner - the stew smelled better than it looked - and found a seat at the end of one of the less crowded tables. Two other lieutenants sat at the far end, deep in conversation about tomorrow’s patrol routes. Charles focused on his food and tried not to feel conspicuous.
The stew was decent - beef and vegetables, thick with barley. Better than some of the meals he’d had on the road. He ate slowly, listening to the conversations around him without quite joining them.
“—told him the requisition was approved weeks ago—”
“—should have seen Rousseau try to jump that fence, horse refused and he went right over its head—”
“—three days of stable duty for showing up drunk—”
Military life. This was military life - not the glory and campaigns he’d imagined, but the daily routine of garrison duty. Paperwork and drilling and enforcing regulations and occasionally jumping fences badly.
He’d known it would be like this, intellectually. But somehow, sitting here in this noisy mess hall eating mediocre stew while conversations flowed around him, it felt different from what he’d imagined during those long talks with his father about restoring the family name.
A lieutenant at the next table stood, raising his tin cup. The conversations died down.
“Gentlemen,” he said, his voice carrying across the room. “A toast.”
Everyone reached for their cups - water, wine, whatever they were drinking. Charles grabbed his own cup hastily, not wanting to be the only one still seated.
The lieutenant grinned. “A cavalry officer is born to fight, ride fast, drink hard, and die young!”
“Die young!” the room chorused, cups raised.
Charles joined in, his voice lost in the general noise. They drank, and the room erupted in laughter and conversation again.
Die young.
He’d read that toast in books about cavalry, heard stories about it from his father’s stableman. But hearing it here, said with that casual good humor by men who might actually face death in combat…
It made it real.
Charles drained his cup and stood. No one paid attention as he left the mess hall, returning his bowl and cup to the serving area on the way out.
The corridor outside was quieter, cooler. Through a window, he could see the parade ground in twilight, shadows stretching across the grass.
Tomorrow. Formation at dawn. Meet his squadron. Start learning how to actually be a cavalry officer instead of a vineyard owner’s son who happened to know horses.
He climbed the stairs to the third floor, made his way down the corridor to room twelve.
His uniform hung on its peg - the jacket freshly brushed, the facings bright, the lieutenant’s epaulettes catching the last light from the window. It looked… official. Real. Like something someone important would wear.
Tomorrow he would earn it.
Charles collapsed onto his bed without undressing, still wearing his boots. He was too tired to care. Nine days of travel, a day of fumbling and small victories and overwhelming newness, and tomorrow it would all begin again.
Through the window, he could hear the sounds of the garrison settling for the night - boots in the corridor, distant voices, someone singing off-key in one of the barracks. The watch called the hour. Eight bells.
Charles closed his eyes.
Tomorrow he would drill with his squadron. Tomorrow he would prove himself. Tomorrow everything would begin.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
