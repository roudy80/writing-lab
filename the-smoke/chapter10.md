# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 10

The battle of Eylau began in snow and ended in snow and in between was something Charles could not find adequate categories for, even afterward, even with the journal open and the pen in his hand.

He had been at the Tagliamento. He had been at the bridge at Günzburg. He had ridden through the gap at the Austrian second line with his saber out and his section behind him and come through it intact, and he had thought, afterward, that he understood what large-scale combat was.

He had not understood.

Eylau was the eighth of February, 1807, a Thursday, which seemed an irrelevant detail and was somehow the one he kept returning to. The army had been maneuvering for a week in temperatures that dropped at night to the point where sentries had to be relieved every hour to prevent frostbite. The Russian army was at Eylau. The French army went to meet it. The mathematics were approximately equal, which was not a situation Napoleon preferred and which produced, in Charles's experience, the worst kind of battle: the kind where the outcome required everyone to pay the full price.

The night before, Major Beaumont assembled the officers in a barn that was warmer than the open ground by perhaps four degrees.

"Tomorrow will be difficult," Beaumont said. He said it the way he said most things: as a fact, without apology. "The ground is frozen, which means the artillery will be effective in ways it is not in softer conditions. The Russian line is well-positioned and they are not short of guns. Our job is to be available where we are needed and to respond quickly when the order comes." He looked at each officer in turn. "Keep your men tight. Do not disperse for any reason until you hear from me. Questions?"

There were none.

"Get some sleep," Beaumont said. "Both of you."

Charles went back to his section's position and sat against a wagon wheel with his coat pulled tight and looked at the sky, which was clear and very cold and full of stars. He could see his breath. He could hear, from somewhere north of them, the sound of the Russian army settling in for the night, which sounded remarkably similar to the sound of the French army doing the same.

He thought about writing a letter. He thought about Tonnerre's shoe, which Mercier had checked twice that evening. He thought about the paper in his boot, Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting.

He thought about Margot Vieillard saying *decide it, don't let it be decided for you*, three weeks ago in Lowicz, and about Beaumont's response to his letter, which had been a single line: *noted. Recommendation forwarded.*

He did not think about the morning.

At some point the cold woke him and he realized he had been asleep, which had not been his intention but was probably his body's correct decision. He found Mercier awake at the fire, which had been reduced to coals.

"Go back to sleep," Mercier said.

"I was asleep."

"Then go back."

He did not go back. He sat with Mercier at the coals until first light, neither of them speaking. This was not uncomfortable. It was the particular silence of two people who have been in enough bad situations together to not need to fill the space before a new one.

* * *

The battle started with artillery.

It always started with artillery. But Eylau's artillery was a different order of magnitude than anything Charles had been near, and the opening exchanges, both sides simultaneously, produced a sound he felt in his sternum and could not locate directionally. It came from everywhere. The ground carried it. The air carried it. Tonnerre stood with his ears flat and his muscles rigid under Charles's hand, and Charles kept his voice low and his hand steady and thought about nothing except the horse.

The French infantry went forward in the snow at half past seven.

Charles watched it from his position on the left flank, which gave him a sight line across the main engagement without being in it. The infantry moved in columns through snow that was ankle-deep in the good places and knee-deep in the drifted sections, and the Russian guns opened at four hundred meters and the columns kept moving, and he could see the gaps appearing in the French lines but could not, at this distance, see the individual men who had been in those gaps a moment before.

He counted his section. He counted again.

At eight-thirty the French left flank collapsed.

He heard it before he saw it: the change in the musketry, a shift in direction and density, and then through the snow and the smoke he could see the French infantry pulling back, not retreating in order but coming apart, the shape of the formation dissolving into individual figures moving in all directions that were not toward the Russian line.

A staff officer arrived at a hard gallop. "Major Beaumont. Murat's order. All available cavalry to the center. Now."

Charles looked at Beaumont.

"You heard it," Beaumont said. "Forward."

* * *

The charge at Eylau was not Charles's charge. It was Murat's charge, eighty squadrons, ten thousand horsemen riding into the center of the Russian line to buy time for the French infantry to reform. Charles was one section in the left flank of a mass of cavalry that was itself a geological event, the ground shaking with the weight of it, the sound of hooves on frozen earth a continuous percussion that you felt in your teeth.

He kept the section together by sound and presence rather than sight. The snow reduced visibility to perhaps thirty meters. He could see Rousseau on his left and Mercier on his right and beyond them shapes that were his men or were other cavalry he could not distinguish. He kept Tonnerre at the pace the formation required and kept his voice in the register that his section knew.

The Russian line was somewhere ahead. The snow told him nothing. Then the musketry came and the snow told him everything.

He did not remember the next minutes clearly. This had happened before, at the Tagliamento gap, the way combat at close quarters stripped itself of sequence and left only discrete images: Tonnerre jumping something that was a man or a piece of equipment, the face of a Russian soldier in gray coat with an expression of absolute focus that was exactly the same as Charles's own expression, the sound of a saber hitting something, his hand still connected to it, pulling back. Rousseau beside him, then not beside him, then beside him again. Mercier somewhere to the right, his voice carrying the tone that meant he was directing men and did not need assistance.

Then the charge was through the line and the formation was re-forming on the far side and Charles was counting and the count came up short.

He counted twice. He counted three times.

He was short two men. Then a figure emerged from the snow from the direction of the Russian line, one of the Girard cousins, walking, leading his horse, the horse limping. One. Still short one.

"Ferrand," Mercier said, beside him. Not a question.

"Yes." He could see Mercier's face clearly enough to know that Mercier had already counted and had arrived at the same number.

They rejoined the mass of cavalry pulling back and reforming for a second pass. Charles looked for Ferrand in the shapes moving through the snow and could not find him. He looked at where the Russian line had been, now reforming itself, the gaps the cavalry had punched through it already closing. He looked at the snow between here and there.

Ferrand was twenty-two. He was from Auxerre. He had a habit of checking his carbine three times before mounting up, which Charles had noted on the first day in Munich as either diligence or anxiety and had eventually concluded was both.

He was somewhere in the snow between the lines and Charles could not go back for him.

The order came to reform for a second pass. Charles put the section in order and moved.

* * *

The battle lasted until dark.

The casualty count, when it came, was twelve hundred French dead at Eylau, closer to three thousand counting wounded. The Russians had lost more. Nobody in the French army seemed to find this ratio particularly satisfying. The army held the field, which was what winning meant, and the Russians withdrew, which was what losing meant, and in the morning the field at Eylau was a landscape that Charles could not look at steadily.

He wrote in the journal that night. Not the lists. He started the list in the back and then could not continue. He closed the journal, sat with it in his lap, opened it again.

He wrote Ferrand's name. *Joffre, Ferrand. Cavalry. February 1807.*

He looked at the name for a while.

He had lost men before. Moreau at the bridge, which had been his mistake and had stayed with him. The men at the Tagliamento who were not in his section but whose faces he had seen. He had lost men in ways that felt connected to decisions, the wrong choice at the wrong moment, the gap between what he had prepared for and what the ground presented.

Ferrand had died because a mass of cavalry had ridden through a Russian line at Eylau on a Thursday in February and Ferrand had been in the wrong place in it. Charles could not identify the decision that would have changed the outcome. He had kept the section together, he had maintained formation, he had done what the situation required of the person who understood it clearly.

Ferrand was twenty-two and from Auxerre and checked his carbine three times before mounting up.

Major Beaumont found him at the horse line. He did not say anything for a moment. He stood beside Charles and looked at the horses.

"Ferrand," Beaumont said.

"Yes, sir."

"I know." A pause. "The remaining Girard cousin."

"His horse will recover. He is unhurt."

"Good." Beaumont was quiet. Then: "You held the section together in the second pass. Mercier told me. The visibility was down to nothing and you held them."

Charles said nothing.

"I am not saying it to comfort you," Beaumont said. "I am saying it so you know it was noted." He turned slightly toward Charles. "The casualty is not a consequence of your decisions today."

"I know that, sir."

"Knowing it and feeling it are different processes."

"Yes."

Beaumont looked at the horses for another moment. "The list in the back of your journal," he said.

Charles looked at him. He had not told Beaumont about the list.

"Mercier mentioned it," Beaumont said, without apology. "He said you had kept it since Günzburg." A pause. "Some officers do not write anything down about the men. Some write official records only. Some keep their own counts." He looked at the dark. "I keep mine in my head. I have kept them in my head for twelve years. I do not know if any method is better than another. I know that the counting is the right thing to do."

He walked back toward headquarters.

Charles stayed with Tonnerre until the horse had settled, then went back to his bedroll and lay down. He did not put his hand on his boot. He lay on his back and looked at the ceiling of the tent and thought about Ferrand checking his carbine three times, which was a thing he would not do again.

He thought about the five-year plan.

He thought about it in the specific, mathematical way he had always thought about it: October 1805 to October 1810, five years, make Captain in the spring, go home in the fall of 1810 with the commission completed and the debt to his father honored. The numbers were unchanged. The plan was unchanged. He had said yes to the promotion and it had been noted and it would proceed and in the fall of 1810 he would go home.

He thought: and after Eylau. And after Ferrand. And after Philippe.

The plan would not change because of those things. He was not going to let it change because of those things. He was going to complete the plan and go home and build something on the Bordeaux estate that was not about destroying things, which was the thing he had been good at for two years and four months now and would be good at for seventeen more months and then he was going to stop.

That was the plan.

He said it to himself several times, which was a thing he had noticed he needed to do more often now than he had at the beginning. At the beginning the plan had been a fact. Now it was a decision he remade each time, which was a different thing. Not worse, he told himself. Different. A decision you kept making was a decision you kept meaning.

He closed his eyes.

Somewhere outside, the other Girard cousin, the one who had come back, was talking to someone. Charles could hear the voice without the words: low, steady, the register of someone telling a story that needed to be told out loud.

He did not go to sleep for a long time. When he did, it was without meaning to, the same way he had fallen asleep before the battle, the body deciding what the mind could not.

In the morning he woke up and the plan was still the plan and Ferrand was still dead, and both of those things were true simultaneously, and he put on his boots and went to check the horses, because the horses needed checking and it was February and the spring was coming whether anyone was ready for it or not.
