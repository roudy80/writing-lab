# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 10

The orders arrived on a Tuesday, which Charles had always found to be an unremarkable day for unremarkable things, and a Tuesday seemed therefore approximately correct for being told that his unit was to delay the enemy advance long enough for the infantry to withdraw through a gap that should not have existed and would not have existed except that someone at Corps level had miscalculated the speed of the French retreat and subsequently the speed of the Allied pursuit.

He read them twice in the adjutant's tent, standing, because there was no chair.

The terrain sketch attached showed a valley approach with limited cover on the left flank, a ridgeline on the right that looked workable until you noticed the elevation marks, and a main avenue of approach across open ground that would be useful for moving cavalry at speed and also, he noted, for the enemy to observe cavalry moving. The enemy force was estimated at three times his strength. The estimate had a small notation at the bottom: subject to confirmation. This notation appeared on most estimates of enemy strength, in his experience, specifically in situations where confirmation was not possible and the estimate was likely wrong in an unfavorable direction.

He folded the orders and put them in his coat pocket and stood for a moment looking at the tent wall.

He found Major Beaumont at headquarters, standing with his hands behind his back, looking at the same terrain sketch pinned to a board. He had been promoted twice since Italy and commanded a brigade now, which meant he spent most of his time at maps and boards rather than at the front, and his hands had gone soft in a way that cavalry hands did not usually go soft. He still moved carefully. He still drank alone.

"Sir," Charles said.

Beaumont turned. He had the look he got when he had been thinking through a problem and had arrived at an answer he did not especially like. It was a specific look, different from his usual settled expression. Charles had learned to recognize it across five years.

"The delaying action," Charles said. "I would like to discuss the approach."

"Yes," Beaumont said. "Go ahead."

"The left flank has no cover past the first tree line. They will see us coming across six hundred meters of open ground. The ridgeline on the right is workable for light screening but not for a sustained action. If we are to hold them for two hours, which is what the infantry requires, we will need to use the tree line as the primary position and accept the exposed approach."

Beaumont nodded slowly.

"The odds. Three to one, possibly higher."

"Yes."

"Sir, this is." He stopped. He had been going to say: this is not a reasonable operation. But that was not exactly what he wanted to say, and it was not exactly accurate. He stood with the unfinished sentence.

Beaumont looked at him, waiting, not unkindly.

"I know what it is," Beaumont said. "But it needs doing."

Charles looked at the sketch. Six hundred meters of open ground. Three to one odds. Two hours required.

"If it needs doing," he said, "I want to select the approach timing. First light, not dawn. The light will be in their eyes on the eastern approach for the first fifteen minutes."

"Agreed," Beaumont said.

"And I want authorization to withdraw to secondary position at the one-hour mark if the tree line becomes untenable."

Beaumont considered this. He had the manner of a man who was accustomed to giving authorizations that were not complete authorizations because complete authorizations were not always available to give.

"You will use your judgment," he said.

This was not authorization. It was also not denial. In Charles's experience, in operations of this particular kind, it was the best available answer.

"Understood, sir," he said.

He went to find his men.

* * *

He told them that evening, sitting under the tree line they would be defending in fourteen hours, the light going gray and the horses tethered in the draw behind them where the ground was softer. He did not use the phrase delaying action, which had a technical meaning most of them understood in the abstract, or the phrase acceptable losses, which had a technical meaning none of them found acceptable. He said: we are covering the withdrawal. The infantry needs two hours. We give them two hours and we come home.

He went through the specific plan: approach timing, tree line positions, withdrawal conditions. He answered questions where he could answer them. He said he did not know where he could not.

Sergeant Mercier, sitting to his left with his three-fingered left hand resting on his knee, said nothing through all of this. This was Mercier saying something.

Rousseau was watching Charles's face with the specific attention of a young man taking a reading on how worried to be. He was twenty now and had grown into his shoulders over the past year, had been given a promotion to Corporal in the summer and taken it with the same practical acceptance he brought to most things. He had the Marseille accent still, sharp at the edges of words, and it had not softened at all in two years of Peninsular War, which Charles had come to find reliable in some way he could not have precisely explained.

"Sir," Rousseau said.

"Corporal."

"The six hundred meters."

"I know," Charles said. "We move fast and we use whatever smoke there is. They will not have clean targets across most of it."

Rousseau accepted this with a small nod that was not quite satisfied but was the nod of a man filing something under: the officer has considered this and I do not have better information, which was different from being reassured but was close enough to function.

Private Pellerin was sharpening a knife with the automatic motions of a man who was not thinking about the knife. He was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been in the unit since the Pyrenees crossing and had a quality Charles valued in infantry that transferred well to cavalry: he did the unglamorous work without complaint. Archambault was sitting with his back against a tree, eyes closed, possibly asleep; he had the ability to sleep in almost any conditions, which was either a gift or a sign of something Charles did not want to examine. Cassouin had a small book and was writing in it with his forearm blocking the page, the way men wrote when they were writing something personal rather than something official.

Charles looked at the tree line in the failing light. Good cover once they reached it. Good lanes. Defensible for two hours if managed correctly.

He checked his boot alone later, when the men were settling. The paper was there, Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting. He had replaced it in Salamanca when the last one wore through at the fold. He thought, briefly, of Margot. He was not sure why she intruded at this particular moment. He had thought about Margot in various contexts over the past year, since the convoy escort, but not usually before operations. He put it away. There was nothing useful in it tonight.

He slept four hours and woke before the call.

* * *

The approach was bad in the way he had known it would be bad, which did not make it better.

Six hundred meters is a measurable distance. He had walked it the morning before. At a walk it took seven minutes. At a trot it took three. At a canter across ground that had taken rain the previous night and was expressing an opinion about bearing cavalry weight, it took four, which was one more minute than he had planned.

He talked to Tonnerre through it. The bay gelding moved with the careful attention of a horse navigating uncertain footing, adjusting his stride in the way he did when the ground said one thing and the pace said another. Charles kept the voice low and steady and the hand on the neck when he could reach it without losing his line. The familiar one-two pat. Tonnerre knew this pat. It meant: I know, keep going.

Enemy fire opened at approximately two hundred meters, which was thirty meters earlier than his pessimistic estimate. He heard the first volley, heard the subsequent sounds, and shouted the order to scatter into pre-arranged lines without breaking pace, because breaking pace in the open was the wrong decision and he had decided this before the morning started. The smoke from the first exchange was already drifting and he navigated by the dark shapes of the tree line, which he could see, and by the sound of fire, which told him where not to aim his horse.

He reached the tree line.

He turned and counted while the unit established positions, which is to say he counted while they moved and was counting before he had made a conscious decision to count. All present except two who were still crossing the open ground, both moving, both upright. Both reached the tree line in the next ninety seconds.

He gave the order to establish positions and the work of defending began.

The two hours proceeded as extended tactical actions proceed: badly in detail, in aggregate well enough. In detail there were moments of chaos that contained no good option, moments where his orders had consequences he had not predicted and had to correct for, moments where he could see that the situation had moved past the plan and he was operating on instinct formed from five years of similar situations rather than from the plan. In aggregate, the tree line held. He kept adjusting positions as the enemy probed for weaknesses, pulled one section left and one forward, used the ridgeline for observation when the left flank drew pressure, kept Rousseau's section in reserve and used it twice to plug gaps that would have cost him the line if they had opened fully.

At the one-hour mark he assessed the tree line. The enemy had identified their main firing positions and was applying concentrated pressure on the right. The right would fail within twenty minutes.

He gave the withdrawal order.

The secondary position was three hundred meters back and slightly higher, which gave them better observation and worse cover. The withdrawal was executed under fire. He moved the sections in sequence, left first, center covering, right last, and he was the last man off the tree line position because that was how withdrawals worked and he had never found a satisfactory argument against it.

At the secondary position he held for forty more minutes. When the signal came that the infantry was clear, he pulled the unit back through the valley in good order, with Tonnerre's ears forward and attentive, reading the sounds that Charles was also reading, and the guns behind them becoming quieter with each kilometer.

At the rally point he counted.

Pellerin was not present.

He counted again. Archambault was not present.

Cassouin was being held upright on his horse by two of his men, one on either side, his arm against his chest and his face the specific color of someone whose body was occupied with a serious problem. Charles rode to him. Cassouin looked at him with eyes that were present and then were less present and then he died, there on the horse, with the small book in his coat pocket that he had been writing in the evening before with his arm blocking the page.

Three.

Charles sat with the number three in the way he had learned to sit with numbers of this kind, which was to say: he did not look away from it, he did not assign it meaning beyond what it was, he let it be the number it was, because the alternative was to not feel it or to feel it in a way that was not functional, and he needed to be functional.

He wrote the letters that night by lamp. He had a form now. He knew what the form was for. Three letters: Pellerin's family in Lyon, Archambault's mother in Metz, Cassouin's young wife in Dijon, because someone at headquarters had mentioned Cassouin was newly married and Charles had stored this fact in the way he stored all facts about his men, precisely and with attention to what it meant.

He opened his field journal when the letters were done. He turned to the list in the back. He added three names.

The list was two full pages now, beginning to press against the margins in places where he had run out of column space and written smaller. He closed the journal and put it in his coat and went outside and stood in the November air until he was cold enough to sleep.

* * *

He did not write the discharge request he had been composing in his head.

He had been composing it, in a general way, for several months. Not in written form. In the form of sentences he tried in his head at odd moments: while inspecting horses, while reviewing manifests, while waiting in the headquarters ante-room for meetings that ran long. When I have made Captain. When Lieutenant Martin has enough seasoning. When this campaign reaches a stable point.

After Pellerin and Archambault and Cassouin he found that the sentences did not come. Not because he had made a decision against them. More as though the mechanism that produced the sentences had stopped engaging. He could still see the arguments in favor of the discharge request. They were still technically valid. He no longer found them persuasive.

The men trusted him. He had not wasted their lives before. He had not taken objectives that were not worth the cost. He had not exposed them to risks he had not calculated and accepted on their behalf. They trusted him because he had been trustworthy, and that trust was weight, and he was not certain what happened to the weight if he set it down.

Lieutenant Martin was capable. He would do good work in time. But Martin had not yet seen an action of this size. There was a period of learning that preceded competence, and that period had costs, and the costs were specific men's lives, and Charles knew which men they were in the way he knew all his men, by name and by how they moved and by what they were afraid of.

He was not the last good officer in Spain. He harbored no illusions about being irreplaceable in any broad sense. But he was specific. These men, this unit. If he walked away from that, men died who might not have died.

This is the logic, he thought, standing at the horse line in the dark. This is exactly the logic of the trap. He could see it clearly and it still governed him, because seeing a trap and stepping out of it are not the same operation.

Mercier was already at the horses. He had a lamp and was working through the inspection sequence on Tonnerre, who stood with the patient expression of a horse who has been inspected ten thousand times and has ceased to find it remarkable. Tonnerre's foreleg had healed fully two years ago. There was nothing in the inspection sequence that required doing on a horse in his current condition. Mercier worked through it anyway: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. His three-fingered hand found each position with the economy of motion that came from doing a thing correctly for twenty-five years.

He finished. Straightened. Gave Tonnerre a brief pat and said, to the horse: "All right."

He picked up the lamp and moved to the next horse.

Charles stood with Tonnerre and listened to Mercier work his way down the line. The inspection sequence in the dark, in the November cold, on horses that did not need it. He did not say anything. Mercier did not say anything. The conversation was complete.

* * *

He was in a garrison city in the spring of 1812, on seven days' leave that the army had granted with the specific reluctance armies display toward all leave but especially toward leave taken by officers whom the army felt were better employed elsewhere. He had not been in a city with a functioning market district in eight months. He had forgotten, specifically, the smell of a morning market: coffee and bread and something sweet from a stall two rows over and the general density of people engaged in ordinary commerce.

He was walking through the market district on the second morning, not looking for anything in particular, when a cart went over a cobblestone badly and the wheel struck a stone post with the sharp report of something going wrong quickly at close range.

He was behind a building stanchion before he had decided to move. He found himself with his back to the wall and his hand on his sword hilt and the cart sitting ten meters away with its wheel at an angle that was not going to correct itself and the driver swearing in Castilian at the horse, which was not paying attention to him.

He straightened. He moved his hand from the sword hilt. He stepped away from the stanchion.

She was watching him from across the market stall. She had a leather satchel and the expression of someone updating a picture they had been carrying of another person and finding the update significant.

"Leblanc," she said.

"Vieillard," he said.

She navigated around a table of goods and a man with a basket and came toward him. She was going to embrace him. He could see her intending it, the slight shift in posture that preceded it. He did not flinch, exactly. He became briefly incapable of receiving an embrace from another person, which produced the same result.

She stopped. She looked at his face.

"What happened?" she said.

He tried to explain the charge. He produced the technically accurate account: the orders, the terrain, the odds, the hours required, the number of men lost. He could hear himself giving this account and could tell that it was doing none of the work he needed it to do. The military words, the report words, the words that described the outline of something without its interior.

She listened without interrupting.

"Were they good men?" she said, when he had finished the outline.

"Yes," he said.

She nodded once. She did not say she was sorry, which he appreciated, because he had heard the phrase enough times that it had become a sound rather than a meaning.

They spent the morning at a warehouse she was using for transit storage, which had a desk in the corner behind towers of crates and a small coal brazier that kept the temperature approximately survivable. She had manifests to review and he sat on a crate and watched her work. She had a way of tracing columns with one finger while she added figures that was completely automatic, her attention apparently elsewhere while the addition happened underneath it.

The warehouse smelled of paper and coal smoke and, faintly, whatever had been stored in the crates most recently. Spices, he thought. Something warm and dry and foreign.

"You are staring," she said, not looking up.

"I am watching you work."

"These are the same thing." She made a note in the margin. "Tell me about the Aragon region. You were there in 1810."

He said he had been. He did not say in what capacity.

She closed the ledger on her finger to hold the place. "My father spent five years building trade relationships in that region. Twelve merchants across four towns. A route that functioned as well as anything in the interior. Trust takes time to build and it builds through consistency. You show up when you say you will. You pay the agreed price. You do not cheat on weights."

She said this with the matter-of-fact tone she used for logistics.

"What happened to the network?" he asked.

"The war happened to it." She looked at him. "Two campaigns through the region. Supply requisitions. A town burned, not by your army specifically, but burned. The merchants lost stock, lost routes, lost confidence that anything they built would survive the next season. Five years in two years."

He was quiet.

"Was it worth it?" she asked. "What you achieved there."

He had the military answer ready: the campaigns had accomplished their tactical objectives, supply lines secured, enemy resources disrupted. He knew the answer. He looked at her face and did not give it.

"I don't know," he said.

She looked at him as though determining whether this was the honest answer or the careful answer. It was both.

"I am rebuilding it," she said. "The Aragon network. Eighteen months, possibly two years. Starting over with whoever is still operating, building back the relationships one at a time." She opened a different ledger, this one organized with tabs she had cut from heavier paper. "The Portuguese route is partly open now. A contact in Lisbon, through my father's Frankfurt connections. If we establish a coastal leg that does not depend on the Spanish interior, the network survives the next disruption."

She was showing him her work. He understood this was what she was doing.

"The Lisbon contact," he said. "How reliable?"

"Unknown yet. That is what the next six months determine." She ran her finger down a column. "Trade relationships are tested by adversity, not by ease. We will see."

He looked at the ledger over her shoulder. The columns were dense with names, dates, figures, marginal notes written in at different angles over time, building on each other in layers. It was a picture of something being built carefully by someone who was thinking about the structure as she built it.

"You think in networks," he said.

"Everyone does. Some people are aware of it." She closed the ledger and turned on the chair to face him. "You think in terrain. Approach routes, defensive positions, cover. Threats to be neutralized. It is the same skill."

He looked at his hands, the calluses from reins and sword. He thought about the Aragon requisitions, the quantities he had recorded in his own ledgers. Grain. Oil. Draft animals. Numbers in a column. He thought about her columns: names, dates, relationships, the trust built through consistency.

"Same skill," he said. "Different purposes."

"Yes," she said. She held his gaze long enough that it became a conversation without words, the kind he had not had with another person in a very long time.

He wanted to tell her that he had thought about her before the charge. He had no reasonable explanation for why he had thought of her before a charge rather than after, which was the ordinary time for thinking about people one cared about. He did not tell her this. It seemed like something that required a better explanation than he currently had.

She stayed in the city for three more days. He had five days remaining. In the evenings they walked the market district when her work was done, which was never before the light failed but usually before full dark. She knew which merchants were trustworthy by indicators he would not have noticed: how they kept their weights, how they spoke about absent competitors, whether they knew their own inventory by count or by approximate. He told her which roads south were reliable and which had been compromised in last winter's operations, and she made mental notes in the way she made all her notes, which was to say she filed them immediately and would produce them accurately six months later without consulting anything.

On the third evening she said: "You are not going home."

It was not a question.

"Not yet," he said.

She looked out at the street. A lamp-lighter was working his way down the block, the specific orange of the new flame appearing and steadying and moving to the next post.

"I thought not," she said.

There was no accusation in it. Something else, something that resembled recognition, or the acceptance of a fact that had been known for some time.

He looked at her profile in the new lamplight. He understood that he loved her in the specific way one loves someone whose mind one respects and whose presence makes one's own thinking clearer, and that this had been true for some time, and that he had no vocabulary for it that did not sound either insufficient or overstated. He was twenty-five and had been at war since he was eighteen and was not sure what else love was supposed to be, which was its own problem.

He did not say any of this. She did not say anything equivalent. They walked back to the inn in the ordinary way, and in the morning she left for the next stop on her route, and he stood in the street watching the cart until it turned the corner.

He wrote her a letter from the road that was three paragraphs about weather and road conditions and one sentence at the end: I found myself thinking more clearly in your company than I have in some time. He sent it before he could decide whether this was too much or not enough.

Her letter back arrived three weeks later: The feeling is mutual. The Lisbon route is progressing. I will write when I have news of the crossing near Badajoz, which my contact says has silted but may be workable with lighter loads.

He read this four times. He found it completely satisfying, which was either evidence that he understood her or evidence that he was not well. He folded it into his field jacket pocket, next to his notebook, and went to find Rousseau.
