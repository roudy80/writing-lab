# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 11

The promotion orders arrived on the fourteenth of March, 1807, by courier from corps headquarters, in a sealed envelope that Sergeant Mercier brought to Charles without comment and stood beside while Charles read it.

*By order of the Emperor, Lieutenant Charles Leblanc is hereby promoted to the rank of Captain, effective the first of March, year of our Lord eighteen hundred and seven...*

He read it to the end. He folded it and set it on the table.

"Well," Mercier said.

"Yes."

Mercier picked up the envelope, looked at the outside of it, set it back down. A rare gesture from him: the thing he did when he was finding words. "Your father's instruction."

"Five years. Make Captain and go home." Charles looked at the orders. "I am two and a half years in."

"Yes."

"The plan says Captain and then home. It does not say Captain by a specific date."

"No," Mercier said. "It does not."

They were quiet for a moment.

"I need to reorganize the company," Charles said. "The restructure means I have additional men coming from Fontaine's old section. I will need a third sergeant."

"Rousseau," Mercier said, without hesitation.

"Yes. I was going to ask your opinion."

"That is my opinion." Mercier picked up his hat. "Congratulations, Captain."

He went out.

Charles sat with the orders for a while. Captain. The thing he had been working toward since Munich, since the garrison, since his father had said five years and Charles had said yes. The thing Beaumont had recommended in January and the army had confirmed in March. The next step in the sequence.

He thought about his father reading the letter that was already on its way. He thought about Elise, who would read it too and would understand things his father would not, the same way she always understood things, at a distance and exactly correctly.

He thought about the second half of the instruction. *Make Captain and come home.*

He had made Captain.

He put the orders in his coat pocket and went to find Rousseau.

* * *

The spring campaign began in earnest in May, the army moving northeast through country that had been fought over twice in the past year and was beginning to show it. The villages were quieter than they should have been. The farms were worked by old men and children. The roads had the particular quality of roads that had carried too much weight: rutted and patched and rutted again, the patches no longer distinguishing themselves from the damage around them.

Charles's company moved with the corps, screening ahead and on the left flank, the work that cavalry did when it was not fighting: watching and reporting and staying between the army and whatever was coming from the other direction. The Russians had withdrawn after Eylau but had not dispersed. They were somewhere northeast, reforming, and the question the spring campaign was designed to answer was whether they were going to fight again or negotiate.

He had sixty-three men now, organized into three sections under Mercier, Bonnard, and Rousseau. He rode the length of the company twice daily, inspecting, adjusting, noting what needed attention. The additional men from Fontaine's section had taken four weeks to integrate cleanly and were now part of the working unit without the seams showing. This was faster than he had expected and was a product of Bonnard's work more than his own.

He told Bonnard this directly, which was the kind of thing Philippe would have done and Charles was still learning to do.

Bonnard looked at the ground when he heard it, which was the response of a man who had not yet learned to receive this kind of thing easily. "Thank you, sir."

"Don't thank me. It's an observation."

"Yes, sir." A pause. "Rousseau helped. He has a way with the new men."

"I know. That's why he's a sergeant."

Bonnard looked up. He was twenty-four now and had a quality that men twice his age took years to develop: the ability to be completely present to the person in front of him. Philippe had had it. Bonnard had grown into it.

"Sir," Bonnard said. "Are you going home? When the war is done?"

Charles looked at him. "Yes."

"I thought so." He said it without judgment. "You have always been going. Even when you are here you are going."

Charles did not know what to do with that, so he said nothing.

"I don't mean it as a criticism," Bonnard said. "It's just a thing I noticed."

He went back to his men. Charles stayed where he was for a moment, looking at the road ahead, which curved north through a stand of bare birch trees toward whatever the spring was going to produce.

* * *

He found Margot Vieillard at a supply depot outside Allenstein on the third of May.

He was not looking for her. He was at the depot for a requisition argument about oats, which was a more complicated argument than it should have been because the depot officer was the same lieutenant who had slowed her paperwork in Lowicz, who apparently slowed everyone's paperwork everywhere as a matter of principle. Charles resolved it with the same tool he always used: specific dates, specific regulations, and the implication of Beaumont's name.

She was coming out of the depot office when he came in. They both stopped.

"Captain," she said. The new rank, noting it.

"You're this far east," he said.

"The route extended again." She looked at him with the particular attention he had learned to recognize, the kind that missed nothing. "You look different."

"The same."

"No." She did not say it critically. "Eylau."

He looked at her. He had not told her about Eylau. "You heard about it."

"Everyone heard about it." She studied him a moment longer, then: "Are you done here? There is a place in the town with adequate coffee."

"I have a requisition."

"I know. I heard you in there. You'll win it in ten minutes." She was already walking. "Find me when you're done."

He won it in twelve minutes. He found her at a table near the window of a narrow inn, her gloves off, the left one with the worn thumb, both hands around a cup. She had her ledger open beside her but she was not reading it. She was watching the market square.

He sat down. A woman brought him coffee without being asked, which meant Margot had ordered it before he arrived, which meant she had been certain he would come.

"You're always certain," he said.

She looked at him. "About some things."

"What things?"

"That you were going to resolve the requisition and come find me." She picked up her cup. "That Eylau was bad. That you are not entirely the same person who came through Posen in November."

He thought about the list in the back of his journal. Two and a half pages now. He thought about Ferrand, twenty-two, from Auxerre.

"I am the same person," he said. "The same plan."

"I know." She looked at the square. "That's not what I meant."

He waited.

"You came in with a different weight," she said. "Not heavier. More settled. Like you stopped arguing with something."

He considered that. He had stopped arguing with something, though he would not have named it that way. After Eylau he had remade the plan deliberately, consciously, as a decision rather than a fact, and in making it consciously he had stopped the internal argument about whether it was still valid. The plan was valid. He was going home. He had stopped debating it with himself.

"The war will end this summer," he said. "Probably. The Russians can't sustain another Eylau and neither can we."

"And then?"

"And then I put in for discharge and go to Bordeaux."

She was quiet for a moment, looking at the square. "When you say Bordeaux," she said, "what do you see?"

He thought about it honestly. "My father's study. The view from the upper fields over the Garonne in the evening. Elise at the table reading." He paused. "The work of building something. The estate. I know what it needs. I have known for two years what it needs and have not been there to do it."

"Building," she said.

"Yes."

She smiled, brief and genuine, the same one he had seen at Posen, the one she gave rarely enough that it meant something. "I've been thinking about that," she said. "After."

"What do you think about it?"

"I'm going to keep running convoys for a while. Maybe two more years. Get the eastern routes stable enough to hand to someone else and then do something else." She picked up her cup. "I don't know what yet. Something that stays in one place."

"That would be different for you."

"Very." She did not sound troubled by it. She said it the way she said most things: as information, something to be assessed and planned for. "My father says I will be bored in a week."

"Will you?"

"Probably not." She looked at him. "I know how to build things. I built this operation. I could build something else."

He looked at her, sitting across the table with her coffee and her ledger and the worn thumb of her left glove, and thought about the build versus destroy conversation at the inn in Hreben, six months ago. She had said: when my part breaks, people die not fighting. She had known then what she was building and why it mattered, and she still knew, and she was already thinking about what came next.

"You'll be good at it," he said.

"Yes." She said it simply, without false modesty. "So will you."

They sat for a while. The market square outside was doing what market squares did in May: the sounds of commerce and animals and ordinary life that had continued through two years of occupation and would continue after the occupation ended. Outside the window a woman was arguing with a vendor about the price of something. The argument was in Polish and Charles could not follow it but the shape of it was universal.

"I have to get back to the depot," Margot said, eventually. She pulled on her gloves, right first. "My next run is Thursday. I'll be through Allenstein again in three weeks."

"I may not be here in three weeks."

"I know." She stood. "I'll look for you anyway."

"I'll look for you," he said.

She nodded once and walked out into the square, and he sat with the coffee and watched her cross to the depot, and thought about the fact that she had said *building* the same way he had said it, with the same specific weight, and had not been surprised by it.

* * *

He wrote home that evening.

*Dear Father,*

*The promotion is confirmed. I am Captain as of the first of March. The plan is on schedule.*

*I want to tell you what I mean when I say that. I do not mean that I am moving automatically toward something I decided before I understood what I was deciding. I mean that I have been here long enough to understand what I am and what I am not, and what I came here to do and what I have done, and the plan remains the right plan. Not because it was always right. Because I have chosen it again, having seen the alternatives.*

*The war will likely end this summer. When it does, I will request discharge. I will come home. I will tell you the rest of it in person, which is the right way to tell it.*

*Your son,*
*Charles*

He sealed it. He sat for a moment, and then wrote a second letter.

*Dear Elise,*

*I am Captain.*

*I know you have already calculated what that means for the timeline, so I will confirm it: I am coming home. Not immediately. When the war ends. But I am coming.*

*There are things I need to tell you that letters are not adequate for. I will tell them to you in person. I expect you already know most of them, because you usually do.*

*Your brother,*
*Charles*

He sealed that one too and put both in the outgoing mail, and went to check the horses.
