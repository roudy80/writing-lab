# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 11

The promotion arrived in the spring of 1812, written in the compact administrative prose that the army used for all important things, as though importance required less language rather than more. Captain Charles Leblanc. Effective the first of April.

He read the letter twice, which he always did with official correspondence, and then sat with it.

He had made Captain. That was the plan. He was twenty-five years old and he had made Captain and the plan, in its original form, said he should now go home. He was aware of this. He had been aware of it since January, when he had first understood that the promotion was coming, and he had been aware, since November, that he was not going to act on it.

He wrote to his father. The letter took three drafts. The first draft said: I have made Captain and will be requesting discharge when the operational situation allows. He crossed this out because it was not true. The second draft explained why the operational situation did not currently allow. He crossed this out because the explanation was too long and sounded like an argument he was making to himself, which it was. The third draft said: I have made Captain. I expect to be in Spain through the summer season at least. The estate is in your hands, which I trust implicitly. He sent this one.

His father wrote back in July, by which time Charles was somewhere different than he had been when he sent the letter, and the reply arrived with a delay that meant the reply was answering a question that Charles had internally moved past. His father's letter said: I understand. Come when you can. The olives are doing well this year. Henri the elder sends his regards.

There was no reproach in this. His father was a man who had done his own service and understood that service had its own schedule. He had served for six years in the old royal army before the Revolution made that service complicated, and he did not talk about it often, and he had never once suggested to Charles that he should not go or should come home. The absence of reproach was in some ways more difficult than reproach would have been. Reproach would have given him something to push against.

Elise wrote separately, as she always did. Her letter said: You made Captain. That was the plan. The rest of the letter was about the estate and a neighbor's daughter who had married well and a horse that had developed a problem with his left foreleg and the olives, which were indeed doing well. She was twenty-five now, which was a fact that arrived with the specific quality of facts about people you had last known at a different age. He had left when she was fourteen. He tried to calculate who she was now from the letters, which was like trying to understand a city from maps.

She did not say more than that sentence about the plan. The shape of what she left out was very large.

He wrote back to both: I will come when I can. He believed this when he wrote it.

He thought, in the months that followed, about what going home would mean in practice. He had a specific image of the vineyard that had not updated since he left: the rows of vines in the spring light, the stone farmhouse, his father's hands, the particular quality of Bordeaux air in the morning. He was aware that the vineyard had continued existing while he was gone, that things had changed in it, that Elise was twenty-five rather than fourteen and his father was eight years older and the horse with the foreleg problem was a horse he had never seen. The image in his head was correct for 1805 and was probably wrong for 1812.

He did not know who he would be there. He knew who he was here: Captain Leblanc, someone who could keep his unit alive under conditions that killed other units. He did not know what remained of Charles Leblanc, Henri's son, Elise's brother, the young man who had ridden away from Bordeaux at eighteen with a horse and a paper in his boot.

He told himself this was a temporary situation. He believed this, in the way that one believes things that are convenient to believe.

* * *

The letter from Margot that summer was different from her usual letters.

She had been writing him practical letters for two years now, since the garrison city meeting. Letters about trade routes and road conditions and the Lisbon contact and the slow progress of rebuilding the Aragon network. He wrote back about terrain and unit movements and weather and the horses. Their letters had the character of two professionals corresponding on matters of shared practical interest, with an undercurrent that neither of them addressed directly in the letters themselves. He thought about her often in the way he thought about things he could not currently act on: with a particular deliberate patience, setting the thought on a shelf and not forgetting it was there.

This letter was still practical. She was describing the eastern network, which she had been rebuilding through 1811, eighteen months of patient work, a dozen merchants, four towns, the relationship structures she had been building one visit at a time.

I rebuilt my father's network in the eastern region, she wrote. The last piece connected last month: a merchant in a town near the Aragon border who was reluctant but eventually agreed to trial shipments. It took four meetings over eight months. He has good contacts with the roads north. If it holds, we will have the route my father designed in 1806 running again.

Then: I do not need to tell you how long this took. You know what the region looked like in 1810. I assume you know because your army was there.

This was the first time she had said this directly in a letter. He read it twice. He thought about what to say. He put the letter away and came back to it the next evening and wrote back: The eastern region in 1810. Yes. He did not say more than that. He did not know what more to say that would be honest and useful simultaneously.

She did not write back for six weeks. When she did: Thank you for not pretending. The eastern merchant's first full shipment came through last week.

He kept this letter in his field jacket with the others. He had a small bundle now, tied with a piece of cord he had taken from a supply kit. He did not reread them often. He kept them because they existed and because the existence of letters from a specific person is a different thing from the letters themselves. The letters were evidence of a continuity that the war regularly threatened to make implausible. She was there, in Frankfurt and Spain and the road between them. The world she was building was continuing to be built. He kept going and she kept going and the letters were proof that this was true.

He thought about this sometimes in the way he thought about Tonnerre: the bay gelding was the same constant the letters were. Tonnerre under him, familiar as his own hands, the neck-pat before anything difficult. The animal had no opinion about wars or plans or what happened to merchant networks in Aragon. He required feeding and exercise and correct care of his feet and a horse he could trust beside him, and in return he was present, which was a quality Charles had come to find underrated in persons and animals alike.

He promoted Rousseau to Sergeant in the fall of 1813. He told him the direct way he had learned worked with Rousseau: "Sergeant. Effective the first."

Rousseau said: "Yes, sir." He said it with the acceptance of a man receiving information he had been expecting. Not performed surprise. Just the thing, received.

He was becoming what Mercier had been. Charles was aware of this in the specific way that one is aware of a thing one is glad of and troubled by simultaneously. He was making Rousseau better at this. He was teaching him the inspection sequence and the way to talk to men and the way to read ground. He was making him capable. This was the work continuing, and the work continuing was good, and the work continuing was also the thing he had been unable to stop.

* * *

He wrote to her: One more season. The campaign is at a stage where withdrawal makes no sense.

She replied to the other things in the letter and left that line unanswered. This was its own kind of answer.

Her vocabulary was full of words like established and rebuilt and connected. His was full of held and cleared and denied. He looked at this sometimes, the two sets of words side by side in his mind, and did not know what to do with the comparison except to know it was accurate.

* * *

Captain Fontaine was killed in September of 1813, which was a year and a half after Charles had made Captain and Fontaine had made Major, and the year after Fontaine had made a point of mentioning the promotion in a headquarters meeting with the specific offhandedness of a man who is not offhand about it.

They had been foils since Italy, which Charles recognized as a category rather than a judgment. Fontaine was not stupid. He was politically astute in the way that men who intend to survive armies are politically astute, and he was genuinely capable in the field, and he had the quality of confidence that came from having always, so far, been right when he took risks. This quality is useful in soldiers. It is also, in the right circumstances, fatal.

The plan had been Charles's originally. A flanking action to take a hilltop position that the enemy was using for observation. It was a good position, worth taking, but the approach was exposed on the right for the last two hundred meters, and the enemy, who was not stupid, had anticipated approaches to a position they were defending. Charles's plan used three elements in sequence: one to draw fire, one to move on the position, one to cover the withdrawal if the second element was repulsed.

Fontaine had a different plan. His plan used all three elements simultaneously and moved faster and did not include a withdrawal option because Fontaine's plans rarely did. His argument was that speed was its own protection: move faster than the enemy expected and you were past the exposed approach before they could concentrate fire.

This argument had worked before. Fontaine had a record of operations where aggressive movement had succeeded precisely because no one expected it. He was not wrong to make the argument. His record supported it.

The operations planning group chose Fontaine's plan. He had a Major's rank and a staff reputation and a way of presenting plans that made caution sound like timidity. Charles's objection, made once and not repeated, was noted and not acted upon.

The operation initially succeeded. Fontaine's simultaneous movement put two elements on the position before the defense concentrated. He was not wrong about speed. The position was taken in fifteen minutes and Fontaine had not lost a man in the approach.

Then the enemy brought up reserves from the second line. The position was overextended. The flanks were not secured because Fontaine's plan had put everything into the assault and nothing into the consolidation. The enemy reserves hit all three elements simultaneously.

The retreat was not orderly. Charles heard this from the officer who survived it, who gave a report that was controlled in tone but showed, in the way the man's eyes moved while he was speaking, that the experience had not been controlled at all. Fontaine had died covering the retreat of his own men, staying in the rear when a faster man might have moved sooner. This was either courage or the specific stubbornness of a man who did not believe in retreat on principle. Both were possible. The man reporting it was not certain which.

He received a hero's funeral. Commendations. His commander spoke of boldness and initiative and a soldier's courage. These things were true: Fontaine had been bold and had shown initiative and had died with courage. None of this addressed the plan, which was also a true thing. The ceremony was clean and well-attended and the tributes were genuine in the specific way that tributes to bold men who die in bold actions are genuine: people believe them because the alternative is to conclude that boldness itself is not a virtue, which is an uncomfortable position in an army.

Charles stood at the funeral and watched the coffin and thought: he died chasing the same thing I wanted at eighteen. The position. The commendation. The recognition that comes from taking the objective that everyone agreed was worth taking. He was not a fool. He was not reckless in a simple way. He was a man who had decided the risk was worth it and the risk came out wrong.

The troubling part, standing at the funeral in the November cold, was the awareness that he could not entirely distinguish Fontaine's decision from decisions he himself had made that had come out differently. He had made bold choices. He had moved fast when fast was right. In each of those cases he had been correct and his men had survived and he had received praise that was similar in kind if not in degree to the praise now being given to Fontaine.

He could not say, with certainty, that the difference between Fontaine's death and his own continued survival was judgment. Some of it was. He believed some of it was. But not all of it, and the part that was not judgment was something else, something less orderable, and he preferred not to look at that directly.

He said nothing about the plan. Who was he to tarnish a dead man's reputation? The plan was wrong and Fontaine was dead and the people at the funeral were grieving. None of that was a conversation for a funeral.

He added Fontaine to the list when he got back to the tent.

* * *

Sergeant Mercier was killed in March of 1814, on a routine patrol in a sector that had been quiet for three weeks.

The report said: Sergeant Laurent Mercier, killed in action, hostile fire. That was what the report said. It had the form that all casualty reports had, which was a form Charles had contributed to writing in his administrative capacity and which he now looked at from the other direction.

He had not been present. He was at headquarters when it happened, working through supply requisition approvals, which was the kind of work that fell to officers of his rank and which he did without complaint because complaining about administrative work was something junior officers did and he was not a junior officer. He found out when a clerk brought the day's casualty list and his name was on it, which was the way he found out about most of his men's deaths now: from lists.

He sat with the list for a moment. Then he asked the clerk to give his apologies to the afternoon meeting and went outside.

The rain was doing what the November rain in Spain did, which was to fall steadily with the specific commitment of something that does not intend to stop. He stood in it and let it be cold.

He thought about the inspection sequence. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. Mercier had taught him this in Munich in the spring of 1805, which was nineteen years after Mercier had first learned it himself from someone else, and it was a correct sequence that produced a correct result and Mercier had done it correctly for twenty-five years and it had not kept him alive.

He had known Mercier since he was a boy, practically, since he was eighteen and Mercier was forty and Spain was not yet a word any of them thought about in a military context. Mercier had taught him what he knew about keeping men alive. He had taught him the inspection sequence and the way to talk to horses and the way to talk to men so that they heard what you were actually saying rather than what they wanted to hear. He had taught him, mostly by example, that the work of being an officer was not about the officer.

Charles walked back inside, eventually, because the rain did not stop and he was wet and there was work to do.

He said a few words at the burial. There were ten men present, several of whom had served with Mercier for five or six years and all of whom were now, Charles noticed, looking at him rather than at the grave with the expression that men wear when they are waiting to see if the words are adequate. He stood in the rain and let himself find the words, which were not the form-words, which were insufficient for this, but were the specific words.

He said that Mercier had been in the army since before most of them were born and had done the work correctly for all of that time, which was a longer span of correctness than most men managed in any field. He said that what Mercier knew about keeping cavalry functioning was the kind of knowledge that did not get put in field manuals because it did not translate into rules, only into demonstrated competence. He said that Mercier had the quality of someone for whom the work was enough, who did not need the work to be praised or recognized, who cared only that it was done correctly. He said that this quality was rarer than it looked.

He stopped when he had said what was true. He was not sure the words were adequate. He did not think adequate was possible.

He walked back to headquarters in the rain.

He turned to the list in the back of his journal that night. It was two and a half pages now. The second column had filled and he had started crowding names into the margins, written smaller to fit, the handwriting changing as it compressed. He was beginning to look at the third page.

He found a space near the bottom of the right margin and added Mercier's name.

He had known, or known a version of, Mercier since 1805. Nine years. He had known him longer than he had known his own men when he was in his own men, which was a peculiarity of army service that he had not thought about until this moment. He had watched Mercier do the inspection sequence on horses that did not need it more times than he could count. He would still be watching Mercier do the inspection sequence in his memory for the rest of his life, because that is what the dead do: they keep doing the thing they were doing when you knew them, in the specific territory of memory where no one ages and nothing changes.

He closed the journal.

He had stopped being in the smoke, he realized, sitting in the tent with the rain on the canvas. He could not identify exactly when. At some point the smoke had moved elsewhere and he had been in headquarters getting reports, and the transition had not announced itself. Mercier's name in a casualty report rather than in an action he had witnessed: that was as close as he could come to marking the moment.

One day you were in it. The next day it was arriving by report. The day after that you were writing the reports yourself.

He had not chosen this. It was what promotion produced. He was a Captain approaching Major rank, and this was what Major rank looked like from the inside: stone walls and lamp oil and reports about men whose names you added to a list at the end of the day. The violence had not stopped. He had stopped being in contact with it.

He did not know if Mercier, doing the inspection sequence in the November dark on a horse that did not need inspecting, had understood this was coming. He suspected Mercier had understood quite a lot that he had not said directly, which was Mercier's consistent method. He suspected Mercier had understood all of it and said nothing because nothing useful could be said.

* * *

Margot's letter that spring, the spring of 1814, described the eastern route in full function for the first time.

He wrote back: I am glad the network is functioning. He put the letter in the bundle in his coat. He looked at the bundle: twelve letters now, three years of correspondence, and the ones most recently received were the ones where the gap between what she was describing and what he was doing had become the subject of the letters rather than something underneath them.

She was building. That was the clear fact of her correspondence. Every letter described something added, something that had not existed and now existed. A new contact, a proven route, a relationship that had been tested through difficulty and was therefore trustworthy. She thought in terms of what was growing. Her vocabulary was full of words like established and secured and connected.

His vocabulary, looking at his own letters, was full of words like held and denied and cleared. These were also legitimate things. He was not under illusions about the work being unnecessary. But they were not the same words.

He wrote to her: I have been thinking about the Aragon conversation. He looked at this line for a long time and then added: I think you were right about the cost. He sent it. He was not sure why he sent it, except that she was the one person he could say this to and mean it, and he had been carrying it for a year and a half.

She wrote back: I know you are not a bad man. I know you are not even a careless one. That is the part that I find difficult. If you were careless, it would be simpler.

He read this six times. He understood exactly what she meant. He found it uncomfortable to understand exactly what she meant and did not try to misunderstand it.

He wrote back: One more season. The campaign is at a stage where withdrawal makes no sense. She did not reply to this line, or if she did, she replied only to the other things in the letter and addressed this line by not addressing it, which was its own kind of answer.

He put the letters back in the bundle and tied the cord and put them in his coat. He went to find Rousseau, who needed to be briefed on the following week's patrol schedule, and who met him with the expression of competent attention that Rousseau always wore for briefings, and who Charles found, at the end of every difficult day, to be an uncomplicated relief.
