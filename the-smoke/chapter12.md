# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 12

Friedland was the fourteenth of June, a Saturday, and the summer had finally arrived in the way that summer in the east arrived: suddenly, completely, as though it had been waiting just past a door someone had left closed.

The army was at Friedland because the Russian general Bennigsen had made a mistake. He had crossed the Alle River with the bulk of his force and posted them with the river at their backs and the town behind them and only two bridges for retreat. Napoleon had seen this and moved immediately, ordering everything toward Friedland, and by eight in the morning the French were arriving in strength and the Russians were discovering that the position they had chosen for a defensive engagement had become a trap.

Charles's company came up with the rest of the cavalry reserve at midmorning. He could hear the battle before he saw it: artillery from both sides, the steady exchange of a well-developed engagement, and underneath it the musketry that meant the infantry was in contact across the whole line. He brought the company to its assigned position and waited, which was the thing cavalry did in most battles: waited until the infantry created a situation that cavalry could exploit.

Waiting before a battle was different now than it had been in the Alps, before the Tagliamento, before Eylau. At the Tagliamento he had felt the anticipation as a physical pressure, something in the chest and the hands. Before Eylau it had been something colder, a clear-eyed calculation. Now it was something else again, something he did not have a precise name for. Not calm exactly. A kind of settled attention, the quality of a man who has done this enough times to know what to do with himself while he waits.

He rode the length of the company once. He checked the horses by eye. He came back to the front.

Sergeant Mercier was beside him, which was where Mercier was always found in these moments, not through instruction but through the long habit of being where he was needed without being asked.

"Bennigsen has given us the battle," Charles said.

"Yes, sir."

"It will be decisive."

"Yes, sir."

"The men are ready."

"Yes, sir." Mercier looked at the smoke rising on the French left. "Private Guillot's horse is favoring the near foreleg. I told him to stay in the rear position."

"Good."

"And Corporal Arnaud has been vomiting since dawn. Nerves. He is recovered and functional."

"Keep an eye on him."

"Yes, sir."

They stood in the June heat and watched the battle develop.

At half past five in the afternoon the order came: cavalry to the left flank, exploit the gap at the river bend.

Charles looked at his company. Sixty-three men, horses rested, sabers ready. He knew every name. He could have given you the home village of every man in the front rank and the condition of every horse in the rear. He had been doing this for two years and he was good at it.

He drew his saber.

"Company. Column of fours. Forward."

* * *

The gap at the river bend was real and it was wide and the Russians had not been able to close it because they were trying to cross two bridges simultaneously and the bridges could not handle what was being asked of them. Charles brought the company through the gap and hit the Russian left flank while it was trying to move, not fight, and drove it back against the river in three hard exchanges that he would not remember cleanly afterward.

He remembered specific things. The way the ground sloped toward the water and how Tonnerre's hooves changed cadence on the slope, adjusting without being told. A Russian officer on a gray horse who turned and charged rather than ran, which was the correct decision and did not save him. The sound of Mercier's voice somewhere to the right, steady and carrying, the voice Charles had heard in every engagement since Munich and had learned to use as a navigation point.

He remembered the moment the Russian line broke. Not a dramatic moment. A threshold reached: enough men had moved away from the fight that the men still in it had no choice but to move too, and the movement became general, and the fight was over.

He pulled the company back from the river's edge, counted, counted again.

All sixty-three. He counted a third time.

All sixty-three.

He stood in his stirrups, looked at the length of his company, at the faces he knew. Mercier, expressionless. Bonnard, checking his men with the systematic attention of a man who had learned the inspection sequence until it was reflex. Rousseau, his face flushed, already talking to the trooper beside him, the energy of the engagement still moving through him. The Girard cousin who had survived Eylau and had a new wariness in his eyes that would not leave.

All sixty-three.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck.

The gelding's neck was wet with sweat and his breathing was fast and even, recovering. The horse had been with him since before the Alps, since Munich, since the garrison. He had carried Charles through the Tagliamento gap and the screen outside Küstrin and the charge at Eylau and this, and he was still here, and his ears were already coming up from their flat-back position, the battle awareness fading, the horse returning to himself.

To the north the French left was pressing the Russian line into the river. To the south the artillery was still working. The battle was not over for everyone in it. For Charles's company, for this hour, it was over.

He looked at the river. The water was moving fast, green, the summer sun on it making it look clean from this distance. He thought about Ferrand, who had not come back from the snow at Eylau. He thought about Philippe, who had bled out against a gun carriage wheel in November. He thought about Moreau at the bridge, the first name on the list, the wife and the infant son, the letter he had written under Beaumont's instruction.

All sixty-three. Today.

He gave thanks for it in the simple way he gave thanks for things: a moment of recognition, nothing performative, just the acknowledgment of a fact that mattered. Then he reformed the company and went to find Beaumont.

* * *

The armistice came through three days later.

It was not entirely a surprise. The battle had been decisive; Bennigsen had lost a third of his force at the river and the Tsar had signaled through channels before the smoke cleared that he was prepared to discuss terms. Napoleon moved fast, as he always moved when he had leverage, and within a week the shape of the negotiations was clear.

The war was ending.

Charles sat with this information in the particular way he sat with large things: turning it over, examining it from different angles, looking for what it required of him. The practical requirements were immediate: he had a company to demobilize to whatever degree the peace terms required, men to account for, horses to assess, equipment to inventory. He started on those the morning after the armistice and worked through them methodically for two days.

On the third day he allowed himself to acknowledge what the armistice meant in terms that were not practical.

The war was ending. He was Captain. He had twenty-six months left on a five-year plan that had produced the captaincy on schedule. The next step was home.

He had been moving toward this for two years. He had made it specific, kept it in view, remade it as a conscious choice every time the weight of the work made it easier to simply continue. He had said yes to dozens of things and no to nothing, and the plan had held not because it was easy but because he had held it.

He walked to the horse line at evening. Tonnerre was at his usual place, working through a bucket of oats with focused attention.

"It's done," Charles said.

The horse did not look up.

"The battle. The campaign." He put his hand on the gelding's neck. "We are going home."

The horse kept eating, which was the correct response to news of any significance.

Charles stayed at the line for a while, hand on the horse, the summer evening cooling slowly around him. The camp noise was different in the days after an armistice: lighter, a different register, the sound of men who had been carrying something and are beginning to put it down. It would take weeks to put down completely. He knew that. He had done this after Italy and the weight had come off slowly, in stages, like a coat that is too familiar to be removed all at once.

But the direction was set. He was going home.

He went to find paper and write to his father, and then to find Mercier, and then to get some sleep, because tomorrow there was still work to do and there would be work to do until the day he left, and doing the work correctly until the last day was the only way to do it.
