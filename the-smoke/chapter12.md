# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 12

The order came in writing, which was always a sign that someone up the chain wanted documentation of compliance. Scorched earth policy, a designated region of eastern Spain. Deny resources to irregular and enemy forces. Requisition all available supplies. Neutralize storage facilities that cannot be transported.

Neutralize meant burn.

He read the order twice in his tent with Sergeant Duval standing at the flap, waiting. Duval had gray at his temples now and the manner of a man who checked things twice as a matter of professional pride and had checked Charles's expression when he started reading and knew, from the expression, what kind of order it was.

Charles went to his colonel.

"Sir," he said. "This will cause famine in the designated villages. The winter stores are already low. The harvest was poor."

The colonel looked at him. He was a competent man, Charles's colonel, a man who had served twenty years and thought carefully about most things. He had not been given this order carelessly and he was not receiving Charles's objection carelessly.

"That is why it will be effective," he said. "No stores means the guerrillas cannot shelter or resupply in those villages."

"Sir, the civilians."

"The civilians are providing material support to irregular forces. This has been documented." He looked at Charles steadily. "I understand your concern, Captain. Execute the order as humanely as the order permits. You will know what that means."

He knew what that meant. Don't take seed grain. Leave the draft animals if you can. Don't burn a house that has people in it. Don't do more than you are ordered to do.

He went back to his tent and told Duval to begin the preparation. Duval said: "Yes, sir," and went to work.

* * *

The execution took eight days.

He organized it into sections, each with clear objectives, each with a senior non-commissioned officer who had his specific instructions about what the order did and did not include. He was present for the first two days in the first village and then managed the operation from a position two kilometers back, moving between sections on Tonnerre, receiving reports, adjusting as problems arose.

He did not watch the burning from close range. He watched through a telescope, which was his job: observe, assess, direct. Through the telescope it was movement. Men going in and coming out. The specific dark shapes of smoke against the November sky, rising in columns and spreading as they rose. Wagons being loaded and moved. People on the road, moving away from the villages, which was the expected result and was described in the operational briefing as civilian displacement, which was accurate.

The reports that came back to him said: requisitioned. X kilos of grain. X liters of oil. X animals. The language was correct. The language was what the language was for.

He checked the paper in his boot each morning before mounting. Bordeaux. Elise's handwriting. He was not going into a battle. He checked it anyway, because the motion had become automatic at a level below decision.

On the fourth day a sergeant from the eastern section brought him a report and added, after the numbers, that a woman had tried to hide a grain sack in a well and had been removed from the area, that her children had been left with a neighbor, that the neighbor's house was outside the requisition perimeter and had not been touched. He listened to this report and said: noted. He added it to the daily record as a notation under civilian interactions. He did not know what the woman's name was.

On the sixth day the eastern section reported that one of the storage buildings had been fired before the contents could be fully removed, because an officer had misread the order sequence, and that approximately half the grain inside had burned. He had not authorized the sequence that produced this. He wrote a correction into the record and made a note to address the officer in question. This was the work.

By the eighth day the designated villages were empty of stores. His report back to the colonel was three pages. Numbers, dates, quantities, actions taken, exceptions noted. It was accurate. He had written it accurately, the way Mercier had taught him to do things: correctly, because correct was the only standard that held.

He gave the report to Duval to carry to headquarters and went to find Rousseau and told him the patrol schedule for the following week.

* * *

Ten days later his unit was moving back through the region on a different assignment.

The smell reached him first.

Not the smell of burning. The burning was ten days old and the rain had been working on it and what was left was the smell of wet ash, which was a different and more persistent smell than fresh smoke. But underneath the ash smell was the other thing: the particular quality of winter air in a place where the usual smells of a village are absent. No wood smoke from morning fires. No bread. No animals.

He knew this quality from other campaigns. His body had catalogued it before his mind caught up.

He rode through.

The market stalls in the main street were empty in the way that closed things are empty in winter, but not the same: closed things have the presence of what will come back. These had the absence of what was not coming back, or not soon. A woman at a well looking at him with the expression of someone who has recalibrated their expectations of what the morning would contain and has arrived at a reduced number. An old man in a doorway with the look of someone whose daily pattern has been removed and who has not yet constructed a new one. Two children at the edge of the road, watching the horses pass.

He did not stop. There was nothing useful he could do by stopping. He was not authorized to redistribute supplies he did not have, and sympathy was not a resource he could give them that would make any practical difference.

He rode through and did not stop and did not look away.

That evening in his tent he opened his journal to write the daily record. He tried several starts.

We passed through the requisitioned area on the return route. The villages showed the expected results of the operation.

He crossed this out. Expected results.

The operation was completed as ordered. The villages are without stores.

He looked at this. It was accurate. He crossed it out.

He sat with the blank page for a long time.

He wrote, finally: I am good at war. War makes children hungry. Therefore I make children hungry.

He looked at this. Then: No. That is not accurate. The enemy makes them hungry by using them as shields.

He crossed that out too. Then: No. We all make them hungry. The war makes them hungry.

He crossed it all out. He sat.

He lay down on his bedroll with his hand resting on his left boot. The paper was there. He was aware of it without thinking about it. He was aware that he was thirty kilometers from where the children had been standing at the edge of the road.

He did not sleep well.

* * *

Margot came to the garrison in March of 1814.

He was glad to see her. He did not know how glad until she was actually standing in the doorway of the headquarters building, in the practical coat with the collar up, with the leather satchel, and he felt something ease in him that had been held tight for several months.

She had business in the region: the southern leg of the Portuguese route was coming together and there were contracts to finalize in the garrison city. She had three days.

They had dinner with two of her merchant contacts on the first night, a long meal in which Charles said little and listened to the mechanics of Portuguese trade routes with attention that surprised him. She was operating across three languages in the conversation, switching without pausing, adjusting her register for each person at the table. He watched her work and thought: this is what competence looks like from outside the thing you are competent at.

He met Rousseau on the second day and introduced him. Rousseau was polite with the specific politeness of a man who has understood from context that this is a person of some importance to his officer and is therefore on his best behavior. Margot asked Rousseau how long he had served with Charles. Rousseau said: four years, not counting the letters she probably already knew about. She looked at Charles briefly when Rousseau said this and he found he had nothing to say to the look.

On the evening of the second day, after the merchant contacts had gone, she said: "There was a village. In the east. My father's partner's family lived there."

He said nothing.

"They are in the city now. The father is looking for work. Three children." She looked at him. "He told me what happened. What the army took and what they burned."

"I know," Charles said.

"You were there."

"I commanded the operation, yes."

She was quiet. Not a silence of accusation. The silence of someone who is deciding whether to say the thing they have been deciding whether to say for several months.

"You are one of the careful ones," she said. "Everyone I have spoken to says this. Leblanc is careful, Leblanc does not waste lives, Leblanc refuses unnecessary actions." She looked at her hands. "And you still did this."

"I was ordered to do it."

"Yes."

"If I refused, someone else did it. Probably with less care."

"Yes," she said. "I know this argument. I have been thinking about this argument since October." She turned to face him directly. "I am not saying you should have refused. I am saying: is that the standard? Less bad than what someone else would do? Is that what you are aiming at?"

He had the other defenses too. The campaign required it. The guerrillas were using those villages as supply points and without the supplies the guerrillas were less effective and fewer soldiers died. This was a true argument. He had made it to himself many times and found it held up under examination.

"The operation achieved its objective," he said. "Irregular activity in the designated region fell by more than half in the subsequent month."

"I know," she said. "I follow these things."

"People did not die in the region who would have died if the campaign continued without the operation."

"Which people?"

He meant soldiers. He meant the men under his command and the men in adjacent units. She knew who he meant. He heard the question anyway: which people get counted and which people don't.

He stopped defending.

"You build things," he said. "I know you build things. I know what they cost and how long they take."

"Yes."

"And I destroy them. That is a fair description."

"I did not say that."

"It is still fair."

She looked at him for a long time. Whatever she was deciding, she decided it slowly.

"I know you are not a bad man," she said. "God help me, I know that. You are an honest one and a careful one and you have never pretended to me about what this is." She turned back to the window. "That is the part I cannot resolve. You are the good version of this. And the good version still does what it does."

She asked the question then, the one she had been building toward since the garrison city in 1812: "You are good at this. You make it work. Without officers like you, wars would function worse and maybe end sooner. Does being the good version make it better? Or does it make it worse, because you make it possible?"

He had no answer. He had been living inside the question for two years. He could see it clearly from every angle. It was still not answerable.

"I do not know what I am without this," he said. "I have been doing this since I was eighteen. I am very good at it. I do not know if there is anything else."

She looked at him with the expression that had no military equivalent.

"There is," she said. "I have seen it. In the letters. In the garrison city." She picked up her satchel. "I just do not know if you can find it from where you are."

She was not leaving. She was going to her room because it was late and they both had work in the morning. But the sentence stayed after she left.

He sat in the chair by the window for a long time after she went up the stairs. He thought about the woman at the well, the children at the road, the old man in the doorway whose daily pattern had been removed. He thought about what Margot's father's partner was doing tonight in the city, looking for work. He thought about the report with its accurate numbers.

He thought about who he had been at eighteen, riding away from Bordeaux with the paper in his boot and the idea that there was a plan and the plan had an end.

He did not know if that person was still present or had been replaced so gradually that there was no single moment of departure he could point to.

He checked his boot in the morning before he mounted. Bordeaux. Elise's handwriting, worn at the fold.

He put it back and rode out to the briefing.
