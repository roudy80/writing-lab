# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 13

The Treaty of Tilsit was signed on the seventh of July, 1807, on a raft in the middle of the Niemen River, which struck Charles as an unnecessarily theatrical venue and which he was not present for. He was present for its effects, which arrived in the form of orders, redeployment schedules, and the particular kind of administrative chaos that follows a major war's end: units dissolving, units reforming, men going home and men being reassigned and men falling through the gaps between the two categories.

His company was assigned to garrison duty in a town called Tilsit, which was the same name as the treaty and which seemed like a coincidence that the army had not noticed or didn't care about. The garrison assignment was indefinite, which was the army's way of saying they had not decided what to do with this particular set of useful men and were not prepared to release them until they had.

Major Beaumont delivered this information at a meeting of the squadron officers in the first week of July. He was direct about it, as he was direct about most things.

"The garrison assignment is indefinite but I expect it to run six months, possibly through the spring. The peace terms require a French presence in the region while the political reorganization proceeds. We are a useful presence."

Charles looked at the map. Tilsit to Bordeaux was roughly fifteen hundred kilometers.

"Captain Leblanc," Beaumont said.

"Sir."

"I know what you're thinking."

"Yes, sir."

"I am not in a position to accelerate your discharge. The garrison assignment is a corps decision."

"I understand."

"What I can do," Beaumont said, "is ensure that when the discharge request goes forward, it goes with my full recommendation. Which it will." He looked at Charles. "Six months. Possibly less."

"Possibly more," Charles said.

Beaumont did not deny it. "Possibly more. That is the honest answer."

Charles looked at the map again. He had been looking at maps for two and a half years and knew how to read what they said and what they did not say. What this map said was: the army had him and was not finished with him.

He knew this. He had known it since December, since Beaumont's conversation in the manor house, since Mercier's warning in October of the year before. He had said yes to the promotion with his eyes open and the conditions noted and the conditions had not been honored, which was entirely predictable and which he had predicted and which did not help.

He was six months from home. Possibly more.

He went back to work.

* * *

Tilsit was a reasonable town that had been through a difficult two years and was attempting to return to the version of itself that had existed before the war arrived. The market ran on Tuesdays and Fridays. There was a Lutheran church and a Catholic one and they had apparently coexisted without incident for long enough that the coexistence was unremarkable. The river was wide and flat and in the summer evenings it reflected the sky in a way that made it look deeper than it was.

Charles quartered his company in a section of the town's eastern district, in buildings that had been used for that purpose before and had the particular quality of spaces that have housed many different groups of men and carry the impression of all of them. He organized the garrison routine the way he organized everything: systematically, with clear responsibilities and predictable structure, because men without clear responsibilities in a garrison situation found their own activities and those activities were rarely beneficial.

He wrote home twice a week. His father's replies were formal and warm in the way his father was warm, the warmth present and not easily named. His father had read between the lines of the letters about the promotion and had written: *I am proud of you, Charles. The work you have described sounds like the work of a good officer. I look forward to hearing the rest of it.* His father did not ask when he was coming. He understood enough about armies to know that question had no useful answer.

Elise wrote more. She always had. Her letters had the quality of conversations rather than dispatches: she followed threads from previous letters, asked questions about things Charles had mentioned in passing, noticed what he had not said. She had written about Philippe: *I thought he was extraordinary, from the way you never talked about him. You don't talk about the things that matter to you directly, you talk around them, and the shape of what you leave out tells me more than what you include. The shape of Philippe in your letters was very large.* She had written about the promotion: *Captain. Father walked around the estate saying it to himself for two days. I heard him in the library at ten at night saying it to no one.* She had written: *Come home, Charles. Not soon necessarily. Just come.*

He wrote to her about Margot Vieillard, which surprised him when he found himself doing it.

*There is a woman I have met several times this year, a contractor running supply convoys on the eastern routes. Her name is Margot Vieillard. She is eighteen, not yet nineteen, and she manages a fourteen-wagon operation with the competence of someone who has been doing it for forty years. I am telling you this because you will ask about it eventually and I would rather tell you directly than have you extract it from the shape of what I leave out.*

*I do not know what to tell you about it beyond the facts. We have spoken honestly about things that are difficult to speak honestly about. She will be on these roads for another year or two and then she will do something else. I will be in Bordeaux in six months, possibly more. I am not certain of anything further than that. You may draw whatever conclusions you like.*

He sealed it, looked at the envelope, and wrote Elise's name on it, and thought about what she would make of the phrase *I am not certain of anything further than that*, which was either an evasion or the most honest thing he had written in a year and he was not certain which.

* * *

He found Mercier at the stable on a Thursday evening in late July, which was where Mercier could always be found when there was no immediate work pressing and sometimes when there was.

He told Mercier about the garrison assignment. The indefinite timeline. Beaumont's six months.

Mercier listened. He was cleaning a bridle with the particular patience of a man who had cleaned a thousand bridles. He did not look up.

"You are going to wait," Mercier said.

"I don't have another option."

"You could resign your commission."

Charles looked at him. "I signed the promotion. I accepted the terms."

"Yes." Mercier set down the bridle. He looked at Charles now, directly, with the quality of attention he reserved for things that mattered. "I am not suggesting you resign. I am noting that you believe you have no options in order to point out that you do have options and are choosing not to use them. That is different from not having options."

Charles absorbed that.

"You are choosing to honor the commitment," Mercier said. "That is the right choice. But calling it the only choice is how men end up resentful of things they freely chose." He picked up the bridle again. "Own it, sir. You chose it."

"I chose it."

"Yes." Mercier ran the bridle through his hands, checking the stitching. "And in six months, or however long it is, you will choose to leave. And that will also be a choice you make, not a thing that happens to you."

Charles said nothing for a moment. Outside the stable the summer evening was doing what summer evenings did in Tilsit: the light lingering longer than it had any right to, the air still, the river carrying some sound from the town on the far bank.

"Mercier," he said.

"Sir."

"When I go. Will you come?"

Mercier went still for a moment, the bridle in his hands. It was the only time Charles had seen him go completely still outside of the second before combat.

"Come," Mercier said.

"To Bordeaux. The estate needs work. Real work, not military work. My father is older. I could use someone who knows horses and knows how to get things done." He paused. "It's not an order. It's a question."

Mercier was quiet for a long time. He set the bridle down on the stable rail, precisely, as though its placement mattered.

"I have been in the army for seventeen years," he said.

"I know."

"I don't know what I am outside it."

"No," Charles said. "Neither do I. But I expect we'll find out." A pause. "There is no obligation. I am asking."

Mercier looked at the bridle for a moment. Then: "I will think about it."

"That's all I'm asking."

Mercier picked up the bridle and went back to the stitching. Charles stayed in the stable for a while, with Tonnerre and the smell of hay and the familiar sounds of horses settling for the evening, and thought that whatever came next, the question had been worth asking.

* * *

Margot Vieillard came through Tilsit on the ninth of August, on a run from Frankfurt that had been extended twice and was now covering a route that would have seemed impossible six months ago when the fighting was still active. She arrived with fifteen wagons and a calm efficiency that had the depot officers sorted and signed within two hours of arrival.

He found her at the depot. She looked at him the way she always looked at him: completing the assessment before speaking.

"Captain," she said.

"Vieillard."

"How long are you in Tilsit?"

"Six months. Possibly more."

She absorbed this without visible reaction. "And then Bordeaux."

"And then Bordeaux."

She nodded. She pulled her gloves off, right first, left second, the worn thumb. "Walk with me. I want to see the river while there is still light."

* * *

The river was wide and calm and the evening light was doing what it had been doing all summer, lingering beyond what was reasonable. They walked along the bank without hurrying. Her eleven men were at the depot; she had an hour before she needed to be back.

"I'm done with the eastern routes after this run," she said. "I'll do the Frankfurt to Posen run through the winter and then I'm going to stop."

"Stop the convoys?"

"Stop everything. For a while." She looked at the water. "My father is in good health. The operation runs without him now, or runs with less of him. I have been thinking about what I said to you in Lowicz, in January."

"Don't let it be decided by the accumulation of small yeses."

"Yes." She walked for a moment. "I have been saying yes to the next run for two years. Each yes was the correct answer. Each run was needed and I was the person who could do it well and the work mattered." She paused. "That is not a reason to stop. But it is also not, by itself, a reason to continue."

He recognized the logic. He had been running the same calculation for eighteen months.

"What will you do?" he said.

"I don't know yet. Something in one place." She glanced at him. "My father has been talking about expanding the operation, a warehouse in Frankfurt, routes to the south. Something I could manage from one location rather than from the road." She was quiet for a moment. "Or something else entirely. I haven't decided."

"You'll be good at whatever it is."

"Yes." She said it simply. "So will you. The estate, the vineyard. Whatever you build there."

They stopped at a place where the bank was clear, the river visible down a long straight stretch. The far bank was Prussia, technically, or whatever Prussia was becoming under the treaty reorganization. The map was being redrawn again.

"You knew I was going before I told you," he said.

She looked at him. "Yes."

"How long?"

She considered it. "Since Posen. You were always going. I told you that. Even when you were here you were going."

"Bonnard said the same thing."

"Then Bonnard is perceptive." She looked at the river. "I am not saying it as a criticism. I am saying it because it is one of the things about you that is true, and it is not a flaw."

He looked at her. "What are the others?"

She looked back at him. The evening light was on her face, the specific quality of late summer light in the east, warm and horizontal and making everything it touched look more itself than usual.

"You notice what needs doing and you do it," she said. "Without needing to be seen doing it. You hold things for people without telling them you are holding things. You find the practical reason for what you were going to do anyway, which is a defense against something, I think, though I don't know against what."

He thought about Elise writing: *competence isn't care.*

"My sister says something similar," he said.

"Your sister sounds like someone worth getting home to."

He looked at the river. "Yes."

They stood for a moment, the water moving past them toward the Baltic, the sound of it filling the space.

"I looked for you in Allenstein," she said. "In June. After Friedland."

"I wasn't there."

"I know. The depot told me your company had moved." She was quiet. "I wanted to know you were all right."

He looked at her.

"I did not know you until November," she said. "I have seen you five times in eight months. That is not enough to know someone fully." She said it carefully, the way she said careful things: plainly, without performing the care. "But I know enough to want to know the rest. And I know that the rest is in Bordeaux, not here."

"Margot."

"Don't apologize for it," she said. "It is not something to apologize for."

"I wasn't going to apologize." He turned toward her. "I was going to say that I thought about you at Eylau. Not during. After. When I was counting the men and they were all there." He paused. "I thought: she would understand this. The specific relief of this particular number."

She looked at him for a long moment.

"I would," she said.

The evening bell rang from the church in the town, the sound carrying clear across the river. She looked at the water a moment longer and then turned back toward the depot.

"Come find me before you leave for Bordeaux," she said. "If the road takes you through Frankfurt."

"The road goes through Frankfurt."

"Then come find me."

He watched her walk back along the river bank, the same unhurried efficiency, the same quality of motion that wasted nothing. At the bend of the path she did not look back, which was entirely in character, and he stood at the river for a while after she was gone, watching the water, thinking about the distance from here to Bordeaux and what it meant that he already knew the road.
