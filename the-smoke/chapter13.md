# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 13

She got the news on a Tuesday in March, from a man named Hoffmann who worked for her father and who had the specific expression of someone who has been rehearsing a conversation on a long road and still does not know how to start it. He was standing in the doorway of the Frankfurt office when she arrived in the morning. He had his hat in his hands.

She understood what the hat meant before he said anything.

Her father had been at the Frankfurt warehouse alone on Saturday evening. He was checking inventory, which was something he had always done himself and had continued doing himself even as the business had grown to the point where he could have delegated it. It was a habit from when the business was small, when knowing the exact inventory was the difference between a profit and a loss, and he had never stopped. Hoffmann did not know exactly what had happened because he had not been there. What he knew was: there had been men in the warehouse district, deserters or displaced soldiers from the retreat or simply men who were in a difficult situation and made a worse decision, and her father had not been the first choice but he had been present. He had tried to protect the remaining goods.

"He did not suffer," Hoffmann said, and then looked like he wished he had not said this, because it was the thing one said and it was probably not accurate and they both knew it was probably not accurate.

"Thank you, Hoffmann," she said. "Come in."

She spent the next four hours with Hoffmann and with the two other senior employees, going through the immediate items. The warehouse inventory. The contracts in progress, three of them at critical stages. The correspondence that had been waiting for her father's signature and was now waiting for hers. The accounts.

She did not cry in the office. There was too much to do and too many people looking to her for direction, and direction was what she could give them, which meant direction was what she gave.

She cried alone that evening in the rooms above the office, for approximately twenty minutes, and then stopped and washed her face and came back downstairs because she had not finished reviewing the Strasbourg contract and it was due to the counterparty in two days.

* * *

She buried him on Thursday. Fast, because the business did not stop and because grief held in suspension is not grief resolved, and she had learned this from watching her father manage loss, which he had done several times over the years, always with the same method: bury it, work, let the work be the container.

Eleven people attended. She said a few words she had written that morning between the contract review and a meeting with the Strasbourg counterparty who had sent a representative rather than coming himself, which told her something about the representative's principal that she filed away for later. She had not slept much. The words came out clearly anyway. She had her father's voice, people said. She had always thought they meant the pitch. She understood now they meant the quality: the voice that stayed level when things were not level.

She looked at the marker after everyone had gone. Henri Vieillard. The years. He had been fifty-three, which was not old but was not young, and he had been in good health until he was not, which was the particular character of the kind of death he had had.

She thought: you were careful for thirty years. You built something that survived two wars. You were in the wrong place on the wrong Saturday.

She did not stay long. She had a meeting in an hour.

* * *

The immediate problem was the eastern route, which her father had been managing personally since she had been handling the southern Portuguese leg. Three merchants in that corridor who had their relationship with the business through her father specifically, through thirty years of dealings, through the particular trust that builds when a man pays on time and shows up when he says he will and does not cheat on weights. She had met them. She was not her father.

She wrote to all three of them the week of the funeral. She stated the change plainly. She did not perform grief in the letters and she did not perform confidence she did not have. She said: my father is dead, I am managing the business, I would like to continue the relationship we have had, and here is my proposal for the next shipment.

The first two wrote back within the week. One agreed to the proposal without modification. One proposed adjusted terms, not unreasonable, which she negotiated over two more letters and settled. The third did not write back for three weeks, and when he did he proposed terms that were predicated on the assumption that a twenty-two-year-old woman alone was a different kind of counterparty than her father.

She wrote back with a clear statement of what she knew about his accounts, his outstanding obligations, and the three other merchants in the corridor who she knew from her father's records had had prior dealings with him that had not reflected well on his credit. She was polite. She was specific. She said she expected the original terms and that her experience suggested the original terms were in his interest.

He wrote back agreeing to the original terms.

She filed the letter in the correspondence folder with the note: adjusts when presented with information. Reliable if monitored. This was the kind of note her father had always made. She understood, writing it, that she had been absorbing his practice for years without knowing she was absorbing it.

* * *

She also let one employee go. His name was Bertrand, he had been with the business for four years, and he was not doing bad work. He was doing adequate work, which meant that with the business at its current size, his salary was a cost that could not be justified, and she could not afford sentiment about it.

She told him directly, gave him two months' pay, gave him a written reference that was accurate rather than generous. He looked at her with the expression of someone who had expected the daughter to be softer than the father. She kept her face as she needed it.

After he left she sat for a moment. She did not second-guess the decision. She had made it with the same method she made all decisions: look at the numbers, consider the people, determine what is necessary.

Her father had taught her this. He had taught her by example over twenty years, by letting her sit in meetings when she was young enough that they probably didn't notice her in the corner, by making her work the routes herself rather than managing from Frankfurt, by letting her make mistakes and not rescuing her from them. He had not been a sentimental man. He had been a fair one, which she understood now was harder.

She thought about Charles.

She thought about him briefly, not at length. She wondered if he knew yet. The letter she had sent him after the garrison city visit in March had been businesslike in tone, and the mail to wherever his unit was in the current phase of the campaign was slow and unreliable. He might not know for months.

She considered writing him a longer letter. She picked up the pen and put it down.

She had work to do. She would write when she had time to write it well.

* * *

His letter arrived in June.

It was short. He had heard about her father from her previous letter, the brief one, and he had written: I am sorry for your loss. I know what Henri Vieillard built and what it meant to you. I would come if I could. I cannot. I am sorry for that too.

He had not dressed the apology or padded it or justified it. She appreciated this. She had known, since the garrison city, that he was not going to come. She had not needed the justification. She had needed the honesty, and the honesty was there.

She wrote back after a week. She told him about the business, the eastern route, the three merchants and the one who had tested her terms. She told him that Bertrand was gone and that the Portuguese leg was holding and that she had her father's desk now and was still adjusting to the sight of it.

At the end she wrote: I am sure you did what you thought necessary.

She looked at this sentence after she wrote it. She was not entirely sure which thing she was referring to: his absence, or the campaigns, or something larger. She sent the letter anyway. She thought he would understand it the way she meant it, which was: I am not angry. I am tired. I am still here.

She sealed it and gave it to Hoffmann to add to the outgoing correspondence.

She picked up the Strasbourg file and went back to work.
