CHAPTER 15

Major Leblanc.

He read the promotion order and folded it and put it in his coat pocket. He had made Major. He was twenty-eight years old and the army was reorganizing under the Bourbon restoration and he had a record that made him precisely the kind of officer the restored monarchy wanted to keep: capable, experienced, not politically dangerous. He was promoted because he was useful, and being useful had no natural terminal point that he could see.

He wrote the letter to his father. He did not take three drafts this time. He wrote: I have been promoted to Major. I expect to be in occupation duty through next year at minimum. The estate is well managed without me and I have full confidence in your handling of it. He sent it without reading it back.

His father replied: I know you will come when you can. I have stopped predicting when that will be.

This was the gentlest possible statement of a thing that was not gentle, which was the register his father had always used and which Charles had always found more difficult than the direct statement would have been.

* * *

Major command was different from Captain command in kind, not only in degree.

He commanded multiple squadrons now. Three hundred men, sometimes more. His role was planning and logistics and the management of other officers. He reviewed plans drafted by lieutenants and captains. He approved supply requests. He attended meetings at which the decisions were made that determined what the plans would address. He wrote the orders that his officers carried into the field.

He was rarely in the field himself.

He understood this was correct. A Major commanding three hundred men was too valuable to the function of the unit to put at direct risk in routine field operations. He was the thing that organized the other things, the node that kept the network functional. He was too useful in the room to be useful in the smoke.

The violence had become entirely abstract.

He moved pins on a map. Reports reached him: we lost thirty men. The thirty men were in a report before they were thirty letters he had to write, and in the old days he had written the letters as close to simultaneously with the deaths as the situation allowed, because the letters were the thing that connected the death to the person. Now the letters were written by the company officers and reviewed by the adjutant and he signed them if they came to his level, which they usually did not. The form had spread. He had been part of making the form and the form had replaced him.

He smelled ink now. Lamp oil. Stone walls in the evening with the muffled quality that headquarters buildings have. He did not smell powder. He had not smelled powder since Waterloo, except at a distance, on operations he had planned but not been present for.

He noticed his own hands sometimes. He had the calluses from reins and from sword handling, the specific hardening along certain fingers. The calluses were not going anywhere. The work that had made them was somewhere else, in the field, happening to other people who were reading his orders.

He thought about Mercier's inspection sequence. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. He still ran through it when he was near the horses, which was every morning. He would run through it for the rest of his life, for whatever horses he was near, regardless of whether the horses needed it. He understood this about himself.

He thought: at least when I was in the smoke, the violence was honest. You could see the thing you were doing. You could feel it. Here the pins moved and the reports came in and the connection between the decision and the consequence was long and indirect and mediated by paper and he was never quite sure, looking at a report, whether he was understanding what had actually happened or only what the report said had happened.

He thought: I miss it.

He looked at this thought and found it uncomfortable. He missed the smoke. He missed being inside something he could read directly. He missed the clarity of the bad choice and the worse choice, which was at least honest about being a choice. The fog was clean. His hands were clean. The consequences were not.

He did not say this to anyone.

* * *

She came to the garrison unannounced, which was something she had never done before, and which told him something before she said anything, because Margot was a woman who announced herself.

He was in the planning room with Sergeant Duval when she appeared in the doorway. He was genuinely glad to see her. He crossed the room to her and she let him take her hand but her face had the look it had worn in the garrison city in 1814, the look of someone who had been working up to something for several months.

He sent Duval out. He found her a chair. He sat across from her.

"There was an operation," she said. "Last November. In the eastern region."

He said nothing.

"My father's old partner was there. His family." She looked at her hands. "He described it to me. The unit that came through. How they worked." A pause. "He said the officer was careful. Said the officer took less than some units and left them the draft animals."

"Yes," Charles said.

"Was that you?"

"My orders, yes. Not my direct command. I was in headquarters."

She looked at him. "From headquarters."

"Yes."

"You moved the pins."

He did not answer this. She had understood it correctly and there was nothing to add.

She leaned forward. She was not angry exactly. She was something beyond anger, something that had been compressed over years into a specific and very patient pressure.

"I build connections," she said. "I spend years creating trust, relationships, routes that work. I spend months rebuilding what the last campaign destroyed and then the next campaign comes through and I start over." She looked at him steadily. "You destroy it in a day. From headquarters now. You don't even have to be there."

"What else can I do?" he said. He meant this as an honest question, not a defense. He had been asking it for three years.

"Refuse," she said.

"And be court-martialed. And someone replaces me who does it with less care."

"So you are better than nothing. That is the standard."

He looked at the map on the table. Pins. Colored markers. A perfectly organized representation of something that was happening to people.

"You know I am not a bad man," he said.

"I know," she said, and her voice broke on the last word, not from weakness but from the specific fracture of something that has been held against pressure for a long time. "God help me, I know that. I know exactly who you are. I have known for years. That is not the problem." She stood up. "You are the best version of this. And the best version of this is still this."

He stood too. He was aware that they were at the point of the conversation that could not be undone. Things said here would remain said.

"I still love you," she said. "I do not know what to do with that."

"I love you too," he said. "I have for a long time."

"Yes." She picked up her satchel. "I know. I am not sure it matters anymore. I am not sure love is sufficient when everything else is."

She walked to the door. She was not leaving in anger. She was leaving because she had said the thing and staying now served no purpose.

"Margot," he said.

She turned.

He wanted to say: I will come home. He wanted to say: after this, after one more, after the occupation settles. He had been saying this in various forms since 1812. He knew what it sounded like.

He said: "I know you are right."

She looked at him for a moment.

"I know you know," she said. She went out.

He stood in the planning room with the map and the pins and listened to her footsteps in the corridor and did not follow.

* * *

He found Beaumont an hour later, in the room that Beaumont used in the evenings, which was a small room off the main corridor with a table and a lamp and a bottle he never finished in a single evening but that was always somewhat lower by the morning. Beaumont was standing at the window with a glass, looking at the courtyard.

Charles said nothing. Beaumont did not turn from the window.

"She leave?" Beaumont said.

"Yes."

Beaumont drank. Set the glass on the table.

"Good work today," he said. "Very efficient. Minimal casualties for the objective achieved." He said it in the tone of the official commendation, the form of praise the army used for operations that went well. Then, in the same tone: "That is what they will say about you one day. Efficient. Effective. Gets results."

He was quiet for a moment.

"And you will stand here, where I am standing, and wonder when you stopped feeling it."

Charles stood in the doorway and looked at Beaumont and looked at the glass on the table and the bottle beside it and the set of Beaumont's shoulders, which were the shoulders of a man who had made his accommodation with what he had become and was not sure the accommodation had been worth making and was past the point of changing it.

He said nothing, because there was nothing to say that was adequate and Beaumont had not asked him to respond. He stood there a moment longer and then went back to his quarters and sat on the edge of the bed with his hands on his knees and stayed there for a long time in the dark.
