# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 15

Frankfurt was three weeks from Tilsit, moving at the pace of men and horses that were not in a hurry.

He had not been in a hurry for the first time since Munich. The sensation was unfamiliar enough that he noticed it in the first day, the absence of urgency in his body, the way the road looked different when you were not moving toward something that required speed. He noted it and did not try to recreate the urgency. He let the road be the road.

Mercier rode beside him most of the day. He talked less than Charles had expected, given that the constraint of military hierarchy was loosening as each kilometer passed. He had always been a man of few words. He was apparently a man of few words outside the army too, which was not a disappointment, merely information. They rode and watched the country and stopped when the horses needed water and ate when they were hungry and made camp before dark.

At night Mercier sat with the others, the eleven discharged men who were spreading gradually in the direction of their various homes, dropping off one or two at a time as the road reached their regions. He did not seek them out and did not avoid them. He was present in the way he had always been present: fully, without performing it.

Charles watched this and thought about what Mercier had said in the stable in Tilsit: *I don't know what I am outside it.* He was finding out on the road, as was Charles, and what they were finding out so far was that they were approximately themselves, which was either a relief or simply a fact.

* * *

Frankfurt appeared on the eleventh day, the city's outline rising above the Main River in the March morning light. Charles rode to the river and looked at it and then turned his horse toward the address he had carried in his coat pocket for three months, since August, since Margot had said *come find me before you leave for Bordeaux.*

The Vieillard depot was on the south side of the river, a substantial building with wagon access and a yard large enough for a full convoy to stage. Charles arrived at midmorning and told the man at the gate his name.

He waited in the yard.

She came out of the depot office in work clothes, ledger in hand, and saw him and stopped. A moment, the familiar pause, the assessment completing itself.

"You came," she said.

"I said I would."

She looked at him for a moment. He was in plain clothes now, the first time she had seen him without the uniform, and he watched her register this, file it.

"Come inside," she said. "I'll be done in an hour."

He waited in the depot office, which smelled of paper and wood and the particular dry smell of grain sacks. He sat in a chair near the window and watched the yard and listened to the sounds of an operation working. Men moving, wagon wheels, the voices of her drivers calling to each other in the shorthand of people who have worked together long enough to not need full sentences. The same sounds as any working operation: organized, purposeful, the sounds of things being built.

She was done in forty minutes.

They walked to the river. It was a clear March day, cold but not biting, the kind of cold that felt like the end of winter rather than the middle of it. The Main was running fast with snowmelt.

"Lyon first," she said. He had written her the route. "Then Bordeaux."

"Yes. Mercier wants to see his sister."

"Good." She looked at the river. "And then the estate."

"The estate."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "Tell me what it looks like. The view you described in the letter. From the upper fields."

He thought about it honestly, trying to see it rather than describe it.

"The Garonne is in the valley," he said. "In the evening light it moves slowly enough that it looks still, but it isn't. The color changes depending on the sky. The vineyards are on the slopes above it, the rows running uphill, and in August when the vines are full the green of them is very particular, a specific shade I have not seen anywhere else." He paused. "I have been away for two and a half years. I don't know what condition it's in."

"You're afraid it won't be what you remember."

"I'm not afraid. I'm uncertain. Those are different."

She glanced at him. "Yes, they are." She looked back at the water. "It will be different. Things always are. But the fields and the river will be there."

"Yes."

"And Elise."

"And Elise."

She smiled, the brief genuine one, the one he had been watching for since Posen. "You talk about her the way you talk about things you trust completely. There aren't many of those."

"No."

"She sounds like someone who will know what to do with you when you get home."

"She usually does."

They stood at the river for a while. Downstream, a barge was working against the current, slow and deliberate, loaded with something he could not identify from this distance.

"I am going to Posen in the summer," she said. "And then I am going to think about what comes next."

"Will you write?"

She looked at him. The pause. "If you do."

"I'll write from Lyon," he said.

"Then I'll write back." She turned from the river. "Come for dinner tonight. My father wants to meet you."

"You told him about me."

"I told him I had met a cavalry officer who understood supply logistics. He was curious." She was already walking. "He will ask you difficult questions about the eastern roads. I suggest you know the answers."

"I know the answers."

"I know you do." She did not look back. "Come at seven."

* * *

He met her father at seven. Henri Vieillard was a man of sixty who had been doing the work of the roads for thirty years and could read a route the way Charles could read ground: quickly, accurately, with the attention of someone who understood that the difference between the right road and the wrong one was consequential. He asked difficult questions about the eastern routes. Charles knew the answers. The old man listened to them with the careful attention of someone evaluating a source.

At the end of the evening Henri Vieillard shook Charles's hand and said: "You are welcome in Frankfurt, Captain."

Charles left at ten and walked back to the inn where Mercier and the others were quartered. The night was clear, the March cold precise, the stars visible over the city's roofline. He could see his breath.

He thought about the view from the upper fields above the Garonne. He thought about Elise at the table. He thought about Margot at seven tomorrow morning, when he was leaving, at the depot gate.

He thought: I am going home.

The thought had a different quality now than it had for two years. It had been a plan, then a decision, then a choice remade daily in the weight of the work. Now it was a direction. A thing he was moving toward rather than holding in front of him.

In the morning he said goodbye to Margot at the depot gate, brief and direct, the way they both said things. She held out her hand and he shook it, the worn left glove, the same one, and she looked at him with the assessment completing itself one more time.

"Bordeaux," she said.

"Frankfurt," he said. "Eventually."

She held his gaze for a moment. Then she turned back to the depot.

He rode south with Mercier.

* * *

Lyon was ten days further, through country that was becoming more familiar the further south they went. The light changed below Lyon, became the light of the south, horizontal and warm even in March, the kind of light that made the stone walls of the farmhouses glow. Mercier spent three days with his sister, who was older than him and had a family and a house full of children who treated Mercier's silence with the casual acceptance of people who had always known him.

Charles waited in Lyon and wrote a letter to Margot from Frankfurt, as he had said he would, and wrote one to Elise that said only: *I am in Lyon. I am two weeks from home. I will be there before the end of the month.*

He sent both and rode south.

* * *

He came home on a Thursday, which seemed again like an irrelevant detail and was the one he kept.

The road from Lyon south and then west took him through country that became more his with every kilometer, the Massif on the right giving way to the flatlands and then the river and then the first vineyards he recognized, the rows familiar, the specific angle of the morning light on the stone walls of the estate boundary.

He stopped at the gate.

The gate was the same gate it had been when he left. Stone posts, iron hinges, the latch his father had repaired himself in the summer of 1803 and had never been fully satisfied with. Beyond it the drive went up through the vineyards toward the house, the house he had grown up in, the view from the upper fields above the Garonne in the evening.

He sat on Tonnerre for a moment.

He put his hand on his coat pocket. The paper was there, Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card. He looked at the gate post, at the stone wall, at the drive going up through the vineyards. He was standing at the address. He took his hand away.

Mercier waited behind him without speaking.

Charles opened the gate and rode through.

* * *

Elise was on the front steps when he came up the drive. She had always had the ability to know when something was arriving, which their father attributed to the dogs and Charles had never believed. She was standing on the steps with her coat on and her hair not yet done for the day, as though she had come out in a hurry, and she watched him ride up the drive with an expression that he could not fully read at this distance and could read completely when he was close enough.

He dismounted.

She walked down the steps and stood in front of him. She was twenty-two now, two years older than when he had left, and there was something in her face that had not been there before, a quality he recognized as the same thing that had arrived in his own face, the residue of having lived through a stretch of time that required something real.

She looked at him for a long moment without speaking.

"You're thinner," she said.

"You're taller."

"I'm not taller. You're thinner." She hit his arm once, not hard, the gesture of someone who has been waiting to do it for a long time. Then she held onto his arm and did not let go.

"I got your letters," she said.

"I know."

"All of them."

"I know."

She looked at him. "There is a great deal I want to ask you."

"I know that too."

"And you're going to actually answer."

He thought about Margot at the depot gate, about Mercier at the stable in Tilsit, about Philippe at the picket line in November adjusting his collar. About Beaumont saying: *knowing it and feeling it are different processes.* About all the things he had not said in letters and had carried instead in the list and the journal and the paper in his boot.

"Yes," he said. "I'm going to answer."

She looked at him steadily. She had always looked at things steadily; it was the quality that had made her right about him since they were children.

"Good," she said. "Come inside. Father is in the library. He has been in the library all morning pretending to read."

He handed Tonnerre's reins to Mercier, who took them without a word and led the horse around toward the stable, already looking at the building with the professional attention of a man assessing what needs to be done. Charles watched him go for a moment.

Then he followed Elise up the steps and through the door, into the house that smelled of books and bread and the specific morning light that came through the eastern windows in March at this latitude, and had always smelled this way, and was exactly itself.

His father was standing in the library doorway, which meant he had heard the horse on the drive. He was older, which Charles had expected, and the same, which Charles had hoped, and he looked at his son with an expression that had no performance in it at all.

"Charles," he said.

"Father."

They stood for a moment. His father moved first, crossing the room, and put his hand on Charles's shoulder, and Charles stood with his father's hand on his shoulder and the morning light through the windows and Elise at his side, and did not say anything, because there was nothing to say that the moment did not already contain.

* * *

He went to the upper fields that evening.

He went alone, after dinner, after the conversation that had gone on for three hours and had covered some of what needed to be covered and had left most of it for later, which was the right amount for the first evening. He walked up through the vineyards in the last of the light, the rows of vines on either side of him in the not-yet-green of early spring, the bare wood of them sharp against the evening sky.

The view from the top was the view he had held in his memory for two years. The Garonne in the valley, moving slowly in the evening light, the color of it exactly as he had remembered, the specific quality he had tried to describe to Margot standing at the Main in Frankfurt. It was the same. It was also different from the memory of it, because the memory had been carrying the weight of everything he was moving toward and the thing itself did not have to carry that weight.

He stood in the last of the light and looked at the river.

He thought about the list. Three pages, dense in the margins, in his coat pocket in the journal. He would keep the list. He would keep it for the rest of his life, because keeping it was the right thing to do, and because the names in it were real and had been real and the record should know they existed.

He thought about Margot, on the road east toward Posen, doing the work she was good at until she decided what came next. He thought about Mercier in the stable below, already assessing what needed to be done with the deliberate attention of a man who had found that he was approximately himself outside the army and was making his peace with that.

He thought about the plan. Five years, make Captain and go home. He had made Captain in two and a half years and had come home in three, which was not the plan exactly but was close enough that his father's instruction had been honored in the way his father had meant it: do what is required, develop what you have, come back and build something with it.

He had not built anything yet. That was what came next.

He looked at the fields in the evening light, the bare vines waiting for spring, the river moving in the valley below, the whole of the Bordeaux evening settling into its own quiet. He thought about what he knew now that he had not known when he left: what it cost to be good at destroying things, and what it meant to be ready to stop.

He thought about starting tomorrow.

The light went, the way light in the south went in March, not gradually but all at once, and he walked back down through the vineyards toward the house, where the windows were lit and Elise was visible through the glass at the table she always sat at, and his father's lamp was on in the library, and Mercier's light was in the stable, and the door was unlocked.

He went inside.
