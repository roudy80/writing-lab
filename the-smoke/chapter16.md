CHAPTER 16

The Frankfurt office had a view of the street that she had always found useful for thinking. Not the view itself, which was a cobbled street with a bakery on the far corner and a chandler's shop that had been there since before she could remember, but the movement: people going somewhere, goods being moved, the ordinary commerce of a city continuing on a Tuesday morning in the way it continued on all mornings.

She was twenty-four. She had been running the business alone for a year.

The business was not failing. This was a fact she had to remind herself of on the bad days, when the bad days made it feel like it was. The eastern route was running. The Portuguese leg was functional. She had four employees now, down from six, and all four were doing the work of six because she had made careful choices about who stayed. The accounts were in order. The business was not failing.

It was not what her father had built. The war had eaten too much of it: routes that had not been reestablished, partners who had found other arrangements in the years of disruption, a reputation in some corridors that had taken thirty years of her father's consistent presence to build and that she was rebuilding from a different starting point. She was good at this. The rebuilding was going well. She was also twenty-four, which meant she was doing the work of rebuilding something that had taken thirty years to build in the first place, with the full awareness that this was the work of decades and she was near the beginning.

She did not find this discouraging. She found it a problem to be addressed over time.

She addressed problems over time.

* * *

The Frankfurt merchant was named Kessler. He had a wool route that ran parallel to a route she was establishing for dry goods, and he had decided, three months earlier, to begin undercutting her on the prices along that corridor. Not dramatically: five percent, then eight, the kind of undercutting that eroded a position over time rather than attacking it directly.

She had watched this for two months before she moved.

She went to see him. She brought the accounts: what the corridor had been producing, what it was producing now, and an analysis of where Kessler's pricing advantage was coming from, which she had spent a week working out and which had a flaw in it that she had identified and which Kessler had not yet identified.

She laid this out on his desk with the matter-of-fact tone she had learned from watching her father lay things on desks. She was not angry. She was accurate.

She said: the flaw in your pricing model is the return leg. You are routing through Heidelberg on the return and absorbing the road costs, which work at the volume you have now. They will not work at the volume you need to sustain this price point at scale. Here is the calculation.

Kessler looked at the calculation. He was a careful man, not a dishonest one, and he saw the calculation was correct.

He proposed a partnership. She was not surprised. It was what the calculation was designed to make him propose.

They negotiated for two hours. She conceded two points he cared about and held three she cared about, and the agreement they reached was better for her than the situation she had walked in with, which was the objective.

She walked back to the office in the morning air and thought about what she had not conceded. She was good at knowing what to concede and what not to concede. Her father had taught her this. She was also good at knowing that being good at this negotiation did not mean she was not tired, and she was tired.

She sat down at the desk. She looked at the street.

She thought about Charles.

She had been thinking about Charles with less regularity since the garrison meeting, which was either progress or avoidance and she was not sure which. She knew what she had said. She had meant all of it. She still loved him, which was a fact that she had decided to stop being surprised by, because being surprised by it every time was inefficient.

She did not want a life of watching him do what he did and calling it love. She had said this. She had not unsaid it.

She also did not want a life that did not have him in it, which was a different statement. The two statements were both true and she had been living with their being simultaneously true for several months.

She picked up the pen.

She started a letter three times.

The first version was too long and said too much. She crossed it out.

The second version was too short and was not honest. She crossed it out.

The third version she stared at for a long time before she started writing it, because she understood what writing it meant and what it did not mean and she wanted to be precise about what she was actually saying.

She was not issuing an ultimatum. She was not asking him to choose between her and the army, which would be unfair and which she did not have the right to ask. She was stating a fact: she was building something real, with her life, and she was not willing to be the person who waited indefinitely at the edge of someone else's indefinite situation.

She was also not ending it. She was not capable of ending it, and honesty required acknowledging this.

She wrote:

I will not tell you what to do. I have never told you what to do and I do not intend to start. You are a grown man who has been making decisions under conditions I cannot fully comprehend, and I respect that.

But I am building something here. Something that is mine. Something I believe in. And I cannot build it indefinitely alongside the knowledge that what you do undoes what I do, and that you are not coming, and that the next season will be the same as this one.

I still love you. I say that with full awareness of what it means and what it does not mean. It does not mean I will wait forever. I cannot. I have a life to build, with or without you.

You know where to find me. You have always known.

If you want to be part of what I am building, come. Come when you can actually come, not when the season allows it and not when the paperwork clears. Come when you have decided to be somewhere other than where you are.

I will be here until I am not.

She sealed it before she could read it again.

She gave it to Hoffmann to add to the outgoing post.

She went back to the desk and opened the Kessler file and began drafting the formal agreement.

Outside, a cart moved along the street with a specific efficient sound of goods being taken somewhere they were needed. She did not look up.
