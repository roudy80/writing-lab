CHAPTER 2

The room smelled of woodsmoke and damp wool. Charles stood at the map table and tried to look like he had been doing this for years.

He had not.

Major Beaumont stood over the map: late forties, careful movements, a way of speaking that assumed he would not need to repeat himself. Charles had seen him briefly when he arrived six months ago, processing orders, but this was the first time he had been in the same room for any purpose that mattered.

"Austrian detachment moving north along this road." Beaumont's finger traced it. "Trying to link up with their main force. They will attempt to cross at Gunzburg, here. Small bridge, single span. You are going to stop them."

Charles studied the ground. The road ran through open country before narrowing at the crossing. Woods on the near side, clear ground beyond. A bottleneck between two low hills. If he positioned the section in the trees, he could take the Austrians as they tried to cross. Solid ground to hold. But if he moved fast, crossed early, took the far ridge, he could hit them in column before they knew he was there. He kept that thought to himself.

"How many, sir?"

"Twenty to thirty cavalry. Light horse, probably dragoons. They are not expecting opposition." Major Beaumont looked at him steadily. "You will have fifteen men. Get there before them. Drive them off if possible, delay them if not. Questions?"

"No, sir."

"Sergeant Mercier will accompany you. He knows the country." Beaumont rolled the map. "You leave within the hour."

* * *

The sky was overcast when Charles reached the paddock. The men were already there, moving through preparations with the ease of soldiers who knew their work. Six months of training had built something; he could see it in the way they moved.

Sergeant Mercier stood near the fence watching, arms folded, the impassive expression of a man who had seen everything twice. Sergeant Duval was working down the line of horses, checking straps and loads with methodical patience.

Charles found Tonnerre ready. The bay's ears were forward, coat brushed. Alert. They had built a real partnership over the months, the horse learning to read him, Charles learning to trust the horse's instincts.

"Morning, Lieutenant."

Lieutenant Philippe Arnaud was beside his own mount, adjusting his collar. Always adjusting his collar. He had that easy look about him, the comfort of a man for whom the social part of soldiering came without effort.

"Nervous?" Philippe asked.

"Should I be?"

"Probably." He tightened a saddle strap. "Sergeant Mercier's with you. That is good."

A young corporal was leading his horse past, too young for whatever age he had claimed when enlisting. "Morning, sirs."

"Rousseau." Charles nodded.

The boy's accent was thick Marseille, broad vowels that the other troopers sometimes could not follow. He grinned, eager. "First real mission, sir?"

"For both of us."

"We will show those Austrians."

"Check your equipment again before we mount," Charles said. "And your horse's hooves."

"Yes, sir." Rousseau hurried off.

Philippe watched him go. "Good kid. Green as spring grass."

"He will be fine," Charles said.

Philippe said nothing, which was its own kind of answer.

* * *

They rode out as the sun broke through morning cloud, fifteen men in column, Sergeant Mercier setting the pace at a steady walk through farmland and then into wooded country where the road narrowed.

After half an hour Charles rode forward to Mercier. "We should pick up the pace. Make sure we arrive with time to set up. Maybe catch them before they reach the bridge."

Sergeant Mercier glanced at him. "Horses can handle more, sir. Not too much. It is a fair distance yet."

"Move to a steady trot."

"As you say." Mercier passed the word.

The column picked up speed. Charles felt the satisfying rhythm of it, the section moving as a unit, Tonnerre reaching into the trot with that long, ground-covering stride.

An hour in, Sergeant Mercier dropped back alongside him. "Sir, might ease back a little. We have a ways to go yet."

Charles looked back down the column. The horses were working but not struggling. "They look fine to me, Sergeant."

"They do now." Mercier's tone was neutral. "Just thinking ahead. Tired horse at the start of a fight can mean the difference."

A flicker of irritation. The horses were cavalry mounts. This was what they were bred for. "We will hold this pace."

"As you say, sir." Something in Mercier's voice made Charles glance at him, but the sergeant's face was empty as he dropped back to his position.

* * *

They came through the trees and saw the river. Sergeant Mercier raised his hand. The column slowed to a walk.

"Bridge is ahead, sir. Three hundred meters through those trees. No sign of the Austrians yet."

Charles read the ground. The river was maybe thirty meters wide, shallow, the current visible over stones. A single stone arch, old Roman work by the look of it. The road approached from the west through open fields with no cover. On the far side, a low ridge with scattered trees. Good position if you got there first and the enemy did not know you were waiting.

He looked at the near side. Trees, ground he could hold, the bottleneck in his favor. The sensible position. Major Beaumont had said hold the bridge, drive them off if possible.

He looked at the far ridge.

"Dismount. Walk the horses, check your equipment."

The men dismounted with evident relief. Several began walking horses in slow circles. Sergeant Duval was checking legs and hooves, unhurried.

Charles walked Tonnerre and thought. If he crossed now, took the ridge, he could hit the Austrians in column before they were alert to the danger. Destroy the detachment rather than just driving it off. That was the better outcome, surely. That was what Major Beaumont actually wanted.

He looked at Sergeant Mercier, who was walking his own horse nearby. The sergeant met his eyes and said nothing.

"Mount up. We are crossing."

The men looked surprised, but they obeyed. Lieutenant Philippe rode up alongside. "Not setting up on this side?"

"Better position on the far ridge. We can see them coming, hit them before they are set."

Philippe nodded slowly. His expression stayed uncertain.

* * *

The bridge was hollow under their hooves, crossing in pairs, the sound carrying over the water. On the far side the road climbed a gentle rise and Charles pushed the section up it at a trot.

Tonnerre was breathing harder now. Charles noticed it and pushed the thought down.

He positioned the section just below the crest, out of silhouette, able to see the approach. "Dismount. Check your weapons."

Sergeant Duval was already running his hands down horse legs. Sergeant Mercier stood apart with his back to Charles, and something in the set of his shoulders was not approval.

"Movement, sir."

Sergeant Mercier had his hand shading his eyes. "On the road. Maybe a kilometer."

Charles stepped up beside him. A dark mass resolving into riders. Twenty, perhaps twenty-five. Austrian dragoons in column, blue coats clear even at distance. Still in column, no change of formation, unaware.

His mouth was dry. These were real soldiers. Real enemies with real sabers.

"Mount up. Form line."

The section pulled into saddles fast. The line spread across the road's width. Not parade-ground perfect, but solid.

Four hundred meters. Three hundred and fifty.

He could see individual faces now. A man with a thick mustache. A young one, younger than Rousseau. The column still moving forward, oblivious.

Two hundred meters.

"Sir." Sergeant Duval's voice, carefully neutral. "Your order?"

Charles swallowed. "Forward at the walk."

Fifteen horses stepped over the crest and started down the slope.

The Austrians saw them. Heads turned. Horses pulling up short, a shout in German.

"Draw sabers. Trot."

His own blade rasped out of the scabbard as the line picked up speed. Down the line, fourteen more cleared leather with that distinctive ring. One hundred and fifty meters. One hundred and twenty.

The Austrians were scrambling to deploy. Some fumbling with weapons.

One hundred meters.

"Charge."

* * *

Tonnerre surged and the Austrian line rushed toward him. Blue coats and pale faces and steel.

A carbine cracked. Something whistled past with a high thin whine.

Then they hit.

The impact jolted through his whole body. Tonnerre slammed into an Austrian horse, both animals staggering. Charles's saber came up and across, training moving his arm, and connected with something that gave way with a wet sound that turned his stomach. A man screamed.

The smoke came. Gun-smoke from carbines and pistols rolling over them thick and acrid. He could not see more than a horse-length through it. His eyes watered. His throat burned. Friend from enemy only by coat color when they appeared through the murk.

Sounds layered: men shouting in French and German, horses screaming, steel on steel, the wet impact of blades finding flesh.

Tonnerre turned beneath him on instinct, responding to leg pressure Charles did not remember giving. The horse operating on old battle reflexes from his previous rider.

Something hit Charles's shoulder, hard. Not a blade but a glancing blow. He nearly lost his seat, caught himself with his knees.

His saber was moving. Slash and recover. He felt resistance, heard screaming, kept moving because stopping meant dying.

The smoke thinned. The road visible now, individual combats scattered across it. Corporal Rousseau, young face set fierce, trading blows with an older Austrian. Lieutenant Philippe, still mounted, pressing an attack with that casual competence he brought to everything.

And on the ground: an Austrian trooper not moving, blood dark on trampled grass.

Charles forced his eyes away.

"Sir. Behind you."

An Austrian was charging from his flank, saber raised. Charles brought his blade up too slow, but Tonnerre sidled and the Austrian's swing missed by inches. Charles cut back wild. His saber caught the man's arm and the Austrian howled, wheeling away.

Charles sat there gasping. His right arm ached from gripping the hilt. Tonnerre stood with sides heaving, head dropping.

Around him the fighting was breaking up. The Austrians withdrawing, spurring back down the road. The French were not pursuing. Could not pursue. The horses were spent.

* * *

He counted his men. Most still mounted. Corporal Rousseau had blood on his sleeve but was upright. Sergeant Duval seemed unhurt. Lieutenant Philippe was dismounted, holding his horse by the reins while it stood on three legs.

"Casualties," Sergeant Mercier called. "Call off."

They responded one by one. Ferrand with a broken arm, thrown when his horse stumbled. Two with cuts, neither serious. Three horses lame including Philippe's.

One man did not respond.

Several troopers had gathered around something on the ground. A man face-down. His shako was gone. Blood dark on the back of his coat.

"Who is it?" Charles asked. His voice came out strange.

"Private Moreau, sir." Sergeant Duval's tone was flat. "Took a cut to the back. Bled out before we could reach him."

Moreau. The thick Marseille accent that made the other troopers grin. The habit of checking his carbine three times before mounting, no matter how many times Sergeant Duval told him once was enough. The letter he had been showing around last week, a son named Jean-Claude, has your eyes, everyone says so.

The smell hit: blood and voided bowels and churned earth. Charles's stomach lurched but he swallowed it down.

"Sir." Sergeant Mercier, quiet. "We need to secure the area. They may regroup."

"Pickets on the ridge. Sergeant Duval, see to the wounded. Philippe."

"My horse is lame but I can help." Lieutenant Philippe was already moving.

"I will take care of him, sir," Sergeant Mercier said. "Check on the rest of the men."

Charles made himself walk the line. Speaking to each man, checking wounds, projecting a steadiness he did not feel. The men seemed functional. Shaken, but functional.

He reached Philippe last. "Your horse?"

"Strained tendon. He will need leading back." Lieutenant Philippe looked down the road where the Austrians had gone. "They are not coming back."

"We drove them off."

"We did." Neutral. "Scattered them pretty well."

Charles walked back to Tonnerre. The bay stood with his head low, neck dark with sweat. Charles ran a hand down it, feeling the exhaustion in the horse under his palm.

If he had held on the near side. If he had listened when Sergeant Mercier mentioned the pace.

"Sir." Mercier, behind him. "We need to move. Get back across. The horses need water and rest."

"Yes. Form up the men."

"One more thing, sir." Very quiet. "We will need to bring Private Moreau. And his effects for his family."

Something cold settled in Charles's chest. "I will see to it."

He knelt beside Moreau's body and made himself look. A few coins, a worn letter, a wooden cross on a cord.

He should not read it. He read it anyway.

My dearest Andre, the baby came three days ago. A boy, healthy and strong. He has your eyes, everyone says so. I have named him Jean-Claude, after your father...

Charles folded the letter and put it in his own pocket with the other effects. He stood up and walked away before his face could betray him.

* * *

They reached the near treeline by late afternoon, the horses in poor shape, the section moving slowly with frequent stops. Sergeant Mercier organized the withdrawal with quiet efficiency. Moreau's body wrapped and secured. The wounded on the steadiest animals. Lookouts watching the ridge as they crossed back over the bridge.

In the treeline Sergeant Mercier called a halt. "We will rest here until the horses can travel. Sergeant Duval, set watches. The rest of you, see to your mounts."

Charles dismounted. Tonnerre stood with his head nearly to his knees. Charles loosened the girth, pulled the saddle carefully, checked the legs. The left foreleg was warm. Not terrible, but swollen. Strained. Charles had pushed too hard.

"Lieutenant."

Major Beaumont stood a few paces away. He must have ridden out when they did not return on schedule.

"Sir."

"Walk with me."

In the trees, out of earshot, Major Beaumont stopped. "Report."

Charles gave it: the ride to the bridge, the decision to cross and take the far ridge, the engagement, one dead, three wounded, several horses lame, the Austrians scattered.

Beaumont was quiet for a long moment.

"You pushed the horses hard getting there."

"Yes, sir."

"Sergeant Mercier mentioned concerns about the pace. You continued anyway."

"I wanted to be in position to catch them in the open, sir."

"And you did. You drove them off." Major Beaumont's voice was measured, not condemning, not approving. "But they escaped. Scattered, yes, but they will regroup. You had tired horses that could not pursue, could not finish what you started."

Charles said nothing.

"You accomplished the mission. The bridge is held, the Austrians are scattered and delayed. That counts for something." A pause. "But a patient officer, one who had rested his horses properly and set up on the near side and let the enemy come to him, might have destroyed that detachment completely. As it stands they will be back in action within days."

"Yes, sir."

"Private Moreau is dead. Men die in combat, and you cannot prevent every casualty. But with fresher horses, with the better position, maybe you end the fight faster. Maybe Moreau is still alive. Maybe not. We cannot know. What we do know is that you had a chance to eliminate an enemy unit completely, and you gave it up because you were impatient."

Charles's throat was tight. "I understand, sir."

"Your sergeants tried to tell you. Did you listen?"

"No, sir. Not enough."

"Then listen next time." Major Beaumont's tone softened slightly. Not much. "You will write to Moreau's widow. You will tell her he died serving France, that his sacrifice mattered. And you will learn from this, that the unglamorous work of horse care and proper pacing is not separate from winning battles. It is how you win battles." He turned to leave. "Get your section home safely. This was your first real command. You will make mistakes. The question is whether you learn from them."

He left Charles standing alone in the trees.

* * *

They reached the forward post after dark. The exhausted section filed through in silence. Moreau's wrapped body drew quiet looks from the men they passed.

Sergeant Mercier and Sergeant Duval handled the horses. The wounded went to the surgeon. Moreau's body to the makeshift mortuary.

When everything was done Charles went to the tent he shared with Lieutenant Philippe.

Philippe was sitting on his bedroll. "Hell of a first command."

Charles sat heavily. The tent smelled of damp canvas and woodsmoke.

"We should have destroyed them completely," Charles said. "If I had listened to Sergeant Mercier about the horses. If we had held the near side."

"Maybe. Or maybe they see us in the trees and never commit. Or the fight goes differently and more of us get hurt." Philippe lay back. "Combat is unpredictable. You cannot know every outcome."

"I can listen to experienced NCOs when they give me good advice."

"Yes," Philippe said. "You can do that."

Charles pulled off his boots. His jacket. The uniform still smelled of powder and blood.

He should eat. Should wash. Should start the letter to Moreau's widow.

He lay down and stared at the tent canvas until exhaustion took him.

* * *

In the morning Charles found Sergeant Mercier in the paddock. Tonnerre was in the corner, left foreleg wrapped with a fresh poultice.

"How is he, Sergeant?"

"He will mend. Strained tendon, nothing torn. Needs rest and easy work for a week or two."

The bay nuzzled his pocket when Charles came close. The familiar gesture tightened something in his chest.

"Sergeant. About yesterday. I drew my saber when I gave the trot command. Should I have waited?"

Sergeant Mercier considered it, moving to the next horse as he spoke. "In a small action like yesterday, it does not matter much. But in a larger battle you do not want to signal your commitment until the last possible moment. Keep them guessing. Make them think you might wheel away. Then when you are close enough that they cannot react, that is when you draw."

"I showed my hand too early."

"In yesterday's fight it did not matter. But it is good practice for when it does." Mercier glanced at him. "You are asking because you are thinking about what you could have done better?"

"Yes."

"That is good. Shows you are learning." He returned to his work. "The most important thing from yesterday was not the saber drill, though."

"The horses."

"The horses," Sergeant Mercier agreed. "Everything else stems from that. Fresh horses and you can pursue, finish decisively. Tired horses and the enemy escapes."

"I pushed too hard on the approach."

"You did. I mentioned it. You kept the pace anyway."

Charles said nothing.

Sergeant Mercier set down his tools and turned to face him directly. The shift was deliberate. Sergeants did not usually look at officers quite like this.

"Sir, what I am about to say I would not say in front of the men. Chain of command matters. But here, now: you need to hear this."

"Go ahead, Sergeant."

"You think fast, you are not afraid to act, you care about the men. But yesterday you made a mistake that cost us a complete victory. The Austrians scattered but they will regroup. With fresh horses we could have run them down, taken or killed the lot. Instead they escaped because our mounts were blown."

Charles felt heat in his face. He listened.

"You asked about the Italian campaign before. About those long marches. It worked because we understood that cavalry without horses is just infantry with spurs. You take care of the basics or you lose your ability to fight. That is not a complex lesson, sir. But you have to actually believe it to follow it when the moment comes."

"I believe it now."

Mercier studied him for a moment.

"See that it stays with you," he said, and went back to the horses.

* * *

Charles wrote the letter to Moreau's widow that afternoon, sitting on an ammunition crate outside the surgeon's tent with the sounds of the camp around him.

He wrote four drafts and burned three of them. The fourth he sealed without rereading.

It said that Moreau had died in service of France, that he had been a good soldier, that his death had mattered. All of it was true. None of it was the whole truth. He did not know how to put the whole truth into a letter to a woman he had never met, about a decision he had made, and a son who would grow up with a dead man's eyes.

He put the letter with the effects in his breast pocket. He would give them to Major Beaumont tomorrow to send through the proper channels.

The camp settled into evening around him. Somewhere in the lines a horse was being walked, slow hoofbeats in the dark. Charles sat where he was for a while longer, not quite ready to go back to the tent.

Making Captain meant getting men killed along the way.

Nobody had mentioned that part.
