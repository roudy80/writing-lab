CHAPTER 2

The forward post in Baden was nothing like the garrison at Strasbourg. No stone buildings, no proper stables—just a commandeered inn, some hastily erected tents, and a paddock for the horses. They'd been here a week, part of the cavalry screen pushing east ahead of the Grande Armée's advance into Bavaria. The Austrians were retreating, trying to concentrate their scattered forces before Napoleon could destroy them piecemeal, and the French cavalry's job was to find them, fix them, and report their movements.
This morning, Charles's section had finally been given a mission that mattered.
He stood in what had once been the inn's common room, now serving as headquarters. Major Beaumont—the same officer who'd processed his orders back in the spring—stood over a map spread across a rough wooden table. Two other officers Charles recognized from the garrison flanked him. The room smelled of woodsmoke and damp wool.
"Leblanc." Beaumont gestured him forward without preamble. "Austrian detachment is moving north along this road—" he tapped the map "—trying to link up with their main force. Intelligence says they'll attempt to cross the Danube here, at Günzburg. Small bridge, single span. You're going to stop them."
Charles stepped closer, studying the map. The road ran through relatively open country before narrowing at the bridge crossing. Woods on the near side, clear ground beyond.
"How many, sir?"
"Twenty to thirty cavalry. Light horse, probably dragoons. They're not expecting opposition—they think our main body is still concentrated near Ulm." Beaumont looked at him. "You'll have fifteen men. Get there before them, hold the bridge. Drive them off if possible, delay them if not. Questions?"
Charles studied the terrain. The road approached the bridge through a bottleneck between two low hills. If he positioned his men in the woods on the near side, he could hit the Austrians as they tried to cross. A solid plan. But if he moved faster, pushed harder, he might catch them before they even reached the bridge—hit them in column on the approach road, scatter them completely. He kept the thought to himself.
"No, sir."
"Sergeant Mercier will accompany you. He knows the country." Beaumont rolled up the map. "You leave within the hour. Dismissed."

The sky was overcast when Charles reached the paddock, threatening rain. Men were already there, moving through their preparations with the easy efficiency of soldiers who knew their work. Charles recognized most of them after six months of training together.
A grizzled sergeant stood near the paddock fence, watching the preparations with the impassive expression of a man who'd seen everything twice. Sergeant Mercier—the NCO who'd mentored Charles after that embarrassing first day at Strasbourg, teaching him the practical lessons that didn't come from books.
"Morning, Sergeant. The men ready?"
"Near enough, sir. Duval's doing final checks on the loads now."
Charles spotted Sergeant Duval across the paddock—steady, reliable, the kind of NCO who quietly kept everything functioning. He was checking saddle straps and saddlebags with methodical precision.
Charles found Tonnerre already prepared. The bay gelding looked alert, ears forward, ready to work. Six months together had built a real partnership.
"Morning, Lieutenant."
Charles turned. Lieutenant Philippe Arnaud stood beside his own mount, adjusting his equipment with quick, practiced movements. Philippe—the one who'd helped Charles navigate the social minefield of officer life, who somehow made everything look easy. They'd become friends over the past months.
"Nervous?" Philippe asked with that easy grin.
"Should I be?"
"Probably. But you'll do fine." Philippe tightened a strap on his saddle. "Mercier's with you—that's good. Man knows his business."
A young corporal led his horse past, couldn't be more than seventeen despite whatever age he'd claimed when enlisting. "Morning, sirs," he said, voice eager.
"Morning, Corporal Rousseau," Charles replied. The kid volunteered for everything, desperate to prove himself. Reminded Charles of himself six months ago, actually.
"First real mission, sir?" Rousseau asked.
"For both of us, Corporal."
Rousseau grinned. "We'll show those Austrians, sir."
"That's the spirit," Philippe said. "Now go check your equipment again. And your horse's hooves."
"Yes, sir." Rousseau hurried off.
Philippe lowered his voice. "Good kid. Green as spring grass, but eager."
"Aren't we all," Charles muttered.

They rode out as the sun broke through the morning clouds, fifteen men in a loose column following Mercier onto the road leading south. Charles rode near the front with Philippe beside him. Behind them, Corporal Rousseau sat his saddle with exaggerated straightness. Sergeant Duval rode further back, his experienced eyes constantly scanning the column.
The road led through farmland initially, past fields being harvested and small stone farmhouses. The country grew more wooded after a few kilometers, the road narrowing.
Mercier set a good pace—steady but not rushed. Charles found himself relaxing into Tonnerre's gait as the nervousness of departure faded.
But they needed to move faster. If they arrived with time to spare, they could set up properly. Or better yet, catch the Austrians before they even reached the bridge.
Charles urged Tonnerre forward until he rode alongside Mercier. "Sergeant, we should pick up the pace."
Mercier glanced at him. "Any particular reason, sir?"
"Make sure we're in position with time to spare. Maybe catch them before they reach the bridge."
"The horses can handle a bit more," Mercier acknowledged. "Not too much, though. It's a fair distance yet."
"Let's move to a steady trot."
Mercier passed the word, and the column picked up speed.

They'd been riding hard for over an hour when Mercier dropped back to ride alongside Charles. "Sir, might want to ease back a bit. We've got a ways to go yet."
Charles looked back at the column. The horses were working, but not struggling. "They look fine to me, Sergeant."
"They do now, sir." Mercier's tone was neutral. "Just thinking ahead. A tired horse at the start of a fight can mean the difference."
Charles felt a flicker of irritation. They were making good time. The horses were cavalry mounts—this was what they were bred for. "We'll hold this pace. The bridge can't be much farther."
"As you say, sir." Something in Mercier's tone made Charles glance at him, but the sergeant's face was expressionless as he dropped back to his position.

When they finally caught sight of the river through the trees, Charles felt a surge of satisfaction. They'd made excellent time. Tonnerre was breathing harder than at the start, but still responsive.
Mercier, riding point, raised his hand. The column slowed to a walk.
"Bridge is visible through those trees, sir." Mercier pointed. "Maybe three hundred meters. No sign of the Austrians yet."
Charles's heart beat faster. They'd made it. They were here first.
"Dismount," Charles ordered. "Walk the horses, check your equipment. We'll move up to position in a few minutes."
The men dismounted with evident relief. Several immediately began walking their horses in slow circles. Charles saw Sergeant Duval checking legs and hooves with his usual thoroughness.
Charles walked Tonnerre, thinking. The bridge was just ahead, through those trees. And beyond the bridge, the approach road where the Austrians would be coming. If his section crossed now, they could catch the enemy in column before they were alert to the danger.
He looked at Mercier, who was also walking his horse. The sergeant met his eyes, seemed to read his thoughts, but said nothing.
Charles made his decision. "Mount up. We're crossing the bridge."
Several of the men looked surprised, but they obeyed. Philippe rode up beside Charles. "We're not setting up on this side?"
"Better position on the far ridge. We can see them coming, hit them before they're ready."
Philippe nodded slowly, though his expression was uncertain.
They emerged from the treeline at a trot. The bridge lay ahead—old stone, Roman construction, single arch spanning the river. Charles's section crossed in pairs, hoofbeats hollow on stone.
On the far side, the road climbed a gentle rise. "We'll take position on that ridge," Charles decided. "We'll see them coming, catch them in the open."
They climbed. Tonnerre was breathing hard now, but still moving well. Charles positioned his men in a loose line just below the crest, where they wouldn't be silhouetted but could still see the approach.
"Dismount. Check your weapons."
The men dismounted and began their preparations. Charles saw Duval running his hands down his horse's legs, checking for problems. Mercier stood slightly apart, his back to Charles, but something in his posture suggested disapproval.
Charles walked Tonnerre in slow circles. The bay's breathing was heavier than he liked, but they were in position now. When the Austrians appeared, his men would be ready.
"Movement, sir."
Mercier was standing at the edge of their position, hand shading his eyes. "On the road. Maybe a kilometer out."
Charles joined him, straining to see. Yes—there. A dark mass resolving into individual riders. Twenty, maybe twenty-five horsemen in column. Austrian dragoons, blue coats visible even at this distance.
His heartbeat quickened. This was real.
"Mount up. Form line."
The men moved quickly, pulling themselves into saddles. The line formed—fifteen riders spread across the road's width. Not perfect, but good enough.
The Austrians were closer now. Four hundred meters. Three hundred and fifty. They hadn't changed formation, hadn't seen the French cavalry waiting.
Charles's mouth was dry. These were real soldiers down there. Real enemies.
Three hundred meters.
Two hundred and fifty.
Charles could see individual faces now—young men, old men, someone with a thick mustache. Real people. The column was still moving forward, oblivious.
Two hundred meters.
"Sir?" That was Duval, voice carefully neutral. "Your order?"
Charles swallowed. "Forward at the walk."
The line moved. Fifteen horses stepped forward, cresting the ridge, starting down the slope.
The Austrians saw them. Heads turned, sudden confusion, horses pulling up short. Someone shouted an order in German.
"Draw sabers! Trot!"
Charles's own blade rasped free as the line picked up speed. Down the line, fourteen more sabers cleared leather with that distinctive ring. One hundred and fifty meters. One hundred and twenty.
The Austrians were scrambling, trying to deploy. Some fumbled with weapons.
One hundred meters.
"Charge!"
Tonnerre surged into a gallop. The Austrian line rushed toward him—blue coats and pale faces and the glint of steel.
A carbine cracked. Something whistled past Charles's head with a high, thin whine.
Then they hit.
The impact jolted through his whole body. Tonnerre slammed into an Austrian horse, both animals staggering. Charles's saber came up and across—pure training—and connected with something that gave way with a wet sound that made his stomach turn. A man screamed.
The smoke came.
Gun-smoke from carbines and pistols, rolling over them thick and acrid. Charles couldn't see more than a horse-length through the choking fog. His eyes watered. His throat burned. He couldn't tell friend from enemy except by coat color when they appeared through the murk.
Sounds layered: men shouting in French and German, horses screaming, steel on steel, hoofbeats and curses and that wet impact of blades finding flesh.
Tonnerre moved beneath him, turning, responding to leg pressure Charles didn't remember giving. The horse was operating on instinct now, old battle reflexes from his previous rider.
Something hit Charles's shoulder—hard. Not a blade, but a glancing blow. He nearly lost his seat, caught himself by gripping with his knees.
His saber was moving—slash and recover, slash and recover. He felt resistance, heard screaming, kept moving because stopping meant dying.
The smoke thinned. Charles could see the road now, French dragoons scattered in individual combats. There—Corporal Rousseau, young face set with fierce concentration, trading blows with an older Austrian. There—Philippe, still mounted somehow, pressing an attack with that casual competence he brought to everything.
And there—an Austrian trooper on the ground, not moving, blood dark on trampled grass.
Charles's stomach clenched, but he forced his eyes away.
"Sir! Behind you!"
Charles turned. An Austrian dragoon was charging from his flank, saber raised. Charles brought his blade up—too slow—but Tonnerre sidled and the Austrian's swing missed by inches.
Charles cut back wildly. His saber caught the Austrian's arm and the man howled, wheeling away clutching his wound.
Charles sat there gasping, heart hammering. His right arm ached from gripping the saber. Tonnerre stood beneath him, sides heaving, head down.
Around him, the fighting was breaking up. The Austrians were withdrawing, spurring their horses back down the road. The French weren't pursuing—couldn't pursue. Their horses were spent.
Charles looked around, counting. Most of his men were still mounted. Corporal Rousseau had blood on his sleeve but was upright. Sergeant Duval seemed unhurt. Philippe was—where was Philippe?
There. Dismounted, holding his horse's reins while the animal stood on three legs. Philippe looked up, caught Charles's eye, shrugged.
"Casualties," Mercier called out, voice hoarse. "Call off."
The men responded one by one. One broken arm—a trooper named Ferrand thrown when his horse stumbled. Two men with cuts, neither serious. Three horses lame, including Philippe's.
And one man who didn't respond.
Charles dismounted on shaking legs. Several men had gathered around something—someone—on the ground. A trooper lying face-down. His shako was gone. Blood dark on the back of his coat.
"Who is it?" Charles asked, voice strange in his own ears.
"Private Moreau, sir." Sergeant Duval's tone was flat. "Took a cut to the back. Bled out before we could reach him."
Private André Moreau. Twenty-two. Married. Had a baby he'd never met. Charles had seen his letter just last week—the private showing it around proudly, a son named Jean-Claude.
The smell hit him—blood and voided bowels and churned earth. Charles's stomach lurched but he swallowed hard, forced it down.
"Sir." Mercier's hand on his shoulder. "We need to secure the area. The Austrians might regroup."
Charles nodded numbly. "Right. Pickets on the ridge. Duval, see to the wounded. Philippe—"
"My horse is lame but I'm fine." Philippe was walking over. "I can help with the others."
"Do that." Charles looked at Moreau's body, throat tight.
"I'll take care of him, sir," Mercier said quietly. "Check on the rest of the men."
Charles forced himself to walk the line, speaking to each trooper, checking wounds, trying to project confidence he didn't feel. The men seemed steady. Shaken, but functional.
When he reached Philippe: "Your horse?"
"Strained tendon. He'll need to be led back." Philippe looked down the road where the Austrians had fled. "They're not coming back."
"We drove them off," Charles said.
"We did." Philippe's tone was neutral. "Scattered them pretty well."
Charles walked back to where Tonnerre stood, head hanging low. He ran his hand down the horse's neck, feeling lather, feeling exhaustion.
If he'd rested them properly at the treeline. If he'd listened to Mercier's concerns. Would they have caught more of the Austrians? Would the enemy have escaped at all?
"Sir."
Mercier stood nearby. "We need to move. Get back across the bridge. The horses need water and rest."
"Yes." Charles straightened. "Form up the men."
"One more thing, sir." Mercier's voice was very quiet. "We'll need to bring Private Moreau. And his personal effects for his family."
Charles felt something cold settle in his chest. "I'll see to it."
He knelt beside Moreau's body, forcing himself to look. The private's pockets held a few coins, a worn letter, a wooden cross on a cord.
The letter was addressed to Moreau. Charles knew he shouldn't read it, but he needed to know.
My dearest André,
The baby came three days ago. A boy, healthy and strong. He has your eyes—everyone says so. I've named him Jean-Claude, after your father. He cries at night but settles when I hold him...
Charles carefully folded the letter and put it in his own pocket with the other effects. A son who would never know his father. A wife waiting for news that would break her heart.
He stood and walked away before his face could betray him.

They made it back across the bridge by late afternoon. The horses were in bad shape—several limping, all exhausted. The section moved slowly, stopping frequently.
Mercier organized everything with quiet efficiency. Lookouts stayed on the ridge during the withdrawal. Moreau's body was wrapped and secured. The wounded rode the steadiest animals.
When they reached the treeline on the near side—the position they should have held—Mercier called a halt.
"We'll rest here until the horses recover enough. Duval, set watches. The rest of you, see to your mounts."
Charles dismounted. Tonnerre stood with his head nearly to his knees. Charles loosened the girth, pulled the saddle off carefully, checked the horse's legs.
The left foreleg was warm. Not terrible, but swollen. Strained, probably. Charles had pushed too hard.
"Lieutenant."
Charles looked up. Major Beaumont stood a few paces away, must have ridden out when they didn't return on schedule.
"Sir." Charles straightened.
"Walk with me."
Charles followed Beaumont into the trees, out of earshot.
When they were alone, Beaumont stopped. "Report."
Charles did. The ride to the bridge. The decision to cross and take the far ridge. The engagement. One dead, three wounded, several horses lame. The Austrians scattered.
Beaumont was silent for a long moment. "You pushed your horses hard getting there."
"Yes, sir."
"Sergeant Mercier mentioned concerns about the pace. You continued anyway."
"I wanted to be in position to catch them in the open, sir."
"And you did. You drove them off." Beaumont's voice was measured, not quite approving but not condemning either. "But they escaped. Scattered, yes, but they'll regroup. You had tired horses that couldn't pursue, couldn't finish what you started."
Charles felt his face heat. "Yes, sir."
"You accomplished the mission, Lieutenant. The bridge is held. The Austrians are scattered and delayed. That counts for something." Beaumont paused. "But a more patient officer—one who'd rested his horses properly, who'd set up on the near side and let the enemy come to him—might have destroyed that detachment completely. Captured them, killed them, prevented any regrouping. As it stands, they'll be back in action within days."
Charles's throat was tight. "Yes, sir."
"Private Moreau is dead. Men die in combat—that's the nature of war, and you can't prevent every casualty. But with fresher horses, with a better position, maybe you'd have ended the fight faster. Maybe Private Moreau would still be writing letters to his wife about that new son of his. Or maybe not—we can't know. What we do know is that you had a chance to eliminate an enemy unit completely, and you gave up that advantage because you were impatient."
"I understand, sir."
"Do you?" Beaumont's expression was stern but not unkind. "You're not a bad officer, Leblanc. You think tactically, you're not afraid to act, your men followed you without hesitation. But tactics without logistics fails. Aggression without preparation costs opportunities. You pushed hard to get into position for a decisive strike, but gave up the ability to deliver it." He paused. "Your sergeants tried to tell you. Did you listen?"
"No, sir. Not enough."
"Then listen next time." Beaumont's tone softened slightly. "You'll write to Moreau's widow. You'll tell her he died serving France, that his sacrifice mattered. And you'll learn from this—that the unglamorous work of horse care and proper pacing isn't separate from winning battles. It's how you win battles." He turned to leave, then stopped. "Get your section home safely. And Lieutenant? This was your first real command. You'll make mistakes. The question is whether you learn from them."
He left Charles standing there alone.

They reached the forward post after dark. The exhausted section filed through in silence, Moreau's wrapped body drawing quiet looks.
Charles saw to the horses—or tried to, but Mercier and Duval had it handled. The wounded went to the surgeon. Moreau's body to the makeshift mortuary.
When everything was done, Charles finally made his way to the tent he shared with Philippe.
Philippe was already there, sitting on his bedroll. "Hell of a first command."
Charles sat heavily. He felt hollowed out.
"We drove them off," Philippe said quietly. "Scattered them pretty well. Beaumont seemed... not happy, but not furious either."
"We should have destroyed them completely," Charles said. "If I'd listened to Mercier about the horses. If we'd set up on the near side like we should have."
"Maybe. Or maybe they'd have seen us and never committed. Or maybe the fight goes differently and more of us get hurt." Philippe lay back. "Combat's unpredictable. You can't know every outcome."
"But I can listen to experienced NCOs when they give me good advice."
"Yes," Philippe agreed. "You can do that."
Charles nodded. He pulled off his boots, his jacket. The uniform still smelled like smoke and blood.
He should eat. Should wash. Should write that letter.
But instead he just lay down and stared at the tent canvas until exhaustion took him.

The next morning, Charles went looking for Sergeant Mercier.
He found him in the paddock, checking horses. Tonnerre was in a corner, left foreleg wrapped with fresh poultice showing beneath the bandage.
"How is he, Sergeant?"
"He'll mend. Strained tendon, nothing torn. Needs rest and easy walking for a week or two."
Charles stepped closer to Tonnerre. The bay nuzzled his pocket. The familiar gesture made his chest tight.
"Sergeant, about yesterday. The charge."
Mercier looked at him. "Sir?"
"I drew my saber at... when I gave the trot command. Ordered the men to draw at the same time."
"You did." Mercier's tone was neutral, waiting.
"Was that... should I have waited?"
Mercier considered this. "In a small action like yesterday? Doesn't matter much. But in a larger battle, you don't want to signal your commitment until the last possible moment." He moved to check another horse as he spoke. "Keep the enemy guessing. Make them think you might wheel away, might be a feint. Then when you're close enough that they can't react—that's when you draw and charge."
Charles nodded slowly. "So I showed my hand too early."
"Like I said, sir, in yesterday's fight it didn't matter. But it's good practice for when it does." Mercier glanced at him. "You asking because you're thinking about what you could have done better?"
"Yes, Sergeant."
"That's good. Shows you're learning." Mercier returned to his work. "Most important thing from yesterday wasn't the saber drill, though."
"The horses."
"The horses," Mercier agreed. "Everything else stems from that. Fresh horses mean you can pursue, can finish the fight decisively. Tired horses mean the enemy escapes."
Charles felt the weight of it. "I pushed too hard on the approach."
"You did." Mercier's voice was matter-of-fact, not judgmental. "I mentioned it. You kept the pace anyway."
"You were right. I should have listened."
Mercier was quiet for a moment. Then he set down his tools and turned to face Charles directly. Charles noticed the shift immediately—sergeants didn't usually look at officers quite this directly, didn't speak quite this frankly.
"Sir, what I'm about to say, I'd never say in front of the men. That's not how it works. Chain of command matters, discipline matters. But here, now, just us?" Mercier paused. "You need to hear this."
Charles straightened slightly. "Go ahead, Sergeant."
"You're learning, and that's good. You think fast, you're not afraid to act, you care about the men. But yesterday you made a mistake that cost us a complete victory. The Austrians scattered, yes, but they'll regroup. If we'd had fresh horses, we could have run them down, captured or killed the lot. Instead they escaped because our mounts were blown."
Charles felt heat in his face but forced himself to listen.
"You asked about the Italian campaign before. About how we managed those long marches." Mercier's voice was firm but not unkind. "It's because we understood that cavalry without horses is just infantry with spurs. You take care of the basics—food, rest, maintenance—or you lose your ability to fight effectively."
"I understand that now."
"Do you?" Mercier studied him. "Because understanding it here, after the fact, is different from remembering it in the moment when you're eager to get into position, when you think pushing a bit harder will give you an advantage."
Charles swallowed. "You're right. I knew you were concerned about the pace, and I dismissed it. I thought I knew better."
"And now?"
"Now I know I don't." Charles met the sergeant's eyes. "Next time, I'll listen."
Mercier nodded slowly. Something in his expression shifted—still serious, but perhaps satisfied. "Good. That's what I needed to hear." He picked up his tools again, and just like that, the unusual directness was gone. His tone returned to the normal sergeant-to-officer formality. "Now you've got a letter to write, sir. Best not to put it off."
Charles understood the shift. The moment of frank correction was over. They were back to their proper roles. But the lesson had been delivered, and Charles had heard it.
"Thank you, Sergeant."
Mercier nodded without looking up from his work, already moving to the next stall.

Charles sat at the camp desk with paper and ink. For a long time he just stared at the blank page.
Finally he began:
Madame Moreau,
It is my painful duty to inform you that your husband, Private André Moreau, was killed in action on October 8th, 1805, during an engagement with Austrian forces near Günzburg...
The words came slowly. How did you tell a woman her husband was dead? How did you explain that her son would grow up without a father?
Charles wrote that André had fought with courage. That he had been respected by his comrades. That he had served France well, that the mission succeeded, that his sacrifice had meaning.
It was all true. But it felt incomplete. The truth was more complicated—that a more patient officer might have achieved more, might have brought everyone home. But you couldn't put that in a letter to a widow.
When he finished, he folded it carefully and sealed it with wax. He sat there holding it, looking at the address.
Madame Jeanne Moreau
 Rue Saint-Martin
 Lyon
A woman waiting for news of her husband. A baby who would never know his father.
This was war. This was what he'd signed up for. The path to glory and honor and making the Leblanc name mean something.
It didn't feel glorious. It felt like responsibility, heavy and cold.
Eventually he stood and walked to the adjutant's tent to send the letter. It was his duty now. That's what being an officer meant.
Outside, the autumn sun was shining. The forward post continued its routine—men drilling, horses being exercised, life going on.
Private André Moreau was dead.
And Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc had learned his first real lesson about command: that competence required more than tactical thinking. It required patience, preparation, and listening to those who knew better.
The difference between a good outcome and a great one often came down to those unglamorous details.
He was beginning to understand that now.
