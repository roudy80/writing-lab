CHAPTER 5
The Italian campaign did not end at the Tagliamento. It kept going, the way campaigns did, the army moving southeast through the summer heat, one engagement becoming the next with barely enough time between them to bury the dead and rest the horses.
Charles kept a record in the back of his field journal, not a diary, just dates and locations and the names of men lost or wounded. By August the list was two pages long. He was not sure why he kept it. He told himself it was practical, that the names mattered for the official record. He did not examine the other reasons closely.
The work of it was what he had not expected. Not the fighting, which was terrible and also contained within itself a clarity that ordinary life did not have. The work between the fighting: the requisition forms, the horse inspections, the discipline cases, the endless correspondence with supply officers about rations and forage, the mapping of roads that turned out to be tracks, the tracking of men who were sick or lame or drunk or simply not where they were supposed to be. He had thought of cavalry as motion and violence. Mostly it was administration.
Sergeant Duval handled most of the administration, had handled it before Charles arrived and would handle it after, but Charles was learning the structure of it, the way the paperwork connected to the reality it described, the way a ration count on paper became hungry men or fed ones depending on whether the numbers were right. He found he was good at it. This did not surprise him. He had grown up tracking the estate accounts, harvest yields, the cost of cooperage against the price of wine in a good year and a bad one.
What surprised him was that being good at administration felt like being good at something small. At eighteen he had imagined himself in the smoke, blade out, leading charges that would be remembered. At nineteen he spent Tuesday afternoons arguing with supply sergeants about whether three kilos of oats per horse was adequate given the distances being covered. It was adequate. He was right. The horses were better for it. Nobody would remember it.

* * *

There was a second engagement in July, a rearguard action outside a small town whose name Charles did not bother to learn because it was not on any map he had access to. The Austrians were pulling back and leaving a cavalry screen to buy time, and Major Beaumont's squadron was sent to push through it. The infantry battle for the town itself was already underway on the right flank when they rode up; Charles could hear it before he could see it, the rolling exchange of musketry, then the heavier percussion of a cannon working at close range, then a sound under all of it that he had no category for yet, a collective human sound, many men together, neither shouting nor screaming but something between.
Charles's section took the left flank. The Austrian screen was a hundred men to his fifteen, which was not as bad as it sounded because they were trying to delay, not fight, and a screen that stands and fights loses its purpose. He recognized this before he gave the order, felt the shape of the situation the way you felt weather coming, and put the section at a diagonal that threatened their line of retreat rather than their line of resistance. To his right the infantry fight continued, smoke thickening over the town roofline, the musketry now in ragged bursts rather than volleys, which meant it had moved into the streets.
The Austrian screen broke without a charge. The cavalry peeled away and rode, and Charles let them go because he did not have the numbers to pursue safely and because letting them go meant no one on his side got hurt. He brought the section to a halt on a low rise and watched the Austrians diminish into the distance, and became aware, now that there was nothing immediate requiring his attention, of what the morning had produced in the field to his right.
The town was two hundred meters away. The infantry had taken it or were taking it; the musketry had slackened to occasional single shots, which usually meant one side had given way. The road running from the town toward his position had a wagon overturned across it, and near the wagon a man in French blue sitting with his back against a wheel and both hands pressed to his abdomen, looking at his hands. Beside him another man lay face-up in the road with no one attending him.
Further along the road two soldiers were walking away from the town, one leaning on the other, moving with the careful deliberate gait of men whose bodies had become suddenly uncertain. Behind them a sergeant directed three men carrying a door they had taken off its hinges, using it as a stretcher. Charles looked at what was on the stretcher long enough to understand what he was looking at, then looked back at his section.
Corporal Rousseau was watching the road. The eagerness that had been on his face at the start of the campaign was present now in a different form, compressed into something quieter and more careful, the expression of a man absorbing information he had not previously had.
The section had come through the morning without a casualty. This was correct. Charles had made the right decisions and they had worked and no one was hurt. Forty meters to his right the infantry had made different decisions, or had not been able to make any decisions because the ground gave them no choices, and the man by the wagon wheel was still looking at his hands.
Sergeant Mercier rode up beside him. Neither of them looked at the road.
"They did not want to fight. So we did not make them."
"No. But a less patient officer would have charged anyway, to finish what was already finished." A pause. "The infantry to our right had no such choice."
"No," Charles said. "They did not."
Sergeant Mercier looked at him for a moment. "The men on that stretcher did their job correctly too. It cost them anyway." He said it without judgment, the way he said most things. "That is also what war is."
They rode back to the squadron. The road took them past the wagon. Charles did not intend to look and looked anyway: the man who had been sitting against the wheel was gone, moved by someone, and there was a dark stain on the road where he had been. They reached the squadron and Charles reported to Major Beaumont: screen broken, no casualties in his section. Beaumont nodded and made a note. That evening he assigned Charles the reconnaissance duties for the following week's advance. Charles understood it to be a form of communication, and felt the shape of what was being communicated, and was not certain it was entirely a good thing.

* * *

Corporal Rousseau had changed over the summer. Not in the ways Charles had expected, not harder or quieter or more careful. The eagerness was still there but it had found a different object. At the start of the campaign Rousseau had wanted to fight. By August he wanted to be good at fighting, which was not the same thing and was considerably more useful.
He started asking different questions. Not when will there be action but how did you know the screen would break, not what is the plan but why that plan and not another. He began taking the younger troopers through the same afternoon training Charles had started in Munich, the horse inspections, the equipment checks, the habit of looking at ground before riding over it. He did not wait to be asked. He simply started doing it.
"You are going to make him a sergeant early," Lieutenant Philippe said one evening, watching Rousseau put a trooper through a saddle inspection for the third time.
"He is not ready yet."
"No. But he will be." Philippe adjusted his collar. "He is like you were in Munich, except warmer."
"I was not cold."
"Charles. You absolutely were." Philippe said it without heat, fondly. "You were correct about everything and communicating that fact to the men by the manner in which you existed in their vicinity."
Charles thought about that. "And now?"
"Now you are correct about most things and have learned to let people discover that themselves rather than announcing it." Philippe paused. "It is an improvement. Rousseau is already there. He has a gift for making people feel capable."
"That is a useful gift."
"More useful than yours or mine, in some respects." Philippe looked at the evening sky. "He is going to be a very good soldier if he lives long enough."
The qualifier sat in the air between them. Neither of them addressed it.

* * *

The promotion came in September, six weeks before the end of the Italian campaign.
Major Beaumont called him into the headquarters tent on a Tuesday morning. Charles stood at attention while Beaumont read the relevant portion of the orders in the flat tone of a man for whom paperwork was paperwork regardless of content.
"By order of the Emperor, Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc is hereby promoted to the rank of Lieutenant, with all privileges and responsibilities attendant thereto. Effective the fifteenth of September, year of our Lord eighteen hundred and six."
Beaumont set the paper down. "Congratulations, Lieutenant."
"Thank you, sir."
A pause. Beaumont picked up the next paper on his desk, which was a requisition form. "That will be all."
Charles walked out of the headquarters tent and stood in the September heat for a moment.
Lieutenant. He had been working toward it since Munich, since his father's five-year plan, since the garrison, since the first fumbled months of learning what cavalry actually meant. He had imagined this moment often, usually in the terms of what it would mean for the Leblanc name, the way his father would read the letter, Elise's response.
What he felt was mainly: three years in. Two more to Captain. He was on schedule.
He went to find Sergeant Mercier to tell him the section would need to sew a new rank insignia onto the spare uniform.

* * *

That evening Lieutenant Philippe produced a bottle of something from his saddlebag that was approximately wine and poured two cups.
"To Lieutenant Leblanc," Philippe said. "Who achieved this distinction by being relentlessly correct about horses and supply chains."
"You make it sound like an insult."
"It is a toast. Drink."
They drank. The wine was not good. The campaign had used up all the good wine weeks ago.
"How does it feel?" Philippe asked.
"The same."
"It always does at first." Philippe held his cup and looked at it. "I made Lieutenant in April. I thought I would feel different. More like an officer."
"Were you not already an officer?"
"In rank. I mean in here." Philippe touched his chest in a way that was only slightly theatrical. "I thought the promotion would settle something. Confirm I was in the right place."
"Did it?"
"No." Philippe drank. "I think the confirmation does not come from the rank. It comes from something else. The men. The way they look at you when it matters." He was quiet for a moment. "Your section follows you, Charles. Not because of your rank. They started following you before you had a rank that warranted it."
Charles did not know what to say to that.
"That is what I mean," Philippe said. "You are already what the rank says you are. The paper is just the army catching up with the fact."
He poured more wine. It did not improve with a second cup.

* * *

The campaign ended in October with the army occupying a line of small Italian towns that the maps called victories and that the towns themselves experienced as a complicated inconvenience. Charles's section was billeted in a village outside Udine whose name he could pronounce by the third day and not before.
He sat one evening on the low wall outside the house they had requisitioned and watched the village doing what villages did in the evening: a woman drawing water from the well, two old men talking at a doorway in a language he did not speak, a dog moving between the houses on some private business. The section was inside, sleeping or gambling or writing letters, the sounds of it muffled through the stone walls.
He had the field journal open, the list in the back. He read through it. Tagliamento: Lacroix broken arm, Virel captured recovered. The rearguard at the unnamed town: no casualties in his section. He had written it that way because that was the accurate entry for his section. The accurate entry for the infantry engagement happening simultaneously would have been written by someone else in a different journal and would have looked different.
He had done this. Not alone, not without Sergeant Mercier and Sergeant Duval and Lieutenant Philippe and the training and the luck that you could not control. But he had done his part correctly. He had read the ground and made the decisions and kept his men alive and accomplished the missions, and the record showed it in the absence of names on his list.
He was good at this.
The thought arrived without ceremony, the way true things often did. Not pride exactly, not satisfaction. A recognition. He had been trying to be good at this for two years and it turned out he was, and the feeling was not what he had expected it to be when he had imagined it at eighteen on a carriage road in Bordeaux.
He had imagined glory. What he had was competence, and competence felt like a different thing entirely. Quieter. More certain. Less like winning and more like understanding a language well enough to stop translating it.
He closed the journal and watched the woman at the well finish drawing water and carry it inside. The old men at the doorway were still talking. The dog had disappeared.
He was good at this. He thought about Moreau's widow, about Lacroix's broken arm, about the four hundred and twelve at the Tagliamento, about Virel in an Austrian field hospital for six weeks. About Beaumont saying when the numbers get comfortable.
Being good at this was not uncomplicated.

* * *

He wrote home about the promotion.
The letter took him two evenings. The first evening he wrote the formal version, the one that reported the promotion and the campaign and the successful conclusion of the Italian operations. He sealed it and then opened it again and wrote a second draft.
The second draft said:
Dear Father and Elise,
I am promoted to Lieutenant. This is the plan working as it should. Two more years to Captain, perhaps less if the campaigns continue to go as this one has. I am on schedule to come home.
I am good at this, Father. I do not know if that is what you expected when you sent me. I am not sure it is what I expected. But I am good at it, which feels important to report, though I am not certain how to explain what it costs.
The section is in good condition. Lacroix's arm healed well. The horses are sound. We are in a village outside Udine that has a well in the square and two old men who talk outside a doorway every evening and have done so, I imagine, for forty years, and who will be doing so again when we are gone.
Tell Elise I am sorry I missed whatever she has done this year. I expect she has done things. Tell her to write and tell me.
Your son,
Charles
He sealed the second draft and addressed it and left the first draft in the candle flame.

* * *

Sergeant Mercier found him at the horses the morning before they broke camp for the march north.
"Sir. A moment."
"Yes, Sergeant."
Mercier ran a hand down Tonnerre's neck, not checking anything, just the habitual contact. "You have done well this campaign. The section has done well."
"Thank you, Sergeant."
"I am not saying it to be pleasant." Mercier's tone was the one that meant he was about to say something he had been thinking about for some time. "I am saying it because I want to ask you something."
Charles waited.
"When this is done. When you have made Captain. What then?"
Charles looked at him. "Home. The estate. That was always the plan."
"I know it was the plan." Mercier was looking at Tonnerre. "I have seen other officers with plans. Good officers. Men who knew what they were doing and why. And I have seen them promoted and given more men and more responsibility, and the plan gets pushed forward. One more campaign. One more season. The army does not give men up easily when they are useful."
"I am aware of that."
"Are you?" Mercier looked at him now, directly. "You are useful, sir. You are more useful than you were six months ago, which means you will be given more to do. That is how it works."
Charles said nothing.
"I am not telling you to leave. I am not telling you to stay. I am an NCO and it is not my place to tell a lieutenant anything about his career." Mercier straightened. "I am telling you to keep the plan in your head. Keep it specific. Captain, then home. Not Captain and then one more thing and then home."
"Yes, Sergeant."
Mercier nodded once and went back to his work.
Charles stood with Tonnerre for a while, the horse warm against his hand in the October cold.
The plan was still clear. He was still on schedule. He was Lieutenant Leblanc, two years from Captain, and after Captain, home.
He told himself this several times, which was not something he had needed to do before.
