CHAPTER 6

The cold in Prussia was a different animal than the cold in the Alps.

In the mountains, cold was vertical. It came off stone faces and glacial wind and the altitude made it clean, if brutal. You understood it. In Prussia, the cold was flat and gray and everywhere, rising from the mud and the dead fields in equal measure, settling into the folds of your coat and the gaps in your boots. By the third week of November, Charles had stopped noticing individual moments of discomfort and begun simply existing inside a general state of it.

They were somewhere east of the Oder, pushing toward Kustrin, following a road that had been churned by three armies and a hundred supply wagons into something barely navigable. The Grande Armee was in pursuit now, mopping up Prussian remnants after Jena. Not the clean work of an advancing campaign but the slow, irregular work of grinding down a broken force that did not know yet it was finished. The Prussians were fighting in patches. A rearguard here, a cavalry screen there. Stubborn holdouts at road junctions that made no sense to hold.

This morning, the squadron had been detached from the main column to secure a junction two kilometers west of a supply depot the army needed.

The briefing had taken ten minutes. Colonel Varennes, a staff officer who had been attached to them from Paris five days ago with new orders and a general's manner, had unrolled his map on an ammunition box and explained the situation in the tone of a man explaining something simple to people he suspected of being slow. The junction was held by Prussian cavalry and a half-battery of four light guns. The approach was four hundred meters of open ground, a harvested beet field, flat and completely without cover. The guns would have clean sightlines for the entire distance.

Major Beaumont had stood across the ammunition box from Varennes, looking at the map. "The eastern track," Beaumont said, not raising his voice. "If we take the horses along the drainage ditch"

"The eastern track is soft ground," Varennes said. "We would lose the element of speed."

"We would lose half the first wave to the battery before they reach the guns."

Varennes folded the map. "The order stands."

* * *

Charles found Philippe that night at the picket line.

It was late, well past the time a man had any business being out of his bedroll, and the temperature had dropped to the point where the mud on the road was beginning to crust around the edges. Philippe was at the far end of the line, checking the knots on Renard's lead rope with the focused attention of someone who had already checked them twice.

"You are not worried about the knots," Charles said.

"The knots are excellent." Philippe did not look up. "I am enjoying the company."

Charles stopped beside him. The horses stood close together in the dark, breath rising in small clouds. Down the line, a sentry moved past, boots crunching on the frosted grass, and then was gone.

"The staff position," Philippe said, after a moment. "Beaumont told me he offered it. In October."

"He did."

"You did not tell me."

Charles said nothing.

"I heard it from Duval, Charles. Duval." Philippe finally looked at him. "You know what Duval told me? He said you turned it down in about thirty seconds. Like there was nothing to think about."

"There was not."

"There was a great deal to think about." Philippe pulled his collar straight, the automatic gesture he had been making since Charles first met him. "A staff position means another three years of service, minimum."

"Yes."

"So you chose this instead."

"Five years," Charles said. "Make Captain and go home. A staff appointment changes the equation."

Philippe looked at him for a long moment, something behind his eyes that was not quite the easy amusement he usually wore. He looked tired. He had looked tired for a week, which was unremarkable in itself. They were all tired. But there was a quality to it now, a quietness.

"Elise wrote me," Philippe said.

Charles did not answer immediately. "I know she writes you."

"She asked me to look after you." Philippe's mouth curved slightly. "I wrote back and told her you did not need it. That you were the most capable man in the squadron and possibly the most infuriating." He paused. "She wrote back and said competence was not care."

The horses shifted. Somewhere in the dark, a horse stamped once and went still.

"She is right, of course," Philippe said. "She usually is, your sister." He adjusted his collar again. "You do not tell people things, Charles. You talk to no one about you. You know that?"

"I know."

"It is not a complaint. It is just. I have watched you for a year. You will talk tactics until midnight. You will sit with a man when he is frightened and you will find exactly the right thing to do. But you talk to no one about yourself." Philippe's voice was mild, not pressing. "I have been trying to figure out if you know it and do not mind, or if you genuinely do not notice."

"I know."

"Which is it?"

Charles thought about that honestly. "Both," he said. "Depending on the day."

Philippe laughed, quietly, a real one. He gave Renard one last pat on the neck and stepped back. "Go home when you can, Charles. That is all I mean. Do not let the plan get longer."

"Five years," Charles said. "Captain and home. Nothing longer."

Philippe nodded. He was already turning back toward the bivouac, collar up against the cold, hands stuffed in his coat. "Goodnight," he said, without looking back.

Charles stayed at the picket line a little longer. Tonnerre was six horses down, and he found him in the dark by feel, the particular shape of the gelding's nose, the warmth of his breath. He stood there a moment, not thinking about anything specific.

Then he went back to his bedroll, lay down with his boots on, and put his hand on the left one. The paper was there. He had put it in before dark, his family's address in Bordeaux in Elise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card. He had done it before every engagement since Gunzburg. He was not sure why, exactly. The address had not changed. Elise would still know where to send the letter regardless.

His hand rested on the boot. He fell asleep like that.

* * *

Morning came gray and cold, which was how all mornings came now.

Sergeant Mercier had the horses ready before Charles reached the line. The sergeant moved through the inspection sequence without looking up: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Then hooves, each one lifted and examined. Then the spine, both hands running from withers to croup. He did it while Charles stood beside him, and he did not hurry.

"Philippe's section goes first wave," Charles said. "We are in support. Second wave, seventy meters back."

Sergeant Mercier set down a hoof, moved to the next horse. "Do not stop in the middle."

"I was not planning to."

"Men slow down when they see the first volley hit. They lose their nerve for a moment. Not deserting, just slowing." Mercier's hands moved along a foreleg, checking for heat. "If you feel the section slow, you push through them. Do not stop in the middle of that field."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Sergeant Mercier straightened, patted the horse's shoulder, moved to the next one.

* * *

Philippe was adjusting his collar when Charles found him before the line.

"It is manageable," Philippe said, with the careful tone of someone building a belief while they say it, testing the weight of the words. "The guns are light. By the time we are at a hundred meters, they will not have time to reload."

"They will have time for two volleys," Charles said.

"Probably two, yes."

They stood beside their horses. Charles wanted to say something. He could feel the shape of it, some version of last night's conversation, something that continued it. But the men were nearby and the moment was not right, and anyway he did not know exactly what he wanted to say.

"My section goes first," Philippe said. Not asking. Just confirming. "You come through when we reach the guns."

"Yes."

Philippe nodded. He looked at Charles for a moment, not the tired look from last night but something steadier, and then he reached up and adjusted his collar one more time, a reflex.

"Brief your men," he said. "Do not let them think too long."

He turned and walked back to his section.

Charles watched him go for a second. Then he went to find his own.

* * *

He found Sergeant Mercier a moment later, checking the girth on his horse for the third time.

"Two volleys," Charles said. "Maybe three if they are fast. Philippe's section takes the battery. We push through, deal with the flanks."

Sergeant Mercier checked the girth. Said nothing.

"Varennes will not move the approach. Beaumont tried."

Mercier released the stirrup. "Do not stop in the middle."

Charles said, "Yes, Sergeant."

Sergeant Mercier swung up.

* * *

Philippe's section moved at ten past nine, when the light was flat and even and gave no shadows.

Charles held his section in column, seventy meters back. From here, he could see the junction, a low stone wall and a farmhouse beyond it, the four gun carriages arranged in a rough line, the Prussian cavalry visible as dark shapes behind them, formed in two groups on the flanks. The beet field between here and there was frozen in ridges from the cart tracks and scattered with the black stumps of harvested plants.

Philippe's section went at the trot, then the canter. Eleven men in an extended line, sabers not yet drawn. They had made it perhaps a hundred and fifty meters when the guns fired.

The sound was flat in the cold air, not the rolling crash of the summer battles but something more compressed, efficient. Two horses went down in the first volley. One immediately, one struggling for a moment and then still. The section kept moving, closing the gap. Charles felt Tonnerre shift beneath him, a muscle-tightening that was not panic, just awareness, and he put a hand briefly on the gelding's neck.

The second volley came at a hundred and thirty meters. One man. The section barely paused. They were close enough now that Charles could hear them, hooves on frozen ground, a shout from somewhere in Philippe's line, and then they were at the guns, and the surface of it became immediate and particular and left no room for watching.

Charles pressed his section forward.

Through the gap in the stone wall. The guns already taken. Prussian artillerymen running or on the ground. He swept left where one of the Prussian cavalry groups was reforming, caught them before they had any order to them, drove them back in two hard exchanges that he would not remember clearly afterward. The second group melted north, into the bare poplar trees at the edge of the field.

Then it was over.

Charles pulled Tonnerre up near the farmhouse wall. His hands were shaking slightly, the fine trembling that came in the aftermath and lasted maybe two minutes before it stopped. He breathed. He counted his section: Rousseau, Sergeant Duval, Marchetti, the two Girard cousins, the others. All of them there, three men with cuts, one with an arm he was holding odd, none of them down. He looked for Philippe.

He did not see him.

* * *

One of Philippe's men was kneeling beside a gun carriage, looking up when Charles arrived.

"Corporal Bonnard," Charles said. "Where is he?"

Bonnard was already on his feet, stepping back to give Charles room, and that was how Charles knew before he saw him. The specific way Bonnard moved, the expression of someone who has realized they need an officer.

Philippe was on the ground behind the gun carriage, back against the wheel. He was conscious. He had both hands pressed to his chest, high left side, and there was blood on the hands and on the front of his coat. His face was pale but his eyes were clear, tracking Charles as Charles knelt beside him.

"The junction," Philippe said.

"Taken. Both flanking groups dispersed."

"Good." He breathed carefully, the breath shallow and deliberate. "The guns?"

"Ours."

"Good," he said again.

Charles put his hand over Philippe's hands and applied pressure. The wound was high and left and there was too much blood for a surface wound. He looked up at Corporal Bonnard. "Surgeon. Now."

Bonnard was already moving.

Charles kept his eyes on Philippe. "Where does it hurt?"

"It is where it is." A pause. A careful breath. "I cannot feel my left hand."

"The surgeon is coming."

Philippe nodded, eyes closing briefly, then opening again. He looked at Charles with the slightly unfocused quality of a man navigating pain. "You are bleeding," he said.

Charles had not noticed. There was a cut on his forearm, shallow, cloth already sticking to it, not serious. "It is nothing."

"Good."

They stayed like that for a moment, Charles's hand over Philippe's, the gun carriage at Philippe's back. The cold was very clear. From somewhere across the junction, men were shouting about horses. The ordinary noise of aftermath.

Charles looked at Philippe's collar. It was turned up wrong on one side, askew from the fall or the press of the engagement. He reached with his free hand and straightened it.

The gesture was ridiculous. The collar made no difference. Philippe watched it happen with an expression that was careful and grateful in a way that had nothing to do with the collar.

"Captain and home," Philippe said. "Still the plan?"

"Still the plan."

"Good." He shifted slightly, winced. "My section will need someone. After."

"Your section is fine," Charles said. "They came through."

"They will need someone to look at them the way you do." Philippe's eyes were tracking carefully, fighting to stay on Charles. "Like they are capable. You do that. You do not notice it."

"Do not use up your breath."

"I am using it correctly." But he stopped talking.

The surgeon arrived in six minutes. Charles counted, afterward, in the journal.

* * *

There were two lucid stretches in the two hours that followed.

The first was brief. Philippe surfacing from the surgeon's work to say something about Renard, that someone should make sure Renard was checked properly, that the gelding had been limping slightly this morning. Charles said he would see to it. Philippe believed him and settled.

The second came later, when the light was already changing. He surfaced clearly, enough that he looked at Charles with real focus.

"Elise wrote me," he said. Not remembering that he had said it last night, or perhaps knowing and saying it again. "She asked me to look after you. I told her you did not need it."

"You told me."

"She was right, though." A breath, slow and even. "You do not need looking after. But you could let people try." He paused. "Go home before it gets comfortable, Charles. The longer you wait, the better you will be at it, and the harder it will be to leave." His voice was quiet, tired, not pressing. Just saying the true thing. "I know how you think. You will start calculating who depends on you. You will find you are useful. It is easy to stay for that."

Charles did not answer.

"Go home when you can." He reached up toward his collar, a reflex, but his hand made it only as far as his chest before it stopped. He could not quite reach it.

Charles reached over and straightened it.

"Thank you," Philippe said. Something in his face settled, the last small tension leaving it.

He went quiet after that, in and out, the breathing slow and occasional. At some point in the late afternoon the breathing changed. Not a moment, not a sound, just a quality of the silence that was different from the silences before it. The surgeon shook his head. He said something to Charles. Charles heard the words and understood them and filed them somewhere for later.

Outside, the junction was their junction now, and men were moving around it, doing the ordinary work.

* * *

He found Sergeant Mercier at the horse line.

The sergeant was going through the inspection sequence, methodical, on a horse that had come through without injury and needed no inspection. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Hooves. Spine. He worked through it in full, and when it was done he started it again, more slowly.

"Philippe is dead," Charles said.

Sergeant Mercier's hands stilled on the horse's flank. He stood that way for a moment, looking at nothing in particular. Then he continued the inspection, the spine, both hands moving steadily.

"I know," he said.

He finished the sequence. He straightened. He looked at Charles with the specific quality of attention Mercier reserved for things that mattered, not long, not dramatic, just clear and direct.

"The pressure is starting," Sergeant Mercier said.

"I know."

"You do not, yet." He moved to the next horse, started at the left foreleg. "You did well today. The men know that. Philippe's section knows that. Varennes knows it, which he will not say, but Major Beaumont does and he will." He pressed two fingers against the foreleg, checking. "Men will start looking at you differently. They will hand you problems because you solve them. They will ask you to take on the next thing because the last thing went well. And you will be able to do it." He paused. "That is the dangerous part."

Charles said nothing.

"The plan was specific," Sergeant Mercier said. "Captain. Then home. Not Captain and the next engagement after that, not Captain and we need you through the winter, not Captain and. It does not finish itself, Lieutenant. It is never finished. There will always be one more problem in front of you that you can solve and they cannot. That is not a reason to stay."

"I know."

"You will feel it differently in six months." Sergeant Mercier moved to the right foreleg. "I am telling you now while you still believe me."

He kept going down the horse, unhurried. He did not look at Charles again.

Charles stayed a moment longer, watching the sequence, and then he turned and went to find his journal.

* * *

The page at the back was two pages now, names in the small careful print he used for entries he wanted to keep. He found the bottom of the second page, found the space below the last name, and wrote Philippe's.

Arnaud, Philippe. Lieutenant. November 1806.

He looked at it for a moment. Then he closed the journal.

Philippe's section was quartered in the farmhouse, twelve men using the main room and the attached barn, and Charles found them at dusk by the state of them. Not disorganized, but quiet in the specific way of men who have lost someone and do not yet know the new shape of things.

Corporal Bonnard looked up when Charles came through the door. Bonnard was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been Philippe's corporal for four months, a steady man who checked everything twice and did not make noise about it. He looked at Charles with the expression of someone who has run out of the thing he was relying on and is deciding, in real time, whether the next available thing will hold weight.

Charles said: "Horses watered and fed?"

"Yes, sir."

"Bonnard. Three of your horses are short on forage. Get with Rousseau and go to the depot before they close it. They will tell you they do not have it. Tell them you need a signature from Major Beaumont authorizing priority issue, and if they want to argue it, I will be along in an hour."

Bonnard was already on his feet. Something in him had straightened slightly.

"The rest of you," Charles said, looking around the room. "Tonight is tonight. Tomorrow morning I want a headcount on equipment. Carbines, sabers, saddlery. Anything that needs attention before we move. Write it down. If you do not write it down, it does not exist." He paused. "You came through the junction. That was well done."

He did not make it more than that.

He met with Sergeant Duval about the casualty report. He walked the perimeter once, looking at the horses. He had the argument with the supply depot. Not Bonnard's forage argument, which Rousseau handled efficiently, but a separate argument about canteens and the fact that the resupply schedule was two days off and someone at the depot had decided that was not his problem. Charles made it his problem, calmly, specifically, with dates and regulations and the promise of Beaumont's signature, and resolved it in twenty minutes.

The depot sergeant watched him leave with the expression of a man who had expected to win that argument.

* * *

At the picket line, late, Tonnerre was working through inadequate hay with the focused patience he brought to most things. Charles stood beside him in the dark. The junction was quiet now, the farmhouse lights low, the sentries moving in their irregular rotation.

Philippe had said it last night and again this afternoon. The words had a different weight now, the second hearing.

When the numbers get comfortable, Major Beaumont had said, four months ago. That is when you should worry.

It is never finished, Sergeant Mercier had said tonight. Not bitterly. Just as a fact.

Charles thought about Corporal Bonnard's face in the farmhouse, that moment of deciding whether the next available thing would hold weight. How quickly Bonnard had come to his feet. How the section had organized itself around two sentences about forage.

He had noticed it. Filed it. Moved on.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck. The gelding kept eating.

Philippe's name was on the list now. Two pages. The list had been one column when he started it in September. Now it ran into the margins.

He had not written to Elise yet. He would have to write to her, and he would have to tell her about Philippe, and he did not know yet how to do that. Philippe had said competence was not care. Elise had said it first, to Philippe, and Philippe had carried it to Charles, and Charles had understood it immediately and had not known what to do with it.

The cold was very clear. Somewhere north of them, past the bare poplars and the frozen fields, there were more Prussians, more junctions, more approaches with four hundred meters of open ground.

But something in the calculation had changed. Not the numbers. The numbers were the same. Something in how the numbers felt. He turned it over and could not find the edge of it.

Charles stood at the picket line for a long time.

He did not sleep.
