# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 8

The Pyrenees were not the Alps.

Charles had thought about that, in the abstract, through the summer while the treaty with Russia settled into something that looked like permanence, and then through the autumn while the orders came in pieces, and then through the winter while the army turned itself southwest and began to move. He had told himself the mountains were mountains. He had been wrong.

The Alps had a quality he recognized now only in retrospect: they had been a gate. You crossed them and arrived somewhere. The enemy was on the other side, organized into units with flags and commanders who answered letters. The crossing was hard but it had direction. You could see where you were going.

The Pyrenees simply stopped being France and started being Spain without announcing themselves. The same rock, the same cold, the same thin air that made the horses labor. But no one on the other side was waiting to negotiate terms.

They crossed in late October. The road through the pass was well-worn, which meant other columns had moved through it recently, which meant the army was already there in force, which meant the situation was already complicated enough to require force. Charles rode beside Tonnerre's steady rhythm and did the arithmetic and did not like where it ended.

Two days before the crossing they passed through a valley where a supply column had been hit.

He had seen plenty of dead men. He had added some of them to his list. The men in the valley were different, and it took him a moment to understand why: they were arranged wrong. A conventional engagement left a particular pattern. Men fell from horses a certain way. Horses fell a certain way. Bodies ended up where momentum and physics put them, which followed a logic you could read if you knew the language.

These men were not arranged by momentum. They were where someone had put them.

He did not stop the column. Stopping accomplished nothing and he was not senior enough to decide it. He rode through at the same pace and kept his eyes on the road ahead and noted, in the quiet part of his mind that catalogued such things, that the wagons had been taken rather than burned. Practical. Organized. Someone had needed the wagons more than they had needed to make a point.

Mercier was riding two lengths to his left.

Charles waited for him to say something. In Italy, Mercier had talked about the ground constantly, in his deliberate way, the sentences arriving one at a time as if he were checking each word before he released it. He had opinions about soil consistency and its effect on cavalry movement, about how hills funneled infantry fire, about the particular problems created by rivers in spring thaw versus winter freeze. He talked about the ground the way some men talked about weather: a continuous commentary that was both practical and somehow comforting, evidence that someone had thought about these things before and survived long enough to develop opinions.

Mercier said nothing for forty minutes. Then he said: "Different wind down here."

That was all.

Charles looked at the road and thought about that. The column moved around them, Bonnard keeping the rear orderly, Rousseau up ahead with the outriders at a distance that Charles had specified and Rousseau had expanded by another twenty meters without being asked, which was the right instinct. Duval was counting things, which Duval always was.

Different wind. It was not wrong.

By the time they reached the first Spanish town of any size, the column had taken on a quality Charles had not felt before, not at Eylau, not in the worst of Prussia. The men who had been through three campaigns, men who had understood fear as a thing with a shape and a direction, were nervous in a way that had no shape. Eylau had been legible. The guns were there, the Russian line was there, you rode at it or you defended against it and either way you knew where the danger was coming from. Spain was not legible. The danger was the ground itself, the passes, the men who had needed those wagons, the closed windows of the town they were riding through right now in bright October sunlight.

A boy stood in a doorway and watched them pass. He was perhaps ten. His face had no expression on it at all, not fear, not curiosity. He watched them with the patience of someone waiting for weather to change.

Charles noted this. He kept riding.

* * *

The depot raid was in February 1809, and it went well.

Good intelligence from a cavalry captain who had spent three weeks in the area before them. Clear maps. A defensible approach and a reasonable exit. Charles had spent two days walking the problem with Bonnard before he presented it to Major Beaumont, and Beaumont had asked four careful questions and approved it with the particular expression he wore when he was satisfied but would not say so, which was the same expression he wore most of the time.

They moved at night. Charles had Rousseau take six men to the east approach while he came in from the north with the main group, and the timing held within ten minutes of what he'd planned, which was as good as timing ever held in practice. The depot guards were overwhelmed before they had organized a proper response. Casualties: two men with minor wounds, one horse, which he noted in his report and felt more than he noted. The depot was secured, the stores catalogued, a messenger dispatched by mid-morning.

The praise, when it came from the colonel, was brief and specific. Charles had learned to distrust fulsome praise; specific praise was accurate and therefore useful. He accepted it and went back to check his men.

Tonnerre had a cut on his left foreleg from a wire that hadn't been visible in the dark. Not deep. Charles knelt in the mud and ran his fingers along it, feeling for heat. The horse stood steady.

"You'll be complaining about it for a week," Charles told him, and patted his neck twice, and went to find Mercier to report the men's condition.

The village they had ridden through on the approach, and again on the return, was not a large place. Twenty houses, a church, a well in the square. On the approach it had been four in the morning and he hadn't expected to see anyone. On the return it was full daylight, the raid concluded, the unit intact, the operation by any military measure a success.

Every door was closed.

He had been in villages after engagements in Italy where the doors were also closed, but that had been a different kind of closing: people inside deciding whether the noise had stopped. This was not that. The doors in this village were closed the way things are closed when they have been closed for a long time, when the act of closing is not a decision but a habit, when you have learned that doors closed means you are not involved and not involved is the safest condition available.

No one in the street. The well sat empty and ordinary in the morning light.

Charles looked at it as he rode past. He looked at the church. He looked at the shut doors of the houses where people were certainly awake by now, certainly aware that a column of French cavalry had just ridden through their village twice in a single night, concluded some military business in the hills above it, and returned.

He looked at Rousseau, who was riding beside him on the return.

Rousseau had a south-coastal way of reading situations, the Marseille in him a kind of sharp skepticism that had evolved, over four years of service, into something quieter and more reliable. He looked at the village and then at Charles and said nothing. Which was its own answer.

They rode through. The column kept its pace. By afternoon they were back at the main camp, and Charles wrote his report, and the report covered everything that a report was meant to cover: approach timing, ammunition expenditure, personnel condition, notes on the road quality for future reference.

* * *

That evening he sat with his journal and the stub of a tallow candle and wrote for forty minutes.

He wrote about the approach route and the features that had made it preferable to the southern alternative. He wrote a paragraph on the wire hazard in the depot yard and how it should be noted for the next unit to operate in the area. He wrote about the behavior of the horses in the cold, which had been better than expected, and about a problem with one of the wagons that had been repaired before it became significant.

He wrote about Rousseau's instinct on the outrider distance, and that he intended to formalize the expanded margin as standard for this theater.

He wrote for a while about the Pyrenean passes and how they differed from Alpine approaches in terms of cavalry utility and in terms of the supply problem, which was worse here because the roads were older and more contested.

He wrote: *The operation proceeded as planned. Men in acceptable condition. Tonnerre's cut minor, no heat in the leg this evening, expecting full recovery.*

He stopped.

He looked at what he had written.

He wrote: *Excellent intelligence made this possible. Need to establish reliable local network, difficult given the current.*

He stopped again. Crossed out "difficult given the current." Wrote nothing else in that space.

He closed the journal. He sat for a moment in the smell of tallow and cold canvas and something else that was Spanish and not yet named in his vocabulary of places, a dryness to the cold that was different from Polish dryness, a particular quality of dust that came in under the tent flap.

Outside, men were talking in low voices. Bonnard had organized the watch rotation without being asked, which was something Philippe would have approved of, and Charles noted this and kept it to himself, which was something Philippe would also have approved of.

He opened his journal again. He looked at the blank space after the crossed-out words.

He closed it again and put it away.

* * *

Margot's letters arrived irregularly, which was the nature of letters in wartime, and he had stopped expecting regularity and started simply being glad when they arrived. He kept them in his field bag in a bundle secured with a piece of cord. He had not counted the bundle. It had gotten heavier since Poland.

She wrote on good paper when she had it and on whatever was available when she didn't, and he could tell the difference by feel without looking, which was information about the state of her business at any given moment and which he found himself tracking with the same attention he gave to supply reports. Her handwriting was precise and somewhat compressed, as if she had learned early that paper was finite and words should carry weight.

Her letters were practical in the way that his were practical, but her practicality covered more ground. She asked about road conditions in the passes with the specificity of someone who intended to use that information: not how difficult the crossing was, but what the surface was like under rain, whether the switchbacks allowed for wagon teams of four or required reducing to two, which villages had water in late autumn and which relied on snowmelt that wouldn't be reliable until spring. She asked about the ports on the northern coast and what the French garrison presence looked like in the major towns, framed as observations rather than questions that would cause trouble for the letter if it was read by the wrong person.

He had noticed this early, her habit of framing military observations as merchant observations, and had found it cleaner than the alternative. She was asking what merchants needed to know. The answer happened to be approximately what a cavalry officer also needed to know.

He answered her questions about roads and conditions and told her about the Pyrenean passes with the specificity she preferred: not the experience of crossing but the practical dimensions. What the surface was. Where the water was. What the approach looked like for a loaded wagon versus a horse column.

He told her about the horses, because the horses were honest subject matter and because she asked after Tonnerre with a consistency that suggested she had filed the horse under reliable intelligence about Charles's general condition. He told her about the terrain and the weather and occasionally about Mercier or Bonnard, identified by name and role but not in terms that would mean anything to a censor.

He did not tell her about the closed doors.

He could not have explained, precisely, why not. He knew what he would have written: something about the difference between people who are afraid of you and people who have simply decided to wait you out, about how the distinction mattered tactically but also in some way he could not name tactically. He had the words for it. He just did not write them.

Her letters contained, occasionally, information that was not its own category of remarkable until after it proved useful.

In January of 1809, roughly three weeks before the depot raid, a letter arrived that had been written in November and traveled badly, the paper damp-wrinkled and re-dried, the ink slightly smeared in one corner. She had been corresponding with a network of German and Dutch merchants who were attempting to reconstruct their Spanish trade connections, most of which had collapsed in the summer when the situation went uncertain. The letter was full of practical logistics: which routes were viable, which were not, what the merchants were hearing from their contacts in Bilbao and Burgos.

Near the end, almost as an aside, she wrote:

*There is a ford at the junction below the Garralda gorge that I am told the local merchants have avoided since midsummer. The reasoning given was irregular activity in the gorge above it. I pass this along for what it's worth, which is probably what a merchant's secondhand word is worth, which is not much. But the merchants who told me this are not dramatic people, and they were specific about the gorge.*

He read this twice.

He had not been planning to use the ford at the Garralda gorge. He had not been planning to pass through that area at all in the coming weeks. He read the letter again, noted the location, put the letter in his field bag.

Two weeks later, orders arrived that modified his assigned patrol area, extending it east. The Garralda ford was now relevant.

He took the longer road, adding three days. He made the decision on a Tuesday morning and did not explain his reasoning in the operations note, which stated only "alternate route selected based on available intelligence." Beaumont had looked at it and said nothing, which was Beaumont's form of agreement.

Three days after he arrived at the eastern staging point by the longer road, a unit from a different regiment was ambushed at the ford below the Garralda gorge. He learned this from a dispatch rider who arrived on a Friday, the horse sweating and the rider's face carrying the expression Charles had seen before: the expression of someone who has seen what ambush does at close range and has not yet found the words to put between himself and the image.

Seven men. He did not know them. He wrote them into the back of his journal, which meant the list was now three pages. He did not know their names, which bothered him in a way that sat in his chest for several days without resolving.

He sat down that evening and wrote to Margot.

The letter was short. He spent some time on it, writing and crossing out and rewriting, which was unusual for him; his letters generally arrived at their shape quickly. The problem was that he wanted to say something accurate and could not find the form for it. He owed her a debt that had no clean name, because she had not known she was saving him, because she had been talking about merchants.

He finally wrote:

*Your advice about the ford below the Garralda. It mattered. I want you to know that it mattered and that I am well. Tell your merchants their word was worth more than they priced it.*

He looked at this for a moment. Then he added:

*Tonnerre's leg has healed completely. The terrain here is harder on them than Italy was, but he manages.*

He sealed the letter and gave it to the courier and did not think about whether she would understand what "it mattered" meant, because she would.

* * *

By the time spring arrived he was twenty-two and had stopped being surprised by the particular quality of silence that a Spanish village kept when a French column passed through it. He had not made peace with it. He had simply stopped being surprised by it.

The war here was not the war he had learned. He had learned a war that was about position and maneuver and the accumulation of ground, a war where the objective was legible and the means of achieving it were disagreeable but comprehensible. You moved toward a thing. You either took it or you didn't. The next day the map looked different.

Spain did not accumulate. You took a town, and then you held it, and holding it cost more than taking it had, and the ground you had taken looked the same on the map but was not the same. The guerrillas were not on the map. They were in the land itself, in the passes and the dry hills and the villages where every door was closed.

He was good at this. He had been good at conventional cavalry operations and he had adapted, had adjusted his instincts and his tactics, had learned to read the Spanish ground the way Mercier had taught him to read Italian ground, which was a different language but the same basic grammar. His men trusted him. Beaumont had stopped explaining his reasoning when he gave Charles orders, which was its own form of statement.

He had handled an ambush in March in a way that had prevented casualties to his own men and to two other units moving in the same area, by reading a hillside correctly at a moment that required reading it correctly. He had done this with no particular drama, in the way that a good surgeon makes a cut look easy, because the difficulty goes into the preparation and the preparation was not dramatic. Bonnard afterward had said only "good eye," which was the highest available praise in their current vocabulary. Mercier had said nothing, which meant the same thing.

He was good at this.

He was less certain, lately, what "this" was.

He sat outside his tent one evening in April and cleaned his pistol and thought about that without resolving it. The evening was dry and the light was going in the way that Spanish light went, which was different from French light and not in a way he had found words for yet. The pistol came apart in his hands and went back together and he checked the mechanism twice, which was Duval's habit and which he had apparently acquired without noticing.

The five-year plan. He counted. Prussia in 1806, Spain in 1808, which made it three years in. Two years remained on the plan, roughly, depending on when the army decided it was done with Spain, which it showed no current signs of deciding. He had thought about captaincy frequently in the first two years and now he thought about it in a different way, not as a destination but as an interval, a marker on a road whose end he could not see clearly because he was too close to the middle of it.

He thought about Elise's last letter, which had arrived in February and had been full of ordinary news: the vineyard, a neighbor's dispute, their father's back, a cousin's wedding. She wrote as if the war were a thing happening elsewhere to other people, which was true, and also as if it would not last much longer, which was also a kind of truth, just a different one. She had enclosed a drawing of the vineyard in autumn, done by someone's child apparently, which was not technically accurate but captured something about the light.

He had looked at the drawing for a long time.

He kept it in his field bag, not folded, which meant it took up more space than anything else he was carrying. He had made this calculation and kept the drawing unfolded.

He thought about Margot's last letter, which had been warmer than usual in some way he could not entirely locate in the specific words, though the words themselves had been practical as always. She had asked about the road surfaces in the Ebro valley with the specificity he had come to expect. She had mentioned that her father's business was expanding into new territory now that the peace with Prussia was holding, which meant she was working more independently, which he had understood to mean she was doing most of the work with Henri Vieillard providing the legitimacy and the capital and the experience. She had mentioned, in passing, that Frankfurt was cold in February and that she sometimes thought about how different one kind of cold was from another.

He had reread that line three times without deciding what it meant.

He had not written back to it. He had answered the question about the Ebro valley and told her about Tonnerre and told her about a particular quality of the light at this latitude in winter that he was still trying to find the right comparison for, and then he had reread his own letter before sealing it and left that last part in because removing it would have made the letter noticeably shorter without the appearance of a good reason.

She had not written back to it either. Which was either its own kind of answer or simply the nature of letters, which could not quite carry tone, which arrived out of order and were read in circumstances their authors had not imagined.

He put the pistol together and checked the mechanism one more time and put it away.

Tonnerre was staked nearby, cropping at the dry grass with the focused patience he brought to any available food source. Charles went to him and put his hand on the horse's neck and stood there for a moment. Tonnerre continued eating with the suggestion that the attention was noted but secondary to the grass.

"Fair enough," Charles said.

He went back into his tent and opened his journal and wrote the date, the fourteenth of April, 1809, and noted the weather and the state of the road to the south and the condition of the horses and the status of the men and the information that had come in from the outriders that afternoon, which was not eventful but was accurate, and accuracy was its own value.

He did not write about the quality of the light.

He did not write about the counting he had done, three years in, two remaining.

He wrote that Rousseau had handled the morning patrol well and that the suggestion he had made about the rotation had been correct and that he had told him so, which was not a thing Charles did often enough and which he was trying to do more deliberately.

He wrote that the list was now three pages and that he did not know seven of the names on it.

He stopped. He sat for a while with the journal open and the candle burning.

Then he reached down, without particular thought, and pulled off his left boot and took out the paper, folded to playing card size, worn at the folds but holding. He opened it. Elise's handwriting, the Bordeaux address, the same as they had always been. He read it not because the information was new. He read it because it was true.

He folded it back to its shape and put it back in the boot and set the boot aside and went to sleep.
