CHAPTER 1

Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc was ruining his first impression.

He knew it even as the words came out. He knew he should stop, that Captain Durand had not asked about the stables, that nobody cared. But the silence after the question had stretched one beat too long and he had filled it the only way he knew how.

"I grew up with horses, sir. My family has a vineyard, and we"

"That is not what I asked."

Heat crawled up the back of his neck. Captain Durand was watching him with the patience of a man deciding whether to bother being angry. His jacket hung open, his desk covered in stacked paper, and he held his quill like a tool he had used ten thousand times.

"No formal cavalry experience, sir. But I rode extensively at home, and I have studied"

"You have studied."

Charles heard what the captain left out of that sentence. He stopped talking.

The quill scratched. Durand signed something, slid it aside, and finally looked up with the expression of a man who had processed a hundred new lieutenants and expected this one to be no different.

"You are assigned to Major Beaumont's squadron. Mess at six bells. Formation at dawn. Report to Sergeant Mercier at the stables to draw your mount." He pushed a paper across the desk. "Quartermaster. Building adjacent to the stables."

"Thank you, sir." Charles took the paper.

He turned for the door. Captain Durand's voice stopped him.

"Leblanc."

"Sir?"

"This is a working garrison. We train cavalry officers here, not vineyard inspectors. Keep that in mind."

"Yes, sir."

Charles closed the door behind him, perhaps a degree more firmly than necessary.


He had been in the garrison for perhaps forty minutes.

The carriage had left him at the gate that afternoon with his trunk and a sentry's indifferent gesture toward the main building. Nine days of travel: post-chaises smelling of other men's sweat, roads that rattled his teeth, water from a canteen that had been sitting in afternoon heat. His uniform was as pressed as he could manage, which was not very. He was eighteen years old and had never been more than thirty kilometers from Bordeaux before last week.

The parade ground had helped, briefly. He had stood at the gate taking it in: forty men drilling in the middle distance, an NCO's commands carrying clearly across open ground, horses and riders moving with a precision that was not yet natural but was becoming so. Real training. Men learning movements that would keep them alive.

His father's parting words had been practical, as they always were. "Five years, Charles. Make Captain and come home." Not a wish. An instruction, with the weight of the family estate and whatever was left of the Leblanc name sitting behind it. Charles had nodded and climbed into the carriage and spent nine days rehearsing how he would present himself when he arrived.

The rehearsal had not included interrupting a senior officer to discuss stable management.


Elise would have laughed at him. His sister had warned him about exactly this, about talking to fill silence, about saying the wrong thing at the wrong moment. "You do that thing where you keep talking," she had told him the last evening before he left. "The horses do not care, Charles, but people do." She had been right. She usually was.

He found the adjutant on the way back: a sergeant graying at the temples, efficient movements, the look of a man who checked things twice. Orders processed, room number confirmed, Sergeant Mercier confirmed, formation at dawn confirmed. Charles left before he could say anything else unnecessary.


His trunk was still at the gate. He hauled it up three flights of creaking stairs to room twelve. The corridor smelled of woodsmoke and wool. The room was small: two narrow beds, a shared wardrobe, a table under the window. One side was occupied, a jacket on a peg, boots underneath, small effects on the shelf. His roommate was on patrol. Back tomorrow evening.

Through the window: the stable complex. Men still moving below in the last of the afternoon light.

He unpacked quickly. Spare uniform into the wardrobe. Books and writing materials onto his shelf. His father's pocket watch on the ledge where he would see it. At the bottom of the trunk, wrapped carefully, his dress uniform, hung and left alone. Elise's going-away gift was the portable writing desk; he set it on the table.

He should write. Let them know he had arrived safely.

He sat on the edge of the bed with the desk across his knees and looked at blank paper. What would he say? He closed the desk. Later, when there was something worth telling.


The stable complex was stone and timber, larger up close than from the window. Wide doors at both ends for air circulation. Inside: thirty stalls each side, most occupied, the dense familiar smell of hay and horses and worked leather. Charles felt his shoulders drop slightly as he walked through the doors.

The horses were good quality, lighter cavalry mounts, bays and chestnuts, well-kept. He could see it without looking closely.

A corporal directed him to the third stall on the right, where a sergeant was working through an inspection of a bay gelding. Methodical, precise: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, then hooves in the same order, then a hand along the spine. The sergeant moved with the economy of someone who did this without thinking.

Charles watched without announcing himself. He recognized the pattern.

The sergeant finished, patted the horse's neck, straightened.

"Sergeant Mercier?"

Mercier turned. One quick look: new uniform, eighteen years old, trying to seem older. The left hand, Charles noticed, was missing the pinky at the knuckle.

"You would be Leblanc."

"Yes, Sergeant. I was told to report here to draw my mount."

Mercier gestured down the row. "Adjutant assigned you the bay gelding. Fourth stall. Go have a look."

The horse watched Charles approach with calm, dark eyes. Bay, black points, fifteen hands or so, well-proportioned. Charles opened the stall and stepped in without rushing, let the horse take his scent, ran a hand down its neck before checking anything else. Good muscle tone. The horse shifted weight and he noted it: sound, no favoring.

He checked the way his father's stableman had taught him. Hands down each leg in sequence, feeling for heat or swelling. The legs were clean. Hooves: good shoes, properly fitted, no problems. Teeth put the horse at around six years. He ran a hand along the back, checking how a saddle would sit.

The horse turned and investigated his jacket with patient curiosity.

"You know horses, sir."

Sergeant Mercier was in the stall doorway, arms crossed. Not a question.

"Yes, Sergeant. Vineyard outside Bordeaux. Mostly working horses, fields, wagons." Charles gave the bay a last pat. "He is in excellent condition."

"They all are. A cavalryman is only as good as his mount."

Charles nodded. Then: "You check horses the old way, Sergeant. My father's stableman did exactly that sequence. He served in the cavalry in the nineties."

Something shifted in Mercier's expression.

"Did he. What unit?"

"I am not certain. He did not talk about it much."

Sergeant Mercier stepped into the stall and looked at the bay for a moment. "Old cavalry school. Too slow for Napoleon's army now, everything is faster. But thorough. Catches problems before they become injuries."

Charles took a breath. "I studied the 1796 Italian campaign. Rivoli, specifically. The accounts say cavalry covered seventy kilometers in two days over mountain roads. How did you keep the horses sound?"

The sergeant's eyebrows moved. He studied Charles for a moment, recalibrating.

"We walked them. Every meter we could afford, we walked. Saved the galloping for when it mattered. Fed the horses before we fed ourselves: horse breaks down, you are useless. Infantry can limp on bad feet. Cavalry cannot." A pause. "Remounts we requisitioned when we could. Took them when we had to. That is war."

"And reading about it is different from doing it."

"At least you have bothered to read. That is something." Sergeant Mercier straightened. "That horse is yours. Name is Tonnerre. Previous officer transferred to the dragoons last month. He is trained and steady. Whether he holds steady under fire you will not know until it happens. Treat him well and he will keep you alive."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier turned to leave, paused in the doorway.

"You know horses, Lieutenant. Tomorrow you will learn the rest."

His boots were quiet on the stable floor going out.

Charles stood with one hand on Tonnerre's neck for a moment. The horse went back to the hay net.

"Well," Charles said, quietly. "At least that went right."


The mess hall was noisy, officers from various companies eating dinner and talking over each other. Charles stood in the doorway long enough to feel conspicuous, then got his bowl and found a seat at the least crowded end of a table. Two lieutenants at the far end were discussing patrol routes and did not notice him.

The stew was decent. He ate and listened.

"Told him the requisition was approved three weeks ago."

"Rousseau tried to jump that fence, horse refused and he went right over its head."

"Stable duty for showing up drunk, and that is lenient."

This was military life. Not campaigns but the daily architecture of a garrison: paperwork, patrol routes, men jumping fences badly. He had known it would be like this. It was different to sit inside it.

A lieutenant at the next table stood and raised his tin cup. The room quieted.

"A cavalry officer is born to fight, ride fast, drink hard, and die young!"

"Die young!" came back across the room, every cup raised.

Charles raised his with the rest. They drank.

Die young. He had read that toast in books, heard it described. Said here by men who meant it the way you mean a thing you have decided not to think too hard about, it landed differently. Like the difference between reading about a river and standing in the current.

He returned his bowl and cup to the serving area and left without anyone noticing.


Room twelve was dark when he got back. He lit the lamp on the table and sat on the bed to pull off his boots. His father's watch on the shelf. The portable writing desk Elise had given him.

Through the window: the parade ground in full dark, lanterns at the gate, the stable lights still burning. He could hear the garrison settling, boots in the corridor below, distant voices, the watch calling the hour. He would learn those rhythms. In a week they would be automatic.

Five years. Make Captain and go home.

He pulled out the writing desk, set a sheet of paper on the table, and dipped a quill.

Dear Father and Elise,

I have arrived. The garrison is larger than I imagined. I report to my squadron tomorrow.

He stopped. Considered the rest of what he might say and did not write it. He sealed the short letter anyway: they would want to know he had arrived. He set it aside to post in the morning.

He put out the lamp and lay back on the bare mattress, still mostly dressed, looking at the ceiling in the dark.

Five years suddenly felt like a very long time.

CHAPTER 2

The room smelled of woodsmoke and damp wool. Charles stood at the map table and tried to look like he had been doing this for years.

He had not.

Major Beaumont stood over the map: late forties, careful movements, a way of speaking that assumed he would not need to repeat himself. Charles had seen him briefly when he arrived six months ago, processing orders, but this was the first time he had been in the same room for any purpose that mattered.

"Austrian detachment moving north along this road." Beaumont's finger traced it. "Trying to link up with their main force. They will attempt to cross at Gunzburg, here. Small bridge, single span. You are going to stop them."

Charles studied the ground. The road ran through open country before narrowing at the crossing. Woods on the near side, clear ground beyond. A bottleneck between two low hills. If he positioned the section in the trees, he could take the Austrians as they tried to cross. Solid ground to hold. But if he moved fast, crossed early, took the far ridge, he could hit them in column before they knew he was there. He kept that thought to himself.

"How many, sir?"

"Twenty to thirty cavalry. Light horse, probably dragoons. They are not expecting opposition." Major Beaumont looked at him steadily. "You will have fifteen men. Get there before them. Drive them off if possible, delay them if not. Questions?"

"No, sir."

"Sergeant Mercier will accompany you. He knows the country." Beaumont rolled the map. "You leave within the hour."

* * *

The sky was overcast when Charles reached the paddock. The men were already there, moving through preparations with the ease of soldiers who knew their work. Six months of training had built something; he could see it in the way they moved.

Sergeant Mercier stood near the fence watching, arms folded, the impassive expression of a man who had seen everything twice. Sergeant Duval was working down the line of horses, checking straps and loads with methodical patience.

Charles found Tonnerre ready. The bay's ears were forward, coat brushed. Alert. They had built a real partnership over the months, the horse learning to read him, Charles learning to trust the horse's instincts.

"Morning, Lieutenant."

Lieutenant Philippe Arnaud was beside his own mount, adjusting his collar. Always adjusting his collar. He had that easy look about him, the comfort of a man for whom the social part of soldiering came without effort.

"Nervous?" Philippe asked.

"Should I be?"

"Probably." He tightened a saddle strap. "Sergeant Mercier's with you. That is good."

A young corporal was leading his horse past, too young for whatever age he had claimed when enlisting. "Morning, sirs."

"Rousseau." Charles nodded.

The boy's accent was thick Marseille, broad vowels that the other troopers sometimes could not follow. He grinned, eager. "First real mission, sir?"

"For both of us."

"We will show those Austrians."

"Check your equipment again before we mount," Charles said. "And your horse's hooves."

"Yes, sir." Rousseau hurried off.

Philippe watched him go. "Good kid. Green as spring grass."

"He will be fine," Charles said.

Philippe said nothing, which was its own kind of answer.

* * *

They rode out as the sun broke through morning cloud, fifteen men in column, Sergeant Mercier setting the pace at a steady walk through farmland and then into wooded country where the road narrowed.

After half an hour Charles rode forward to Mercier. "We should pick up the pace. Make sure we arrive with time to set up. Maybe catch them before they reach the bridge."

Sergeant Mercier glanced at him. "Horses can handle more, sir. Not too much. It is a fair distance yet."

"Move to a steady trot."

"As you say." Mercier passed the word.

The column picked up speed. Charles felt the satisfying rhythm of it, the section moving as a unit, Tonnerre reaching into the trot with that long, ground-covering stride.

An hour in, Sergeant Mercier dropped back alongside him. "Sir, might ease back a little. We have a ways to go yet."

Charles looked back down the column. The horses were working but not struggling. "They look fine to me, Sergeant."

"They do now." Mercier's tone was neutral. "Just thinking ahead. Tired horse at the start of a fight can mean the difference."

A flicker of irritation. The horses were cavalry mounts. This was what they were bred for. "We will hold this pace."

"As you say, sir." Something in Mercier's voice made Charles glance at him, but the sergeant's face was empty as he dropped back to his position.

* * *

They came through the trees and saw the river. Sergeant Mercier raised his hand. The column slowed to a walk.

"Bridge is ahead, sir. Three hundred meters through those trees. No sign of the Austrians yet."

Charles read the ground. The river was maybe thirty meters wide, shallow, the current visible over stones. A single stone arch, old Roman work by the look of it. The road approached from the west through open fields with no cover. On the far side, a low ridge with scattered trees. Good position if you got there first and the enemy did not know you were waiting.

He looked at the near side. Trees, ground he could hold, the bottleneck in his favor. The sensible position. Major Beaumont had said hold the bridge, drive them off if possible.

He looked at the far ridge.

"Dismount. Walk the horses, check your equipment."

The men dismounted with evident relief. Several began walking horses in slow circles. Sergeant Duval was checking legs and hooves, unhurried.

Charles walked Tonnerre and thought. If he crossed now, took the ridge, he could hit the Austrians in column before they were alert to the danger. Destroy the detachment rather than just driving it off. That was the better outcome, surely. That was what Major Beaumont actually wanted.

He looked at Sergeant Mercier, who was walking his own horse nearby. The sergeant met his eyes and said nothing.

"Mount up. We are crossing."

The men looked surprised, but they obeyed. Lieutenant Philippe rode up alongside. "Not setting up on this side?"

"Better position on the far ridge. We can see them coming, hit them before they are set."

Philippe nodded slowly. His expression stayed uncertain.

* * *

The bridge was hollow under their hooves, crossing in pairs, the sound carrying over the water. On the far side the road climbed a gentle rise and Charles pushed the section up it at a trot.

Tonnerre was breathing harder now. Charles noticed it and pushed the thought down.

He positioned the section just below the crest, out of silhouette, able to see the approach. "Dismount. Check your weapons."

Sergeant Duval was already running his hands down horse legs. Sergeant Mercier stood apart with his back to Charles, and something in the set of his shoulders was not approval.

"Movement, sir."

Sergeant Mercier had his hand shading his eyes. "On the road. Maybe a kilometer."

Charles stepped up beside him. A dark mass resolving into riders. Twenty, perhaps twenty-five. Austrian dragoons in column, blue coats clear even at distance. Still in column, no change of formation, unaware.

His mouth was dry. These were real soldiers. Real enemies with real sabers.

"Mount up. Form line."

The section pulled into saddles fast. The line spread across the road's width. Not parade-ground perfect, but solid.

Four hundred meters. Three hundred and fifty.

He could see individual faces now. A man with a thick mustache. A young one, younger than Rousseau. The column still moving forward, oblivious.

Two hundred meters.

"Sir." Sergeant Duval's voice, carefully neutral. "Your order?"

Charles swallowed. "Forward at the walk."

Fifteen horses stepped over the crest and started down the slope.

The Austrians saw them. Heads turned. Horses pulling up short, a shout in German.

"Draw sabers. Trot."

His own blade rasped out of the scabbard as the line picked up speed. Down the line, fourteen more cleared leather with that distinctive ring. One hundred and fifty meters. One hundred and twenty.

The Austrians were scrambling to deploy. Some fumbling with weapons.

One hundred meters.

"Charge."

* * *

Tonnerre surged and the Austrian line rushed toward him. Blue coats and pale faces and steel.

A carbine cracked. Something whistled past with a high thin whine.

Then they hit.

The impact jolted through his whole body. Tonnerre slammed into an Austrian horse, both animals staggering. Charles's saber came up and across, training moving his arm, and connected with something that gave way with a wet sound that turned his stomach. A man screamed.

The smoke came. Gun-smoke from carbines and pistols rolling over them thick and acrid. He could not see more than a horse-length through it. His eyes watered. His throat burned. Friend from enemy only by coat color when they appeared through the murk.

Sounds layered: men shouting in French and German, horses screaming, steel on steel, the wet impact of blades finding flesh.

Tonnerre turned beneath him on instinct, responding to leg pressure Charles did not remember giving. The horse operating on old battle reflexes from his previous rider.

Something hit Charles's shoulder, hard. Not a blade but a glancing blow. He nearly lost his seat, caught himself with his knees.

His saber was moving. Slash and recover. He felt resistance, heard screaming, kept moving because stopping meant dying.

The smoke thinned. The road visible now, individual combats scattered across it. Corporal Rousseau, young face set fierce, trading blows with an older Austrian. Lieutenant Philippe, still mounted, pressing an attack with that casual competence he brought to everything.

And on the ground: an Austrian trooper not moving, blood dark on trampled grass.

Charles forced his eyes away.

"Sir. Behind you."

An Austrian was charging from his flank, saber raised. Charles brought his blade up too slow, but Tonnerre sidled and the Austrian's swing missed by inches. Charles cut back wild. His saber caught the man's arm and the Austrian howled, wheeling away.

Charles sat there gasping. His right arm ached from gripping the hilt. Tonnerre stood with sides heaving, head dropping.

Around him the fighting was breaking up. The Austrians withdrawing, spurring back down the road. The French were not pursuing. Could not pursue. The horses were spent.

* * *

He counted his men. Most still mounted. Corporal Rousseau had blood on his sleeve but was upright. Sergeant Duval seemed unhurt. Lieutenant Philippe was dismounted, holding his horse by the reins while it stood on three legs.

"Casualties," Sergeant Mercier called. "Call off."

They responded one by one. Ferrand with a broken arm, thrown when his horse stumbled. Two with cuts, neither serious. Three horses lame including Philippe's.

One man did not respond.

Several troopers had gathered around something on the ground. A man face-down. His shako was gone. Blood dark on the back of his coat.

"Who is it?" Charles asked. His voice came out strange.

"Private Moreau, sir." Sergeant Duval's tone was flat. "Took a cut to the back. Bled out before we could reach him."

Moreau. The thick Marseille accent that made the other troopers grin. The habit of checking his carbine three times before mounting, no matter how many times Sergeant Duval told him once was enough. The letter he had been showing around last week, a son named Jean-Claude, has your eyes, everyone says so.

The smell hit: blood and voided bowels and churned earth. Charles's stomach lurched but he swallowed it down.

"Sir." Sergeant Mercier, quiet. "We need to secure the area. They may regroup."

"Pickets on the ridge. Sergeant Duval, see to the wounded. Philippe."

"My horse is lame but I can help." Lieutenant Philippe was already moving.

"I will take care of him, sir," Sergeant Mercier said. "Check on the rest of the men."

Charles made himself walk the line. Speaking to each man, checking wounds, projecting a steadiness he did not feel. The men seemed functional. Shaken, but functional.

He reached Philippe last. "Your horse?"

"Strained tendon. He will need leading back." Lieutenant Philippe looked down the road where the Austrians had gone. "They are not coming back."

"We drove them off."

"We did." Neutral. "Scattered them pretty well."

Charles walked back to Tonnerre. The bay stood with his head low, neck dark with sweat. Charles ran a hand down it, feeling the exhaustion in the horse under his palm.

If he had held on the near side. If he had listened when Sergeant Mercier mentioned the pace.

"Sir." Mercier, behind him. "We need to move. Get back across. The horses need water and rest."

"Yes. Form up the men."

"One more thing, sir." Very quiet. "We will need to bring Private Moreau. And his effects for his family."

Something cold settled in Charles's chest. "I will see to it."

He knelt beside Moreau's body and made himself look. A few coins, a worn letter, a wooden cross on a cord.

He should not read it. He read it anyway.

My dearest Andre, the baby came three days ago. A boy, healthy and strong. He has your eyes, everyone says so. I have named him Jean-Claude, after your father...

Charles folded the letter and put it in his own pocket with the other effects. He stood up and walked away before his face could betray him.

* * *

They reached the near treeline by late afternoon, the horses in poor shape, the section moving slowly with frequent stops. Sergeant Mercier organized the withdrawal with quiet efficiency. Moreau's body wrapped and secured. The wounded on the steadiest animals. Lookouts watching the ridge as they crossed back over the bridge.

In the treeline Sergeant Mercier called a halt. "We will rest here until the horses can travel. Sergeant Duval, set watches. The rest of you, see to your mounts."

Charles dismounted. Tonnerre stood with his head nearly to his knees. Charles loosened the girth, pulled the saddle carefully, checked the legs. The left foreleg was warm. Not terrible, but swollen. Strained. Charles had pushed too hard.

"Lieutenant."

Major Beaumont stood a few paces away. He must have ridden out when they did not return on schedule.

"Sir."

"Walk with me."

In the trees, out of earshot, Major Beaumont stopped. "Report."

Charles gave it: the ride to the bridge, the decision to cross and take the far ridge, the engagement, one dead, three wounded, several horses lame, the Austrians scattered.

Beaumont was quiet for a long moment.

"You pushed the horses hard getting there."

"Yes, sir."

"Sergeant Mercier mentioned concerns about the pace. You continued anyway."

"I wanted to be in position to catch them in the open, sir."

"And you did. You drove them off." Major Beaumont's voice was measured, not condemning, not approving. "But they escaped. Scattered, yes, but they will regroup. You had tired horses that could not pursue, could not finish what you started."

Charles said nothing.

"You accomplished the mission. The bridge is held, the Austrians are scattered and delayed. That counts for something." A pause. "But a patient officer, one who had rested his horses properly and set up on the near side and let the enemy come to him, might have destroyed that detachment completely. As it stands they will be back in action within days."

"Yes, sir."

"Private Moreau is dead. Men die in combat, and you cannot prevent every casualty. But with fresher horses, with the better position, maybe you end the fight faster. Maybe Moreau is still alive. Maybe not. We cannot know. What we do know is that you had a chance to eliminate an enemy unit completely, and you gave it up because you were impatient."

Charles's throat was tight. "I understand, sir."

"Your sergeants tried to tell you. Did you listen?"

"No, sir. Not enough."

"Then listen next time." Major Beaumont's tone softened slightly. Not much. "You will write to Moreau's widow. You will tell her he died serving France, that his sacrifice mattered. And you will learn from this, that the unglamorous work of horse care and proper pacing is not separate from winning battles. It is how you win battles." He turned to leave. "Get your section home safely. This was your first real command. You will make mistakes. The question is whether you learn from them."

He left Charles standing alone in the trees.

* * *

They reached the forward post after dark. The exhausted section filed through in silence. Moreau's wrapped body drew quiet looks from the men they passed.

Sergeant Mercier and Sergeant Duval handled the horses. The wounded went to the surgeon. Moreau's body to the makeshift mortuary.

When everything was done Charles went to the tent he shared with Lieutenant Philippe.

Philippe was sitting on his bedroll. "Hell of a first command."

Charles sat heavily. The tent smelled of damp canvas and woodsmoke.

"We should have destroyed them completely," Charles said. "If I had listened to Sergeant Mercier about the horses. If we had held the near side."

"Maybe. Or maybe they see us in the trees and never commit. Or the fight goes differently and more of us get hurt." Philippe lay back. "Combat is unpredictable. You cannot know every outcome."

"I can listen to experienced NCOs when they give me good advice."

"Yes," Philippe said. "You can do that."

Charles pulled off his boots. His jacket. The uniform still smelled of powder and blood.

He should eat. Should wash. Should start the letter to Moreau's widow.

He lay down and stared at the tent canvas until exhaustion took him.

* * *

In the morning Charles found Sergeant Mercier in the paddock. Tonnerre was in the corner, left foreleg wrapped with a fresh poultice.

"How is he, Sergeant?"

"He will mend. Strained tendon, nothing torn. Needs rest and easy work for a week or two."

The bay nuzzled his pocket when Charles came close. The familiar gesture tightened something in his chest.

"Sergeant. About yesterday. I drew my saber when I gave the trot command. Should I have waited?"

Sergeant Mercier considered it, moving to the next horse as he spoke. "In a small action like yesterday, it does not matter much. But in a larger battle you do not want to signal your commitment until the last possible moment. Keep them guessing. Make them think you might wheel away. Then when you are close enough that they cannot react, that is when you draw."

"I showed my hand too early."

"In yesterday's fight it did not matter. But it is good practice for when it does." Mercier glanced at him. "You are asking because you are thinking about what you could have done better?"

"Yes."

"That is good. Shows you are learning." He returned to his work. "The most important thing from yesterday was not the saber drill, though."

"The horses."

"The horses," Sergeant Mercier agreed. "Everything else stems from that. Fresh horses and you can pursue, finish decisively. Tired horses and the enemy escapes."

"I pushed too hard on the approach."

"You did. I mentioned it. You kept the pace anyway."

Charles said nothing.

Sergeant Mercier set down his tools and turned to face him directly. The shift was deliberate. Sergeants did not usually look at officers quite like this.

"Sir, what I am about to say I would not say in front of the men. Chain of command matters. But here, now: you need to hear this."

"Go ahead, Sergeant."

"You think fast, you are not afraid to act, you care about the men. But yesterday you made a mistake that cost us a complete victory. The Austrians scattered but they will regroup. With fresh horses we could have run them down, taken or killed the lot. Instead they escaped because our mounts were blown."

Charles felt heat in his face. He listened.

"You asked about the Italian campaign before. About those long marches. It worked because we understood that cavalry without horses is just infantry with spurs. You take care of the basics or you lose your ability to fight. That is not a complex lesson, sir. But you have to actually believe it to follow it when the moment comes."

"I believe it now."

Mercier studied him for a moment.

"See that it stays with you," he said, and went back to the horses.

* * *

Charles wrote the letter to Moreau's widow that afternoon, sitting on an ammunition crate outside the surgeon's tent with the sounds of the camp around him.

He wrote four drafts and burned three of them. The fourth he sealed without rereading.

It said that Moreau had died in service of France, that he had been a good soldier, that his death had mattered. All of it was true. None of it was the whole truth. He did not know how to put the whole truth into a letter to a woman he had never met, about a decision he had made, and a son who would grow up with a dead man's eyes.

He put the letter with the effects in his breast pocket. He would give them to Major Beaumont tomorrow to send through the proper channels.

The camp settled into evening around him. Somewhere in the lines a horse was being walked, slow hoofbeats in the dark. Charles sat where he was for a while longer, not quite ready to go back to the tent.

Making Captain meant getting men killed along the way.

Nobody had mentioned that part.

CHAPTER 3

The first week of winter quarters passed in a blur of routine. Morning formation, drill, stable work, evening mess. Charles fell into the rhythm of it, grateful for the predictability after the chaos of Gunzburg. But predictability could not silence the memory of Moreau's face.

Six weeks they had been back now. The army had settled into billets around Munich, the campaign season over, the fields too hard and the roads too uncertain for large movements until spring. Charles drilled his section each morning on the frozen parade ground, reviewed requisitions in the afternoons, ate with the other junior officers in the evenings. The days had a shape. The nights were harder.

During drill one morning he found himself automatically counting his section. Twelve men. He used to have thirteen.

* * *

It happened during the third week. A young lieutenant from another section, newly arrived from Paris with good boots and very little else, decided that the best way to establish his authority was to ride his horse hard at every opportunity. Charles saw it across the parade ground: the animal lathering up in the cold, the lieutenant pushing it through the same movements again and again with no rest between.

Sergeant Mercier was watching too.

"He will lame that horse inside a month," Sergeant Mercier said, not quite to anyone.

"Should someone tell him?"

"I did. Yesterday." Mercier's tone was flat. "He told me he knew how to ride."

They watched the lieutenant put the horse through another fast circuit. The animal's stride was shortening.

"What do you do with a man like that?" Charles asked.

Sergeant Mercier was quiet for a moment. "You wait," he said finally. "He will either learn or he will not. If he learns, good. If he does not, the horse suffers and the man loses his mount and has to answer for it." A pause. "I have been doing this twenty-five years, sir. You cannot make a man careful. You can show him. You can tell him. But the understanding has to come from inside, or it does not come at all."

"What made it come for you?"

Sergeant Mercier looked at him. "Same thing it came from for you at the bridge. A mistake that cost something."

He walked away before Charles could answer.

* * *

The discipline problem with Ferrand came two weeks after that. The trooper had shown up to morning formation visibly drunk, swaying at the end of the line, reeking of something sour. Sergeant Duval had spotted it before Charles did and positioned himself close without drawing attention.

Charles walked the line and stopped in front of Ferrand. The man's eyes were glassy, his jacket buttoned wrong.

"Ferrand."

"Sir." The word came out slightly wrong.

Charles kept his voice level. "Report to Sergeant Duval after formation. You are excused from drill."

"Sir, I can."

"You are excused from drill," Charles said again, the same tone. "Report to Sergeant Duval."

After formation, Sergeant Duval appeared at his elbow. "Stable duty for a week, sir? Or would you prefer I handle the specifics."

"A week of stable duty. And he writes to whoever he needs to write to about where his pay went."

Sergeant Duval nodded and went.

Lieutenant Philippe had watched the whole thing from a few paces away. "That was well handled," he said.

"I did not raise my voice."

"No. That is why it was well handled."

Charles thought about that. He had wanted to. He had felt the irritation moving up his throat and had kept it back by thinking of Major Beaumont in the trees outside Gunzburg: cold, precise, not needing to perform anger because the weight was already there. Authority did not come from volume. He was beginning to understand that.

* * *

He had begun working with some of the greener troopers in the afternoons. Not formally assigned to it, just something that had started because Corporal Rousseau kept appearing at the stable door with questions about horse care, and the other young ones followed.

He taught them the inspection sequence Sergeant Mercier had shown him. Hands down each leg in order, hooves, spine. The specific things to feel for. How to tell a sound horse from one that was hiding a problem. He showed them how to check saddle fit, how to read sweat patterns for where a horse was carrying tension.

They asked clear questions with clear answers. It was easier than almost anything else he did.

"You are a better teacher than you think," Sergeant Mercier told him one afternoon, watching from the doorway.

"I am just repeating what you taught me."

"That is what teaching is." Mercier's tone held something that was not quite approval but was close enough. "The ones who learn well are the ones who can pass it on. The others just carry it."

* * *

Lieutenant Philippe found him in the stables on the evening of the garrison ball, which was not a surprise to either of them.

"You are not dressed," Philippe said.

"I noticed."

"The invitation was not optional, Charles. Major Beaumont said officers are expected."

"I am an officer." Charles did not stop brushing Tonnerre. "I am here."

"You are in a stable."

"Tonnerre does not care about my social skills."

Lieutenant Philippe leaned against the stall door, adjusting his collar with the habitual gesture. He had the look of a man who was going to win this argument and knew it. "I am told there will be a merchant from Lyon and his daughter who speaks three languages and apparently has opinions about cavalry tactics. I thought of you immediately."

"That is suspicious."

"It is a gift. Come. One hour, then you can come back and talk to your horse."

Charles looked at Tonnerre. The bay looked back with the expression of a horse who had no strong feelings either way.

"One hour," Charles said.

* * *

The ballroom was in the requisitioned residence of some local nobleman who had made himself absent when the army arrived. The ceilings were high and the candles many and the room was warm enough that Charles felt overdressed within five minutes of arriving.

He stood at the edge of the floor and watched. Officers in dress uniform, local merchants and their families in the best they had, a few women who moved through the room with the practiced ease of people who knew everyone. The noise was considerable. Someone was playing violin in the corner. Several conversations were happening at once and he could not follow any of them.

Lieutenant Philippe disappeared almost immediately into a knot of people who were apparently delighted to see him. This was always how it went.

Charles accepted a glass of wine from a passing servant, found a position near the wall that allowed him to observe without being observed, and settled in.

He lasted approximately ten minutes before a captain appeared at his elbow.

"Leblanc. From the third section."

"Yes, sir."

The captain was perhaps twenty-five, well-turned-out in a way that suggested money as well as rank, with the kind of easy confidence that came from having always been at the center of whatever room he was in. He extended his hand. "Fontaine. I have been meaning to introduce myself. Heard about the bridge."

"Yes."

"Good result. Could have been cleaner, of course." Captain Fontaine said it pleasantly, as though commenting on the weather. "If you had held the near side as originally intended, you might have destroyed the detachment entirely rather than just driving them off. But for a first command, not bad."

Charles looked at him. "That is what I was told."

"By Major Beaumont, yes. He is cautious." Fontaine's tone made caution sound like a mild affliction. "Some officers believe in patience as a virtue. I tend to find that hesitation costs more than boldness, in the end." He smiled. "But we will see each other in the field. Our approaches will speak for themselves."

Captain Fontaine moved away before Charles could find an answer, which was probably deliberate.

Charles stood there with his glass for a moment.

Fontaine was wrong about the bridge. Charles knew it because Major Beaumont had told him and Sergeant Mercier had told him and he had seen it himself in the lame horses and Moreau's wrapped body. But Fontaine had said it smoothly enough that refusing it felt like the smaller move. That bothered Charles more than the substance of it.

* * *

He found Major Beaumont later, standing at the far edge of the room with a glass he was not drinking from, watching the floor with the expression of a man who had seen too many of these evenings to find them interesting.

Charles positioned himself nearby without quite intruding. After a moment Major Beaumont glanced at him.

"Fontaine found you," Beaumont said.

"He did."

"He finds everyone. It is a skill." Major Beaumont looked back at the room. "Do not mistake the skill for substance."

Charles said nothing.

"He is a competent officer," Beaumont continued, after a pause that suggested he was choosing words with care. "He is also ambitious in a way that will get men killed if it is not managed. He will be told the same thing about you, by someone, and it will be equally true and equally incomplete."

"Is that advice?"

"It is an observation." Major Beaumont drank from his glass. "You are both young officers who have been in one real engagement. The difference between you and Captain Fontaine is that you came back from the bridge asking what you should have done differently. I have not seen Fontaine ask that question yet." He set the glass down. "That may matter. Eventually."

He walked away into the crowd.

Charles stood where he was for a moment, turning that over.

* * *

He left as soon as he could extract himself from Philippe's orbit, which took another twenty minutes and two introductions that went badly in different ways.

The walk back through December cold was a relief after the stuffiness of the ballroom. His breath made small clouds. The garrison was quiet, the parade ground empty, the stables lit from within.

Lieutenant Philippe caught up with him at the stable door.

"You lasted almost two hours," Philippe said. "I am impressed."

"I spoke to four people."

"Five, if you count Major Beaumont, which I do. He never speaks to anyone at these things." Philippe fell into step beside him. "The merchant's daughter was asking about you."

"I did not meet the merchant's daughter."

"No, you were in the corner calculating the minimum number of conversations required to satisfy military protocol. She noticed. Apparently she found it interesting."

Charles stopped at Tonnerre's stall. The bay put his head over the door. "I can barely talk to the men. Adding women to the list seems unwise."

"You will need to learn eventually. What are you going to do, marry your horse?"

"Tonnerre has better manners than most officers I have met."

Philippe laughed. "That is not wrong." He leaned against the wall. "You spoke to Major Beaumont tonight. That was good. He does not speak to just anyone."

"He spoke to me at Gunzburg too. Mostly to tell me what I did wrong."

"And now he speaks to you at garrison balls to tell you how to think about Captain Fontaine. That is different." Lieutenant Philippe straightened. "Get some sleep. Drill at dawn."

He went.

Charles stood with his hand on Tonnerre's neck for a while. The stable was warm and smelled of hay. The horse was unbothered by all of it.

* * *

The ball was three weeks past. The letter had been sitting unwritten since before it.

He had written the letter to Moreau's widow in October. That had taken four attempts. The letter home to his father and Elise had taken until December, which was longer than he wanted to admit.

He sat at the small table in his quarters, Elise's writing desk open, and looked at the blank paper for a while.

He wrote:

Dear Father and Elise,

Winter quarters near Munich. The horses are well and the men in reasonable condition after the autumn campaign. I have learned a great deal from Sergeant Mercier, who served in Italy under the previous campaigns and teaches things the academy does not cover.

He stopped. Read it back. It sounded like a report.

He continued:

I had my first real command in October. We held a bridge crossing near Gunzburg and drove off an Austrian detachment. One man was killed. His name was Andre Moreau. He had a son he had not met. I wrote to his widow.

I think of home often. How was the harvest this year. Is the east vineyard still giving you trouble with the drainage.

He stopped again. There was more he could say and nothing he knew how to say it. He sealed the letter before he could keep revising.

Elise would read between the lines. She always did.

* * *

The orders came in the second week of March. Italy. The army would move south in a fortnight, joining the broader campaign. Charles read his copy of the orders in the paddock, Tonnerre's warm breath on his neck.

Lieutenant Philippe appeared behind him. "Italy, then. Real war, he says."

"We have been at real war."

"Gunzburg was a skirmish. Major Beaumont keeps saying Italy." Philippe adjusted his collar. "He has that tone where you know he has seen the thing he is talking about and you have not yet."

Charles folded the orders and pocketed them. He ran a hand down Tonnerre's foreleg, checking for heat out of habit. The tendon had healed well. The horse was sound.

He thought of Moreau's face on the ground. Of the letter folded in Moreau's pocket. Of Major Beaumont standing in the trees saying your sergeants tried to tell you.

He knew what war cost now. He had one piece of it, from one afternoon on a ridge outside Gunzburg. Italy would have more pieces.

He stayed in the stables until the watch called the late hour, running through equipment checks that did not need running through, because there was nothing else useful to do with the knowledge that in a fortnight everything would be larger.

He knew what war cost now. He had one piece of it. Italy would be larger. He did not know yet what larger meant.

CHAPTER 4

They crossed the Alps in April, which was not the worst time to cross them and was not a good time either.

The road through the passes was narrow enough in places that the column moved single file, horses picking their way along ledges that dropped away to nothing on one side. Tonnerre kept his head down and his feet careful, which was all Charles could ask. The wind came off the snowfields and cut through the wool of his uniform as though it was not there.

The column stretched ahead and behind further than Charles could see, infantry and artillery mixed with cavalry, supply wagons lurching over stones that had been worn smooth by centuries of feet that had nothing to do with war. He had read about this route. Hannibal. Caesar. Napoleon himself, the first Italian campaign a decade ago. The road did not care who used it.

Corporal Rousseau rode beside him for a stretch that afternoon, looking at the peaks with the expression of a man who had grown up in Marseille and had not previously considered that the world contained this much empty space above the treeline.

"I did not know they were this big," Rousseau said.

"The books do not convey it well."

"No, sir." A pause. "Do you think we will find much fighting when we get down?"

Charles looked at the valley somewhere below the cloud layer, invisible from where they rode. "Yes," he said. "I expect we will."

Rousseau considered that. "Good," he said, with the conviction of someone who had not yet found out what he meant by it.

Charles said nothing.

* * *

The plain south of the passes was a different world. Flat, warm, the sky large in a way that made you aware of your own smallness. And the army.

Charles had thought he understood what the Grande Armee was. He had read the dispatches. He had been part of it for eight months. He had not understood it until he rode out of the mountains and saw the camp spread across ten kilometers of Po Valley, a city of canvas and smoke and horses, two hundred thousand men organized into something that moved and fed itself and killed with a precision that no individual man inside it could fully comprehend.

Major Beaumont found him standing at the edge of a rise looking at it.

"First time seeing the full thing?" Beaumont said.

"Yes, sir."

"It does not get smaller." Beaumont looked at it with the expression Charles had come to recognize: not hollow yet, but the distance there, the long familiarity that had taken the wonder out of it. "Get used to it. We march southeast tomorrow. We will be attached to Marshal Massena's corps for the duration."

"Yes, sir. What is the objective?"

"The Austrians have a defensive line along the Tagliamento. We are going to break it." Beaumont turned his horse. "Make sure your section is rested. We will cover forty kilometers tomorrow and fight the day after."

He rode back toward headquarters.

Charles stayed on the rise for another minute, looking at the camp. Somewhere in that mass of men were the engineers who would build the bridges, the artillery that would soften the Austrian line, the infantry who would go in first and take the casualties that cavalry did not have to take. And somewhere was the order that would tell Major Beaumont where to put his squadron, and Major Beaumont would tell Second Lieutenant Leblanc where to put his section, and Second Lieutenant Leblanc would put his section there.

The machine needed all of its parts.

* * *

Lieutenant Philippe found him cleaning his saber that evening, which he had already cleaned twice.

"You are going to wear the blade down," Philippe said, settling onto the ground beside him.

"It occupies the hands."

Philippe pulled out his own blade and inspected it without beginning to clean it. He reached up and adjusted his collar. A habit of his, that: going through the motions of preparation without the purpose. "Beaumont briefed the officers this afternoon. Did you get the same?"

"Tagliamento crossing. We screen the artillery approach, then exploit any gap in their line."

"That is the one." Philippe set the blade across his knees. "How many of your men have been in a real battle? Not a skirmish. A battle."

Charles thought about it. "Sergeant Mercier. Maybe three others."

"Same for me." Philippe looked at the blade. "The rest will find out tomorrow what the difference is."

"Is there a way to prepare them?"

"Mercier says no. Says the only preparation that counts is knowing your job well enough that your hands do it without your head being involved." Philippe was quiet for a moment. "He is probably right."

From somewhere in the camp, a man was singing. Something from Provence, slow and minor, carrying in the still air. Charles did not know the song but recognized the register: the one people used when they were not performing for anyone.

"Philippe."

"Yes."

"Tomorrow. If something happens to me."

"Nothing is going to happen to you."

"If it does. Elise is in Bordeaux. The estate is outside the city, southeast, near the Garonne. My father's name is Henri."

Philippe was quiet for a moment. "I know."

"I wrote it down. It is in my left boot."

"Charles."

"In case."

"I know where Bordeaux is," Philippe said. "I have had your family's address memorized for four months. You wrote it down and put it in your boot last October, the night before we left for the first patrol." He adjusted his collar. "You do this every time."

Charles looked at him. "I do?"

"Every time. Boot, left side, folded small." Philippe stood, sheathed his blade. "Get some sleep. I will see you in the morning."

Charles sat with the cleaned saber in his lap for a while after Philippe left, listening to the singing he could no longer hear.

* * *

Morning came gray and cold, which was how all mornings came now.

Sergeant Mercier had the horses ready before Charles reached the line. The sergeant moved through the inspection sequence without looking up: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Then hooves, each one lifted and examined. Then the spine, both hands running from withers to croup, the missing pinky of his left hand pressing steadily along the horse's back. He did it while Charles stood beside him, and he did not hurry.

"Philippe's section goes first wave," Charles said. "We are in support. Second wave, seventy meters back."

Sergeant Mercier set down a hoof, moved to the next horse. "Do not stop in the middle."

"I was not planning to."

"Men slow down when they see the first volley hit. They lose their nerve for a moment. Not deserting, just slowing." Mercier's hands moved along a foreleg, checking for heat. "If you feel the section slow, you push through them. Do not stop in the middle of that field."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Sergeant Mercier straightened, patted the horse's shoulder, moved to the next one.

* * *

Philippe was adjusting his collar when Charles found him before the line.

"It is manageable," Philippe said, with the careful tone of someone building a belief while they say it, testing the weight of the words. "The guns are light. By the time we are at a hundred meters, they will not have time to reload."

"They will have time for two volleys," Charles said.

"Probably two, yes."

They stood beside their horses. Charles wanted to say something. He could feel the shape of it, some version of last night's conversation, something that continued it. But the men were nearby and the moment was not right, and anyway he did not know exactly what he wanted to say.

"My section goes first," Philippe said. Not asking. Just confirming. "You come through when we reach the guns."

"Yes."

Philippe nodded. He looked at Charles for a moment, not the tired look from last night but something steadier, and then he reached up and adjusted his collar one more time, a reflex.

"Brief your men," he said. "Do not let them think too long."

He turned and walked back to his section.

Charles watched him go for a second. Then he went to find his own.

* * *

He found Sergeant Mercier a moment later, checking the girth on his horse for the third time.

"Two volleys," Charles said. "Maybe three if they are fast. Philippe's section takes the battery. We push through, deal with the flanks."

Sergeant Mercier checked the girth. Said nothing.

"Varennes will not move the approach. Beaumont tried."

Mercier released the stirrup. "Do not stop in the middle."

Charles said, "Yes, Sergeant."

Sergeant Mercier swung up.

* * *

Philippe's section moved at ten past nine, when the light was flat and even and gave no shadows.

Charles held his section in column, seventy meters back. From here, he could see the junction, a low stone wall and a farmhouse beyond it, the four gun carriages arranged in a rough line, the Prussian cavalry visible as dark shapes behind them, formed in two groups on the flanks. The beet field between here and there was frozen in ridges from the cart tracks and scattered with the black stumps of harvested plants.

Philippe's section went at the trot, then the canter. Eleven men in an extended line, sabers not yet drawn. They had made it perhaps a hundred and fifty meters when the guns fired.

The sound was flat in the cold air, not the rolling crash of the summer battles but something more compressed, efficient. Two horses went down in the first volley. One immediately, one struggling for a moment and then still. The section kept moving, closing the gap. Charles felt Tonnerre shift beneath him, a muscle-tightening that was not panic, just awareness, and he put a hand briefly on the gelding's neck.

The second volley came at a hundred and thirty meters. One man. The section barely paused. They were close enough now that Charles could hear them, hooves on frozen ground, a shout from somewhere in Philippe's line, and then they were at the guns, and the surface of it became immediate and particular and left no room for watching.

Charles pressed his section forward.

Through the gap in the stone wall. The guns already taken. Prussian artillerymen running or on the ground. He swept left where one of the Prussian cavalry groups was reforming, caught them before they had any order to them, drove them back in two hard exchanges that he would not remember clearly afterward. The second group melted north, into the bare poplar trees at the edge of the field.

Then it was over.

Charles pulled Tonnerre up near the farmhouse wall. His hands were shaking slightly, the fine trembling that came in the aftermath and lasted maybe two minutes before it stopped. He breathed. He counted his section: Rousseau, Sergeant Duval, Marchetti, the two Girard cousins, the others. All of them there, three men with cuts, one with an arm he was holding odd, none of them down. He looked for Philippe.

He did not see him.

* * *

One of Philippe's men was kneeling beside a gun carriage, looking up when Charles arrived.

"Corporal Bonnard," Charles said. "Where is he?"

Bonnard was already on his feet, stepping back to give Charles room, and that was how Charles knew before he saw him. The specific way Bonnard moved, the expression of someone who has realized they need an officer.

Philippe was on the ground behind the gun carriage, back against the wheel. He was conscious. He had both hands pressed to his chest, high left side, and there was blood on the hands and on the front of his coat. His face was pale but his eyes were clear, tracking Charles as Charles knelt beside him.

"The junction," Philippe said.

"Taken. Both flanking groups dispersed."

"Good." He breathed carefully, the breath shallow and deliberate. "The guns?"

"Ours."

"Good," he said again.

Charles put his hand over Philippe's hands and applied pressure. The wound was high and left and there was too much blood for a surface wound. He looked up at Corporal Bonnard. "Surgeon. Now."

Bonnard was already moving.

Charles kept his eyes on Philippe. "Where does it hurt?"

"It is where it is." A pause. A careful breath. "I cannot feel my left hand."

"The surgeon is coming."

Philippe nodded, eyes closing briefly, then opening again. He looked at Charles with the slightly unfocused quality of a man navigating pain. "You are bleeding," he said.

Charles had not noticed. There was a cut on his forearm, shallow, cloth already sticking to it, not serious. "It is nothing."

"Good."

They stayed like that for a moment, Charles's hand over Philippe's, the gun carriage at Philippe's back. The cold was very clear. From somewhere across the junction, men were shouting about horses. The ordinary noise of aftermath.

Charles looked at Philippe's collar. It was turned up wrong on one side, askew from the fall or the press of the engagement. He reached with his free hand and straightened it.

The gesture was ridiculous. The collar made no difference. Philippe watched it happen with an expression that was careful and grateful in a way that had nothing to do with the collar.

"Captain and home," Philippe said. "Still the plan?"

"Still the plan."

"Good." He shifted slightly, winced. "My section will need someone. After."

"Your section is fine," Charles said. "They came through."

"They will need someone to look at them the way you do." Philippe's eyes were tracking carefully, fighting to stay on Charles. "Like they are capable. You do that. You do not notice it."

"Do not use up your breath."

"I am using it correctly." But he stopped talking.

The surgeon arrived in six minutes. Charles counted, afterward, in the journal.

* * *

There were two lucid stretches in the two hours that followed.

The first was brief. Philippe surfacing from the surgeon's work to say something about Renard, that someone should make sure Renard was checked properly, that the gelding had been limping slightly this morning. Charles said he would see to it. Philippe believed him and settled.

The second came later, when the light was already changing. He surfaced clearly, enough that he looked at Charles with real focus.

"Elise wrote me," he said. Not remembering that he had said it last night, or perhaps knowing and saying it again. "She asked me to look after you. I told her you did not need it."

"You told me."

"She was right, though." A breath, slow and even. "You do not need looking after. But you could let people try." He paused. "Go home before it gets comfortable, Charles. The longer you wait, the better you will be at it, and the harder it will be to leave." His voice was quiet, tired, not pressing. Just saying the true thing. "I know how you think. You will start calculating who depends on you. You will find you are useful. It is easy to stay for that."

Charles did not answer.

"Go home when you can." He reached up toward his collar, a reflex, but his hand made it only as far as his chest before it stopped. He could not quite reach it.

Charles reached over and straightened it.

"Thank you," Philippe said. Something in his face settled, the last small tension leaving it.

He went quiet after that, in and out, the breathing slow and occasional. At some point in the late afternoon the breathing changed. Not a moment, not a sound, just a quality of the silence that was different from the silences before it. The surgeon shook his head. He said something to Charles. Charles heard the words and understood them and filed them somewhere for later.

Outside, the junction was their junction now, and men were moving around it, doing the ordinary work.

* * *

He found Sergeant Mercier at the horse line.

The sergeant was going through the inspection sequence, methodical, on a horse that had come through without injury and needed no inspection. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Hooves. Spine. He worked through it in full, and when it was done he started it again, more slowly.

"Philippe is dead," Charles said.

Sergeant Mercier's hands stilled on the horse's flank. He stood that way for a moment, looking at nothing in particular. Then he continued the inspection, the spine, both hands moving steadily.

"I know," he said.

He finished the sequence. He straightened. He looked at Charles with the specific quality of attention Mercier reserved for things that mattered, not long, not dramatic, just clear and direct.

"The pressure is starting," Sergeant Mercier said.

"I know."

"You do not, yet." He moved to the next horse, started at the left foreleg. "You did well today. The men know that. Philippe's section knows that. Varennes knows it, which he will not say, but Major Beaumont does and he will." He pressed two fingers against the foreleg, checking. "Men will start looking at you differently. They will hand you problems because you solve them. They will ask you to take on the next thing because the last thing went well. And you will be able to do it." He paused. "That is the dangerous part."

Charles said nothing.

"The plan was specific," Sergeant Mercier said. "Captain. Then home. Not Captain and the next engagement after that, not Captain and we need you through the winter, not Captain and. It does not finish itself, Lieutenant. It is never finished. There will always be one more problem in front of you that you can solve and they cannot. That is not a reason to stay."

"I know."

"You will feel it differently in six months." Sergeant Mercier moved to the right foreleg. "I am telling you now while you still believe me."

He kept going down the horse, unhurried. He did not look at Charles again.

Charles stayed a moment longer, watching the sequence, and then he turned and went to find his journal.

* * *

The page at the back was two pages now, names in the small careful print he used for entries he wanted to keep. He found the bottom of the second page, found the space below the last name, and wrote Philippe's.

Arnaud, Philippe. Lieutenant. November 1806.

He looked at it for a moment. Then he closed the journal.

Philippe's section was quartered in the farmhouse, twelve men using the main room and the attached barn, and Charles found them at dusk by the state of them. Not disorganized, but quiet in the specific way of men who have lost someone and do not yet know the new shape of things.

Corporal Bonnard looked up when Charles came through the door. Bonnard was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been Philippe's corporal for four months, a steady man who checked everything twice and did not make noise about it. He looked at Charles with the expression of someone who has run out of the thing he was relying on and is deciding, in real time, whether the next available thing will hold weight.

Charles said: "Horses watered and fed?"

"Yes, sir."

"Bonnard. Three of your horses are short on forage. Get with Rousseau and go to the depot before they close it. They will tell you they do not have it. Tell them you need a signature from Major Beaumont authorizing priority issue, and if they want to argue it, I will be along in an hour."

Bonnard was already on his feet. Something in him had straightened slightly.

"The rest of you," Charles said, looking around the room. "Tonight is tonight. Tomorrow morning I want a headcount on equipment. Carbines, sabers, saddlery. Anything that needs attention before we move. Write it down. If you do not write it down, it does not exist." He paused. "You came through the junction. That was well done."

He did not make it more than that.

He met with Sergeant Duval about the casualty report. He walked the perimeter once, looking at the horses. He had the argument with the supply depot. Not Bonnard's forage argument, which Rousseau handled efficiently, but a separate argument about canteens and the fact that the resupply schedule was two days off and someone at the depot had decided that was not his problem. Charles made it his problem, calmly, specifically, with dates and regulations and the promise of Beaumont's signature, and resolved it in twenty minutes.

The depot sergeant watched him leave with the expression of a man who had expected to win that argument.

* * *

At the picket line, late, Tonnerre was working through inadequate hay with the focused patience he brought to most things. Charles stood beside him in the dark. The junction was quiet now, the farmhouse lights low, the sentries moving in their irregular rotation.

Philippe had said it last night and again this afternoon. The words had a different weight now, the second hearing.

When the numbers get comfortable, Major Beaumont had said, four months ago. That is when you should worry.

It is never finished, Sergeant Mercier had said tonight. Not bitterly. Just as a fact.

Charles thought about Corporal Bonnard's face in the farmhouse, that moment of deciding whether the next available thing would hold weight. How quickly Bonnard had come to his feet. How the section had organized itself around two sentences about forage.

He had noticed it. Filed it. Moved on.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck. The gelding kept eating.

Philippe's name was on the list now. Two pages. The list had been one column when he started it in September. Now it ran into the margins.

He had not written to Elise yet. He would have to write to her, and he would have to tell her about Philippe, and he did not know yet how to do that. Philippe had said competence was not care. Elise had said it first, to Philippe, and Philippe had carried it to Charles, and Charles had understood it immediately and had not known what to do with it.

The cold was very clear. Somewhere north of them, past the bare poplars and the frozen fields, there were more Prussians, more junctions, more approaches with four hundred meters of open ground.

But something in the calculation had changed. Not the numbers. The numbers were the same. Something in how the numbers felt. He turned it over and could not find the edge of it.

Charles stood at the picket line for a long time.

He did not sleep.

CHAPTER 5

The Italian campaign did not end at the Tagliamento. It kept going, the way campaigns did, the army moving southeast through the summer heat, one engagement becoming the next with barely enough time between them to bury the dead and rest the horses.

Charles kept a record in the back of his field journal, not a diary, just dates and locations and the names of men lost or wounded. By August the list was two pages long. He was not sure why he kept it. He told himself it was practical, that the names mattered for the official record. He did not examine the other reasons closely.

The work of it was what he had not expected. Not the fighting, which was terrible and also contained within itself a clarity that ordinary life did not have. The work between the fighting: the requisition forms, the horse inspections, the discipline cases, the endless correspondence with supply officers about rations and forage, the mapping of roads that turned out to be tracks, the tracking of men who were sick or lame or drunk or simply not where they were supposed to be. He had thought of cavalry as motion and violence. Mostly it was administration.

Sergeant Duval handled most of the administration, had handled it before Charles arrived and would handle it after, but Charles was learning the structure of it, the way the paperwork connected to the reality it described, the way a ration count on paper became hungry men or fed ones depending on whether the numbers were right. He found he was good at it. This did not surprise him. He had grown up tracking the estate accounts, harvest yields, the cost of cooperage against the price of wine in a good year and a bad one.

What surprised him was that being good at administration felt like being good at something small. At eighteen he had imagined himself in the smoke, blade out, leading charges that would be remembered. At nineteen he spent Tuesday afternoons arguing with supply sergeants about whether three kilos of oats per horse was adequate given the distances being covered. It was adequate. He was right. The horses were better for it. Nobody would remember it.

* * *

There was a second engagement in July, a rearguard action outside a small town whose name Charles did not bother to learn because it was not on any map he had access to. The Austrians were pulling back and leaving a cavalry screen to buy time, and Major Beaumont's squadron was sent to push through it. The infantry battle for the town itself was already underway on the right flank when they rode up; Charles could hear it before he could see it, the rolling exchange of musketry, then the heavier percussion of a cannon working at close range, then a sound under all of it that he had no category for yet, a collective human sound, many men together, neither shouting nor screaming but something between.

Charles's section took the left flank. The Austrian screen was a hundred men to his fifteen, which was not as bad as it sounded because they were trying to delay, not fight, and a screen that stands and fights loses its purpose. He recognized this before he gave the order, felt the shape of the situation the way you felt weather coming, and put the section at a diagonal that threatened their line of retreat rather than their line of resistance. To his right the infantry fight continued, smoke thickening over the town roofline, the musketry now in ragged bursts rather than volleys, which meant it had moved into the streets.

The Austrian screen broke without a charge. The cavalry peeled away and rode, and Charles let them go because he did not have the numbers to pursue safely and because letting them go meant no one on his side got hurt. He brought the section to a halt on a low rise and watched the Austrians diminish into the distance, and became aware, now that there was nothing immediate requiring his attention, of what the morning had produced in the field to his right.

The town was two hundred meters away. The infantry had taken it or were taking it; the musketry had slackened to occasional single shots, which usually meant one side had given way. The road running from the town toward his position had a wagon overturned across it, and near the wagon a man in French blue sitting with his back against a wheel and both hands pressed to his abdomen, looking at his hands. Beside him another man lay face-up in the road with no one attending him.

Further along the road two soldiers were walking away from the town, one leaning on the other, moving with the careful deliberate gait of men whose bodies had become suddenly uncertain. Behind them a sergeant directed three men carrying a door they had taken off its hinges, using it as a stretcher. Charles looked at what was on the stretcher long enough to understand what he was looking at, then looked back at his section.

Corporal Rousseau was watching the road. The eagerness that had been on his face at the start of the campaign was present now in a different form, compressed into something quieter and more careful, the expression of a man absorbing information he had not previously had.

The section had come through the morning without a casualty. This was correct. Charles had made the right decisions and they had worked and no one was hurt. Forty meters to his right the infantry had made different decisions, or had not been able to make any decisions because the ground gave them no choices, and the man by the wagon wheel was still looking at his hands.

Sergeant Mercier rode up beside him. Neither of them looked at the road.

"They did not want to fight. So we did not make them."

"No. But a less patient officer would have charged anyway, to finish what was already finished." A pause. "The infantry to our right had no such choice."

"No," Charles said. "They did not."

Sergeant Mercier looked at him for a moment. "The men on that stretcher did their job correctly too. It cost them anyway." He said it without judgment, the way he said most things. "That is also what war is."

They rode back to the squadron. The road took them past the wagon. Charles did not intend to look and looked anyway: the man who had been sitting against the wheel was gone, moved by someone, and there was a dark stain on the road where he had been. They reached the squadron and Charles reported to Major Beaumont: screen broken, no casualties in his section. Beaumont nodded and made a note. That evening he assigned Charles the reconnaissance duties for the following week's advance. Charles understood it to be a form of communication, and felt the shape of what was being communicated, and was not certain it was entirely a good thing.

* * *

Corporal Rousseau had changed over the summer. Not in the ways Charles had expected, not harder or quieter or more careful. The eagerness was still there but it had found a different object. At the start of the campaign Rousseau had wanted to fight. By August he wanted to be good at fighting, which was not the same thing and was considerably more useful.

He started asking different questions. Not when will there be action but how did you know the screen would break, not what is the plan but why that plan and not another. He began taking the younger troopers through the same afternoon training Charles had started in Munich, the horse inspections, the equipment checks, the habit of looking at ground before riding over it. He did not wait to be asked. He simply started doing it.

"You are going to make him a sergeant early," Lieutenant Philippe said one evening, watching Rousseau put a trooper through a saddle inspection for the third time.

"He is not ready yet."

"No. But he will be." Philippe adjusted his collar. "He is like you were in Munich, except warmer."

"I was not cold."

"Charles. You absolutely were." Philippe said it without heat, fondly. "You were correct about everything and communicating that fact to the men by the manner in which you existed in their vicinity."

Charles thought about that. "And now?"

"Now you are correct about most things and have learned to let people discover that themselves rather than announcing it." Philippe paused. "It is an improvement. Rousseau is already there. He has a gift for making people feel capable."

"That is a useful gift."

"More useful than yours or mine, in some respects." Philippe looked at the evening sky. "He is going to be a very good soldier if he lives long enough."

The qualifier sat in the air between them. Neither of them addressed it.

* * *

The promotion came in September, six weeks before the end of the Italian campaign.

Major Beaumont called him into the headquarters tent on a Tuesday morning. Charles stood at attention while Beaumont read the relevant portion of the orders in the flat tone of a man for whom paperwork was paperwork regardless of content.

"By order of the Emperor, Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc is hereby promoted to the rank of Lieutenant, with all privileges and responsibilities attendant thereto. Effective the fifteenth of September, year of our Lord eighteen hundred and six."

Beaumont set the paper down. "Congratulations, Lieutenant."

"Thank you, sir."

A pause. Beaumont picked up the next paper on his desk, which was a requisition form. "That will be all."

Charles walked out of the headquarters tent and stood in the September heat for a moment.

Lieutenant. He had been working toward it since Munich, since his father's five-year plan, since the garrison, since the first fumbled months of learning what cavalry actually meant. He had imagined this moment often, usually in the terms of what it would mean for the Leblanc name, the way his father would read the letter, Elise's response.

What he felt was mainly: three years in. Two more to Captain. He was on schedule.

He went to find Sergeant Mercier to tell him the section would need to sew a new rank insignia onto the spare uniform.

* * *

That evening Lieutenant Philippe produced a bottle of something from his saddlebag that was approximately wine and poured two cups.

"To Lieutenant Leblanc," Philippe said. "Who achieved this distinction by being relentlessly correct about horses and supply chains."

"You make it sound like an insult."

"It is a toast. Drink."

They drank. The wine was not good. The campaign had used up all the good wine weeks ago.

"How does it feel?" Philippe asked.

"The same."

"It always does at first." Philippe held his cup and looked at it. "I made Lieutenant in April. I thought I would feel different. More like an officer."

"Were you not already an officer?"

"In rank. I mean in here." Philippe touched his chest in a way that was only slightly theatrical. "I thought the promotion would settle something. Confirm I was in the right place."

"Did it?"

"No." Philippe drank. "I think the confirmation does not come from the rank. It comes from something else. The men. The way they look at you when it matters." He was quiet for a moment. "Your section follows you, Charles. Not because of your rank. They started following you before you had a rank that warranted it."

Charles did not know what to say to that.

"That is what I mean," Philippe said. "You are already what the rank says you are. The paper is just the army catching up with the fact."

He poured more wine. It did not improve with a second cup.

* * *

The campaign ended in October with the army occupying a line of small Italian towns that the maps called victories and that the towns themselves experienced as a complicated inconvenience. Charles's section was billeted in a village outside Udine whose name he could pronounce by the third day and not before.

He sat one evening on the low wall outside the house they had requisitioned and watched the village doing what villages did in the evening: a woman drawing water from the well, two old men talking at a doorway in a language he did not speak, a dog moving between the houses on some private business. Across the square, a merchant stall stood empty, its shelves cleared where the quartermasters had been through. Charles noticed it and looked away.

He had the field journal open, the list in the back. He read through it. Tagliamento: Lacroix broken arm, Virel captured recovered. The rearguard at the unnamed town: no casualties in his section. He had written it that way because that was the accurate entry for his section. The accurate entry for the infantry engagement happening simultaneously would have been written by someone else in a different journal and would have looked different.

He had done this. Not alone, not without Sergeant Mercier and Sergeant Duval and Lieutenant Philippe and the training and the luck that you could not control. But he had done his part correctly. He had read the ground and made the decisions and kept his men alive and accomplished the missions, and the record showed it in the absence of names on his list.

He was good at this.

The thought arrived without ceremony, the way true things often did. Not pride exactly, not satisfaction. A recognition. He had been trying to be good at this for two years and it turned out he was, and the feeling was not what he had expected it to be when he had imagined it at eighteen on a carriage road in Bordeaux.

He had imagined glory. What he had was competence, and competence felt like a different thing entirely. Quieter. More certain. Less like winning and more like understanding a language well enough to stop translating it.

He closed the journal and watched the woman at the well finish drawing water and carry it inside. The old men at the doorway were still talking. The dog had disappeared.

He was good at this. He thought about Moreau's widow, about Lacroix's broken arm, about the four hundred and twelve at the Tagliamento, about Virel in an Austrian field hospital for six weeks. About Beaumont saying when the numbers get comfortable.

Being good at this was not uncomplicated.

* * *

He wrote home about the promotion.

The letter took him two evenings. The first evening he wrote the formal version, the one that reported the promotion and the campaign and the successful conclusion of the Italian operations. He sealed it and then opened it again and wrote a second draft.

The second draft said:

Dear Father and Elise,

I am promoted to Lieutenant. This is the plan working as it should. Two more years to Captain, perhaps less if the campaigns continue to go as this one has. I am on schedule to come home.

I am good at this, Father. I do not know if that is what you expected when you sent me. I am not sure it is what I expected. But I am good at it, which feels important to report, though I am not certain how to explain what it costs.

The section is in good condition. Lacroix's arm healed well. The horses are sound. We are in a village outside Udine that has a well in the square and two old men who talk outside a doorway every evening and have done so, I imagine, for forty years, and who will be doing so again when we are gone.

Tell Elise I am sorry I missed whatever she has done this year. I expect she has done things. Tell her to write and tell me.

Your son,

Charles

He sealed the second draft and addressed it and left the first draft in the candle flame.

* * *

Sergeant Mercier found him at the horses the morning before they broke camp for the march north.

"Sir. A moment."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier ran a hand down Tonnerre's neck, not checking anything, just the habitual contact. "You have done well this campaign. The section has done well."

"Thank you, Sergeant."

"I am not saying it to be pleasant." Mercier's tone was the one that meant he was about to say something he had been thinking about for some time. "I am saying it because I want to ask you something."

Charles waited.

"When this is done. When you have made Captain. What then?"

Charles looked at him. "Home. The estate. That was always the plan."

"I know it was the plan." Mercier was looking at Tonnerre. "I have seen other officers with plans. Good officers. Men who knew what they were doing and why. And I have seen them promoted and given more men and more responsibility, and the plan gets pushed forward. One more campaign. One more season. The army does not give men up easily when they are useful."

"I am aware of that."

"Are you?" Mercier looked at him now, directly. "You are useful, sir. You are more useful than you were six months ago, which means you will be given more to do. That is how it works."

Charles said nothing.

"I am not telling you to leave. I am not telling you to stay. I am an NCO and it is not my place to tell a lieutenant anything about his career." Mercier straightened. "I am telling you to keep the plan in your head. Keep it specific. Captain, then home. Not Captain and then one more thing and then home."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier nodded once and went back to his work.

Charles stood with Tonnerre for a while, the horse warm against his hand in the October cold.

The plan was still clear. He was still on schedule. He was Lieutenant Leblanc, two years from Captain, and after Captain, home.

He told himself this several times, which was not something he had needed to do before.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 6

The cold in Prussia was a different animal than the cold in the Alps.

In the mountains, cold was vertical: it came off stone faces and glacial wind and the altitude made it clean, if brutal. You understood it. In Prussia, the cold was flat and gray and everywhere, rising from the mud and the dead fields in equal measure, settling into the folds of your coat and the gaps in your boots. By the third week of November, Charles had stopped noticing individual moments of discomfort and begun simply existing inside a general state of it.

They were somewhere east of the Oder, pushing toward Küstrin, following a road that had been churned by three armies and a hundred supply wagons into something barely navigable. The Grande Armée was in pursuit now, mopping up Prussian remnants after Jena, not the clean work of an advancing campaign but the slow, irregular work of grinding down a broken force that didn't know yet it was finished. The Prussians were fighting in patches. A rear guard here, a cavalry screen there. Stubborn holdouts at road junctions that made no sense to hold.

This morning, the squadron had been detached from the main column to secure a junction two kilometers west of a supply depot the army needed.

The briefing had taken ten minutes. Colonel Varennes, a staff officer attached from Paris five days ago with new orders and a general's manner, had unrolled his map on an ammunition box and explained the situation in the tone of a man explaining something simple to people he suspected of being slow. The junction was held by Prussian cavalry and a half-battery of four light guns. The approach was four hundred meters of open ground, a harvested beet field flat and completely without cover. The guns would have clean sight lines for the entire distance.

Beaumont had stood across the ammunition box from Varennes, looking at the map. "The eastern track," Beaumont said, not raising his voice. "If we take the horses along the drainage ditch..."

"The eastern track is soft ground," Varennes said. "We'd lose the element of speed."

"We'd lose half the first wave to the battery before they reach the guns."

Varennes folded the map. "The order stands."

* * *

Charles found Philippe that night at the picket line.

It was late, well past the time a man had any business being out of his bedroll, and the temperature had dropped to the point where the mud on the road was beginning to crust around the edges. Philippe was at the far end of the line, checking the knots on Renard's lead rope, checking them with the focused attention of someone who'd already checked them twice.

"You're not worried about the knots," Charles said.

"The knots are excellent." Philippe didn't look up. "I'm enjoying the company."

Charles stopped beside him. The horses stood close together in the dark, breath rising in small clouds. Down the line, a sentry moved past, boots crunching on the frosted grass, and then was gone.

"The staff position," Philippe said, after a moment. "Beaumont told me he offered it. In October."

"He did."

"You didn't tell me."

Charles said nothing.

"I heard it from Duval, Charles. Duval." Philippe finally looked at him. "You know what Duval told me? He said you turned it down in about thirty seconds. Like there was nothing to think about."

"There wasn't."

"There was a great deal to think about." Philippe pulled his collar straight, the automatic gesture he'd been making since Charles first met him. "A staff position means another three years of service, minimum."

"Yes."

"So you chose this instead."

"Five years," Charles said. "Make Captain and go home. A staff appointment changes the equation."

Philippe looked at him for a long moment, something behind his eyes that wasn't quite the easy amusement he usually wore. He looked tired. He had looked tired for a week, which was unremarkable in itself, they were all tired, but there was a quality to it now, a quietness.

"Élise wrote me," Philippe said.

Charles didn't answer immediately. "I know she writes you."

"She asked me to look after you." Philippe's mouth curved slightly. "I wrote back and told her you didn't need it. That you were the most capable man in the squadron and possibly the most infuriating." He paused. "She wrote back and said competence wasn't care."

The horses shifted. Somewhere in the dark, a horse stamped once and went still.

"She's right, of course," Philippe said. "She usually is, your sister." He adjusted his collar again. "You don't tell people things, Charles. You talk to no one about you. You know that?"

"I know."

"It's not a complaint. I have just watched you for a year. You'll talk tactics until midnight. You'll sit with a man when he's frightened and you'll find the exactly right thing to do. But you talk to no one about yourself." Philippe's voice was mild, not pressing. "I've been trying to figure out if you know it and don't mind, or if you genuinely don't notice."

"I know."

"Which is it?"

Charles thought about that honestly. "Both," he said. "Depending on the day."

Philippe laughed, quietly, a real one. He gave Renard one last pat on the neck and stepped back. "Go home when you can, Charles. That's all I mean. Don't let the plan get longer."

"Five years," Charles said. "Captain and home. Nothing longer."

Philippe nodded. He was already turning back toward the bivouac, collar up against the cold, hands stuffed in his coat. "Good night," he said, without looking back.

Charles stayed at the picket line a little longer. Tonnerre was six horses down, and he found him in the dark by feel, the particular shape of the gelding's nose, the warmth of his breath. He stood there a moment, not thinking about anything specific.

Then he went back to his bedroll, lay down with his boots on, and put his hand on the left one. The paper was there. He had put it in before dark, his family's address in Bordeaux in Élise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card. He'd done it before every engagement since Günzburg. He wasn't sure why, exactly. The address hadn't changed. Élise would still know where to send the letter regardless.

His hand rested on the boot. He fell asleep like that.

* * *

Morning came gray and cold, which was how all mornings came now.

Mercier had the horses ready before Charles reached the line. The sergeant moved through the inspection sequence without looking up: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Then hooves, each one lifted and examined. Then the spine, both hands running from withers to croup. He did it while Charles stood beside him, and he did not hurry.

"Philippe's section goes first wave," Charles said. "We're in support. Second wave, seventy meters back."

Mercier set down a hoof, moved to the next horse. "Don't stop in the middle."

"I wasn't planning to."

"Men slow down when they see the first volley hit. They lose their nerve for a moment. Not deserting, just slowing." Mercier's hands moved along a foreleg, checking for heat. "If you feel the section slow, you push through them. Don't stop in the middle of that field."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier straightened, patted the horse's shoulder, moved to the next one.

* * *

Philippe was adjusting his collar when Charles found him before the line.

"It's manageable," Philippe said, with the careful tone of someone building a belief while they say it. Testing the weight of the words. "The guns are light. By the time we're at a hundred meters, they won't have time to reload."

"They'll have time for two volleys," Charles said.

"Probably two, yes."

They stood beside their horses. Charles wanted to say something. He could feel the shape of it, some version of last night's conversation, something that continued it, but the men were nearby and the moment wasn't right, and anyway he didn't know exactly what he wanted to say.

"My section goes first," Philippe said. Not asking. Just confirming. "You come through when we reach the guns."

"Yes."

Philippe nodded. He looked at Charles for a moment, not the tired look from last night but something steadier, and then he reached up and adjusted his collar one more time, a reflex.

"Brief your men," he said. "Don't let them think too long."

He turned and walked back to his section.

Charles watched him go for a second. Then he went to find his own.

* * *

He found Mercier a moment later, checking the girth on his horse for the third time.

"Two volleys," Charles said. "Maybe three if they're fast. Philippe's section takes the battery. We push through, deal with the flanks."

Mercier checked the girth. Said nothing.

"Varennes won't move the approach. Beaumont tried."

Mercier released the stirrup. "Don't stop in the middle."

Charles said, "Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier swung up.

* * *

Philippe's section moved at ten past nine, when the light was flat and even and gave no shadows.

Charles held his section in column, seventy meters back. From here, he could see the junction: a low stone wall and a farmhouse beyond it, the four gun carriages arranged in a rough line, the Prussian cavalry visible as dark shapes behind them, formed in two groups on the flanks. The beet field between here and there was frozen in ridges from the cart tracks and scattered with the black stumps of harvested plants.

Philippe's section went at the trot, then the canter. Eleven men in an extended line, sabers not yet drawn. They had made it perhaps a hundred and fifty meters when the guns fired.

The sound was flat in the cold air, not the rolling crash of the summer battles but something more compressed, efficient. Two horses went down in the first volley. One immediately, one struggling for a moment and then still. The section kept moving, closing the gap. Charles felt Tonnerre shift beneath him, a muscle-tightening that wasn't panic, just awareness, and he put a hand briefly on the gelding's neck.

The second volley came at a hundred and thirty meters. One man. The section barely paused. They were close enough now that Charles could hear them, hooves on frozen ground, a shout from somewhere in Philippe's line, and then they were at the guns, and the surface of it became immediate and particular and left no room for watching.

Charles pressed his section forward.

Through the gap in the stone wall. The guns already taken. Prussian artillerymen running or on the ground. He swept left where one of the Prussian cavalry groups was reforming, caught them before they had any order to them, drove them back in two hard exchanges that he would not remember clearly afterward. The second group melted north, into the bare poplar trees at the edge of the field.

Then it was over.

Charles pulled Tonnerre up near the farmhouse wall. His hands were shaking slightly, the fine trembling that came in the aftermath and lasted maybe two minutes before it stopped. He breathed. He counted his section: Rousseau, Duval, Marchetti, the two Girard cousins, the others, all of them there, three men with cuts, one with an arm he was holding odd, none of them down. He looked for Philippe.

He did not see him.

* * *

One of Philippe's men was kneeling beside a gun carriage, looking up when Charles arrived.

"Corporal Bonnard," Charles said. "Where is he?"

Bonnard was already on his feet, stepping back to give Charles room, and that was how Charles knew before he saw him, the specific way Bonnard moved, the expression of someone who has realized they need an officer.

Philippe was on the ground behind the gun carriage, back against the wheel. He was conscious. He had both hands pressed to his chest, high left side, and there was blood on them and on the front of his coat. His face was pale but his eyes were clear, tracking Charles as Charles knelt beside him.

"The junction," Philippe said.

"Taken. Both flanking groups dispersed."

"Good." He breathed carefully, the breath shallow and deliberate. "The guns?"

"Ours."

"Good," he said again.

Charles put his hand over Philippe's hands and applied pressure. The wound was high and left and there was too much blood for a surface wound. He looked up at Bonnard. "Surgeon. Now."

Bonnard was already moving.

Charles kept his eyes on Philippe. "Where does it hurt?"

"It is where it is." A pause. A careful breath. "I can't feel my left hand."

"The surgeon's coming."

Philippe nodded, eyes closing briefly, then opening again. He looked at Charles with the slightly unfocused quality of a man navigating pain. "You're bleeding," he said.

Charles hadn't noticed. There was a cut on his forearm, shallow, cloth already sticking to it, not serious. "It's nothing."

"Good."

They stayed like that for a moment, Charles's hand over Philippe's, the gun carriage at Philippe's back. The cold was very clear. From somewhere across the junction, men were shouting about horses. The ordinary noise of aftermath.

Charles looked at Philippe's collar. It was turned up wrong on one side, askew from the fall or the press of the engagement. He reached with his free hand and straightened it.

The gesture was ridiculous. The collar made no difference. Philippe watched it happen with an expression that was careful and grateful in a way that had nothing to do with the collar.

"Captain and home," Philippe said. "Still the plan?"

"Still the plan."

"Good." He shifted slightly, winced. "My section will need someone. After."

"Your section's fine," Charles said. "They came through."

"They'll need someone to look at them the way you do." Philippe's eyes were tracking carefully, fighting to stay on Charles. "Like they're capable. You do that. You don't notice it."

"Don't use up your breath."

"I'm using it correctly." But he stopped talking.

The surgeon arrived in six minutes. Charles counted, afterward, in the journal.

* * *

There were two lucid stretches in the two hours that followed.

The first was brief, Philippe surfacing from the surgeon's work to say something about Renard, that someone should make sure Renard was checked properly, that the gelding had been limping slightly this morning. Charles said he would see to it. Philippe believed him and settled.

The second came later, when the light was already changing. He surfaced clearly, enough that he looked at Charles with real focus.

"Élise wrote me," he said. Not remembering that he'd said it last night, or perhaps knowing and saying it again. "She asked me to look after you. I told her you didn't need it."

"You told me."

"She was right, though." A breath, slow and even. "You don't need looking after. But you could let people try." He paused. "Go home before it gets comfortable, Charles. The longer you wait, the better you'll be at it, and the harder it will be to leave." His voice was quiet, tired, not pressing. Just saying the true thing. "I know how you think. You'll start calculating who depends on you. You'll find you're useful. It's easy to stay for that."

Charles didn't answer.

"Go home when you can." He reached up toward his collar, a reflex, but his hand made it only as far as his chest before it stopped. He couldn't quite reach it.

Charles reached over and straightened it.

"Thank you," Philippe said. Something in his face settled, the last small tension leaving it.

He went quiet after that, in and out, the breathing slow and occasional. At some point in the late afternoon the breathing changed, not a moment, not a sound, just a quality of the silence that was different from the silences before it. The surgeon shook his head. He said something to Charles. Charles heard the words and understood them and filed them somewhere for later.

Outside, the junction was their junction now, and men were moving around it, doing the ordinary work.

* * *

He found Mercier at the horse line.

The sergeant was going through the inspection sequence, methodical, on a horse that had come through without injury and needed no inspection. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Hooves. Spine. He worked through it in full, and when it was done he started it again, more slowly.

"Philippe is dead," Charles said.

Mercier's hands stilled on the horse's flank. He stood that way for a moment, looking at nothing in particular. Then he continued the inspection, the spine, both hands moving steadily.

"I know," he said.

He finished the sequence. He straightened. He looked at Charles with the specific quality of attention Mercier reserved for things that mattered, not long, not dramatic, just clear and direct.

"The pressure is starting," Mercier said.

"I know."

"You don't, yet." He moved to the next horse, started at the left foreleg. "You did well today. The men know that. Philippe's section knows that. Varennes knows it, which he won't say, but Beaumont does and he will." He pressed two fingers against the foreleg, checking. "Men will start looking at you differently. They'll hand you problems because you solve them. They'll ask you to take on the next thing because the last thing went well. And you'll be able to do it." He paused. "That's the dangerous part."

Charles said nothing.

"The plan was specific," Mercier said. "Captain. Then home. Not Captain and the next engagement after that, not Captain and we need you through the winter, not Captain and something else. It does not finish itself, Lieutenant. It is never finished. There will always be one more problem in front of you that you can solve and they cannot. That is not a reason to stay."

"I know."

"You will feel it differently in six months." Mercier moved to the right foreleg. "I'm telling you now while you still believe me."

He kept going down the horse, unhurried. He did not look at Charles again.

Charles stayed a moment longer, watching the sequence, and then he turned and went to find his journal.

* * *

The page at the back was two pages now, names in the small careful print he used for entries he wanted to keep. He found the bottom of the second page, found the space below the last name, and wrote Philippe's.

*Arnaud, Philippe. Lieutenant. November 1806.*

He looked at it for a moment. Then he closed the journal.

Philippe's section was quartered in the farmhouse, twelve men using the main room and the attached barn, and Charles found them at dusk by the state of them, not disorganized but quiet in the specific way of men who have lost someone and don't yet know the new shape of things.

Bonnard looked up when Charles came through the door. Bonnard was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been Philippe's corporal for four months, a steady man who checked everything twice and didn't make noise about it. He looked at Charles with the expression of someone who has run out of the thing he was relying on and is deciding, in real time, whether the next available thing will hold weight.

Charles said: "Horses watered and fed?"

"Yes, sir."

"Bonnard. Your horses are watered but three of them are short on forage. I checked on my way in." He hadn't. He was guessing from the supply situation, from what he knew of what had come in that afternoon. "Get with Rousseau and go to the depot before they close it. They'll tell you they don't have it. Tell them you need a signature from Major Beaumont authorizing priority issue, and if they want to argue it, I'll be along in an hour."

Bonnard was already on his feet. Something in him had straightened slightly.

"The rest of you," Charles said, looking around the room. "Tonight is tonight. Tomorrow morning I want a head count on equipment, carbines, sabers, saddlery, anything that needs attention before we move. Write it down. If you don't write it down, it doesn't exist." He paused. "You came through the junction. That was well done."

He didn't make it more than that.

He met with Duval about the casualty report. He walked the perimeter once, looking at the horses. He had the argument with the supply depot. Not Bonnard's forage argument, which Rousseau handled efficiently, but a separate argument about canteens and the fact that the resupply schedule was two days off and someone at the depot had decided that wasn't his problem. Charles made it his problem, calmly, specifically, with dates and regulations and the promise of Beaumont's signature, and resolved it in twenty minutes.

The depot sergeant watched him leave with the expression of a man who had expected to win that argument.

* * *

At the picket line, late, Tonnerre was working through inadequate hay with the focused patience he brought to most things. Charles stood beside him in the dark. The junction was quiet now, the farmhouse lights low, the sentries moving in their regular rotation.

He was still on schedule.

The thought arrived without ceremony, the way his mind worked when it was calculating rather than feeling. Captain in, call it three more years if the campaigns continued to go this way. Home before thirty. The plan was specific. The plan was intact.

*Go home before it gets comfortable.*

Philippe had said it last night and again this afternoon. The words had a different weight now, the second hearing.

*When the numbers get comfortable*, Beaumont had said, four months ago. *That's when you should worry.*

*It is never finished*, Mercier had said tonight. Not bitterly. Just as a fact.

Charles thought about Bonnard's face in the farmhouse, that moment of deciding whether the next available thing would hold weight. How quickly Bonnard had come to his feet. How the section had organized itself around two sentences about forage.

He had noticed it. Filed it. Moved on.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck. The gelding kept eating.

Philippe's name was on the list now. Two pages. The list had been one column when he started it in September. Now it ran into the margins.

He had not written to Élise yet. He would have to write to her, and he would have to tell her about Philippe, and he did not know yet how to do that. Philippe had said *competence wasn't care*. Élise had said it first, to Philippe, and Philippe had carried it to Charles, and Charles had understood it intellectually and immediately and had not known what to do with it.

The cold was very clear. Somewhere north of them, past the bare poplars and the frozen fields, there were more Prussians, more junctions, more approaches with four hundred meters of open ground.

He was still on schedule. Five years, make Captain, go home. The plan was specific.

But something in the calculation had changed. Not the numbers. The numbers were the same. Something in how the numbers felt. He turned it over and couldn't find the edge of it.

Charles stood at the picket line for a long time.

He did not sleep.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 7

The assignment came on a Tuesday morning, nine days after the junction at Küstrin, and Charles received it the way he received most things he didn't want: without visible reaction, which was the only reaction that helped.

Three days escorting a supply convoy on the eastern road. Twelve wagons, a contractor's outfit running flour and dried meat and medicine from the depot at Frankfurt to the forward supply point near Posen. The convoy had its own outriders but not enough of them. Two men, which was not adequate for the roads east of the Oder in November of 1806, where Prussian irregulars were still operating in small groups and the difference between a guarded convoy and an unguarded one was the difference between the army eating next week or not.

Beaumont gave him six men and three days and the name of the convoy's point of contact: a Vieillard, contractor, Frankfurt address.

"It's not what you want," Beaumont said. He wasn't apologizing. He was acknowledging a fact.

"It's what's needed," Charles said.

Beaumont looked at him for a moment. "Yes," he said. "Get it done."

* * *

Mercier did not say anything when Charles told him. He was working through the morning inspection, left foreleg, right foreleg, and he continued through it without changing his pace.

"Six men," Charles said. "I'll take Rousseau, Marchetti, the Girard cousins, Ferrand, and Bonnard."

Mercier set down a hoof. "Bonnard."

"He needs something to do. Three days without purpose is worse for him than three days on the road."

Mercier moved to the next horse. "Reasonable."

"I wasn't asking your opinion."

"No, sir. You were telling me your reasoning. That's different." He pressed two fingers against a foreleg, checking for heat. "You do that when you've made a decision you're not certain about."

Charles said nothing.

"Bonnard is a good choice," Mercier said, after a moment, in the tone of a man closing a subject.

* * *

The convoy was staging on the eastern edge of the town, and Charles found it at first light the next morning by the sound of it: the creak of wagon wheels, the particular complaint of draft horses in cold weather, men's voices carrying across the frozen ground.

Twelve wagons was what Beaumont had said. There were fourteen. The two additional ones were smaller, a different type, their canvas covers secured differently from the contractor's main outfit. Charles noted this and filed it.

He was looking for Vieillard when he saw the problem.

The lead wagon had stopped. Not stopped waiting. Stopped stuck, the left rear wheel sunk to the axle in a patch of ground that had looked solid from the road but wasn't, a thin crust of frost over mud that had not frozen through. The driver was still on the box, uncertain. Two men stood beside the wheel looking at it with the expression of men who have decided the problem belongs to someone else.

A third person was already on the ground behind the wagon. Crouched, looking at the wheel and the mud. Not standing back from it. Close, close enough that the mud was on their boots and the hem of their coat.

Young. A woman. She straightened, looked at the two men standing beside the wheel, and said something Charles couldn't hear at this distance. The tone carried: not shouting, not asking. Telling. The two men looked at each other and then began moving.

Charles stopped walking.

She had seen the ground wrong from the road, same as the driver. But she'd assessed what needed doing faster than anyone else near her, which meant she'd already moved past the mistake to the solution before most people had finished registering the problem. That was something. Charles noted it the way he noted things: filed it, moved on.

He waited until the wagon was clear. The men had gotten a plank under the wheel and she'd directed the driver to ease forward. Then he approached.

"Vieillard," he said.

She was brushing mud off her coat. She looked up. She was perhaps eighteen, dark-haired, with the specific quality of attention of someone who is always in the middle of three things at once and has learned to assess new information quickly to determine whether it requires any of those three things to change.

"Vieillard is my father," she said. "I'm Margot Vieillard. You're our escort."

"Second Lieutenant Leblanc." He looked at the wheel. "The road is soft for another fifty meters. You'll want the planks out before the next wagon hits that stretch."

She looked at him. A pause. Not rude, not long. The pause of someone finishing a count before responding.

"I know," she said. "I've already sent someone for them." She held out her hand. "It's good to meet you, Lieutenant."

He shook it. Her grip was direct, her glove worn at the thumb. "And you."

She had already turned back to the wagon by the time he finished the sentence.

* * *

They made fourteen kilometers the first day, which was good for the road conditions and the size of the outfit. Charles rode the length of the column twice in the morning, assessed the weak points. The third wagon's harness needed attention, two of the draft horses were laboring on the softer ground. and brought his concerns to Rousseau, who handled them without needing further direction. By midday the column was moving steadily.

He found himself watching Margot Vieillard without intending to.

Not watching the way Philippe would have watched. Philippe had had an easy appreciation for a pretty woman that required no particular justification. This was different. She was managing the convoy the way Charles managed a section: continuously, with a particular alertness that looked like calm from the outside. She moved up and down the column. She checked the loads, checked the animals, checked the drivers. She knew the name of every man in her outfit, which was eleven people, and she addressed them by name and with specificity, not general direction.

He couldn't hear most of what she said. He heard the results of it.

At the midday halt she was tallying something in a small ledger, standing at the side of the road, the ledger open against her forearm. Her lips moved slightly when she counted. She finished, looked up, and he realized he had been watching her for perhaps thirty seconds without any obvious pretense.

"Lieutenant," she said. It was not unfriendly.

"The third wagon's harness," he said. "The right trace is fraying. It should hold today but I'd look at it tonight."

She looked at him. "I saw it this morning. My man Lefebvre is handling it at the halt."

"Good."

He had no further reason to be standing there. He turned his horse and rode back to the head of the column, and he could feel, without looking, the specific quality of her attention following him for a moment before she returned to her ledger.

* * *

That evening they made camp in the lee of a farmhouse whose occupants had either fled or been evacuated, the windows shuttered, the yard stripped of everything useful. Margot's men had the routine down: wagons in a square, animals inside, fire at the center. Efficient. Not military-efficient, which was a specific kind of efficiency built around command and compliance, but a different kind: the efficiency of people who'd worked together long enough to know what each other would do.

Charles posted his men and walked the perimeter once, then found himself with the particular problem of the evening halt on escort duty: nothing specific to do. His section at the bivouac had always had something. Here, the waiting was the work.

He was checking Tonnerre's near foreleg when he heard footsteps on the frozen ground and looked up.

Margot Vieillard was carrying two tin cups. She held one out.

"Coffee," she said. "Or what we're calling coffee."

He took it. "Thank you."

She stood beside him, her own cup in both hands. The fire at the center of the wagon square threw light across the yard. One of her drivers was singing something, low, a working song of some kind.

"You've been watching my operation all day," she said. Not accusing. Noting.

"Your operation is worth watching. It runs well."

She looked at him over the rim of her cup. "That's either a compliment or an inspection."

Charles thought about that. "Both," he said. Then, because it was accurate: "More compliment than inspection. You handled the wheel problem faster than anyone I've watched handle a stuck wagon."

"I've had practice."

"How long have you been running the convoy?"

She was quiet for a moment. "Since May. My father was ill in the spring. He recovered, but the contracts didn't wait, so I went ahead with the run." She paused. "He recovered fully. He decided I could keep managing the runs."

"How old are you?"

She looked at him. Not offended. Something else. The look of someone accustomed to the question who has not yet decided how to feel about it.

"Eighteen," she said. "How old are you?"

"Twenty."

"And you're a lieutenant."

"Yes."

"With six men and an escort assignment."

He heard it. Not mockery, just precision. "Yes."

She looked at the fire. "My father has been contracting supply runs for the army for eleven years. He started when I was seven. I grew up on these roads." She said it plainly, without apology or pride, the way Mercier talked about things he'd learned, as simple fact. "I know the eastern road from Frankfurt to Posen in three seasons. I don't know it in deep winter, not east of the Oder, but I'll learn it."

"When does deep winter start?"

"Six weeks. Maybe four."

Charles did the arithmetic. The army would push toward Warsaw before then. The supply lines would extend further. The convoys would run in worse conditions than this.

"The irregulars will be more active when the temperature drops," he said. "They'll need the supplies themselves. Two outriders isn't sufficient."

"I know. I've been requesting three since August." She looked at him. "The army allocates what it allocates."

He recognized the observation. He'd made the same one about different problems. He didn't say so.

She finished her coffee and held out her hand for his cup, which he gave her. "Good night, Lieutenant," she said. "We're moving at first light."

"I know."

She looked at him for a moment, that pause again, like she was finishing a count. Then she turned and walked back toward the wagon square, her boots crunching on the frost.

* * *

The second day was harder. The road worsened east of the second creek crossing, the drainage ditch on the south side having overflowed and frozen in ridges across the road surface that the wagon wheels caught badly. They lost an hour mid-morning to a wheel replacement on the seventh wagon, the old wheel having cracked on a frozen rut, and another half-hour in the afternoon to a section of road so soft they had to unload two wagons, move them through light, and reload on the far side.

Charles worked the problem both times. He didn't make himself the center of it. Rousseau handled the wheel replacement, the Girard cousins handled the unloading. But he assessed, directed, kept moving. At the unloading point he worked alongside Margot's men for the better part of an hour, moving sacks of flour from the wagon bed to the roadside and back, because there were not enough hands and standing around watching was not a useful contribution.

When it was done he was cold and his back ached and there was flour on his coat.

Margot was checking the reloaded wagon. She glanced at his coat.

"You didn't have to do that," she said.

"Faster with another pair of hands."

"Yes." She checked the securing rope on the wagon cover. "Your men follow you well. The younger one. Bonnard. He works hard."

"He's having a difficult week."

She looked at him. A question she didn't ask.

"A friend of mine died nine days ago," Charles said. "Bonnard was in his section. He's looking for something to put his hands on." He paused. He hadn't meant to say that. "The work helps."

She held his gaze. "I'm sorry about your friend."

"Thank you."

She went back to the wagon cover, found the rope correctly tensioned, moved on. He watched her go and thought, with the odd clarity that cold and physical work sometimes produced, that she was the first person who had said that simply, without adding anything to it. Everyone else had surrounded it with other words, with what came next, with duty and schedule and the shape of the days ahead. She had just said it, and moved on, which was somehow exactly right.

* * *

The afternoon brought a thin snow, not heavy but persistent, and the road became worse. They made camp two kilometers short of where Charles had planned, at a crossroads with a stone shrine and enough flat ground for the wagons. Not ideal. Adequate.

He was posting the watch rotation when Margot appeared at his elbow.

"The road south at this crossroads," she said. "It runs eight kilometers to a village called Hreben. There's an inn there. Dry shelter, a proper stable for the animals."

"Eight kilometers south is eight kilometers off the route."

"It's also warm and dry, which the horses need more than we need to be sixteen kilometers along by tomorrow morning instead of fourteen." She said it matter-of-factly. "The animals are working in conditions they weren't bred for. They need to be out of the cold tonight."

Charles looked at the horses. He'd been thinking about the route. He hadn't been thinking about the horses.

"I'll look at the south road," he said.

He rode it himself, in the dark, with Rousseau. It was frozen solid. Better footing than the main road had been all day. The village was real, the inn was real, the stable was large enough for the draft horses. They were back at the crossroads in forty minutes.

"We go south," he told Margot.

"I know," she said. "I sent Lefebvre ahead an hour ago to arrange it."

He looked at her.

"You would decide yes," she said, without apology. "So I sent Lefebvre while you were deciding."

"You were certain."

"I've made this run eight times. I know when the animals need shelter and when they can camp." She held his gaze. "If I'd been wrong, I'd have recalled Lefebvre."

He thought about this. She hadn't waited for his authority because she'd already made the assessment and the outcome was clear to her and the delay of waiting for a lieutenant to confirm what she already knew would have cost an hour she didn't want to spend.

He knew that logic. He used it himself, occasionally, when Beaumont was available but the decision was obvious and the delay would cost something. The difference was that she was eighteen and running a supply convoy and he was the one with the commission.

"Next time," he said, "tell me before you send the man."

She nodded. Not an apology. An acknowledgment. The distinction was clear in the way she did it.

"Agreed," she said.

* * *

The inn at Hreben was run by a Prussian couple who had made a pragmatic peace with the French occupation and now fed soldiers with the same flat efficiency with which they'd fed travelers before the war. The stable was warm and dry and smelled of hay and old timber, and Charles made sure the horses were settled before he went inside.

The inn's main room had a long table and a fireplace and Margot's eleven men and his own six filling most of the available space. He found a seat at the end of the table, ate what was put in front of him, and listened to the room.

Bonnard was talking. This was new. Bonnard had been quiet for nine days, since the junction, since Philippe. He was talking to one of Margot's drivers, something about horses, and the driver was answering with the enthusiasm of a man who would rather talk about horses than most things. Bonnard's face had a quality Charles hadn't seen in it for a week. Not happy. Not that. But present.

Margot was sitting three places down, eating, reading from a small book she'd propped against her cup. Charles watched her for a moment. He was doing it again. The watching. He was aware of it now.

"What are you reading?" he said.

She looked up. A beat. "A history of the Hanseatic League," she said.

"Why?"

"Because the trade routes they established are still the routes we use. Understanding why they made the choices they made helps me understand the roads." She looked at him. "Why does that surprise you?"

"It doesn't," he said, which was only partially true.

She closed the book against her palm. "What do you read?"

"Tactical histories. Cavalry engagements, mostly. De Brack. Lasalle's methods. Older material when I can find it: Marlborough's campaigns, the Dutch wars."

"So you study the same thing I study. How to move things from one place to another most effectively."

He thought about that. "What I study is how to destroy things most effectively."

"Is it different?"

He picked up his cup. "Yes," he said. "Entirely."

She looked at him for a long moment, not challenging, thinking. "My convoy moves food and medicine. Without it the army stops. Without the army stopping things, the supply lines don't exist. Without the supply lines, there's no convoy." She tilted her head slightly. "We're part of the same machine."

"Different parts," Charles said.

"Of course. But when your part breaks, people die fighting. When my part breaks, people die not fighting." She said it without drama, the same flat accuracy with which she said everything. "I used to think supply work was the lesser work. The background. But eleven runs have convinced me otherwise."

He looked at her. "What changed your mind?"

"The bridge at Glogau," she said. "Last August. The army needed to cross before the Prussians destroyed it. The crossing worked. But the reason the army could hold the crossing point for the three days it took, the reason those men ate and kept their ammunition, was two convoys that reached them on time." She paused. "I was the second convoy. Six hours to spare."

She said it the way Mercier talked about things he'd done: not modestly, not immodestly. As fact. Charles filed it.

"Six hours is a narrow margin," he said.

"The road was worse than expected. A ford had shifted." She shrugged, one shoulder. "You plan for the road you know and adjust for the road you find."

He recognized that too. He had said essentially the same thing to Mercier about a different kind of road. He didn't say so.

"The bridge at Glogau," he said. "I know the action. Two squadrons of the 5th Hussars held that crossing point."

"Yes." She looked at him. "Were you there?"

"No. I read the report."

She nodded. Picked up her book. "Then we've both studied it."

* * *

The third day was clear, the snow having stopped overnight and left the road hard and clean and better footing than anything they'd had since Frankfurt. They made good time, better than Charles had projected, and reached the forward supply point at Posen two hours before dark.

The handoff was efficient. Margot's men had done it before, the supply point officers had a routine for receiving the convoy, and the whole process took ninety minutes from arrival to the wagons stripped and logged and signed over. Charles watched it the way he'd watched the rest of the convoy's operations: with the specific attention of someone cataloguing what worked and why.

Margot was at the depot office with the supply point's administrator, going through the manifest, when Charles found her. She finished the last page, received the receipt, folded it into her coat. She looked up.

"We're done," she said.

"You're returning to Frankfurt?"

"Tomorrow morning. The road is better going west than it was coming east." She paused. "Your section is at the garrison north of Posen?"

"Yes."

She held out her hand. "Thank you for the escort, Lieutenant. Your men were useful."

He shook it. Her grip was the same as the first morning. Direct. The worn thumb of the glove. He had noticed that already. He noticed it again.

"The two additional wagons," he said. "The smaller ones at the back of the column. They weren't on the manifest."

She looked at him.

"I noticed them the first morning," he said. "I didn't press it. They came through with the convoy without incident, which is the outcome that mattered. But I noticed."

She held his gaze. A pause. Not the counting pause, something else, something that might have been consideration.

"Medical supplies," she said. "A surgeon at a forward post south of Posen requisitioned them privately. The army's allocation process takes six weeks. He needed them in ten days." She did not break eye contact. "The paperwork follows the supplies, Lieutenant. It doesn't always lead them."

Charles thought about the Küstrin depot in November, the supply sergeant who'd decided the resupply schedule wasn't his problem until Charles had made it his problem with dates and regulations. He thought about what Mercier had told him about the difference between the army working correctly and the army working.

"The paperwork follows the supplies," he said.

"Yes."

He let it go. It was the right call. Not because the paperwork didn't matter, but because a surgeon's requisition moving faster than the official channel was the machine self-correcting around a broken part, and that was usually the right outcome.

"Safe road back," he said.

"And you." She looked at him a moment longer. The pause again, finishing something. "You watched my operation for three days as if you were trying to figure out what category it belonged to."

He didn't answer immediately.

"Did you decide?" she said.

"Not yet," he said.

She smiled. It was brief and genuine and he hadn't seen it before. She smiled less than Philippe had, which meant it meant more when she did. "Come find me if you do," she said. "I'll be on the Frankfurt road most of the winter."

Then she turned and walked back toward her wagons, and he watched her go, and he did not have a useful thought about what had just happened.

* * *

He rode back to the garrison north of Posen with his six men in the late afternoon, the sun dropping behind the bare poplars on the western horizon. Bonnard rode beside him for a stretch without speaking, which was a new thing. Bonnard had been quiet since the junction but the quality of the quiet had changed somewhere in the last three days. Less submerged. More simply quiet.

"The convoy woman," Bonnard said, after a while. Not a question.

"What about her."

Bonnard was quiet for a moment, looking at the road. "She reminded me of you. The way she ran things."

Charles said nothing.

"Like she was always three steps ahead, not cold about it." Bonnard considered the road. "Like she cared about getting it right because the wrong answer cost something real, not because she wanted to be seen getting it right."

Charles looked at the horizon. He didn't say anything.

"Anyway," Bonnard said, and fell back.

* * *

That night Charles wrote in his journal. Not the lists. The journal proper, which he used rarely, the running record he'd been keeping since Günzburg. He wrote about the three days on the eastern road in the practical way he wrote about most things: the conditions, the distances, the decisions. The wheel at the first morning. The unloading at the frozen rut. The south road to Hreben.

He wrote: *convoy well-managed. Vieillard, M. Eighteen, contractor's outfit, Frankfurt. Eight runs. Knows the road. Assessed correctly at every decision point. The additional wagons: medical supplies to forward post, paperwork to follow. Right call.*

He looked at what he'd written.

He wrote: *she said we study the same thing. How to move things from one place to another. I said no, I study how to destroy things. Both true.*

He closed the journal.

He thought about what she'd said at the inn: *when my part breaks, people die not fighting.* The same weight on both sides, counted differently. He'd spent a year learning to think about the weight on his side. He hadn't thought about the accounting on hers.

The plan was still the plan. Captain and home. Five years from the start, which was now three and a half years from now, minus whatever the winter brought, minus whatever came east of Posen when the army moved on Warsaw. The numbers were the same.

Something in the calculation was moving again. Not the numbers. The thing underneath them.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck. The gelding was stalled for the night, close enough to touch, and the horse turned his head without lifting it, the slow acknowledgment of a familiar hand.

He thought about the worn thumb of her glove. He thought about *come find me if you do.* He thought about the way she had said *I'm sorry about your friend* and then moved on, which had been exactly right.

He did not write any of this down.

He lay on his bedroll with his boots on, hand on the left one, the paper still there. Bordeaux, Élise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card.

Three and a half years.

He closed his eyes.

CHAPTER 8

The Pyrenees were not the Alps.

Charles had thought about that, in the abstract, through the summer while the treaty with Russia settled into something that looked like permanence, and then through the autumn while the orders came in pieces, and then through the winter while the army turned itself southwest and began to move. He had told himself the mountains were mountains. He had been wrong.

The Alps had a quality he recognized now only in retrospect: they had been a gate. You crossed them and arrived somewhere. The enemy was on the other side, organized into units with flags and commanders who answered letters. The crossing was hard but it had direction. You could see where you were going.

The Pyrenees simply stopped being France and started being Spain without announcing themselves. The same rock, the same cold, the same thin air that made the horses labor. But no one on the other side was waiting to negotiate terms.

They crossed in late October. The road through the pass was well-worn, which meant other columns had moved through it recently, which meant the army was already there in force, which meant the situation was already complicated enough to require force. Charles rode beside Tonnerre's steady rhythm and did the arithmetic and did not like where it ended.

Two days before the crossing they passed through a valley where a supply column had been hit.

He had seen plenty of dead men. He had added some of them to his list. The men in the valley were different, and it took him a moment to understand why: they were arranged wrong. A conventional engagement left a particular pattern. Men fell from horses a certain way. Horses fell a certain way. Bodies ended up where momentum and physics put them, which followed a logic you could read if you knew the language.

These men were not arranged by momentum. They were where someone had put them.

He did not stop the column. Stopping accomplished nothing and he was not senior enough to decide it. He rode through at the same pace and kept his eyes on the road ahead and noted, in the quiet part of his mind that catalogued such things, that the wagons had been taken rather than burned. Practical. Organized. Someone had needed the wagons more than they had needed to make a point.

Mercier was riding two lengths to his left.

Charles waited for him to say something. In Italy, Mercier had talked about the ground constantly, in his deliberate way, the sentences arriving one at a time as if he were checking each word before he released it. He had opinions about soil consistency and its effect on cavalry movement, about how hills funneled infantry fire, about the particular problems created by rivers in spring thaw versus winter freeze. He talked about the ground the way some men talked about weather: a continuous commentary that was both practical and somehow comforting, evidence that someone had thought about these things before and survived long enough to develop opinions.

Mercier said nothing for forty minutes. Then he said: "Different wind down here."

That was all.

Charles looked at the road and thought about that. The column moved around them, Bonnard keeping the rear orderly, Rousseau up ahead with the outriders at a distance that Charles had specified and Rousseau had expanded by another twenty meters without being asked, which was the right instinct. Duval was counting things, which Duval always was.

Different wind. It was not wrong.

By the time they reached the first Spanish town of any size, the column had taken on a quality Charles had not felt before, not at Eylau, not in the worst of Prussia. The men who had been through three campaigns, men who had understood fear as a thing with a shape and a direction, were nervous in a way that had no shape. Eylau had been legible. The guns were there, the Russian line was there, you rode at it or you defended against it and either way you knew where the danger was coming from. Spain was not legible. The danger was the ground itself, the passes, the men who had needed those wagons, the closed windows of the town they were riding through right now in bright October sunlight.

A boy stood in a doorway and watched them pass. He was perhaps ten. His face had no expression on it at all, not fear, not curiosity. He watched them with the patience of someone waiting for weather to change.

Charles noted this. He kept riding.

* * *

The depot raid was in February 1809, and it went well.

Good intelligence from a cavalry captain who had spent three weeks in the area before them. Clear maps. A defensible approach and a reasonable exit. Charles had spent two days walking the problem with Bonnard before he presented it to Major Beaumont, and Beaumont had asked four careful questions and approved it with the particular expression he wore when he was satisfied but would not say so, which was the same expression he wore most of the time.

They moved at night. Charles had Rousseau take six men to the east approach while he came in from the north with the main group, and the timing held within ten minutes of what he'd planned, which was as good as timing ever held in practice. The depot guards were overwhelmed before they had organized a proper response. Casualties: two men with minor wounds, one horse, which he noted in his report and felt more than he noted. The depot was secured, the stores catalogued, a messenger dispatched by mid-morning.

The praise, when it came from the colonel, was brief and specific. Charles had learned to distrust fulsome praise; specific praise was accurate and therefore useful. He accepted it and went back to check his men.

Tonnerre had a cut on his left foreleg from a wire that hadn't been visible in the dark. Not deep. Charles knelt in the mud and ran his fingers along it, feeling for heat. The horse stood steady.

"You'll be complaining about it for a week," Charles told him, and patted his neck twice, and went to find Mercier to report the men's condition.

The village they had ridden through on the approach, and again on the return, was not a large place. Twenty houses, a church, a well in the square. On the approach it had been four in the morning and he hadn't expected to see anyone. On the return it was full daylight, the raid concluded, the unit intact, the operation by any military measure a success.

Every door was closed.

He had been in villages after engagements in Italy where the doors were also closed, but that had been a different kind of closing: people inside deciding whether the noise had stopped. This was not that. The doors in this village were closed the way things are closed when they have been closed for a long time, when the act of closing is not a decision but a habit, when you have learned that doors closed means you are not involved and not involved is the safest condition available.

No one in the street. The well sat empty and ordinary in the morning light.

Charles looked at it as he rode past. He looked at the church. He looked at the shut doors of the houses where people were certainly awake by now, certainly aware that a column of French cavalry had just ridden through their village twice in a single night, concluded some military business in the hills above it, and returned.

He looked at Rousseau, who was riding beside him on the return.

Rousseau had a south-coastal way of reading situations, the Marseille in him a kind of sharp skepticism that had evolved, over four years of service, into something quieter and more reliable. He looked at the village and then at Charles and said nothing. Which was its own answer.

They rode through. The column kept its pace. By afternoon they were back at the main camp, and Charles wrote his report, and the report covered everything that a report was meant to cover: approach timing, ammunition expenditure, personnel condition, notes on the road quality for future reference.

* * *

That evening he sat with his journal and the stub of a tallow candle and wrote for forty minutes.

He wrote about the approach route and the features that had made it preferable to the southern alternative. He wrote a paragraph on the wire hazard in the depot yard and how it should be noted for the next unit to operate in the area. He wrote about the behavior of the horses in the cold, which had been better than expected, and about a problem with one of the wagons that had been repaired before it became significant.

He wrote about Rousseau's instinct on the outrider distance, and that he intended to formalize the expanded margin as standard for this theater.

He wrote for a while about the Pyrenean passes and how they differed from Alpine approaches in terms of cavalry utility and in terms of the supply problem, which was worse here because the roads were older and more contested.

He wrote: *The operation proceeded as planned. Men in acceptable condition. Tonnerre's cut minor, no heat in the leg this evening, expecting full recovery.*

He stopped.

He looked at what he had written.

He wrote: *Excellent intelligence made this possible. Need to establish reliable local network, difficult given the current.*

He stopped again. Crossed out "difficult given the current." Wrote nothing else in that space.

He closed the journal. He sat for a moment in the smell of tallow and cold canvas and something else that was Spanish and not yet named in his vocabulary of places, a dryness to the cold that was different from Polish dryness, a particular quality of dust that came in under the tent flap.

Outside, men were talking in low voices. Bonnard had organized the watch rotation without being asked, which was something Philippe would have approved of, and Charles noted this and kept it to himself, which was something Philippe would also have approved of.

He opened his journal again. He looked at the blank space after the crossed-out words.

He closed it again and put it away.

* * *

Margot's letters arrived irregularly, which was the nature of letters in wartime, and he had stopped expecting regularity and started simply being glad when they arrived. He kept them in his field bag in a bundle secured with a piece of cord. He had not counted the bundle. It had gotten heavier since Poland.

She wrote on good paper when she had it and on whatever was available when she didn't, and he could tell the difference by feel without looking, which was information about the state of her business at any given moment and which he found himself tracking with the same attention he gave to supply reports. Her handwriting was precise and somewhat compressed, as if she had learned early that paper was finite and words should carry weight.

Her letters were practical in the way that his were practical, but her practicality covered more ground. She asked about road conditions in the passes with the specificity of someone who intended to use that information: not how difficult the crossing was, but what the surface was like under rain, whether the switchbacks allowed for wagon teams of four or required reducing to two, which villages had water in late autumn and which relied on snowmelt that wouldn't be reliable until spring. She asked about the ports on the northern coast and what the French garrison presence looked like in the major towns, framed as observations rather than questions that would cause trouble for the letter if it was read by the wrong person.

He had noticed this early, her habit of framing military observations as merchant observations, and had found it cleaner than the alternative. She was asking what merchants needed to know. The answer happened to be approximately what a cavalry officer also needed to know.

He answered her questions about roads and conditions and told her about the Pyrenean passes with the specificity she preferred: not the experience of crossing but the practical dimensions. What the surface was. Where the water was. What the approach looked like for a loaded wagon versus a horse column.

He told her about the horses, because the horses were honest subject matter and because she asked after Tonnerre with a consistency that suggested she had filed the horse under reliable intelligence about Charles's general condition. He told her about the terrain and the weather and occasionally about Mercier or Bonnard, identified by name and role but not in terms that would mean anything to a censor.

He did not tell her about the closed doors.

He could not have explained, precisely, why not. He knew what he would have written: something about the difference between people who are afraid of you and people who have simply decided to wait you out, about how the distinction mattered tactically but also in some way he could not name tactically. He had the words for it. He just did not write them.

Her letters contained, occasionally, information that was not its own category of remarkable until after it proved useful.

In January of 1809, roughly three weeks before the depot raid, a letter arrived that had been written in November and traveled badly, the paper damp-wrinkled and re-dried, the ink slightly smeared in one corner. She had been corresponding with a network of German and Dutch merchants who were attempting to reconstruct their Spanish trade connections, most of which had collapsed in the summer when the situation went uncertain. The letter was full of practical logistics: which routes were viable, which were not, what the merchants were hearing from their contacts in Bilbao and Burgos.

Near the end, almost as an aside, she wrote:

*There is a ford at the junction below the Garralda gorge that I am told the local merchants have avoided since midsummer. The reasoning given was irregular activity in the gorge above it. I pass this along for what it's worth, which is probably what a merchant's secondhand word is worth, which is not much. But the merchants who told me this are not dramatic people, and they were specific about the gorge.*

He read this twice.

He had not been planning to use the ford at the Garralda gorge. He had not been planning to pass through that area at all in the coming weeks. He read the letter again, noted the location, put the letter in his field bag.

Two weeks later, orders arrived that modified his assigned patrol area, extending it east. The Garralda ford was now relevant.

He took the longer road, adding three days. He made the decision on a Tuesday morning and did not explain his reasoning in the operations note, which stated only "alternate route selected based on available intelligence." Beaumont had looked at it and said nothing, which was Beaumont's form of agreement.

Three days after he arrived at the eastern staging point by the longer road, a unit from a different regiment was ambushed at the ford below the Garralda gorge. He learned this from a dispatch rider who arrived on a Friday, the horse sweating and the rider's face carrying the expression Charles had seen before: the expression of someone who has seen what ambush does at close range and has not yet found the words to put between himself and the image.

Seven men. He did not know them. He wrote them into the back of his journal, which meant the list was now three pages. He did not know their names, which bothered him in a way that sat in his chest for several days without resolving.

He sat down that evening and wrote to Margot.

The letter was short. He spent some time on it, writing and crossing out and rewriting, which was unusual for him; his letters generally arrived at their shape quickly. The problem was that he wanted to say something accurate and could not find the form for it. He owed her a debt that had no clean name, because she had not known she was saving him, because she had been talking about merchants.

He finally wrote:

*Your advice about the ford below the Garralda. It mattered. I want you to know that it mattered and that I am well. Tell your merchants their word was worth more than they priced it.*

He looked at this for a moment. Then he added:

*Tonnerre's leg has healed completely. The terrain here is harder on them than Italy was, but he manages.*

He sealed the letter and gave it to the courier and did not think about whether she would understand what "it mattered" meant, because she would.

* * *

By the time spring arrived he was twenty-two and had stopped being surprised by the particular quality of silence that a Spanish village kept when a French column passed through it. He had not made peace with it. He had simply stopped being surprised by it.

The war here was not the war he had learned. He had learned a war that was about position and maneuver and the accumulation of ground, a war where the objective was legible and the means of achieving it were disagreeable but comprehensible. You moved toward a thing. You either took it or you didn't. The next day the map looked different.

Spain did not accumulate. You took a town, and then you held it, and holding it cost more than taking it had, and the ground you had taken looked the same on the map but was not the same. The guerrillas were not on the map. They were in the land itself, in the passes and the dry hills and the villages where every door was closed.

He was good at this. He had been good at conventional cavalry operations and he had adapted, had adjusted his instincts and his tactics, had learned to read the Spanish ground the way Mercier had taught him to read Italian ground, which was a different language but the same basic grammar. His men trusted him. Beaumont had stopped explaining his reasoning when he gave Charles orders, which was its own form of statement.

He had handled an ambush in March in a way that had prevented casualties to his own men and to two other units moving in the same area, by reading a hillside correctly at a moment that required reading it correctly. He had done this with no particular drama, in the way that a good surgeon makes a cut look easy, because the difficulty goes into the preparation and the preparation was not dramatic. Bonnard afterward had said only "good eye," which was the highest available praise in their current vocabulary. Mercier had said nothing, which meant the same thing.

He was good at this.

He was less certain, lately, what "this" was.

He sat outside his tent one evening in April and cleaned his pistol and thought about that without resolving it. The evening was dry and the light was going in the way that Spanish light went, which was different from French light and not in a way he had found words for yet. The pistol came apart in his hands and went back together and he checked the mechanism twice, which was Duval's habit and which he had apparently acquired without noticing.

The five-year plan. He counted. Prussia in 1806, Spain in 1808, which made it three years in. Two years remained on the plan, roughly, depending on when the army decided it was done with Spain, which it showed no current signs of deciding. He had thought about captaincy frequently in the first two years and now he thought about it in a different way, not as a destination but as an interval, a marker on a road whose end he could not see clearly because he was too close to the middle of it.

He thought about Elise's last letter, which had arrived in February and had been full of ordinary news: the vineyard, a neighbor's dispute, their father's back, a cousin's wedding. She wrote as if the war were a thing happening elsewhere to other people, which was true, and also as if it would not last much longer, which was also a kind of truth, just a different one. She had enclosed a drawing of the vineyard in autumn, done by someone's child apparently, which was not technically accurate but captured something about the light.

He had looked at the drawing for a long time.

He kept it in his field bag, not folded, which meant it took up more space than anything else he was carrying. He had made this calculation and kept the drawing unfolded.

He thought about Margot's last letter, which had been warmer than usual in some way he could not entirely locate in the specific words, though the words themselves had been practical as always. She had asked about the road surfaces in the Ebro valley with the specificity he had come to expect. She had mentioned that her father's business was expanding into new territory now that the peace with Prussia was holding, which meant she was working more independently, which he had understood to mean she was doing most of the work with Henri Vieillard providing the legitimacy and the capital and the experience. She had mentioned, in passing, that Frankfurt was cold in February and that she sometimes thought about how different one kind of cold was from another.

He had reread that line three times without deciding what it meant.

He had not written back to it. He had answered the question about the Ebro valley and told her about Tonnerre and told her about a particular quality of the light at this latitude in winter that he was still trying to find the right comparison for, and then he had reread his own letter before sealing it and left that last part in because removing it would have made the letter noticeably shorter without the appearance of a good reason.

She had not written back to it either. Which was either its own kind of answer or simply the nature of letters, which could not quite carry tone, which arrived out of order and were read in circumstances their authors had not imagined.

He put the pistol together and checked the mechanism one more time and put it away.

Tonnerre was staked nearby, cropping at the dry grass with the focused patience he brought to any available food source. Charles went to him and put his hand on the horse's neck and stood there for a moment. Tonnerre continued eating with the suggestion that the attention was noted but secondary to the grass.

"Fair enough," Charles said.

He went back into his tent and opened his journal and wrote the date, the fourteenth of April, 1809, and noted the weather and the state of the road to the south and the condition of the horses and the status of the men and the information that had come in from the outriders that afternoon, which was not eventful but was accurate, and accuracy was its own value.

He did not write about the quality of the light.

He did not write about the counting he had done, three years in, two remaining.

He wrote that Rousseau had handled the morning patrol well and that the suggestion he had made about the rotation had been correct and that he had told him so, which was not a thing Charles did often enough and which he was trying to do more deliberately.

He wrote that the list was now three pages and that he did not know seven of the names on it.

He stopped. He sat for a while with the journal open and the candle burning.

Then he reached down, without particular thought, and pulled off his left boot and took out the paper, folded to playing card size, worn at the folds but holding. He opened it. Elise's handwriting, the Bordeaux address, the same as they had always been. He read it not because the information was new. He read it because it was true.

He folded it back to its shape and put it back in the boot and set the boot aside and went to sleep.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 9

The convoy assembled before dawn, which was the only reasonable time to assemble anything in July if you wanted the mules to still be moving by midmorning. Charles counted wagons from horseback, Tonnerre shifting under him with the particular restlessness of a horse that had slept badly and knew the day would not improve this. Fourteen wagons, mixed cargo, three of them belonging to Vieillard and Sons according to the manifest he had been handed the night before by a harried clerk who had not looked up from his desk once during the entire transaction.

He found the point of contact at the head of the convoy, standing beside the lead wagon with a lantern and a ledger.

He had not expected her to be the point of contact.

He had known, from letters, that she was sometimes in Spain. Her father Henri managed the Frankfurt operations while she moved with the trade routes, which in her letters she had described with the same brisk practicality she brought to everything, as a matter of geography and necessity rather than adventure. He had imagined this, in the abstract way one imagines things described in letters, as somewhere she existed, somewhere south, somewhere contested. He had not imagined the specific person who would be standing next to the lead wagon of his assigned convoy at four in the morning with a lantern and a ledger.

She had grown four years older. He had not quite processed this, in the letters, because letters do not show you the actual person. She was taller than he remembered from Poland, or perhaps he had simply been standing too far away in Poland to judge correctly. Her hair was pulled back and mostly covered. She was wearing a practical coat with the collar turned up against the early chill, and she had a leather satchel over one shoulder that looked like it weighed more than was sensible.

"Leblanc," she said, when he gave his name, and looked up from the ledger with the expression of someone checking a figure. "We have been corresponding for four years."

"We have."

"You are shorter than I expected."

He was five foot nine, which was not short by any reasonable measure, but he found he could not produce an objection that did not sound defensive, so he said nothing, and she made a small sound that might have been amusement and turned back to the ledger.

"Fourteen wagons," she said. "Three of mine. I have the route through Salamanca, but if you are planning to push straight through on the main road, I want to discuss that before we move."

He had been planning to push straight through on the main road. He had a map, prepared by someone in his headquarters who had presumably looked at the roads from a sufficient distance, which showed the main road as the most direct option. He said, with military bearing, that he had reviewed the route.

She looked at him the way he suspected she looked at manifests that did not add up.

"The ford at the river crossing," she said. "What does your map show?"

He described what his map showed.

"Your map is wrong," she said, not unkindly. "The ford silted in two years ago. Locals built a bridge a quarter-mile upstream. Lighter wagons can use it. Your supply loads cannot. There is a second crossing four miles east. Add time but avoid losing a wagon."

He looked at his map. He looked at her.

"You have made this run before," he said.

"Four times." She closed the ledger with a sound like punctuation. "The roads change. The maps do not. I would have thought the military would have solved this problem by now, but perhaps the military finds it useful to have inaccurate maps."

It was the particular kind of observation that is technically not rude but lands precisely where a rude remark would land. He was aware that she was watching him to see how he would take it.

"Four miles east," he said. "I will want to see it before we commit the convoy."

She nodded once, which was either approval or simple acknowledgment, and turned to speak to her driver. He went to find Corporal Rousseau, who had the watch, and told him to adjust the planned route.

"Change of plan, Corporal?" Rousseau asked, which was the question Rousseau asked about all changes of plan, with his Marseille accent smoothing the edges of it into something that sounded merely conversational.

"The ford is not where the map shows," Charles said. "We go east four miles to the second crossing."

Rousseau absorbed this without visible reaction, which was one of the things Charles valued about him. He went to pass the word. Charles looked back toward the convoy, where Margot had climbed onto the seat of the lead Vieillard wagon and was saying something to her driver that involved pointing at two of the other wagons in a way that suggested she was reorganizing the order of march without consulting anyone.

He considered intervening. He decided to watch what she was doing first.

What she was doing was putting the two heaviest wagons third and fourth instead of first and second, which was correct. Heavy wagons set the pace for a convoy and if they led, the whole train moved at heavy-wagon speed on the flat and bunched badly on inclines. He would have rearranged this himself at the first incline. She had arranged it before they started.

He moved Tonnerre up alongside the wagon.

"You have done this before," he said.

"We have established that," she said.

"I meant the order of march."

She glanced at him. "You were going to leave the Orosco wagons at the front. They are carrying iron hardware. They would have stalled on the first slope and lost us an hour."

"I was going to rearrange them at the first slope."

"I arranged them before the first slope." She looked back to the road ahead, where the first gray light was beginning to separate sky from terrain. "Is there a protocol I am violating?"

There was a protocol she was technically violating. He decided the protocol was less important than the order of march.

"No," he said.

She said nothing, and he could not see her face from this angle, but he had the distinct impression she was filing this away.

* * *

The convoy made good time on the first day. The second crossing was where she had said it would be. Three of his men walked the approaches while the wagons queued, and he marked the corrected position on his map in ink, because the pencil notation his headquarters had made would only confuse the next officer who received this map.

He rode alongside the Vieillard wagons through most of the morning, which was practical because this was where the convoy's most organized point of contact was, and also because Margot spoke Spanish, which he did not, and three times during the morning she translated brief exchanges with locals who appeared on the roadside. One turned out to be offering information about road conditions ahead. Two appeared to be trying to sell things, though he was not clear on what.

"What did he want?" he asked, after the third one.

"Cheese," she said. "He was very persistent about the cheese."

"You did not buy any."

"I have enough cheese." She shifted in the seat and reached into the satchel for the ledger, which she opened across her knees without particularly looking at the road, suggesting she trusted the horse. "The man at the second farm, the one with the blue shutters, he said there was cavalry activity on the road north of Pedroso two days ago. Partisans or French, he was not certain. You should know this."

He should know this. He had not gotten it from the man with the blue shutters because he did not speak Spanish.

"Thank you," he said.

She glanced up from the ledger, a brief assessment, and went back to her figures.

At midday they stopped to water the horses and mules. He had the men eat on a rotation so the convoy stayed ready. Margot sat on the step of her wagon and ate bread and something wrapped in cloth that smelled like salt fish, and worked on the ledger. He ate standing, which was habit, watching the road in both directions while Tonnerre pulled at a patch of sparse grass near the verge.

"Do you always eat standing?" she asked, without looking up.

"Yes."

"That seems uncomfortable."

"Less comfortable than being surprised," he said.

She wrote something in the ledger. "You have been doing this for three years?"

"Three and some months."

"And before that?"

"Cadet school. Before that, Bordeaux." He paused. "You know this from the letters."

"Knowing a thing from letters and knowing it from the person are different," she said, which was a thing he had been finding true all morning. "In the letters you write about the operations as though they are puzzles. Routes and timing and the logistics of moving men across distances. You did not write much about the men."

He had written about the men. He had written about Mercier and his missing pinky and Bonnard's steadiness and Rousseau's developing eye for terrain. He said this.

"You wrote about what they were useful for," she said. "That is not quite the same thing."

He thought about this.

"It is hard to write about the men without writing about what happened to some of them," he said. "And that is not the kind of letter I want to send."

She looked up from the ledger then. She looked at him directly, with the assessing quality she brought to most things. He was aware, in a way that was not entirely comfortable, that she was actually seeing him rather than extracting information.

"No," she said. "I imagine not." And went back to the ledger.

They moved out again in twenty minutes. He rode to the head of the column and did not look back at the wagon, because looking back would have been noted and would not have accomplished anything.

* * *

The second day was slower. One of the Orosco wagons threw a wheel, which cost two hours while the driver and one of Charles's men with carpentry experience coaxed it back into condition with insufficient tools and considerable improvisation. Margot came to look at it, assessed the situation in about thirty seconds, and produced from somewhere in her satchel a specific wooden wedge that fit the hub better than what they had been using. She did not explain where the wedge had come from. She handed it to the carpenter and went back to her wagon.

"Where did she get that?" Rousseau asked, appearing at Charles's elbow.

"I have no idea," Charles said.

"Does she carry a complete wagon repair kit in that bag?"

"She might."

Rousseau looked at the Vieillard wagon with the expression he usually reserved for particularly defensible terrain. "Practical woman," he said, and went back to his post.

They made camp that evening in a field outside a small village. Charles posted the watch, checked the perimeter, ate with his sergeants. After, when the camp had settled into its nighttime sounds and the fire had died down to coals, he walked the wagon line on his way to check the night watch, and she was sitting outside the lead wagon with the lantern and the ledger again.

"You work late," he said.

"The accounts do not complete themselves." She moved the lantern slightly to improve the light. "Sit down. I will not bite."

He sat on an upended crate across from her. The lantern threw a circle of yellow light. Somewhere in the village a dog was barking at something.

"Tell me about the route tomorrow," she said.

He told her. She listened, asked two questions that were better than the questions a staff officer would have asked, and corrected one assumption he had made about the road surface near the river based on information from her driver, who had taken this road the previous winter.

"How do you keep all of this?" he asked. "The route information. The road conditions."

"Letters," she said. "And memory. And the ledger, when something is specific enough to record." She turned a page. "Your predecessor on this convoy last year took a different route. It added two days and lost a mule. I sent your headquarters a note about the better route. I do not know if they read it."

He was fairly certain they had not read it. He had not been given any note from a merchant advisor when he received his orders.

"I will write it up myself when we return," he said.

She looked at him again with that brief, checking quality. "Most officers do not bother," she said.

"Most officers probably do not need to take this route again."

"You might."

"Then I will need the route information."

She smiled at this, not broadly but a real one, the kind that implied something was genuinely funny rather than merely tolerable. He noticed this and did not say anything about it, because there was nothing to say that would not have been worse than silence.

"Your letters," she said. "They are better than most people's letters."

"I write them carefully."

"I know. You write them the way you approach a problem, which is methodically but not without interest. It is unusual." She glanced down at the ledger. "My father's other correspondents write letters that are entirely about themselves. You ask questions."

"Questions are more useful than statements."

"Most men do not believe this in practice."

He thought about what to say to this, and then decided on the truth. "I was given good advice early. Ask questions before you assume you know the shape of a thing."

"Who gave you that advice?"

"Sergeant Mercier. Early on, when I was making a great many wrong assumptions about the shape of things."

"Is he still with you?"

"Yes."

"Write to me something about him," she said. "Not what he is useful for. Something about him."

He thought about Mercier. The deliberate speech. The missing finger. The way he checked the boots of a new conscript before he checked their weapons, because boots were what killed men on long marches, and new conscripts did not know this yet.

"He checks new men's boots," Charles said. "Before anything else. Most sergeants check the weapons."

She looked up.

"He checks the boots," Charles said, "because he knows what kills men in the first week, and it is not bad weapons. It is blisters and wet feet and being told to keep moving regardless."

She was quiet for a moment. The dog in the village had stopped barking.

"Write me that in a letter," she said. "Write me the things that matter and not just the useful things. I think you know the difference."

He thought he probably did. He also thought this was a harder thing to write than route information, and that he was not sure he could do it without it becoming something he was not ready to send.

"I will try," he said, which was honest.

She closed the ledger. "Get some sleep," she said. "You have watch at three."

"How do you know I have watch at three?"

"You told your corporal at supper. You were not far away."

He stood. He put his hands in his pockets, a habit he had not entirely broken since Bordeaux.

"Good night," he said.

"Good night, Lieutenant."

He walked the wagon line in the dark, checked the watch positions, told Rousseau everything was in order. He lay down on his bedroll and looked at the stars and thought, with the part of his mind that ran underneath everything else like a ground current, that four years of letters and three days of actual proximity were producing something he did not have words for yet, and that the lack of words was probably sensible, and that he should go to sleep.

He went to sleep.

* * *

On the morning of the third day they reached the handoff point, a junction town with a small garrison and a market square that smelled of animal and dust. The convoy would continue under garrison protection from here. Charles's assignment ended at this town.

He oversaw the transfer, checked the paperwork, confirmed that the garrison lieutenant understood which wagons contained what and in which order the handoff should proceed. Margot supervised from her wagon, and at one point corrected the garrison lieutenant's count of the Vieillard cargo, which she did politely but without hesitation, and was correct.

After, when the wagons were sorted and his men were watering the horses, she came to where he was standing with Tonnerre at the edge of the square.

"The ford," she said.

"Your ford that my map had wrong."

"There is another one. Not on this route, but on the road between here and the valley below Pedroso. Your maps will show a ford at the widest point, which is where anyone would look for one. The actual safe crossing is fifty yards upstream, where the rocks create a shelf. The wide ford goes deep in the center. Fast water under the surface even when it looks calm."

He took out the map and she pointed, and he marked it.

"How do you know this one?" he asked.

"Rodrigo, my driver. He is from this region. He swims in rivers the way you walk on roads." She put her hand on the map to orient it. "Here. Mark it clearly. Fifty yards upstream from the ford your map shows."

He marked it clearly.

"Thank you," he said.

She stepped back. In the morning light he noticed what he had noticed in parts over three days and was noticing as a whole now: that she was not a girl from a brief meeting in Poland, which was what he had been holding in his mind alongside the letters, superimposing the two and getting a composite that was less accurate than either. She was a person he had been talking to for four years who was also an actual person in front of him, and these two things had only just aligned.

She looked at him steadily.

"You were better than I expected," she said.

"At escorting the convoy?"

"At listening." She adjusted the strap of the satchel. "Most officers do not listen to merchants. They listen at merchants, which is different."

"You know things I do not know," he said. "Listening seems practical."

"Most people do not find it practical."

He had no argument with this.

"Safe travel," he said.

"And you," she said. "Use the ford I told you about."

She went back to her wagons. He watched for one moment, the length of time it takes to establish that you are not watching particularly, and then turned to find Rousseau waiting a discreet ten feet away with an expression of elaborate non-observation.

"Convoy handed off," Charles said.

"Yes, sir."

"We move out in twenty minutes."

"Yes, sir."

Rousseau said nothing further. Charles was aware this restraint was costing him something.

Two weeks later, moving the unit to a new position, Charles chose the route through the valley below Pedroso. He found the ford where the map showed it, looked at it, thought about the rocks creating a shelf fifty yards upstream, and moved the column upstream. The water there was knee-deep and steady, the rock shelf solid under the horses' hooves. They crossed in twelve minutes without difficulty.

He marked the map again, adding the note: *safe crossing, shelf 50m upstream from map position. Do not use wide ford. Deceptive depth.*

Two days later he heard from a courier that a cavalry patrol had been badly shot up in the valley below Pedroso, at the wide ford, where they had bunched up in the center while the water went deep around them.

He sat with this information for a while. Then he wrote it into his notebook under route assessments and went to check on the horses.

* * *

The young man's name was Sergeant Arnaud Fouet. He was twenty-one years old and had come to the unit from a line regiment, transferred after demonstrating in three engagements that he had a particular aptitude for reading terrain that his previous command had not known what to do with. Charles had known what to do with it. He had spent four months developing it, walking Fouet through the specific work of evaluating a ridge line, reading shadows for covered approach routes, estimating enemy strength from dust clouds and the sound of movement before it became visible.

Fouet had been quick and serious and had the quality Charles valued most in men he was developing: he asked better questions as he learned more rather than fewer, because learning made him see more of what he did not yet understand.

Charles had recommended him for a placement with a screening unit to the west, because the screening unit needed someone with exactly Fouet's terrain sense and the assignment would develop him further. He had written the recommendation himself. He had been specific about Fouet's capabilities, which was what the recommendation required.

The screening unit was engaged at a ridge complex southeast of their position. The engagement went badly from the start. The screening unit had taken a covered approach they had not evaluated correctly, and the ridge they moved to hold turned out to be dominated by a higher feature two hundred yards east. Three men killed, one of them Sergeant Arnaud Fouet, who had been at the front of the approach because terrain assessment was what he was there for.

Charles received the report in the morning. He read it twice, put it on the table, and went to check on Tonnerre, who had been favoring his left foreleg slightly since the previous day's march and who stood patiently while Charles ran his hand down the leg and found nothing specific, only the general wear of hard roads.

He wrote the letter to Fouet's family in the afternoon. He had written enough of these letters now that the form was established: the service, the circumstances, the character, the expression of condolence. The form was useful because it held the shape of the thing and did not require him to find new words each time. The form was not comfort. It was a structure he completed correctly, and then it was done, and the letter went.

He sat at the table after and did not write anything else for a while.

The specific thought he was working around was this: if he had not written the recommendation, Fouet would not have been with the screening unit. If he had not developed Fouet's terrain sense, there would have been no recommendation to write, because there would have been no exceptional capability to recommend. If he had left Fouet to develop on his own, or not at all, Fouet would have remained in the line regiment, doing adequate work, without the specific expertise that had made him the right man for the wrong assignment.

He had made Fouet better. A better sergeant gets harder assignments. He had made Fouet valuable enough to be spent.

He was aware that this logic, followed far enough, would make the mentorship of any soldier into a liability, which was absurd. He was aware that the ridge complex had been badly handled and that someone else's failure in terrain assessment was the proximate cause. He was aware that Fouet had been a soldier, doing a soldier's work, in a war that did not particularly negotiate with individual quality.

He was aware of all of this. It did not help much.

He took the list out of the inner pocket where he kept it and added Fouet's name in the margin of the third page, because there was no more room in the columns. He looked at the list for a moment, then folded it back and put it away.

The form of the letter was correct. He had expressed the condolence properly. The work was done.

He went to find Mercier, because Mercier had known Fouet, and because there was nothing to say but it was better to be standing with someone who had also known the man than to be sitting alone with the form letter and the list.

Mercier was cleaning his kit by the fire, slowly and thoroughly, which was what Mercier did when he was processing something he did not intend to speak about directly. They sat together for an hour. They did not talk about Fouet. Mercier made coffee, strong enough to function as a structural material, and Charles drank it and watched the fire and let the hour pass.

"The ridge complex," Mercier said finally, not looking up from a boot he was examining.

"Yes."

"He should not have been at the front of the approach."

"No."

Mercier put the boot down. He looked at his hand, at the gap where the pinky had been. "He was good," he said.

"Very good."

"Good enough for someone to put him somewhere his good was not well used."

Charles said nothing.

"That is not your doing," Mercier said, which was the end of it, because Mercier did not repeat himself.

They sat another half hour. Then Charles stood and said good night and went to bed.

* * *

The orders for the requisition came through in September, routed through two levels of command before reaching him with the kind of phrasing that was very clear about what was required and entirely silent on what it would mean for the people it was required from.

He went to his superior, a colonel who had the look of a man who had already had this conversation with someone above him and preferred not to have it again.

"Sir," Charles said. "The villages on the eastern section of the line are smaller. The requisition at the same rate will take a higher proportion of what they have." He had the numbers. He always had the numbers. "If we concentrate the requisition on three larger settlements to the north, we can meet the supply requirement without the same impact."

The colonel looked at the requisition order. He looked at Charles.

"These are your orders," the colonel said. "The settlements designated are designated for logistical reasons that are not within your scope to revise. Execute the order."

"Yes, sir," Charles said.

He executed the order.

The execution was organized the way he organized everything: systematically, with attention to what the order required and what it permitted. It required a specific quantity of grain, dried provisions, and draft animals for military use. It did not specify that he must take seed grain. He did not take seed grain, which required him to be more precise about the measurements, because seed grain and eating grain are stored together and the men doing the loading needed to be told clearly which was which. He told them clearly. He also did not take the working horses of families that had no alternative transport, taking instead from households that had additional animals, which required more stops and a longer day but achieved the supply requirement. The order did not require him to make these distinctions. He made them anyway, because the order did not forbid it and because he had the latitude and chose to use it.

The village at the end of the eastern section was small, perhaps thirty houses. The square was empty when they arrived except for a dog and two elderly men who watched from a doorway and did not approach. His men knew the work by then and moved through it efficiently: the wagons positioned, the counting done, the loading organized. He supervised from the center of the square, moving as needed, speaking to his men in the low voice he used when he wanted things done quickly and without performance.

The woman was in the doorway of a house at the edge of the square. Not one of the houses being requisitioned from, just a doorway. She was perhaps fifty years old, wearing dark clothing, with her arms folded in the particular way that is not cold, and she watched. She did not speak, and he did not speak to her, and she continued watching while his men finished loading the wagons and he did the final count and gave the order to move out.

He looked at her once, at the end. She was still watching. He turned away and mounted Tonnerre and rode to the head of the column.

That evening he wrote in his journal. He recorded the settlement, the date, the quantities requisitioned: forty-two measures of grain, eighteen measures of dried provisions, four draft animals, two carts. He noted that seed stores had been left intact. He noted that working animals for households with no alternative transport had been left. He noted that the requisition had met the specified quantities.

He did not write about the woman in the doorway.

He read back over what he had written, which was accurate and complete, and closed the journal.

* * *

Margot's letter arrived in October, forwarded through the supply chain with the usual delay, written in late August and six weeks in transit. He read it in the evening, by the fire, while Rousseau went over the next day's route with Bonnard somewhere behind him.

She wrote about Frankfurt primarily, because her father had needed her there for the summer, which she described with precise notation of what had needed to be done and what had been done, though with an undertone of affection for the Frankfurt office that was not in the letters she wrote from the road. She had apparently reorganized the filing system, which had been in a state she characterized as creative, and had settled a dispute with a partner firm that had been running two years longer than it should have.

Toward the end of the letter she wrote about Spain. She wrote: *Father's partner in the region you escorted through has gone entirely silent. Castellon, who manages the network there, stopped writing in August. The contacts who relied on that network for news of supply movements have likewise gone quiet. Father is concerned but has no one to send for information. I suspect the situation in the region has become difficult. If you are nearby and have any intelligence that does not violate your obligations, I would be grateful for the picture.*

He read this section twice.

The settlement Castellon's network had covered, or what he suspected was the same network from the geographic correspondence, was on the eastern section of the line. He did not know the name Castellon specifically, but he knew the region, and he knew what had happened in the region in September, and the silence that had fallen over the supply contacts was a silence he could account for with reasonable confidence.

He put the letter down and looked at the fire.

He did not write back that night. He thought about what he would say and could not find the shape of it, which was unusual for him with letters, so he let it sit the way he let difficult terrain assessments sit, giving them time to resolve into something he could see clearly.

When he wrote back, ten days later, he wrote about the convoy escort, which he had not described in any detail because it had arrived after his last letter. He wrote about the roads and the ford she had corrected him on, and that he had used the second ford and it had served well. He wrote about something she had asked him to write about, which was Mercier and the boots, and he found that once he started he could do it: describe Mercier at the boots of a new conscript with the patience of someone who knows that this particular five minutes is worth more than the next hour of weapons drill. He tried to write about Mercier as a person, which required noticing different things than he usually recorded, and which took him three drafts before it felt accurate rather than performed.

He wrote about the roads continuing to change faster than the maps, and that he had taken to maintaining his own annotated copy because the official maps were maintained by people who were not in Spain.

He did not write about the requisition.

He did not write that he knew why Castellon's network had gone silent, or what it would take to restart it, or that the quantities required by the order had been met with precision while the seed stores had been left intact, which was the best he had been able to do within what was permitted, and which had not been enough to prevent the silence.

He wrote: *I hope the Frankfurt filing system holds. I hope your father's concerns for the region resolve. The situation has been difficult across much of the eastern line this autumn and I suspect travel and correspondence have been disrupted by the general pressure of operations. I cannot offer a more specific picture. I am sorry I cannot offer more.*

He read it back. It was accurate. It said precisely what it said, and nothing that it did not say.

He folded the letter and addressed it and put it with the outgoing post.

* * *

He was twenty-three years old in October and would turn twenty-four in January, which was a thing he noted for the record and did not otherwise particularly mark. He had been in service for three years and some months. He was a lieutenant who had not disgraced himself, who had kept his men alive at a rate that was above the general average for the work they were doing, who could read a road and assess a position and write a cogent report and execute an uncomfortable order within the limits of what could be done within it.

He had met Margot Vieillard in person for the first time in four years. He had found that she was exactly as competent as her letters suggested, that the competence was slightly different in person because letters were a kind of editing and people were not, and that the difference was more interesting than the editing. He had used the ford she told him about and it had been exactly where she said. He had learned that she kept a wooden wedge in her satchel for wheel hub repairs, and that she smiled when something was genuinely funny, and that she thought he listened in a way that was uncommon.

He had not allowed himself to think past the practical inventory of these facts, which was the correct way to handle things when he was not sure what handling them correctly would require.

The letters continued. They were, on his end, better than they had been. He tried to write the things that mattered and not only the useful things, because she had asked him to and because he had found it was possible. It cost something to write that way. He was not sure yet what it cost or what the cost was for, but he wrote the letters carefully and sent them and waited for her return letters with a patience that was almost entirely convincing.

The list had one more name than it had before October. He kept it in the inner pocket, adding to it when he needed to. He kept the paper from Elise in the left boot, as always, before any movement that might become something he needed to move quickly out of.

The two papers occupied different pockets and served different purposes and he did not think about the relationship between them.

The autumn deepened. The rearguard work continued, unglamorous as advertised, the long grinding work of maintaining a line while larger forces moved around it like weather. He reported. He assessed. He wrote letters. He kept the accounts as accurate as he could manage, in the journal and in the requisition records and in the annotated maps and in the careful correspondence that was becoming, without him having fully decided this, one of the things he did with the parts of himself that the official work did not reach.

He did not write about the woman in the doorway.

But he remembered her, the way you remember the specific gravity of a thing that has no other place to go.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 10

The orders arrived on a Tuesday, which Charles had always found to be an unremarkable day for unremarkable things, and a Tuesday seemed therefore approximately correct for being told that his unit was to delay the enemy advance long enough for the infantry to withdraw through a gap that should not have existed and would not have existed except that someone at Corps level had miscalculated the speed of the French retreat and subsequently the speed of the Allied pursuit.

He read them twice in the adjutant's tent, standing, because there was no chair.

The terrain sketch attached showed a valley approach with limited cover on the left flank, a ridgeline on the right that looked workable until you noticed the elevation marks, and a main avenue of approach across open ground that would be useful for moving cavalry at speed and also, he noted, for the enemy to observe cavalry moving. The enemy force was estimated at three times his strength. The estimate had a small notation at the bottom: subject to confirmation. This notation appeared on most estimates of enemy strength, in his experience, specifically in situations where confirmation was not possible and the estimate was likely wrong in an unfavorable direction.

He folded the orders and put them in his coat pocket and stood for a moment looking at the tent wall.

He found Major Beaumont at headquarters, standing with his hands behind his back, looking at the same terrain sketch pinned to a board. He was at a senior staff position now, which meant he spent most of his time at maps and boards rather than at the front, and his hands had gone soft in a way that cavalry hands did not usually go soft. He still moved carefully. He still drank alone.

"Sir," Charles said.

Beaumont turned. He had the look he got when he had been thinking through a problem and had arrived at an answer he did not especially like. It was a specific look, different from his usual settled expression. Charles had learned to recognize it across five years.

"The delaying action," Charles said. "I would like to discuss the approach."

"Yes," Beaumont said. "Go ahead."

"The left flank has no cover past the first tree line. They will see us coming across six hundred meters of open ground. The ridgeline on the right is workable for light screening but not for a sustained action. If we are to hold them for two hours, which is what the infantry requires, we will need to use the tree line as the primary position and accept the exposed approach."

Beaumont nodded slowly.

"The odds. Three to one, possibly higher."

"Yes."

"Sir, this is." He stopped. He had been going to say: this is not a reasonable operation. But that was not exactly what he wanted to say, and it was not exactly accurate. He stood with the unfinished sentence.

Beaumont looked at him, waiting, not unkindly.

"I know what it is," Beaumont said. "But it needs doing."

Charles looked at the sketch. Six hundred meters of open ground. Three to one odds. Two hours required.

"If it needs doing," he said, "I want to select the approach timing. First light, not dawn. The light will be in their eyes on the eastern approach for the first fifteen minutes."

"Agreed," Beaumont said.

"And I want authorization to withdraw to secondary position at the one-hour mark if the tree line becomes untenable."

Beaumont considered this. He had the manner of a man who was accustomed to giving authorizations that were not complete authorizations because complete authorizations were not always available to give.

"You will use your judgment," he said.

This was not authorization. It was also not denial. In Charles's experience, in operations of this particular kind, it was the best available answer.

"Understood, sir," he said.

He went to find his men.

* * *

He told them that evening, sitting under the tree line they would be defending in fourteen hours, the light going gray and the horses tethered in the draw behind them where the ground was softer. He did not use the phrase delaying action, which had a technical meaning most of them understood in the abstract, or the phrase acceptable losses, which had a technical meaning none of them found acceptable. He said: we are covering the withdrawal. The infantry needs two hours. We give them two hours and we come home.

He went through the specific plan: approach timing, tree line positions, withdrawal conditions. He answered questions where he could answer them. He said he did not know where he could not.

Sergeant Mercier, sitting to his left with his three-fingered left hand resting on his knee, said nothing through all of this. This was Mercier saying something.

Rousseau was watching Charles's face with the specific attention of a young man taking a reading on how worried to be. He was twenty now and had grown into his shoulders over the past year, had been given a promotion to Corporal in the summer and taken it with the same practical acceptance he brought to most things. He had the Marseille accent still, sharp at the edges of words, and it had not softened at all in two years of Peninsular War, which Charles had come to find reliable in some way he could not have precisely explained.

"Sir," Rousseau said.

"Corporal."

"The six hundred meters."

"I know," Charles said. "We move fast and we use whatever smoke there is. They will not have clean targets across most of it."

Rousseau accepted this with a small nod that was not quite satisfied but was the nod of a man filing something under: the officer has considered this and I do not have better information, which was different from being reassured but was close enough to function.

Private Pellerin was sharpening a knife with the automatic motions of a man who was not thinking about the knife. He was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been in the unit since the Pyrenees crossing and had a quality Charles valued in infantry that transferred well to cavalry: he did the unglamorous work without complaint. Archambault was sitting with his back against a tree, eyes closed, possibly asleep; he had the ability to sleep in almost any conditions, which was either a gift or a sign of something Charles did not want to examine. Cassouin had a small book and was writing in it with his forearm blocking the page, the way men wrote when they were writing something personal rather than something official.

Charles looked at the tree line in the failing light. Good cover once they reached it. Good lanes. Defensible for two hours if managed correctly.

He checked his boot alone later, when the men were settling. The paper was there, Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting. He had replaced it in Salamanca when the last one wore through at the fold. He thought, briefly, of Margot. He was not sure why she intruded at this particular moment. He had thought about Margot in various contexts over the past year, since the convoy escort, but not usually before operations. He put it away. There was nothing useful in it tonight.

He slept four hours and woke before the call.

* * *

The approach was bad in the way he had known it would be bad, which did not make it better.

Six hundred meters is a measurable distance. He had walked it the morning before. At a walk it took seven minutes. At a trot it took three. At a canter across ground that had taken rain the previous night and was expressing an opinion about bearing cavalry weight, it took four, which was one more minute than he had planned.

He talked to Tonnerre through it. The bay gelding moved with the careful attention of a horse navigating uncertain footing, adjusting his stride in the way he did when the ground said one thing and the pace said another. Charles kept the voice low and steady and the hand on the neck when he could reach it without losing his line. The familiar one-two pat. Tonnerre knew this pat. It meant: I know, keep going.

Enemy fire opened at approximately two hundred meters, which was thirty meters earlier than his pessimistic estimate. He heard the first volley, heard the subsequent sounds, and shouted the order to scatter into pre-arranged lines without breaking pace, because breaking pace in the open was the wrong decision and he had decided this before the morning started. The smoke from the first exchange was already drifting and he navigated by the dark shapes of the tree line, which he could see, and by the sound of fire, which told him where not to aim his horse.

He reached the tree line.

He turned and counted while the unit established positions, which is to say he counted while they moved and was counting before he had made a conscious decision to count. All present except two who were still crossing the open ground, both moving, both upright. Both reached the tree line in the next ninety seconds.

He gave the order to establish positions and the work of defending began.

The two hours proceeded as extended tactical actions proceed: badly in detail, in aggregate well enough.

In detail: the first ten minutes were the worst. The unit was still shaken from the approach, horses unsettled, the men finding their positions in the tree line with the particular inefficiency that came from moving fast under fire and then having to stop and think. He rode the line twice, giving orders he had already given, because orders given once under fire are heard once under fire and that is not the same as understood. By the fifteen-minute mark the positions were set and the fire was disciplined and the enemy, who had expected less resistance, was adjusting.

The problem with the left flank developed at the forty-minute mark. An enemy section was moving into a fold in the ground that his original position estimate had not fully accounted for. If they reached the fold they would have an angle on the tree line that would make the left position untenable. He could see this developing. He had approximately eight minutes before it became a problem and three minutes after that before it became a disaster.

He sent Rousseau with his section to the left, to a secondary tree line that was thinner cover but covered the fold. "Hold the angle," he said. "Not the ground. The angle."

Rousseau looked at where he was pointing and nodded once with the expression of a man who has understood the geometry. He moved his section in the good clean way he had developed over two years in Spain.

The fold was blocked. The left held.

In the center, around the one-hour mark, the fire intensified to a level that told him the enemy had identified the center positions as the primary threat and was concentrating resources. A man went down on his left. He did not have time to look. He gave the order to pull the center back twenty meters to secondary cover within the tree line, which gave up some firing angle but gave the enemy less of a target, and the fire eased.

He kept adjusting positions as the enemy probed, moving sections in response to information as it arrived, always one adjustment ahead of failure. He used the ridgeline for observation when the left flank drew pressure. He kept Rousseau's section as a reserve after the fold was secured and used it twice more to fill gaps that would have cost him the line if they had opened fully.

He knew what he was doing. That was the particular quality of these two hours: he knew what he was doing. He had done enough of this that he recognized the shape of the action as it was happening and could respond to it the way one responds to a language one speaks. The men were in trouble. He kept them out of more trouble. That was the work.

At the one-hour mark he assessed the tree line. The enemy had identified their main firing positions and was applying concentrated pressure on the right. The right would fail within twenty minutes.

He gave the withdrawal order.

The secondary position was three hundred meters back and slightly higher, which gave them better observation and worse cover. The withdrawal was executed under fire. He moved the sections in sequence, left first, center covering, right last, and he was the last man off the tree line position because that was how withdrawals worked and he had never found a satisfactory argument against it.

At the secondary position he held for forty more minutes. When the signal came that the infantry was clear, he pulled the unit back through the valley in good order, with Tonnerre's ears forward and attentive, reading the sounds that Charles was also reading, and the guns behind them becoming quieter with each kilometer.

At the rally point he counted.

Pellerin was not present.

He counted again. Archambault was not present.

Cassouin was being held upright on his horse by two of his men, one on either side, his arm against his chest and his face the specific color of someone whose body was occupied with a serious problem. Charles rode to him. Cassouin looked at him with eyes that were present and then were less present and then he died, there on the horse, with the small book in his coat pocket that he had been writing in the evening before with his arm blocking the page.

Three.

Charles sat with the number three in the way he had learned to sit with numbers of this kind, which was to say: he did not look away from it, he did not assign it meaning beyond what it was, he let it be the number it was, because the alternative was to not feel it or to feel it in a way that was not functional, and he needed to be functional.

He wrote the letters that night by lamp. He had a form now. He knew what the form was for and why it had become a form rather than a fresh composition each time: because fresh composition required him to find words adequate to the thing, and there were no words adequate to the thing, and the form at least provided a structure that did not pretend to be adequate. It said: your son died in service. It said: he was a good soldier. It said: his loss is felt by those who served with him. These things were true. They were also the surface of something much deeper and much less orderable.

Three letters: Pellerin's family in Lyon, Archambault's mother in Metz, Cassouin's young wife in Dijon. He had the addresses because he made it his business to know his men's addresses from the moment they joined the unit, which had seemed like morbid preparation the first time he did it and now seemed simply practical.

He did not know Cassouin's wife's name. He had not asked, and Cassouin had not volunteered it, because men in units generally did not talk about home in the specific way. He addressed the letter: To the family of Private Bernard Cassouin. He thought about the small book with the blocked page. He had put it in Cassouin's coat pocket when they laid him out. He did not read it. He thought that whoever received that book would understand it was a letter that had never needed sending.

He opened his field journal when the letters were done. He turned to the list in the back. He added three names.

The list was two full pages now, beginning to press against the margins in places where he had run out of column space and written smaller. He closed the journal and put it in his coat and went outside and stood in the November air until he was cold enough to sleep.

* * *

He did not write the discharge request he had been composing in his head.

He had been composing it, in a general way, for several months. Not in written form. In the form of sentences he tried in his head at odd moments: while inspecting horses, while reviewing manifests, while waiting in the headquarters ante-room for meetings that ran long. When I have made Captain. When Lieutenant Martin has enough seasoning. When this campaign reaches a stable point.

After Pellerin and Archambault and Cassouin he found that the sentences did not come. Not because he had made a decision against them. More as though the mechanism that produced the sentences had stopped engaging. He could still see the arguments in favor of the discharge request. They were still technically valid. He no longer found them persuasive.

The men trusted him. He had not wasted their lives before. He had not taken objectives that were not worth the cost. He had not exposed them to risks he had not calculated and accepted on their behalf. They trusted him because he had been trustworthy, and that trust was weight, and he was not certain what happened to the weight if he set it down.

Lieutenant Martin was capable. He would do good work in time. But Martin had not yet seen an action of this size. There was a period of learning that preceded competence, and that period had costs, and the costs were specific men's lives, and Charles knew which men they were in the way he knew all his men, by name and by how they moved and by what they were afraid of.

He was not the last good officer in Spain. He harbored no illusions about being irreplaceable in any broad sense. But he was specific. These men, this unit. If he walked away from that, men died who might not have died.

This is the logic, he thought, standing at the horse line in the dark. This is exactly the logic of the trap. He could see it clearly and it still governed him, because seeing a trap and stepping out of it are not the same operation.

Mercier was already at the horses. He had a lamp and was working through the inspection sequence on Tonnerre, who stood with the patient expression of a horse who has been inspected ten thousand times and has ceased to find it remarkable. Tonnerre's foreleg had healed fully two years ago. There was nothing in the inspection sequence that required doing on a horse in his current condition. Mercier worked through it anyway: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. His three-fingered hand found each position with the economy of motion that came from doing a thing correctly for twenty-five years.

He finished. Straightened. Gave Tonnerre a brief pat and said, to the horse: "All right."

He picked up the lamp and moved to the next horse.

Charles stood with Tonnerre and listened to Mercier work his way down the line. The inspection sequence in the dark, in the November cold, on horses that did not need it. He did not say anything. Mercier did not say anything. The conversation was complete.

* * *

He was in a garrison city in the spring of 1812, on seven days' leave that the army had granted with the specific reluctance armies display toward all leave but especially toward leave taken by officers whom the army felt were better employed elsewhere. He had not been in a city with a functioning market district in eight months. He had forgotten, specifically, the smell of a morning market: coffee and bread and something sweet from a stall two rows over and the general density of people engaged in ordinary commerce.

He was walking through the market district on the second morning, not looking for anything in particular, when a cart went over a cobblestone badly and the wheel struck a stone post with the sharp report of something going wrong quickly at close range.

He was behind a building stanchion before he had decided to move. He found himself with his back to the wall and his hand on his sword hilt and the cart sitting ten meters away with its wheel at an angle that was not going to correct itself and the driver swearing in Castilian at the horse, which was not paying attention to him.

He straightened. He moved his hand from the sword hilt. He stepped away from the stanchion.

She was watching him from across the market stall. She had a leather satchel and the expression of someone updating a picture they had been carrying of another person and finding the update significant.

"Leblanc," she said.

"Vieillard," he said.

She navigated around a table of goods and a man with a basket and came toward him. She was going to embrace him. He could see her intending it, the slight shift in posture that preceded it. He did not flinch, exactly. He became briefly incapable of receiving an embrace from another person, which produced the same result.

She stopped. She looked at his face.

"What happened?" she said.

He tried to explain the charge. He produced the technically accurate account: the orders, the terrain, the odds, the hours required, the number of men lost. He could hear himself giving this account and could tell that it was doing none of the work he needed it to do. The military words, the report words, the words that described the outline of something without its interior.

She listened without interrupting.

"Were they good men?" she said, when he had finished the outline.

"Yes," he said.

She nodded once. She did not say she was sorry, which he appreciated, because he had heard the phrase enough times that it had become a sound rather than a meaning.

They spent the morning at a warehouse she was using for transit storage, which had a desk in the corner behind towers of crates and a small coal brazier that kept the temperature approximately survivable. She had manifests to review and he sat on a crate and watched her work. She had a way of tracing columns with one finger while she added figures that was completely automatic, her attention apparently elsewhere while the addition happened underneath it.

The warehouse smelled of paper and coal smoke and, faintly, whatever had been stored in the crates most recently. Spices, he thought. Something warm and dry and foreign.

"You are staring," she said, not looking up.

"I am watching you work."

"These are the same thing." She made a note in the margin. "Tell me about the Aragon region. You were there in 1810."

He said he had been. He did not say in what capacity.

She closed the ledger on her finger to hold the place. "My father spent five years building trade relationships in that region. Twelve merchants across four towns. A route that functioned as well as anything in the interior. Trust takes time to build and it builds through consistency. You show up when you say you will. You pay the agreed price. You do not cheat on weights."

She said this with the matter-of-fact tone she used for logistics.

"What happened to the network?" he asked.

"The war happened to it." She looked at him. "Two campaigns through the region. Supply requisitions. A town burned, not by your army specifically, but burned. The merchants lost stock, lost routes, lost confidence that anything they built would survive the next season. Five years in two years."

He was quiet.

"Was it worth it?" she asked. "What you achieved there."

He had the military answer ready: the campaigns had accomplished their tactical objectives, supply lines secured, enemy resources disrupted. He knew the answer. He looked at her face and did not give it.

"I don't know," he said.

She looked at him as though determining whether this was the honest answer or the careful answer. It was both.

"I am rebuilding it," she said. "The Aragon network. Eighteen months, possibly two years. Starting over with whoever is still operating, building back the relationships one at a time." She opened a different ledger, this one organized with tabs she had cut from heavier paper. "The Portuguese route is partly open now. A contact in Lisbon, through my father's Frankfurt connections. If we establish a coastal leg that does not depend on the Spanish interior, the network survives the next disruption."

She was showing him her work. He understood this was what she was doing.

"The Lisbon contact," he said. "How reliable?"

"Unknown yet. That is what the next six months determine." She ran her finger down a column. "Trade relationships are tested by adversity, not by ease. We will see."

He looked at the ledger over her shoulder. The columns were dense with names, dates, figures, marginal notes written in at different angles over time, building on each other in layers. It was a picture of something being built carefully by someone who was thinking about the structure as she built it.

"You think in networks," he said.

"Everyone does. Some people are aware of it." She closed the ledger and turned on the chair to face him. "You think in terrain. Approach routes, defensive positions, cover. Threats to be neutralized. It is the same skill."

He looked at his hands, the calluses from reins and sword. He thought about the Aragon requisitions, the quantities he had recorded in his own ledgers. Grain. Oil. Draft animals. Numbers in a column. He thought about her columns: names, dates, relationships, the trust built through consistency.

"Same skill," he said. "Different purposes."

"Yes," she said.

She looked at him with something in her expression that had no military equivalent. Not anger. Not accusation. Something more careful than either of those.

"You took the supplies so that an army could keep moving," she said. "The army moved. The campaign continued. There is a logic to it."

"Yes," he said.

"And the villages went hungry."

"Some of them."

"And the merchant networks that depended on those villages having surplus to trade collapsed." She said this in the same tone she had used to describe the Aragon network, the fact-stating tone. "And my father's partner in the region lost his principal business relationship. And his family lives on considerably less now than they did before 1810."

He sat with this. He had, at various points over the past two years, constructed the defense. The army needed supplies. Without the army the campaign failed. Without the campaign Spain remained under a government that had been installed by conquest rather than consent. The calculation, in the abstract, had an answer.

"I know the argument," she said, as though she could hear him constructing it. "The army is necessary and the army needs supplies and therefore the supplies are necessary and the villages are an acceptable cost." She set her ledger aside. "I am not arguing that the army should have gone hungry. I am saying that I see what happens after. I am the person who comes through the region six months later trying to rebuild what someone else's necessary action destroyed."

He thought about Mercier's inspection sequence. The automatic thing, done correctly, twenty-five years of practice. The thing he would do for the rest of his life.

"What would you have had me do?" he said.

"Nothing different," she said. "I am not saying you should have refused the orders. I am saying: this is what it costs. Someone has to know what it costs. You should know."

He did know. He had known, in a general way, since the woman in the doorway in 1810. He had known it and had not allowed himself to follow the knowing to its conclusion, because the conclusion led somewhere he could not afford to go and still do the work.

"I know," he said.

She looked at him. She held his gaze long enough that it became a conversation without words, the kind he had not had with another person in a very long time.

He wanted to tell her that he had thought about her before the charge. He had no reasonable explanation for why he had thought of her before a charge rather than after, which was the ordinary time for thinking about people one cared about. He did not tell her this. It seemed like something that required a better explanation than he currently had.

She stayed in the city for three more days. He had five days remaining. In the evenings they walked the market district when her work was done, which was never before the light failed but usually before full dark. She knew which merchants were trustworthy by indicators he would not have noticed: how they kept their weights, how they spoke about absent competitors, whether they knew their own inventory by count or by approximate. He told her which roads south were reliable and which had been compromised in last winter's operations, and she made mental notes in the way she made all her notes, which was to say she filed them immediately and would produce them accurately six months later without consulting anything.

On the third evening she said: "You are not going home."

It was not a question.

"Not yet," he said.

She looked out at the street. A lamp-lighter was working his way down the block, the specific orange of the new flame appearing and steadying and moving to the next post.

"I thought not," she said.

There was no accusation in it. Something else, something that resembled recognition, or the acceptance of a fact that had been known for some time.

He looked at her profile in the new lamplight. He understood that he loved her in the specific way one loves someone whose mind one respects and whose presence makes one's own thinking clearer, and that this had been true for some time, and that he had no vocabulary for it that did not sound either insufficient or overstated. He was twenty-five and had been at war since he was eighteen and was not sure what else love was supposed to be, which was its own problem.

He did not say any of this. She did not say anything equivalent. They walked back to the inn in the ordinary way, and in the morning she left for the next stop on her route, and he stood in the street watching the cart until it turned the corner.

He wrote her a letter from the road that was three paragraphs about weather and road conditions and one sentence at the end: I found myself thinking more clearly in your company than I have in some time. He sent it before he could decide whether this was too much or not enough.

Her letter back arrived three weeks later: The feeling is mutual. The Lisbon route is progressing. I will write when I have news of the crossing near Badajoz, which my contact says has silted but may be workable with lighter loads.

He read this four times. He found it completely satisfying, which was either evidence that he understood her or evidence that he was not well. He folded it into his field jacket pocket, next to his notebook, and went to find Rousseau.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 11

The promotion arrived in the spring of 1812, written in the compact administrative prose that the army used for all important things, as though importance required less language rather than more. Captain Charles Leblanc. Effective the first of April.

He read the letter twice, which he always did with official correspondence, and then sat with it.

He had made Captain. That was the plan. He was twenty-five years old and he had made Captain and the plan, in its original form, said he should now go home. He was aware of this. He had been aware of it since January, when he had first understood that the promotion was coming, and he had been aware, since November, that he was not going to act on it.

He wrote to his father. The letter took three drafts. The first draft said: I have made Captain and will be requesting discharge when the operational situation allows. He crossed this out because it was not true. The second draft explained why the operational situation did not currently allow. He crossed this out because the explanation was too long and sounded like an argument he was making to himself, which it was. The third draft said: I have made Captain. I expect to be in Spain through the summer season at least. The estate is in your hands, which I trust implicitly. He sent this one.

His father wrote back in July, by which time Charles was somewhere different than he had been when he sent the letter, and the reply arrived with a delay that meant the reply was answering a question that Charles had internally moved past. His father's letter said: I understand. Come when you can. The olives are doing well this year. Henri the elder sends his regards.

There was no reproach in this. His father was a man who had done his own service and understood that service had its own schedule. He had served for six years in the old royal army before the Revolution made that service complicated, and he did not talk about it often, and he had never once suggested to Charles that he should not go or should come home. The absence of reproach was in some ways more difficult than reproach would have been. Reproach would have given him something to push against.

Elise wrote separately, as she always did. Her letter said: You made Captain. That was the plan. The rest of the letter was about the estate and a neighbor's daughter who had married well and a horse that had developed a problem with his left foreleg and the olives, which were indeed doing well. She was twenty-five now, which was a fact that arrived with the specific quality of facts about people you had last known at a different age. He had left when she was fourteen. He tried to calculate who she was now from the letters, which was like trying to understand a city from maps.

She did not say more than that sentence about the plan. The shape of what she left out was very large.

He wrote back to both: I will come when I can. He believed this when he wrote it.

He thought, in the months that followed, about what going home would mean in practice. He had a specific image of the vineyard that had not updated since he left: the rows of vines in the spring light, the stone farmhouse, his father's hands, the particular quality of Bordeaux air in the morning. He was aware that the vineyard had continued existing while he was gone, that things had changed in it, that Elise was twenty-five rather than fourteen and his father was eight years older and the horse with the foreleg problem was a horse he had never seen. The image in his head was correct for 1805 and was probably wrong for 1812.

He did not know who he would be there. He knew who he was here: Captain Leblanc, someone who could keep his unit alive under conditions that killed other units. He did not know what remained of Charles Leblanc, Henri's son, Elise's brother, the young man who had ridden away from Bordeaux at eighteen with a horse and a paper in his boot.

He told himself this was a temporary situation. He believed this, in the way that one believes things that are convenient to believe.

* * *

The letter from Margot that summer was different from her usual letters.

She had been writing him practical letters for two years now, since the garrison city meeting. Letters about trade routes and road conditions and the Lisbon contact and the slow progress of rebuilding the Aragon network. He wrote back about terrain and unit movements and weather and the horses. Their letters had the character of two professionals corresponding on matters of shared practical interest, with an undercurrent that neither of them addressed directly in the letters themselves. He thought about her often in the way he thought about things he could not currently act on: with a particular deliberate patience, setting the thought on a shelf and not forgetting it was there.

This letter was still practical. She was describing the eastern network, which she had been rebuilding through 1811, eighteen months of patient work, a dozen merchants, four towns, the relationship structures she had been building one visit at a time.

I rebuilt my father's network in the eastern region, she wrote. The last piece connected last month: a merchant in a town near the Aragon border who was reluctant but eventually agreed to trial shipments. It took four meetings over eight months. He has good contacts with the roads north. If it holds, we will have the route my father designed in 1806 running again.

Then: I do not need to tell you how long this took. You know what the region looked like in 1810. I assume you know because your army was there.

This was the first time she had said this directly in a letter. He read it twice. He thought about what to say. He put the letter away and came back to it the next evening and wrote back: The eastern region in 1810. Yes. He did not say more than that. He did not know what more to say that would be honest and useful simultaneously.

She did not write back for six weeks. When she did: Thank you for not pretending. The eastern merchant's first full shipment came through last week.

He kept this letter in his field jacket with the others. He had a small bundle now, tied with a piece of cord he had taken from a supply kit. He did not reread them often. He kept them because they existed and because the existence of letters from a specific person is a different thing from the letters themselves. The letters were evidence of a continuity that the war regularly threatened to make implausible. She was there, in Frankfurt and Spain and the road between them. The world she was building was continuing to be built. He kept going and she kept going and the letters were proof that this was true.

He thought about this sometimes in the way he thought about Tonnerre: the bay gelding was the same constant the letters were. Tonnerre under him, familiar as his own hands, the neck-pat before anything difficult. The animal had no opinion about wars or plans or what happened to merchant networks in Aragon. He required feeding and exercise and correct care of his feet and a horse he could trust beside him, and in return he was present, which was a quality Charles had come to find underrated in persons and animals alike.

He promoted Rousseau to Sergeant in the fall of 1813. He told him the direct way he had learned worked with Rousseau: "Sergeant. Effective the first."

Rousseau said: "Yes, sir." He said it with the acceptance of a man receiving information he had been expecting. Not performed surprise. Just the thing, received.

He was becoming what Mercier had been. Charles was aware of this in the specific way that one is aware of a thing one is glad of and troubled by simultaneously. He was making Rousseau better at this. He was teaching him the inspection sequence and the way to talk to men and the way to read ground. He was making him capable. This was the work continuing, and the work continuing was good, and the work continuing was also the thing he had been unable to stop.

* * *

He wrote to her: One more season. The campaign is at a stage where withdrawal makes no sense.

She replied to the other things in the letter and left that line unanswered. This was its own kind of answer.

Her vocabulary was full of words like established and rebuilt and connected. His was full of held and cleared and denied. He looked at this sometimes, the two sets of words side by side in his mind, and did not know what to do with the comparison except to know it was accurate.

* * *

Captain Fontaine was killed in September of 1813, which was a year and a half after Charles had made Captain and Fontaine had made Major, and the year after Fontaine had made a point of mentioning the promotion in a headquarters meeting with the specific offhandedness of a man who is not offhand about it.

They had been foils since Italy, which Charles recognized as a category rather than a judgment. Fontaine was not stupid. He was politically astute in the way that men who intend to survive armies are politically astute, and he was genuinely capable in the field, and he had the quality of confidence that came from having always, so far, been right when he took risks. This quality is useful in soldiers. It is also, in the right circumstances, fatal.

The plan had been Charles's originally. A flanking action to take a hilltop position that the enemy was using for observation. It was a good position, worth taking, but the approach was exposed on the right for the last two hundred meters, and the enemy, who was not stupid, had anticipated approaches to a position they were defending. Charles's plan used three elements in sequence: one to draw fire, one to move on the position, one to cover the withdrawal if the second element was repulsed.

Fontaine had a different plan. His plan used all three elements simultaneously and moved faster and did not include a withdrawal option because Fontaine's plans rarely did. His argument was that speed was its own protection: move faster than the enemy expected and you were past the exposed approach before they could concentrate fire.

This argument had worked before. Fontaine had a record of operations where aggressive movement had succeeded precisely because no one expected it. He was not wrong to make the argument. His record supported it.

The operations planning group chose Fontaine's plan. He had a Major's rank and a staff reputation and a way of presenting plans that made caution sound like timidity. Charles's objection, made once and not repeated, was noted and not acted upon.

The operation initially succeeded. Fontaine's simultaneous movement put two elements on the position before the defense concentrated. He was not wrong about speed. The position was taken in fifteen minutes and Fontaine had not lost a man in the approach.

Then the enemy brought up reserves from the second line. The position was overextended. The flanks were not secured because Fontaine's plan had put everything into the assault and nothing into the consolidation. The enemy reserves hit all three elements simultaneously.

The retreat was not orderly. Charles heard this from the officer who survived it, who gave a report that was controlled in tone but showed, in the way the man's eyes moved while he was speaking, that the experience had not been controlled at all. Fontaine had died covering the retreat of his own men, staying in the rear when a faster man might have moved sooner. This was either courage or the specific stubbornness of a man who did not believe in retreat on principle. Both were possible. The man reporting it was not certain which.

He received a hero's funeral. Commendations. His commander spoke of boldness and initiative and a soldier's courage. These things were true: Fontaine had been bold and had shown initiative and had died with courage. None of this addressed the plan, which was also a true thing. The ceremony was clean and well-attended and the tributes were genuine in the specific way that tributes to bold men who die in bold actions are genuine: people believe them because the alternative is to conclude that boldness itself is not a virtue, which is an uncomfortable position in an army.

Charles stood at the funeral and watched the coffin and thought: he died chasing the same thing I wanted at eighteen. The position. The commendation. The recognition that comes from taking the objective that everyone agreed was worth taking. He was not a fool. He was not reckless in a simple way. He was a man who had decided the risk was worth it and the risk came out wrong.

The troubling part, standing at the funeral in the November cold, was the awareness that he could not entirely distinguish Fontaine's decision from decisions he himself had made that had come out differently. He had made bold choices. He had moved fast when fast was right. In each of those cases he had been correct and his men had survived and he had received praise that was similar in kind if not in degree to the praise now being given to Fontaine.

He could not say, with certainty, that the difference between Fontaine's death and his own continued survival was judgment. Some of it was. He believed some of it was. But not all of it, and the part that was not judgment was something else, something less orderable, and he preferred not to look at that directly.

He said nothing about the plan. Who was he to tarnish a dead man's reputation? The plan was wrong and Fontaine was dead and the people at the funeral were grieving. None of that was a conversation for a funeral.

He added Fontaine to the list when he got back to the tent.

* * *

Sergeant Mercier was killed in March of 1814, on a routine patrol in a sector that had been quiet for three weeks.

The report said: Sergeant Laurent Mercier, killed in action, hostile fire. That was what the report said. It had the form that all casualty reports had, which was a form Charles had contributed to writing in his administrative capacity and which he now looked at from the other direction.

He had not been present. He was at headquarters when it happened, working through supply requisition approvals, which was the kind of work that fell to officers of his rank and which he did without complaint because complaining about administrative work was something junior officers did and he was not a junior officer. He found out when a clerk brought the day's casualty list and his name was on it, which was the way he found out about most of his men's deaths now: from lists.

He sat with the list for a moment. Then he asked the clerk to give his apologies to the afternoon meeting and went outside.

The rain was doing what the November rain in Spain did, which was to fall steadily with the specific commitment of something that does not intend to stop. He stood in it and let it be cold.

He thought about the inspection sequence. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. Mercier had taught him this in Munich in the spring of 1805, which was nineteen years after Mercier had first learned it himself from someone else, and it was a correct sequence that produced a correct result and Mercier had done it correctly for twenty-five years and it had not kept him alive.

He had known Mercier since he was a boy, practically, since he was eighteen and Mercier was forty and Spain was not yet a word any of them thought about in a military context. Mercier had taught him what he knew about keeping men alive. He had taught him the inspection sequence and the way to talk to horses and the way to talk to men so that they heard what you were actually saying rather than what they wanted to hear. He had taught him, mostly by example, that the work of being an officer was not about the officer.

Charles walked back inside, eventually, because the rain did not stop and he was wet and there was work to do.

He said a few words at the burial. There were ten men present, several of whom had served with Mercier for five or six years and all of whom were now, Charles noticed, looking at him rather than at the grave with the expression that men wear when they are waiting to see if the words are adequate. He stood in the rain and let himself find the words, which were not the form-words, which were insufficient for this, but were the specific words.

He said that Mercier had been in the army since before most of them were born and had done the work correctly for all of that time, which was a longer span of correctness than most men managed in any field. He said that what Mercier knew about keeping cavalry functioning was the kind of knowledge that did not get put in field manuals because it did not translate into rules, only into demonstrated competence. He said that Mercier had the quality of someone for whom the work was enough, who did not need the work to be praised or recognized, who cared only that it was done correctly. He said that this quality was rarer than it looked.

He stopped when he had said what was true. He was not sure the words were adequate. He did not think adequate was possible.

He walked back to headquarters in the rain.

He turned to the list in the back of his journal that night. It was two and a half pages now. The second column had filled and he had started crowding names into the margins, written smaller to fit, the handwriting changing as it compressed. He was beginning to look at the third page.

He found a space near the bottom of the right margin and added Mercier's name.

He had known, or known a version of, Mercier since 1805. Nine years. He had known him longer than he had known his own men when he was in his own men, which was a peculiarity of army service that he had not thought about until this moment. He had watched Mercier do the inspection sequence on horses that did not need it more times than he could count. He would still be watching Mercier do the inspection sequence in his memory for the rest of his life, because that is what the dead do: they keep doing the thing they were doing when you knew them, in the specific territory of memory where no one ages and nothing changes.

He closed the journal.

He had stopped being in the smoke, he realized, sitting in the tent with the rain on the canvas. He could not identify exactly when. At some point the smoke had moved elsewhere and he had been in headquarters getting reports, and the transition had not announced itself. Mercier's name in a casualty report rather than in an action he had witnessed: that was as close as he could come to marking the moment.

One day you were in it. The next day it was arriving by report. The day after that you were writing the reports yourself.

He had not chosen this. It was what promotion produced. He was a Captain approaching Major rank, and this was what Major rank looked like from the inside: stone walls and lamp oil and reports about men whose names you added to a list at the end of the day. The violence had not stopped. He had stopped being in contact with it.

He did not know if Mercier, doing the inspection sequence in the November dark on a horse that did not need inspecting, had understood this was coming. He suspected Mercier had understood quite a lot that he had not said directly, which was Mercier's consistent method. He suspected Mercier had understood all of it and said nothing because nothing useful could be said.

* * *

Margot's letter that spring, the spring of 1814, described the eastern route in full function for the first time.

He wrote back: I am glad the network is functioning. He put the letter in the bundle in his coat. He looked at the bundle: twelve letters now, three years of correspondence, and the ones most recently received were the ones where the gap between what she was describing and what he was doing had become the subject of the letters rather than something underneath them.

She was building. That was the clear fact of her correspondence. Every letter described something added, something that had not existed and now existed. A new contact, a proven route, a relationship that had been tested through difficulty and was therefore trustworthy. She thought in terms of what was growing. Her vocabulary was full of words like established and secured and connected.

His vocabulary, looking at his own letters, was full of words like held and denied and cleared. These were also legitimate things. He was not under illusions about the work being unnecessary. But they were not the same words.

He wrote to her: I have been thinking about the Aragon conversation. He looked at this line for a long time and then added: I think you were right about the cost. He sent it. He was not sure why he sent it, except that she was the one person he could say this to and mean it, and he had been carrying it for a year and a half.

She wrote back: I know you are not a bad man. I know you are not even a careless one. That is the part that I find difficult. If you were careless, it would be simpler.

He read this six times. He understood exactly what she meant. He found it uncomfortable to understand exactly what she meant and did not try to misunderstand it.

He wrote back: One more season. The campaign is at a stage where withdrawal makes no sense. She did not reply to this line, or if she did, she replied only to the other things in the letter and addressed this line by not addressing it, which was its own kind of answer.

He put the letters back in the bundle and tied the cord and put them in his coat. He went to find Rousseau, who needed to be briefed on the following week's patrol schedule, and who met him with the expression of competent attention that Rousseau always wore for briefings, and who Charles found, at the end of every difficult day, to be an uncomplicated relief.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 12

The order came in writing, which was always a sign that someone up the chain wanted documentation of compliance. Scorched earth policy, a designated region of eastern Spain. Deny resources to irregular and enemy forces. Requisition all available supplies. Neutralize storage facilities that cannot be transported.

Neutralize meant burn.

He read the order twice in his tent with Sergeant Duval standing at the flap, waiting. Duval had gray at his temples now and the manner of a man who checked things twice as a matter of professional pride and had checked Charles's expression when he started reading and knew, from the expression, what kind of order it was.

Charles went to his colonel.

"Sir," he said. "This will cause famine in the designated villages. The winter stores are already low. The harvest was poor."

The colonel looked at him. He was a competent man, Charles's colonel, a man who had served twenty years and thought carefully about most things. He had not been given this order carelessly and he was not receiving Charles's objection carelessly.

"That is why it will be effective," he said. "No stores means the guerrillas cannot shelter or resupply in those villages."

"Sir, the civilians."

"The civilians are providing material support to irregular forces. This has been documented." He looked at Charles steadily. "I understand your concern, Captain. Execute the order as humanely as the order permits. You will know what that means."

He knew what that meant. Don't take seed grain. Leave the draft animals if you can. Don't burn a house that has people in it. Don't do more than you are ordered to do.

He went back to his tent and told Duval to begin the preparation. Duval said: "Yes, sir," and went to work.

* * *

The execution took eight days.

He organized it into sections, each with clear objectives, each with a senior non-commissioned officer who had his specific instructions about what the order did and did not include. He was present for the first two days in the first village and then managed the operation from a position two kilometers back, moving between sections on Tonnerre, receiving reports, adjusting as problems arose.

He did not watch the burning from close range. He watched through a telescope, which was his job: observe, assess, direct. Through the telescope it was movement. Men going in and coming out. The specific dark shapes of smoke against the November sky, rising in columns and spreading as they rose. Wagons being loaded and moved. People on the road, moving away from the villages, which was the expected result and was described in the operational briefing as civilian displacement, which was accurate.

The reports that came back to him said: requisitioned. X kilos of grain. X liters of oil. X animals. The language was correct. The language was what the language was for.

He checked the paper in his boot each morning before mounting. Bordeaux. Elise's handwriting. He was not going into a battle. He checked it anyway, because the motion had become automatic at a level below decision.

On the fourth day a sergeant from the eastern section brought him a report and added, after the numbers, that a woman had tried to hide a grain sack in a well and had been removed from the area, that her children had been left with a neighbor, that the neighbor's house was outside the requisition perimeter and had not been touched. He listened to this report and said: noted. He added it to the daily record as a notation under civilian interactions. He did not know what the woman's name was.

On the sixth day the eastern section reported that one of the storage buildings had been fired before the contents could be fully removed, because an officer had misread the order sequence, and that approximately half the grain inside had burned. He had not authorized the sequence that produced this. He wrote a correction into the record and made a note to address the officer in question. This was the work.

By the eighth day the designated villages were empty of stores. His report back to the colonel was three pages. Numbers, dates, quantities, actions taken, exceptions noted. It was accurate. He had written it accurately, the way Mercier had taught him to do things: correctly, because correct was the only standard that held.

He gave the report to Duval to carry to headquarters and went to find Rousseau and told him the patrol schedule for the following week.

* * *

Ten days later his unit was moving back through the region on a different assignment.

The smell reached him first.

Not the smell of burning. The burning was ten days old and the rain had been working on it and what was left was the smell of wet ash, which was a different and more persistent smell than fresh smoke. But underneath the ash smell was the other thing: the particular quality of winter air in a place where the usual smells of a village are absent. No wood smoke from morning fires. No bread. No animals.

He knew this quality from other campaigns. His body had catalogued it before his mind caught up.

He rode through.

The market stalls in the main street were empty in the way that closed things are empty in winter, but not the same: closed things have the presence of what will come back. These had the absence of what was not coming back, or not soon. A woman at a well looking at him with the expression of someone who has recalibrated their expectations of what the morning would contain and has arrived at a reduced number. An old man in a doorway with the look of someone whose daily pattern has been removed and who has not yet constructed a new one. Two children at the edge of the road, watching the horses pass.

He did not stop. There was nothing useful he could do by stopping. He was not authorized to redistribute supplies he did not have, and sympathy was not a resource he could give them that would make any practical difference.

He rode through and did not stop and did not look away.

That evening in his tent he opened his journal to write the daily record. He tried several starts.

We passed through the requisitioned area on the return route. The villages showed the expected results of the operation.

He crossed this out. Expected results.

The operation was completed as ordered. The villages are without stores.

He looked at this. It was accurate. He crossed it out.

He sat with the blank page for a long time.

He wrote, finally: I am good at war. War makes children hungry. Therefore I make children hungry.

He looked at this. Then: No. That is not accurate. The enemy makes them hungry by using them as shields.

He crossed that out too. Then: No. We all make them hungry. The war makes them hungry.

He crossed it all out. He sat.

He lay down on his bedroll with his hand resting on his left boot. The paper was there. He was aware of it without thinking about it. He was aware that he was thirty kilometers from where the children had been standing at the edge of the road.

He did not sleep well.

* * *

Margot came to the garrison in March of 1814.

He was glad to see her. He did not know how glad until she was actually standing in the doorway of the headquarters building, in the practical coat with the collar up, with the leather satchel, and he felt something ease in him that had been held tight for several months.

She had business in the region: the southern leg of the Portuguese route was coming together and there were contracts to finalize in the garrison city. She had three days.

They had dinner with two of her merchant contacts on the first night, a long meal in which Charles said little and listened to the mechanics of Portuguese trade routes with attention that surprised him. She was operating across three languages in the conversation, switching without pausing, adjusting her register for each person at the table. He watched her work and thought: this is what competence looks like from outside the thing you are competent at.

He met Rousseau on the second day and introduced him. Rousseau was polite with the specific politeness of a man who has understood from context that this is a person of some importance to his officer and is therefore on his best behavior. Margot asked Rousseau how long he had served with Charles. Rousseau said: four years, not counting the letters she probably already knew about. She looked at Charles briefly when Rousseau said this and he found he had nothing to say to the look.

On the evening of the second day, after the merchant contacts had gone, she said: "There was a village. In the east. My father's partner's family lived there."

He said nothing.

"They are in the city now. The father is looking for work. Three children." She looked at him. "He told me what happened. What the army took and what they burned."

"I know," Charles said.

"You were there."

"I commanded the operation, yes."

She was quiet. Not a silence of accusation. The silence of someone who is deciding whether to say the thing they have been deciding whether to say for several months.

"You are one of the careful ones," she said. "Everyone I have spoken to says this. Leblanc is careful, Leblanc does not waste lives, Leblanc refuses unnecessary actions." She looked at her hands. "And you still did this."

"I was ordered to do it."

"Yes."

"If I refused, someone else did it. Probably with less care."

"Yes," she said. "I know this argument. I have been thinking about this argument since October." She turned to face him directly. "I am not saying you should have refused. I am saying: is that the standard? Less bad than what someone else would do? Is that what you are aiming at?"

He had the other defenses too. The campaign required it. The guerrillas were using those villages as supply points and without the supplies the guerrillas were less effective and fewer soldiers died. This was a true argument. He had made it to himself many times and found it held up under examination.

"The operation achieved its objective," he said. "Irregular activity in the designated region fell by more than half in the subsequent month."

"I know," she said. "I follow these things."

"People did not die in the region who would have died if the campaign continued without the operation."

"Which people?"

He meant soldiers. He meant the men under his command and the men in adjacent units. She knew who he meant. He heard the question anyway: which people get counted and which people don't.

He stopped defending.

"You build things," he said. "I know you build things. I know what they cost and how long they take."

"Yes."

"And I destroy them. That is a fair description."

"I did not say that."

"It is still fair."

She looked at him for a long time. Whatever she was deciding, she decided it slowly.

"I know you are not a bad man," she said. "God help me, I know that. You are an honest one and a careful one and you have never pretended to me about what this is." She turned back to the window. "That is the part I cannot resolve. You are the good version of this. And the good version still does what it does."

She asked the question then, the one she had been building toward since the garrison city in 1812: "You are good at this. You make it work. Without officers like you, wars would function worse and maybe end sooner. Does being the good version make it better? Or does it make it worse, because you make it possible?"

He had no answer. He had been living inside the question for two years. He could see it clearly from every angle. It was still not answerable.

"I do not know what I am without this," he said. "I have been doing this since I was eighteen. I am very good at it. I do not know if there is anything else."

She looked at him with the expression that had no military equivalent.

"There is," she said. "I have seen it. In the letters. In the garrison city." She picked up her satchel. "I just do not know if you can find it from where you are."

She was not leaving. She was going to her room because it was late and they both had work in the morning. But the sentence stayed after she left.

He sat in the chair by the window for a long time after she went up the stairs. He thought about the woman at the well, the children at the road, the old man in the doorway whose daily pattern had been removed. He thought about what Margot's father's partner was doing tonight in the city, looking for work. He thought about the report with its accurate numbers.

He thought about who he had been at eighteen, riding away from Bordeaux with the paper in his boot and the idea that there was a plan and the plan had an end.

He did not know if that person was still present or had been replaced so gradually that there was no single moment of departure he could point to.

He checked his boot in the morning before he mounted. Bordeaux. Elise's handwriting, worn at the fold.

He put it back and rode out to the briefing.

She got the news on a Tuesday in March, from a man named Hoffmann who worked for her father and who had the specific expression of someone who has been rehearsing a conversation on a long road and still does not know how to start it. He was standing in the doorway of the Frankfurt office when she arrived in the morning. He had his hat in his hands.

She understood what the hat meant before he said anything.

Her father had been at the Frankfurt warehouse alone on Saturday evening. He was checking inventory, which was something he had always done himself and had continued doing himself even as the business had grown to the point where he could have delegated it. It was a habit from when the business was small, when knowing the exact inventory was the difference between a profit and a loss, and he had never stopped. Hoffmann did not know exactly what had happened because he had not been there. What he knew was: there had been men in the warehouse district, deserters or displaced soldiers from the retreat or simply men who were in a difficult situation and made a worse decision, and her father had not been the first choice but he had been present. He had tried to protect the remaining goods.

"He did not suffer," Hoffmann said, and then looked like he wished he had not said this, because it was the thing one said and it was probably not accurate and they both knew it was probably not accurate.

"Thank you, Hoffmann," she said. "Come in."

She spent the next four hours with Hoffmann and with the two other senior employees, going through the immediate items. The warehouse inventory. The contracts in progress, three of them at critical stages. The correspondence that had been waiting for her father's signature and was now waiting for hers. The accounts.

She did not cry in the office. There was too much to do and too many people looking to her for direction, and direction was what she could give them, which meant direction was what she gave.

She cried alone that evening in the rooms above the office, for approximately twenty minutes, and then stopped and washed her face and came back downstairs because she had not finished reviewing the Strasbourg contract and it was due to the counterparty in two days.

* * *

She buried him on Thursday. Fast, because the business did not stop and because grief held in suspension is not grief resolved, and she had learned this from watching her father manage loss, which he had done several times over the years, always with the same method: bury it, work, let the work be the container.

Eleven people attended. She said a few words she had written that morning between the contract review and a meeting with the Strasbourg counterparty who had sent a representative rather than coming himself, which told her something about the representative's principal that she filed away for later. She had not slept much. The words came out clearly anyway. She had her father's voice, people said. She had always thought they meant the pitch. She understood now they meant the quality: the voice that stayed level when things were not level.

She looked at the marker after everyone had gone. Henri Vieillard. The years. He had been fifty-three, which was not old but was not young, and he had been in good health until he was not, which was the particular character of the kind of death he had had.

She thought: you were careful for thirty years. You built something that survived two wars. You were in the wrong place on the wrong Saturday.

She did not stay long. She had a meeting in an hour.

* * *

The immediate problem was the eastern route, which her father had been managing personally since she had been handling the southern Portuguese leg. Three merchants in that corridor who had their relationship with the business through her father specifically, through thirty years of dealings, through the particular trust that builds when a man pays on time and shows up when he says he will and does not cheat on weights. She had met them. She was not her father.

She wrote to all three of them the week of the funeral. She stated the change plainly. She did not perform grief in the letters and she did not perform confidence she did not have. She said: my father is dead, I am managing the business, I would like to continue the relationship we have had, and here is my proposal for the next shipment.

The first two wrote back within the week. One agreed to the proposal without modification. One proposed adjusted terms, not unreasonable, which she negotiated over two more letters and settled. The third did not write back for three weeks, and when he did he proposed terms that were predicated on the assumption that a twenty-two-year-old woman alone was a different kind of counterparty than her father.

She wrote back with a clear statement of what she knew about his accounts, his outstanding obligations, and the three other merchants in the corridor who she knew from her father's records had had prior dealings with him that had not reflected well on his credit. She was polite. She was specific. She said she expected the original terms and that her experience suggested the original terms were in his interest.

He wrote back agreeing to the original terms.

She filed the letter in the correspondence folder with the note: adjusts when presented with information. Reliable if monitored. This was the kind of note her father had always made. She understood, writing it, that she had been absorbing his practice for years without knowing she was absorbing it.

* * *

She also let one employee go. His name was Bertrand, he had been with the business for four years, and he was not doing bad work. He was doing adequate work, which meant that with the business at its current size, his salary was a cost that could not be justified, and she could not afford sentiment about it.

She told him directly, gave him two months' pay, gave him a written reference that was accurate rather than generous. He looked at her with the expression of someone who had expected the daughter to be softer than the father. She kept her face as she needed it.

After he left she sat for a moment. She did not second-guess the decision. She had made it with the same method she made all decisions: look at the numbers, consider the people, determine what is necessary.

Her father had taught her this. He had taught her by example over twenty years, by letting her sit in meetings when she was young enough that they probably didn't notice her in the corner, by making her work the routes herself rather than managing from Frankfurt, by letting her make mistakes and not rescuing her from them. He had not been a sentimental man. He had been a fair one, which she understood now was harder.

She thought about Charles.

She thought about him briefly, not at length. She wondered if he knew yet. The letter she had sent him after the garrison city visit in March had been businesslike in tone, and the mail to wherever his unit was in the current phase of the campaign was slow and unreliable. He might not know for months.

She considered writing him a longer letter. She picked up the pen and put it down.

She had work to do. She would write when she had time to write it well.

* * *

His letter arrived in June.

It was short. He had heard about her father from her previous letter, the brief one, and he had written: I am sorry for your loss. I know what Henri Vieillard built and what it meant to you. I would come if I could. I cannot. I am sorry for that too.

He had not dressed the apology or padded it or justified it. She appreciated this. She had known, since the garrison city, that he was not going to come. She had not needed the justification. She had needed the honesty, and the honesty was there.

She wrote back after a week. She told him about the business, the eastern route, the three merchants and the one who had tested her terms. She told him that Bertrand was gone and that the Portuguese leg was holding and that she had her father's desk now and was still adjusting to the sight of it.

At the end she wrote: I am sure you did what you thought necessary.

She looked at this sentence after she wrote it. She was not entirely sure which thing she was referring to: his absence, or the campaigns, or something larger. She sent the letter anyway. She thought he would understand it the way she meant it, which was: I am not angry. I am tired. I am still here.

She sealed it and gave it to Hoffmann to add to the outgoing correspondence.

She picked up the Strasbourg file and went back to work.

CHAPTER 14

He had been filling out discharge paperwork for three months when the news came from Elba.

Napoleon had returned. The paperwork went into a drawer. He went to find Rousseau.

He had not wanted to go back. He was aware of this as a new fact about himself, the way one becomes aware of new facts about oneself by noticing a response that was not present before. At eighteen he had ridden toward the Tagliamento with the specific eagerness of someone who does not yet know what is ahead. At twenty-one he had ridden toward Eylau with clear eyes and the specific preparation of someone who knows the cost and has accepted it. At twenty-eight he rode toward the Belgian border with the exhaustion of someone who knows exactly what the work costs and is going anyway because there is no other choice that makes sense in the structure of his life as it currently exists. Not because he wants to. Not because he believes in the campaign. Because stopping, at this point, requires an act of will he cannot locate.

He thought about this distinction on the road north. It seemed important. He had never been a man who fought for glory or advancement or the abstract satisfaction of service. He had fought because stopping had always cost more than continuing. The logic was the same now. The feeling underneath it was different.

The army assembled with the efficiency that Napoleon always extracted from it, the machinery of the Grande Armée cranking back into motion after eight months of dormancy with a speed that was almost offensive given what the last three years had been. Charles moved through the preparation the way he moved through all preparations: the horses, the men, the equipment, the orders. He knew what to do at every stage because he had done it many times and his hands knew the work before his mind engaged. This efficiency was either a sign of competence or a sign that the work had become so automatic that it no longer required him to be entirely present, and he was not sure which.

Rousseau reported to him at the staging area looking exactly as Rousseau always looked: present, attending, the Marseille accent sharpened by three years of sergeant's work into something that landed between conversation and instruction depending on who he was talking to. He had the quality Charles had been developing in him since the convoy escort in 1810: the quiet confidence of a man who has been tested enough to know what he can do.

"Well," Rousseau said.

"Well," Charles said.

That was the conversation. They had been through enough together that most conversations were not necessary.

He checked the paper in his boot before they moved out. Bordeaux. The fold was worn nearly through again. He would need to replace it before the next engagement. He found a piece of paper and copied the address in the evening, folded it to the same size. Elise's handwriting was not his. He looked at his own copy and then put it in his boot and told himself it was the address that mattered, not the hand.

* * *

He had been in large actions. The Tagliamento in the dark. Eylau in the snow, the scale of it. The years in Spain, the particular grinding accumulation of smaller actions that together produced a weight comparable to a single large one.

Waterloo was larger than all of them.

He understood this before the battle from the numbers: the army assembled, the dispositions, the scale of what Wellington had across the ridge. Numbers he could read the way a merchant reads accounts: they described what was coming. He had been in enough actions to understand the gap between numbers and experience, but even allowing for that gap, the numbers for the eighteenth of June described something he had not been in before.

It rained the night before. The kind of June rain that should not have been cold but was, that got under everything and turned the fields to the particular consistency that cavalry horses find maximally objectionable. He spent two hours with Tonnerre in the early darkness, the bay gelding pressing against him with the specific agitation of a horse that knows something is coming and does not know when. He talked him through it. Low voice, hand on the neck, the one-two pat until the agitation settled into something more like watchful attention.

"I know," he said to Tonnerre, several times. "I know."

He thought about Margot. He put it away. He thought about Elise and his father and the vineyard and the smell of the Gironde in the morning. He put that away too. He had learned over ten years that before a large action, sentimentality was not useful and deliberate attention to the work was. He went through the unit disposition in his head until he could see it clearly and then he slept for three hours on the ground with his coat over him.

* * *

The battle was noise and mud and the specific disorientation that comes from being inside something too large to see all of.

He was commanding a squadron, sixty men, positioned with the cavalry reserve. His role was to respond to developing situations, to be the mobile element that went where the action needed it. This meant he spent the morning watching through a telescope from his command position and reading the battle the way he had learned to read battles: not the grand picture, which was above his position to see and not his job to manage, but the local picture, the ground in front of him, the movement patterns that told him what the enemy was doing and what the gaps were.

The noise was the Eylau noise but sustained. He had experienced Eylau as a single overwhelming event, a contained thing that you moved through and came out the other side. Waterloo was that same noise for nine hours, and the difference between those two experiences was not primarily the noise but what the sustained duration did to the men, and to him.

By the third hour the cannon fire had become, in the specific way that sustained loud things become, more like a condition of the environment than an event. He noticed himself registering it as background rather than threat, which was the adaptation the body made under sustained fire, and which was not entirely safe. He kept himself in the active attention that kept the adaptation from becoming inattention, which required effort that was harder to locate by the third hour than it had been by the first.

He watched his squadron. He watched for the quality that was the first sign of a unit coming apart: the specific kind of looking-around that was not tactical observation but displacement, men looking for something to orient to when the orientation they had was not sufficient. He saw it developing in two men on the left edge around the fourth hour, and he repositioned them to the center and put them adjacent to Rousseau's section, where Rousseau's steady presence had the stabilizing effect on men that competent sergeants always had. This was exactly what Charles had promoted Rousseau to sergeant for. He had done it correctly. Rousseau was doing it correctly. The unit held.

He read the ground through the telescope, through the smoke, through the accumulated visual noise of nine hours of battle. He kept his men functional. He sent sections when sections were needed and pulled them back when the calculation said pull back. He made decisions. He had been making these decisions for ten years and his decisions were correct.

The cost of correct decisions was something he had stopped calculating by the Peninsular War. He made the decisions and he paid the cost later and this was the only available method.

Around the fifth hour a gap opened on the right flank of the position his squadron was covering. He could see it developing: an enemy section moving through broken ground toward a ridge that, if they reached it, would give them an angle on the flank that would compromise the whole defensive position. The gap needed to be closed before the enemy section reached the ridge. It needed a fast-moving section with a good sergeant who could execute without waiting for further orders once the objective was clear.

He called Rousseau.

He explained the objective through the telescope: the ridge, the angle of approach, the enemy section's current position and rate of movement. He said: take the ridge, hold the angle, you have six minutes.

Rousseau looked through the telescope, looked at the ground, looked at Charles.

"Yes, sir," he said.

He moved his section.

Charles watched through the telescope.

The section moved well. Rousseau had them in a good line, moving fast, using the broken ground for cover where it was available and committing to the open ground where it was not, which was the correct decision and Charles could see it was the correct decision as he watched it being executed.

They reached the ridge before the enemy section.

The maneuver succeeded.

He was watching through the telescope when Rousseau went down.

He saw it clearly enough to recognize him. He was at the ridge, his section had taken it, there was brief fire from the enemy section that had not quite reached them and then the fire that came from a different direction, the direction Charles had not fully accounted for when he gave the orders. Rousseau was there and then he was not, and Charles could see from the way he went down that this was not a wound.

The maneuver succeeded. The position held. The gap on the flank was closed. His plan worked.

He stood with the telescope in his hands and said nothing.

He gave the next order, because the next order was required, because the battle was continuing and his men needed the orders and nothing useful was served by stopping. He gave the order. He watched the section on the ridge complete the consolidation. He looked through the telescope at the ridge and confirmed the position was held.

He did not go to the ridge until the position was secure and the situation around his squadron had stabilized. This was correct procedure. Going earlier would have been a choice about how he felt rather than a choice about what was needed.

He rode to the ridge when he could. Rousseau was there with two others. His section had laid him out correctly. Someone had folded his coat under his head.

Charles crouched beside him. Rousseau's face had the quality that faces have when the person is no longer in them, which Charles had seen enough times to recognize and which he still found, every time, as irreducible. His journal was in his coat pocket.

He took the journal. He did not know why he took it, except that leaving it felt wrong. He put it in his own coat pocket and rode back to his command position.

* * *

Napoleon was defeated at Waterloo. The army collapsed over the following days in the specific way that armies collapse when the central energy that holds them is gone: not all at once but in a series of decisions that individually seemed rational and together produced a rout. Charles managed his squadron through the withdrawal in good order. He lost no additional men in the retreat that he had not lost in the battle.

That night, in a farmhouse somewhere south of the field, he opened Rousseau's journal.

The entries were in Rousseau's hand: compact, practical, the observations of a man who had spent five years in the Peninsular War developing an eye for terrain and for what men did under pressure. He read through them in reverse, starting from the most recent. The penultimate entry was from the night before the battle: rain, cold, a note about the condition of the horses, a note about a man in the section who had been quiet that day, something to watch.

The last entry was from the morning of the battle. It was short.

The Captain says we flank left if the opportunity presents. He usually sees the opportunity before I do. I trust him completely. If he thinks it is right, it must be right.

He read this twice.

He sat with it for a long time in the farmhouse, with the sound of the retreat moving on the road outside, and did not look away from it.

Rousseau had trusted him completely. That was true. Rousseau was dead. That was true. His plan had been correct. That was also true. He had given the order with care and had identified the right section and the right sergeant and the right objective, and the maneuver had succeeded and Rousseau was dead and these two things were both true simultaneously and could not be resolved into a single thing.

He thought about the list. He thought about Pellerin and Archambault and Cassouin, killed in the charge he had also managed correctly. He thought about Mercier, killed on a routine patrol he had not commanded. He thought about Fontaine, killed on a plan he had argued against.

He had made a correct decision. Rousseau was dead because of a correct decision.

He thought: there is no right way. Just different costs.

He opened the journal to the list. He found the space at the bottom of the right margin, where the names were written smaller because the columns were full, and he added Rousseau's name. Then he looked at the list: three pages now, dense in the margins, the names filling every available space on all three pages.

He closed the journal. He put Rousseau's journal on the table beside his own and looked at both of them.

He did not sleep.

In the morning he mounted Tonnerre and rode toward whatever came next.

He was the thing that organized the other things, the node that kept the network functional. He was too useful in the room to be useful in the smoke.

The violence had become entirely abstract.

He moved pins on a map. Reports reached him: we lost thirty men. The thirty men were in a report before they were thirty letters he had to write, and in the old days he had written the letters as close to simultaneously with the deaths as the situation allowed, because the letters were the thing that connected the death to the person. Now the letters were written by the company officers and reviewed by the adjutant and he signed them if they came to his level, which they usually did not. The form had spread. He had been part of making the form and the form had replaced him.

He smelled ink now. Lamp oil. Stone walls in the evening with the muffled quality that headquarters buildings have. He did not smell powder. He had not smelled powder since Waterloo, except at a distance, on operations he had planned but not been present for.

He noticed his own hands sometimes. He had the calluses from reins and from sword handling, the specific hardening along certain fingers. The calluses were not going anywhere. The work that had made them was somewhere else, in the field, happening to other people who were reading his orders.

He thought about Mercier's inspection sequence. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. He still ran through it when he was near the horses, which was every morning. He would run through it for the rest of his life, for whatever horses he was near, regardless of whether the horses needed it. He understood this about himself.

He thought: at least when I was in the smoke, the violence was honest. You could see the thing you were doing. You could feel it. Here the pins moved and the reports came in and the connection between the decision and the consequence was long and indirect and mediated by paper and he was never quite sure, looking at a report, whether he was understanding what had actually happened or only what the report said had happened.

He thought: I miss it.

He looked at this thought and found it uncomfortable. He missed the smoke. He missed being inside something he could read directly. He missed the clarity of the bad choice and the worse choice, which was at least honest about being a choice. The fog was clean. His hands were clean. The consequences were not.

He did not say this to anyone.

* * *

She came to the garrison unannounced, which was something she had never done before, and which told him something before she said anything, because Margot was a woman who announced herself.

He was in the planning room with Sergeant Duval when she appeared in the doorway. He was genuinely glad to see her. He crossed the room to her and she let him take her hand but her face had the look it had worn in the garrison city in 1814, the look of someone who had been working up to something for several months.

He sent Duval out. He found her a chair. He sat across from her.

"There was an operation," she said. "Last November. In the eastern region."

He said nothing.

"My father's old partner was there. His family." She looked at her hands. "He described it to me. The unit that came through. How they worked." A pause. "He said the officer was careful. Said the officer took less than some units and left them the draft animals."

"Yes," Charles said.

"Was that you?"

"My orders, yes. Not my direct command. I was in headquarters."

She looked at him. "From headquarters."

"Yes."

"You moved the pins."

He did not answer this. She had understood it correctly and there was nothing to add.

She leaned forward. She was not angry exactly. She was something beyond anger, something that had been compressed over years into a specific and very patient pressure.

"I build connections," she said. "I spend years creating trust, relationships, routes that work. I spend months rebuilding what the last campaign destroyed and then the next campaign comes through and I start over." She looked at him steadily. "You destroy it in a day. From headquarters now. You don't even have to be there."

"What else can I do?" he said. He meant this as an honest question, not a defense. He had been asking it for three years.

"Refuse," she said.

"And be court-martialed. And someone replaces me who does it with less care."

"So you are better than nothing. That is the standard."

He looked at the map on the table. Pins. Colored markers. A perfectly organized representation of something that was happening to people.

"You know I am not a bad man," he said.

"I know," she said, and her voice broke on the last word, not from weakness but from the specific fracture of something that has been held against pressure for a long time. "God help me, I know that. I know exactly who you are. I have known for years. That is not the problem." She stood up. "You are the best version of this. And the best version of this is still this."

He stood too. He was aware that they were at the point of the conversation that could not be undone. Things said here would remain said.

"I still love you," she said. "I do not know what to do with that."

"I love you too," he said. "I have for a long time."

"Yes." She picked up her satchel. "I know. I am not sure it matters anymore. I am not sure love is sufficient when everything else is."

She walked to the door. She was not leaving in anger. She was leaving because she had said the thing and staying now served no purpose.

"Margot," he said.

She turned.

He wanted to say: I will come home. He wanted to say: after this, after one more, after the occupation settles. He had been saying this in various forms since 1812. He knew what it sounded like.

He said: "I know you are right."

She looked at him for a moment.

"I know you know," she said. She went out.

He stood in the planning room with the map and the pins and listened to her footsteps in the corridor and did not follow.

* * *

He found Beaumont an hour later, in the room that Beaumont used in the evenings, which was a small room off the main corridor with a table and a lamp and a bottle he never finished in a single evening but that was always somewhat lower by the morning. Beaumont was standing at the window with a glass, looking at the courtyard.

Charles said nothing. Beaumont did not turn from the window.

"She leave?" Beaumont said.

"Yes."

Beaumont drank. Set the glass on the table.

"Good work today," he said. "Very efficient. Minimal casualties for the objective achieved." He said it in the tone of the official commendation, the form of praise the army used for operations that went well. Then, in the same tone: "That is what they will say about you one day. Efficient. Effective. Gets results."

He was quiet for a moment.

"And you will stand here, where I am standing, and wonder when you stopped feeling it."

Charles stood in the doorway and looked at Beaumont and looked at the glass on the table and the bottle beside it and the set of Beaumont's shoulders, which were the shoulders of a man who had made his accommodation with what he had become and was not sure the accommodation had been worth making and was past the point of changing it.

He said nothing, because there was nothing to say that was adequate and Beaumont had not asked him to respond. He stood there a moment longer and then went back to his quarters and sat on the edge of the bed with his hands on his knees and stayed there for a long time in the dark.

CHAPTER 16

The Frankfurt office had a view of the street that she had always found useful for thinking. Not the view itself, which was a cobbled street with a bakery on the far corner and a chandler's shop that had been there since before she could remember, but the movement: people going somewhere, goods being moved, the ordinary commerce of a city continuing on a Tuesday morning in the way it continued on all mornings.

She was twenty-four. She had been running the business alone for a year.

The business was not failing. This was a fact she had to remind herself of on the bad days, when the bad days made it feel like it was. The eastern route was running. The Portuguese leg was functional. She had four employees now, down from six, and all four were doing the work of six because she had made careful choices about who stayed. The accounts were in order. The business was not failing.

It was not what her father had built. The war had eaten too much of it: routes that had not been reestablished, partners who had found other arrangements in the years of disruption, a reputation in some corridors that had taken thirty years of her father's consistent presence to build and that she was rebuilding from a different starting point. She was good at this. The rebuilding was going well. She was also twenty-four, which meant she was doing the work of rebuilding something that had taken thirty years to build in the first place, with the full awareness that this was the work of decades and she was near the beginning.

She did not find this discouraging. She found it a problem to be addressed over time.

She addressed problems over time.

* * *

The Frankfurt merchant was named Kessler. He had a wool route that ran parallel to a route she was establishing for dry goods, and he had decided, three months earlier, to begin undercutting her on the prices along that corridor. Not dramatically: five percent, then eight, the kind of undercutting that eroded a position over time rather than attacking it directly.

She had watched this for two months before she moved.

She went to see him. She brought the accounts: what the corridor had been producing, what it was producing now, and an analysis of where Kessler's pricing advantage was coming from, which she had spent a week working out and which had a flaw in it that she had identified and which Kessler had not yet identified.

She laid this out on his desk with the matter-of-fact tone she had learned from watching her father lay things on desks. She was not angry. She was accurate.

She said: the flaw in your pricing model is the return leg. You are routing through Heidelberg on the return and absorbing the road costs, which work at the volume you have now. They will not work at the volume you need to sustain this price point at scale. Here is the calculation.

Kessler looked at the calculation. He was a careful man, not a dishonest one, and he saw the calculation was correct.

He proposed a partnership. She was not surprised. It was what the calculation was designed to make him propose.

They negotiated for two hours. She conceded two points he cared about and held three she cared about, and the agreement they reached was better for her than the situation she had walked in with, which was the objective.

She walked back to the office in the morning air and thought about what she had not conceded. She was good at knowing what to concede and what not to concede. Her father had taught her this. She was also good at knowing that being good at this negotiation did not mean she was not tired, and she was tired.

She sat down at the desk. She looked at the street.

She thought about Charles.

She had been thinking about Charles with less regularity since the garrison meeting, which was either progress or avoidance and she was not sure which. She knew what she had said. She had meant all of it. She still loved him, which was a fact that she had decided to stop being surprised by, because being surprised by it every time was inefficient.

She did not want a life of watching him do what he did and calling it love. She had said this. She had not unsaid it.

She also did not want a life that did not have him in it, which was a different statement. The two statements were both true and she had been living with their being simultaneously true for several months.

She picked up the pen.

She started a letter three times.

The first version was too long and said too much. She crossed it out.

The second version was too short and was not honest. She crossed it out.

The third version she stared at for a long time before she started writing it, because she understood what writing it meant and what it did not mean and she wanted to be precise about what she was actually saying.

She was not issuing an ultimatum. She was not asking him to choose between her and the army, which would be unfair and which she did not have the right to ask. She was stating a fact: she was building something real, with her life, and she was not willing to be the person who waited indefinitely at the edge of someone else's indefinite situation.

She was also not ending it. She was not capable of ending it, and honesty required acknowledging this.

She wrote:

I will not tell you what to do. I have never told you what to do and I do not intend to start. You are a grown man who has been making decisions under conditions I cannot fully comprehend, and I respect that.

But I am building something here. Something that is mine. Something I believe in. And I cannot build it indefinitely alongside the knowledge that what you do undoes what I do, and that you are not coming, and that the next season will be the same as this one.

I still love you. God help me, I still love you, and I say that with full awareness of what it means and what it does not mean. It does not mean I will wait forever. I cannot. I have a life to build, with or without you.

You know where to find me. You have always known.

If you want to be part of what I am building, come. Come when you can actually come, not when the season allows it and not when the paperwork clears. Come when you have decided to be somewhere other than where you are.

I will be here until I am not.

She sealed it before she could read it again.

She gave it to Hoffmann to add to the outgoing post.

She went back to the desk and opened the Kessler file and began drafting the formal agreement.

Outside, a cart moved along the street with a specific efficient sound of goods being taken somewhere they were needed. She did not look up.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 17

The plan was brilliant and he knew it was brilliant, which was a different experience than it had been at twenty-two.

At twenty-two he had been pleased with good plans in the way that young men are pleased with things they have made: the satisfaction of craft, the hope of recognition. Now he sat with a plan that he knew was good and felt something closer to weariness. It worked. He could see how it worked. He had reviewed it three times and the geometry was clean and the timing was achievable and the risk was distributed correctly across the units involved.

He was thirty years old.

The operation was against irregular forces in a region that had been a persistent problem for eighteen months. The irregulars were effective because they knew the terrain and used it well and had support among the local population that was hard to locate and harder to remove. They had survived three previous operations against them by the simple method of becoming less visible when visibility was dangerous and returning when the army moved on. Standard approach, standard result.

His plan addressed this differently. Three elements, timed to arrive at the same moment from three different directions, closing the routes by which the irregulars had previously escaped. The timing was the difficult part: the three elements needed to coordinate across terrain that did not permit visual communication, using signal fires and pre-arranged schedules. He had planned the timing in detail, accounted for realistic travel speeds over the terrain types involved, built in margin where the margin was available, and accepted the risk where it was not.

He presented it to his brigade commander, who asked three questions. He answered all three. The commander approved the plan.

He went back to his quarters and looked at the map for a long time. The pins. The lines of approach. The clean geometry of a thing designed to work.

He thought: this is what I have become good at.

He thought: I am very good at this.

* * *

He did not go into the field. This had been true for two years. The operation was commanded by three captains he had trained and trusted, executing his plan, and he was in the command position with a telescope and the same view of the battle he had always had from command positions: the high ground, the good sight-line, the distance from the thing itself.

He watched through the telescope as the three elements moved.

They moved well. The timing held. He could see each element from his position, which was elevated enough to give him the overview that the field commanders could not have. He watched the irregulars respond to the first element, which drew them toward the western corridor, which was what the plan needed. He watched the second element close the eastern route, which was the route the irregulars would take when they understood what the first element was doing. He watched the third element move into the blocking position, which was the crux of the plan, the element that closed the trap.

The trap closed.

Minimal casualties on his side. The operation accomplished its objective: the irregular force was contained and broken.

He had not smelled powder in two years. He smelled it now, faintly, at this distance, carried on the wind from the ground where the action was. He noticed this. He stood on the elevated position with the telescope and smelled the powder and listened to the reports coming in from the field and felt, for a moment, something that was not quite nostalgia and not quite relief and was possibly just recognition. He had been inside that smoke. He knew what was happening on the ground in a way that was different from reading reports, because he had been inside it.

The reports came in. Numbers. Acceptable losses. A good outcome by any military measure.

Then he saw the names.

A young lieutenant, dead. He had been at the garrison for eight months. His name was Villard. He had the quality that Charles recognized from twenty-two, the earnest preparation, the careful briefings, the specific hope of someone who has studied the right things and believes the right things and has not yet been in a situation where both prove insufficient. He had been the first officer off his academy class to volunteer for this operation. He had reviewed the plan three times, come to Charles with two questions that showed he understood it, and gone into the field with the bearing of someone who intended to come back.

A sergeant named Corot, dead. Four years in Charles's unit. He wrote home every week; Charles knew this because he had seen the letters in the outgoing post more times than he could count, always two pages, always sealed with the same red wax. A family man, someone had told him. Three children.

Three troopers. Charles had trained two of them personally, in the year after Waterloo, when he had still been doing direct training with enlisted men before the scope of his command had grown to make this impossible.

He looked at the names in the report.

His plan had killed them. The plan was correct. The plan was a good plan. He had given the orders that put these men in the positions where they died, and the positions had been necessary for the plan to work, and the plan had worked.

He put the report on the table. He went outside and stood in the evening air.

He thought: I know exactly what I am. I have known for some time. I am someone who can calculate how many men need to die to achieve an objective. I am someone who makes those calculations correctly. I am someone who is promoted for being good at this.

He had a letter to write. He wrote it that evening alone, by lamp. Not to a family. Not to a superior. To no one.

To Margot.

He wrote:

You asked what I am. I know now.

I am someone who can calculate how many men need to die to achieve an objective. I am someone who makes those calculations correctly. I make them from a room, with a map, with pins and lines. I send young men into positions I have identified as necessary. They trust me. They die because my calculation said their position was necessary. The calculation was correct.

What am I without war? I do not know. I have been at war since I was eighteen. What am I with war? I am not sure I can live with the answer.

You said come when I have decided to be somewhere other than where I am. I am trying to locate the decision. I am trying to find it. I do not know if I still exist outside of this, or if I left that person somewhere in Spain and did not notice.

I know where to find you. You have always known that I know.

He folded the letter. He did not seal it. He put it in the journal next to the list.

He opened the journal to the list. He looked at it: three pages, dense in the margins, the names filling every space he had found for them over twelve years.

He did not add the names from today's report. He sat with this for a long time. Then he added them, because they were his, because they had died because of his plan, because the list was the only accounting he had.

He closed the journal.

He lay down on his bed with his coat on and did not sleep for a long time, and when he finally slept it was without dreams, which was either a mercy or a sign that his mind had run out of the material it needed to produce them.

CHAPTER 18

The volleys cracked across the field in the way that military volleys do: not the chaotic sound of combat but the ordered crack of an intended salute, three shots together, the sound absorbed by the open ground and the gray November sky. The gun-smoke drifted.

He smelled it.

He had always smelled it. He had been inside it. He had been at the edge of it. He had watched it from elevated positions through a telescope and smelled it carried on the wind. It was the one constant: wherever the violence was, the smoke came eventually, at one distance or another.

Lieutenant Villard's burial was full military honors. Charles had insisted on full honors, which required a degree of insistence because Villard was a lieutenant and the full honors were typically reserved for more senior officers, but Charles had outranked everyone in the insistence and the honors had been arranged.

The parents had traveled far. The mother was a small woman who had her son's eyes and who looked, in the way that bereaved parents look at a grave, as though she was trying to understand how she had arrived at this specific moment, this specific field, this marker in the cold ground. The father stood beside her with his hat in his hand.

Charles spoke to them after the ceremony, when the other officers had paid their respects and moved toward the warmth of the garrison. He had spoken these words many times. He knew the form. He did not use the form.

"He was thorough," Charles said. "He came to me three times before the operation to make sure he understood the plan. He understood it. He had good questions."

The mother looked at him. "He wrote about you," she said. "Said you were a good commander."

Charles looked at the marker.

"He was a good officer," he said. "I am sorry."

The father shook his hand with the grip of a man who was holding on to something because the alternative was not holding on. Charles held the grip until the father released it.

They went to find their transport. He stood among the markers.

* * *

The regimental cemetery was not large. Two rows of markers, most with names and ranks and dates that were close together, the dates of men who had died in the same engagements rather than across many years. He had not counted them before.

He counted now.

He walked the rows slowly, reading each marker. Some he recognized: men who had died in actions he had commanded, men from his unit over twelve years, men whose names were on the list in the back of his journal. Some he did not recognize: they had died before he arrived, in other commands, in the campaigns that preceded his posting.

He stood at the end of the second row.

Some had died from his mistakes. He had made mistakes, early in Spain, and men had died from them, and he had learned from the mistakes and the learning had cost the men who died from them. Some had died from his correct decisions: the charge in 1811, the Waterloo flank, the operation last week. There was no category that was clean.

He thought: there is no right way. Just different costs.

He thought about Mercier's inspection sequence. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind, hooves, spine. He ran through it from memory, standing in the cemetery, his hands slightly apart at his sides at the positions the sequence required. He had done this sequence on Tonnerre ten thousand times. He had done it on every cavalry horse he had worked with for twelve years. There was no horse here. He did it anyway. He would do it for the rest of his life, for whatever horses he happened to be near, and in the evenings when he was not near horses he would run through it anyway, the way certain things become part of the body rather than the mind.

He thought about Rousseau. About the last journal entry: The Captain says we flank left. I trust him completely. He thought about Pellerin sharpening a knife with the automatic motions of a man not thinking about the knife. About Archambault asleep against the tree. About Cassouin's small book with the page blocked.

He thought about Margot in the warehouse in 1812, tracing columns with her finger while the addition happened beneath her attention. He thought about her in the garrison doorway in 1814 with the specific expression of someone who had been working up to a conversation for months. He thought about her letter: come when you have decided to be somewhere other than where you are.

He did not know when he had decided. He was not sure he had made a decision. He was standing in a cemetery at thirty years old and Villard was twenty-two and the two facts were in some relationship to each other that he could feel without being able to articulate it.

He thought: I have been here for twelve years.

He thought: I do not know what else I am.

He thought: I need to find out.

The light was failing. The gun-smoke had nearly cleared from where the volleys had been fired, drifting south on the light wind, thinning until it was more an impression than a presence. He watched it go. Almost clear. Not yet.

An aide was crossing the cemetery toward him. He could see the man's breath in the cold air before he was close enough to make out his face. He was carrying something.

He handed Charles the letter without speaking, because the ceremony had been recent enough that speaking felt wrong, and saluted, and went back toward the garrison.

Charles looked at the letter.

He knew the handwriting before he turned it over. He had received enough letters in this hand over twelve years that he could have identified it in worse light than this, with worse eyes than these. Margot's handwriting. Slightly compressed, the product of writing quickly with a great deal to say. The seal was pressed firmly, as though she had waited until she was certain before she sealed it.

He held the letter.

He did not open it.

He stood between the markers and the failing light, with the gun-smoke nearly cleared, and looked at the letter in his hand. He was afraid of what it said. He was afraid it was ending. He was afraid it was a last chance and he would not know which until he read it, and the act of reading it would determine which it was.

The list was in his coat. Three pages, dense in the margins. The journal in the other pocket. The paper in his boot was the replacement paper, his own hand rather than Elise's. He had carried it into the operation last week from habit, though he had not been in the field. He carried it anyway because not carrying it would have required a decision and the habit was older than his ability to make that decision.

He stood between what he had built from destruction and a future he did not know how to create.

The light was going. It would be full dark in twenty minutes. He should go inside.

He stood there.

He stood between the graves and the sunset, between the past he had built from destruction and a future he did not know how to create, holding a letter he was too afraid to read.

The gun-smoke cleared. The last of it.

He was still standing there.
