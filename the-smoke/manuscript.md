# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 1


The hired carriage lurched to a stop at the garrison gate, and Charles Leblanc felt his stomach lurch with it. Through the dusty window, he could see the guard post - a simple wooden structure with two sentries who barely glanced at the approaching vehicle. Beyond it, the garrison spread out across a broad expanse of trampled grass and worn stone.
This was it. This was real.
He’d been traveling for nine days, rattling across France in a succession of post-chaises that smelled of other people’s sweat and cheap tobacco. His uniform was rumpled despite his best efforts to keep it pressed between stops. His boots were dusty. He probably looked exactly like what he was: an eighteen-year-old from a Bordeaux vineyard who’d never been more than thirty kilometers from home before last week.
The driver opened the door. Charles climbed out, legs stiff from the long day’s ride, and stood blinking in the afternoon sun.
The parade ground stretched before him, a broad rectangle of grass worn bare in patches from constant use. Men were drilling there now - maybe forty of them, arranged in loose formations. Cavalry, he could tell from the way they moved, from the controlled chaos of it. They were practicing… something. Wheeling movements? He couldn’t quite tell from this distance. An NCO’s voice carried across the field, sharp commands Charles couldn’t make out over the general noise of the garrison.
They weren’t parade-ground perfect. This was training, real training, men learning or relearning movements that would keep them alive in combat. But there was a professionalism to it, a seriousness. These men knew what they were doing.
I’m going to do that, Charles thought. In a few days, maybe tomorrow, I’ll be out there with them.
His chest felt tight. Excitement or fear? Both, probably.
“Your trunk, monsieur?”
Charles turned. The driver was already unloading his belongings - one battered trunk that held everything he owned. His father’s parting words echoed in his head: “Five years, Charles. Make Captain and come home.”
“Yes, thank you.” Charles fumbled for coins, counting out the fare with fingers that felt clumsy. The driver took the money, touched his hat, and climbed back onto his perch without ceremony. The carriage pulled away, leaving Charles standing beside his trunk at the garrison gate with no clear idea of what to do next.
One of the sentries was watching him now. Charles straightened, tried to look like he belonged here.
“Second Lieutenant Leblanc,” he said. “Reporting for—”
“Adjutant’s inside, sir.” The sentry gestured vaguely toward the main building. “He’ll process your orders.”
“Right. Yes. Thank you.”
The sentry had already lost interest.
Charles looked at his trunk, then at the main building - a solid stone structure three stories high, windows reflecting the late afternoon sun. Other buildings clustered around it: long, low barracks, what looked like storehouses, and off to the left, a large stable complex that seemed to be the busiest part of the entire garrison. Men and horses moving in and out, the sounds of work carrying clearly across the parade ground.
He’d have to leave the trunk here for now. He couldn’t very well drag it with him to report.
The main building’s entrance was impressive - heavy wooden doors, worn stone steps. Charles climbed them, straightened his uniform jacket one more time, and stepped inside.
The interior was cooler, dim after the bright sunlight. A corridor stretched ahead of him, doors opening off to either side. Somewhere deeper in the building, he could hear voices, boots on wooden floors, the ordinary sounds of a military post at work.
An officer emerged from one of the side rooms - older, maybe thirty, wearing a lieutenant’s uniform that had seen better days. He had a sheaf of papers in one hand and the distracted look of someone dealing with too much administrative work.
“Excuse me,” Charles said. “I’m looking for the adjutant? I’m—”
“New posting?” The officer didn’t slow down. “Second door on the right. He’s expecting you.”
“Yes, thank you, I—”
But the officer was already gone, disappearing around a corner.
Charles found the second door on the right. It stood open, revealing a cramped office lined with wooden filing cabinets. A sergeant sat at a desk covered in paperwork, scratching away at a ledger with a quill that needed trimming.
Charles knocked on the doorframe.
The sergeant looked up. He was perhaps forty, graying at the temples, with the weathered face of a man who’d spent years outdoors before being assigned to administrative duty. His eyes swept over Charles in one quick, assessing glance - taking in the new uniform, the nervous posture, the dust from nine days of travel.
“Second Lieutenant Leblanc,” Charles said. “Reporting from—”
“Papers.”
Charles fumbled for the sealed orders he’d been carrying since leaving Paris. His hands felt clumsy as he extracted them from his jacket pocket. One of the seals had cracked during the journey, leaving a smudge of red wax on his fingers.
The sergeant broke the remaining seal without ceremony and scanned the contents. His face showed nothing - not approval, not disapproval, just the blank efficiency of a man who’d processed a hundred such orders before.
“You’re assigned to Captain Beaumont’s company,” the sergeant said. “Second squadron. Your quarters are in the junior officers’ wing, third floor, room twelve. Your roommates are on patrol, won’t be back until tomorrow evening.”
Charles nodded, trying to absorb all of this at once. Third floor. Room twelve. Captain Beaumont. Second squadron.
“You’ll need to draw your mount from the stables. Report there after you’ve settled in. The stable sergeant is Mercier - he’ll see you’re properly equipped.”
“Yes, thank you. Should I—”
“Morning formation is at six, on the parade ground. Don’t be late.” The sergeant was already turning back to his ledger. “Captain Durand will see you now to complete your processing. Down the corridor, last door on the left.”
“Captain Durand?”
“To process your orders,” the sergeant said without looking up. “Last door on the left.”
Charles stood there for a moment longer, waiting for… something. Further instruction? Welcome to the garrison? But the sergeant had returned to his work, quill scratching steadily across the page.
Right. Last door on the left.
Charles retreated into the corridor, turned left, and made his way deeper into the building. The sounds of the garrison felt muffled here, distant. His boots echoed on the wooden floor.
The last door on the left was closed. Charles knocked.
“Enter.”
The office beyond was larger than the adjutant’s, with a window overlooking the parade ground. A captain sat behind a desk - forty-something, broad-shouldered, with the look of a man who’d rather be anywhere but behind a desk. His uniform jacket hung open, his desk was covered with papers arranged in haphazard stacks, and he was frowning at something he’d just written as Charles entered.
“Second Lieutenant Leblanc, sir,” Charles said. “Reporting for—”
The captain held up one hand without looking at him.
Charles stopped mid-sentence and stood there, suddenly very aware of every small sound - the scratch of the captain’s quill, the distant voices from the parade ground, his own breathing.
The captain finished whatever he was writing, signed it with a flourish, and set it aside. Only then did he look up.
“Leblanc. From Bordeaux.”
“Yes, sir.”
“Cavalry experience?”
“I grew up with horses, sir. My family has a vineyard, and we—”
“That’s not what I asked.”
Charles felt heat creep up his neck. “No formal cavalry experience, sir. But I rode extensively at home, and I’ve studied the—”
“You’ve studied.” The captain’s tone made it clear what he thought of studying. “The stables here are magnificent, Lieutenant. The horses are in excellent condition, and the stable management is—”
“I didn’t mean to imply—” Charles started, then caught the look on the captain’s face.
The captain was staring at him. Not angry, exactly. Just… staring. Waiting.
Charles’s mind raced. What had he done wrong? He’d just been trying to—
Oh.
Report and shut up. That was the protocol. Report your presence, answer direct questions, don’t volunteer commentary about the garrison’s facilities like you’re inspecting the place.
“I apologize, sir,” Charles said.
The captain studied him for another long moment, then returned his attention to the papers on his desk. He shuffled through a stack, found what he was looking for, and made a notation.
“You’re assigned to Captain Beaumont’s company, second squadron. Your quarters are on the third floor - the adjutant gave you the room number?”
“Yes, sir. Room twelve.”
“Mess is at six bells. Formation at dawn. You’ll report to Sergeant Mercier at the stables to draw your mount - he’ll assess your riding and assign you appropriately.” The captain signed another paper, slid it across the desk. “Take this to the quartermaster for your kit issue. Building adjacent to the stables. Dismissed.”
Charles took the paper. “Thank you, sir.”
He turned toward the door, relieved to be leaving, but the captain’s voice stopped him.
“Leblanc.”
“Sir?”
“This is a working garrison. We train cavalry officers here, not vineyard inspectors. Keep that in mind.”
“Yes, sir.”
Charles left, closing the door behind him perhaps a bit more firmly than necessary.
The corridor felt longer on the way back. He passed the adjutant’s office - the sergeant was still scratching away at his ledger - and emerged into the main entrance hall.
Through the open doors, he could see the parade ground. The drilling had stopped. Men were leading horses back toward the stables, the day’s training complete. The sun was lower now, casting long shadows across the grass.
Vineyard inspector.
His face still burned.
He’d fumbled it. First conversation with a senior officer and he’d fumbled it like a green recruit. Tried to make conversation when he should have just reported and shut his mouth. The captain wasn’t even cavalry - probably infantry or artillery, stuck on administrative duty - and Charles had started babbling about stables like…
Like an idiot.
He took a breath, squared his shoulders. Fine. He’d learn. That’s what he was here for, wasn’t it? To learn how to be an officer.
His trunk was still sitting by the gate where the carriage had left it. Charles walked over, hefted it - not heavy, but awkward - and started looking for the junior officers’ quarters.
The third floor was reached by a narrow staircase that creaked under his boots. The corridor was dim, lit by windows at either end, and smelled faintly of woodsmoke and unwashed wool. Room twelve was halfway down, the door standing slightly ajar.
Charles pushed it open.
The room was small - perhaps three meters by four - with two narrow beds on opposite walls, a single shared wardrobe, and a small table beneath the window. One bed was clearly occupied: personal effects on the shelf above it, a jacket hanging from a peg, boots tucked underneath. The other bed was bare, waiting.
His bed, he supposed.
Charles set his trunk down and looked around. This would be home for… how long? Months? Years? Until he made Captain and could go back to Bordeaux with his head high and the family name restored.
Five years, his father had said. Maybe six.
Through the window, he could see the stable complex. Men were still moving around down there, tending to horses, putting away equipment. The busiest part of the garrison, even now as the day wound down.
He should go there next. Report to this Sergeant Mercier, draw his mount. But first…
Charles opened his trunk and began unpacking. His spare uniform went into the wardrobe - he’d have to share the space with his roommate, whoever that was. His personal effects - a few books, writing materials, his father’s old pocket watch - went onto the shelf above his bed. At the bottom of the trunk, carefully wrapped, was his dress uniform. He’d wear that for the first time at… when? Some formal occasion? He wasn’t sure.
He pulled out his writing desk - a small portable one, a gift from Élise before he’d left. Inside were paper, ink, quills, sealing wax. Everything he’d need to write home.
He should write. Let them know he’d arrived safely.
Charles sat on the edge of his bed, opened the writing desk across his knees, and stared at a blank sheet of paper.
Dear Father and Élise,
I have arrived safely at the garrison near Strasbourg. The journey was long but uneventful. The garrison is…
Was what? Impressive? Professional? He’d barely seen it. And what would he say about today? That he’d already embarrassed himself with a senior officer? That he didn’t know the proper way to report? That he’d fumbled the simplest interaction like a schoolboy?
Charles set down the quill.
He’d finish it later. After he had something worth reporting. After he’d drawn his mount, met his squadron, done something that didn’t make him look like a fool.
He closed the writing desk and stood, straightening his uniform. The stables. Sergeant Mercier. His assigned mount. That was next.
At least with horses, he knew what he was doing.
The stable complex was larger up close than it had seemed from a distance. The main building was stone and timber, solidly built, with a peaked roof and wide doors at either end to allow air circulation. Charles could hear horses inside - the stamp of hooves, the occasional whinny - and smell them too. That familiar scent of hay and horse and leather that reminded him of home.
The yard around the stables was packed earth, worn smooth by countless boots and hooves. Several soldiers were working there - some grooming horses, others cleaning tack, one repairing a saddle under the fading sunlight. They glanced at Charles as he approached but didn’t stop their work.
A corporal looked up from the bridle he was mending. “Help you, sir?”
“I’m looking for Sergeant Mercier. I was told to report here to draw my mount.”
“Inside, sir. Third stall on the right.”
Charles nodded his thanks and stepped through the open doors into the stable’s dim interior.
The building stretched back further than he’d expected - maybe thirty stalls on each side, most of them occupied. The horses were good quality. He could see that immediately. Not the heavy warhorses used by cuirassiers, but lighter cavalry mounts - built for speed and endurance rather than shock charges. Chasseurs’ horses. Most were bays or chestnuts, a few grays, all in good condition despite the work they must be doing daily.
He walked down the center aisle, boots quiet on the packed earth floor. Third stall on the right.
A man was there, examining a horse - running practiced hands down its forelegs, checking for heat or swelling. The horse stood patiently, clearly accustomed to the inspection.
The man was older - mid-forties, Charles guessed - with the weathered face and calloused hands of someone who’d spent decades working with horses. His uniform jacket was worn but clean, sergeant’s stripes visible on the sleeve. He moved with the efficient economy of long practice, checking the horse’s legs, then moving to examine its hooves with a hoof pick.
Charles watched him work. The sergeant had a specific pattern to his inspection - left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Then hooves in the same order. Then he ran his hand along the horse’s spine, checking for saddle pressure points. It was methodical, thorough, old-fashioned. The kind of inspection Charles’s father’s stableman used to do, before the war, before everything changed.
The sergeant finished his inspection and straightened, patting the horse’s neck.
“Sergeant Mercier?” Charles said.
The sergeant turned. His eyes swept over Charles in one quick glance - new uniform, nervous posture, eighteen years old and trying to look older. He’d seen a hundred young officers just like this.
“You’d be Leblanc.”
“Yes, Sergeant. I was told to report here to draw my mount.”
Mercier gestured to a stall further down the row. “Adjutant assigned you the bay gelding, fourth stall. Go have a look.”
Charles walked to the indicated stall. The horse inside was a bay - dark brown with black points - maybe fifteen hands high, well-proportioned. It watched him approach with calm, intelligent eyes.
Good confirmation, Charles thought automatically. Clean legs. Good chest depth. The horse shifted its weight, and Charles noted the movement - sound, no favoring. He opened the stall door and stepped inside.
The horse didn’t shy, just turned its head to investigate this newcomer.
“Easy,” Charles murmured. He ran his hand down the horse’s neck, feeling the muscle tone beneath the coat. Good condition. Well cared for.
He did what his father’s stableman had taught him years ago - checked the horse thoroughly. Hands down each leg, feeling for heat or swelling that might indicate old injuries. The legs were clean. He picked up each hoof in turn, checking for stones, examining the shoes, looking for any sign of thrush in the frogs. The shoes were in good condition, properly fitted. He checked the horse’s teeth - maybe six years old, good age for a cavalry mount - then ran his hands along the back, checking how the saddle would sit.
The horse stood patiently through all of this, occasionally turning to sniff at Charles’s jacket.
“You know horses, sir?”
Charles looked up. Mercier was standing in the stall doorway, arms crossed, watching.
“Yes, Sergeant. I grew up on a vineyard outside Bordeaux. Working horses for the fields and wagons.” Charles finished his inspection, patted the bay’s shoulder. “He’s in excellent condition.”
“They all are.” Mercier’s tone was matter-of-fact. “We work them hard but we don’t break them. A cavalryman’s only as good as his mount.”
Charles nodded, still running his hand along the horse’s neck. The bay leaned into the touch slightly - enjoying the attention or just a friendly temperament. Good either way.
He looked at Mercier more carefully now. Mid-forties. Twenty-plus years of service, probably. The way he’d inspected that other horse - that old-fashioned, methodical sequence…
“You check horses the old way, Sergeant,” Charles said. “My father’s stableman did it like that - served in the cavalry in the ’90s.”
Something shifted in Mercier’s expression. Not quite surprise, but… interest.
“Did he now.” Mercier stepped into the stall, examined Charles more carefully. “What unit?”
“I’m not sure. He didn’t talk about it much. But he always checked the horses exactly the way you just did - that specific pattern, foreleg to hindleg, then hooves, then spine.”
Mercier grunted. “Old cavalry school. They don’t teach it that way anymore - too slow for Napoleon’s army. Everything’s faster now.” He patted the bay’s shoulder. “But it’s thorough. Catches problems before they become injuries.”
Charles took a breath. This was either going to work or make him look like a fool. “Italy, Sergeant? I studied the ‘96 campaign. Rivoli, specifically. The accounts say cavalry covered seventy kilometers in two days over mountain roads.” He gestured to the bay. “How’d you manage that without breaking half your mounts?”
Mercier’s eyebrows rose slightly. He studied Charles for a long moment - reassessing.
“You’ve studied, at least,” he said finally. “More than most who show up here.” He stepped back, leaned against the stall door. “Rivoli, we walked them. Walked them whenever we could, saved the galloping for when it mattered. Fed them better than we fed ourselves - horse breaks down, you’re useless. Infantry can limp along on bad feet. Cavalry can’t.”
“And remounts?”
“Requisitioned when we could, stole when we had to.” Mercier’s tone was dry. “Not proud of it, but that’s war. You use what’s available.” He straightened. “Reading about it and doing it are different things, Lieutenant. But at least you’ve bothered to read. That’s something.”
It wasn’t warm. It wasn’t quite approval. But it was acknowledgment - more than Charles had gotten from anyone else since arriving.
“Thank you, Sergeant.”
Mercier nodded toward the bay. “That’s yours now. Name’s Tonnerre - Thunder. Previous owner was a lieutenant who transferred to dragoons last month. Horse is trained, responsive, won’t panic under fire - though you won’t know that until it happens. Treat him well and he’ll keep you alive.”
“I will.”
“Equipment’s in the tack room - I’ll have one of the corporals show you. Formation’s at dawn tomorrow on the parade ground. You’ll meet your squadron then. Captain Beaumont will assign you to a section.”
“Yes, Sergeant.”
Mercier turned to leave, then paused in the doorway. “Lieutenant. You know horses - that’s good. But cavalry’s not just about horses. Tomorrow you’ll learn the rest.”
“I understand, Sergeant.”
“We’ll see.”
Mercier left, his boots quiet on the stable floor.
Charles stood there in the stall for a moment, one hand still on Tonnerre’s neck. The horse turned to look at him with those calm, intelligent eyes.
“Well,” Charles said quietly. “At least one thing went right today.”
Tonnerre snorted softly, then returned to investigating the hay net in the corner.
The mess hall was on the ground floor of the main building - a long room with wooden tables arranged in rows and a serving area at one end where two cooks were ladling stew into tin bowls. The room was noisy, full of officers from various companies eating dinner and talking over each other.
Charles stood in the doorway, uncertain. Where should he sit? With the other lieutenants? But he didn’t know any of them. Would it be presumptuous to just… join a conversation?
Most of the tables were already occupied. At the far end, a group of captains sat together, talking in low voices. Closer, several lieutenants were laughing about something, one of them gesturing animatedly with his spoon. Another table held what looked like artillery officers - their uniforms slightly different, gold trim instead of silver.
No one was looking at Charles. No one had even noticed him arrive.
He got his dinner - the stew smelled better than it looked - and found a seat at the end of one of the less crowded tables. Two other lieutenants sat at the far end, deep in conversation about tomorrow’s patrol routes. Charles focused on his food and tried not to feel conspicuous.
The stew was decent - beef and vegetables, thick with barley. Better than some of the meals he’d had on the road. He ate slowly, listening to the conversations around him without quite joining them.
“—told him the requisition was approved weeks ago—”
“—should have seen Rousseau try to jump that fence, horse refused and he went right over its head—”
“—three days of stable duty for showing up drunk—”
Military life. This was military life - not the glory and campaigns he’d imagined, but the daily routine of garrison duty. Paperwork and drilling and enforcing regulations and occasionally jumping fences badly.
He’d known it would be like this, intellectually. But somehow, sitting here in this noisy mess hall eating mediocre stew while conversations flowed around him, it felt different from what he’d imagined during those long talks with his father about restoring the family name.
A lieutenant at the next table stood, raising his tin cup. The conversations died down.
“Gentlemen,” he said, his voice carrying across the room. “A toast.”
Everyone reached for their cups - water, wine, whatever they were drinking. Charles grabbed his own cup hastily, not wanting to be the only one still seated.
The lieutenant grinned. “A cavalry officer is born to fight, ride fast, drink hard, and die young!”
“Die young!” the room chorused, cups raised.
Charles joined in, his voice lost in the general noise. They drank, and the room erupted in laughter and conversation again.
Die young.
He’d read that toast in books about cavalry, heard stories about it from his father’s stableman. But hearing it here, said with that casual good humor by men who might actually face death in combat…
It made it real.
Charles drained his cup and stood. No one paid attention as he left the mess hall, returning his bowl and cup to the serving area on the way out.
The corridor outside was quieter, cooler. Through a window, he could see the parade ground in twilight, shadows stretching across the grass.
Tomorrow. Formation at dawn. Meet his squadron. Start learning how to actually be a cavalry officer instead of a vineyard owner’s son who happened to know horses.
He climbed the stairs to the third floor, made his way down the corridor to room twelve.
His uniform hung on its peg - the jacket freshly brushed, the facings bright, the lieutenant’s epaulettes catching the last light from the window. It looked… official. Real. Like something someone important would wear.
Tomorrow he would earn it.
Charles collapsed onto his bed without undressing, still wearing his boots. He was too tired to care. Nine days of travel, a day of fumbling and small victories and overwhelming newness, and tomorrow it would all begin again.
Through the window, he could hear the sounds of the garrison settling for the night - boots in the corridor, distant voices, someone singing off-key in one of the barracks. The watch called the hour. Eight bells.
Charles closed his eyes.
Tomorrow he would drill with his squadron. Tomorrow he would prove himself. Tomorrow everything would begin.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​





CHAPTER 2
The forward post in Baden was nothing like the garrison at Strasbourg. No stone buildings, no proper stables—just a commandeered inn, some hastily erected tents, and a paddock for the horses. They'd been here a week, part of the cavalry screen pushing east ahead of the Grande Armée's advance into Bavaria. The Austrians were retreating, trying to concentrate their scattered forces before Napoleon could destroy them piecemeal, and the French cavalry's job was to find them, fix them, and report their movements.
This morning, Charles's section had finally been given a mission that mattered.
He stood in what had once been the inn's common room, now serving as headquarters. Major Beaumont—the same officer who'd processed his orders back in the spring—stood over a map spread across a rough wooden table. Two other officers Charles recognized from the garrison flanked him. The room smelled of woodsmoke and damp wool.
"Leblanc." Beaumont gestured him forward without preamble. "Austrian detachment is moving north along this road—" he tapped the map "—trying to link up with their main force. Intelligence says they'll attempt to cross the Danube here, at Günzburg. Small bridge, single span. You're going to stop them."
Charles stepped closer, studying the map. The road ran through relatively open country before narrowing at the bridge crossing. Woods on the near side, clear ground beyond.
"How many, sir?"
"Twenty to thirty cavalry. Light horse, probably dragoons. They're not expecting opposition—they think our main body is still concentrated near Ulm." Beaumont looked at him. "You'll have fifteen men. Get there before them, hold the bridge. Drive them off if possible, delay them if not. Questions?"
Charles studied the terrain. The road approached the bridge through a bottleneck between two low hills. If he positioned his men in the woods on the near side, he could hit the Austrians as they tried to cross. A solid plan. But if he moved faster, pushed harder, he might catch them before they even reached the bridge—hit them in column on the approach road, scatter them completely. He kept the thought to himself.
"No, sir."
"Sergeant Mercier will accompany you. He knows the country." Beaumont rolled up the map. "You leave within the hour. Dismissed."

The sky was overcast when Charles reached the paddock, threatening rain. Men were already there, moving through their preparations with the easy efficiency of soldiers who knew their work. Charles recognized most of them after six months of training together.
A grizzled sergeant stood near the paddock fence, watching the preparations with the impassive expression of a man who'd seen everything twice. Sergeant Mercier—the NCO who'd mentored Charles after that embarrassing first day at Strasbourg, teaching him the practical lessons that didn't come from books.
"Morning, Sergeant. The men ready?"
"Near enough, sir. Duval's doing final checks on the loads now."
Charles spotted Sergeant Duval across the paddock—steady, reliable, the kind of NCO who quietly kept everything functioning. He was checking saddle straps and saddlebags with methodical precision.
Charles found Tonnerre already prepared. The bay gelding looked alert, ears forward, ready to work. Six months together had built a real partnership.
"Morning, Lieutenant."
Charles turned. Lieutenant Philippe Arnaud stood beside his own mount, adjusting his equipment with quick, practiced movements. Philippe—the one who'd helped Charles navigate the social minefield of officer life, who somehow made everything look easy. They'd become friends over the past months.
"Nervous?" Philippe asked with that easy grin.
"Should I be?"
"Probably. But you'll do fine." Philippe tightened a strap on his saddle. "Mercier's with you—that's good. Man knows his business."
A young corporal led his horse past, couldn't be more than seventeen despite whatever age he'd claimed when enlisting. "Morning, sirs," he said, voice eager.
"Morning, Corporal Rousseau," Charles replied. The kid volunteered for everything, desperate to prove himself. Reminded Charles of himself six months ago, actually.
"First real mission, sir?" Rousseau asked.
"For both of us, Corporal."
Rousseau grinned. "We'll show those Austrians, sir."
"That's the spirit," Philippe said. "Now go check your equipment again. And your horse's hooves."
"Yes, sir." Rousseau hurried off.
Philippe lowered his voice. "Good kid. Green as spring grass, but eager."
"Aren't we all," Charles muttered.

They rode out as the sun broke through the morning clouds, fifteen men in a loose column following Mercier onto the road leading south. Charles rode near the front with Philippe beside him. Behind them, Corporal Rousseau sat his saddle with exaggerated straightness. Sergeant Duval rode further back, his experienced eyes constantly scanning the column.
The road led through farmland initially, past fields being harvested and small stone farmhouses. The country grew more wooded after a few kilometers, the road narrowing.
Mercier set a good pace—steady but not rushed. Charles found himself relaxing into Tonnerre's gait as the nervousness of departure faded.
But they needed to move faster. If they arrived with time to spare, they could set up properly. Or better yet, catch the Austrians before they even reached the bridge.
Charles urged Tonnerre forward until he rode alongside Mercier. "Sergeant, we should pick up the pace."
Mercier glanced at him. "Any particular reason, sir?"
"Make sure we're in position with time to spare. Maybe catch them before they reach the bridge."
"The horses can handle a bit more," Mercier acknowledged. "Not too much, though. It's a fair distance yet."
"Let's move to a steady trot."
Mercier passed the word, and the column picked up speed.

They'd been riding hard for over an hour when Mercier dropped back to ride alongside Charles. "Sir, might want to ease back a bit. We've got a ways to go yet."
Charles looked back at the column. The horses were working, but not struggling. "They look fine to me, Sergeant."
"They do now, sir." Mercier's tone was neutral. "Just thinking ahead. A tired horse at the start of a fight can mean the difference."
Charles felt a flicker of irritation. They were making good time. The horses were cavalry mounts—this was what they were bred for. "We'll hold this pace. The bridge can't be much farther."
"As you say, sir." Something in Mercier's tone made Charles glance at him, but the sergeant's face was expressionless as he dropped back to his position.

When they finally caught sight of the river through the trees, Charles felt a surge of satisfaction. They'd made excellent time. Tonnerre was breathing harder than at the start, but still responsive.
Mercier, riding point, raised his hand. The column slowed to a walk.
"Bridge is visible through those trees, sir." Mercier pointed. "Maybe three hundred meters. No sign of the Austrians yet."
Charles's heart beat faster. They'd made it. They were here first.
"Dismount," Charles ordered. "Walk the horses, check your equipment. We'll move up to position in a few minutes."
The men dismounted with evident relief. Several immediately began walking their horses in slow circles. Charles saw Sergeant Duval checking legs and hooves with his usual thoroughness.
Charles walked Tonnerre, thinking. The bridge was just ahead, through those trees. And beyond the bridge, the approach road where the Austrians would be coming. If his section crossed now, they could catch the enemy in column before they were alert to the danger.
He looked at Mercier, who was also walking his horse. The sergeant met his eyes, seemed to read his thoughts, but said nothing.
Charles made his decision. "Mount up. We're crossing the bridge."
Several of the men looked surprised, but they obeyed. Philippe rode up beside Charles. "We're not setting up on this side?"
"Better position on the far ridge. We can see them coming, hit them before they're ready."
Philippe nodded slowly, though his expression was uncertain.
They emerged from the treeline at a trot. The bridge lay ahead—old stone, Roman construction, single arch spanning the river. Charles's section crossed in pairs, hoofbeats hollow on stone.
On the far side, the road climbed a gentle rise. "We'll take position on that ridge," Charles decided. "We'll see them coming, catch them in the open."
They climbed. Tonnerre was breathing hard now, but still moving well. Charles positioned his men in a loose line just below the crest, where they wouldn't be silhouetted but could still see the approach.
"Dismount. Check your weapons."
The men dismounted and began their preparations. Charles saw Duval running his hands down his horse's legs, checking for problems. Mercier stood slightly apart, his back to Charles, but something in his posture suggested disapproval.
Charles walked Tonnerre in slow circles. The bay's breathing was heavier than he liked, but they were in position now. When the Austrians appeared, his men would be ready.
"Movement, sir."
Mercier was standing at the edge of their position, hand shading his eyes. "On the road. Maybe a kilometer out."
Charles joined him, straining to see. Yes—there. A dark mass resolving into individual riders. Twenty, maybe twenty-five horsemen in column. Austrian dragoons, blue coats visible even at this distance.
His heartbeat quickened. This was real.
"Mount up. Form line."
The men moved quickly, pulling themselves into saddles. The line formed—fifteen riders spread across the road's width. Not perfect, but good enough.
The Austrians were closer now. Four hundred meters. Three hundred and fifty. They hadn't changed formation, hadn't seen the French cavalry waiting.
Charles's mouth was dry. These were real soldiers down there. Real enemies.
Three hundred meters.
Two hundred and fifty.
Charles could see individual faces now—young men, old men, someone with a thick mustache. Real people. The column was still moving forward, oblivious.
Two hundred meters.
"Sir?" That was Duval, voice carefully neutral. "Your order?"
Charles swallowed. "Forward at the walk."
The line moved. Fifteen horses stepped forward, cresting the ridge, starting down the slope.
The Austrians saw them. Heads turned, sudden confusion, horses pulling up short. Someone shouted an order in German.
"Draw sabers! Trot!"
Charles's own blade rasped free as the line picked up speed. Down the line, fourteen more sabers cleared leather with that distinctive ring. One hundred and fifty meters. One hundred and twenty.
The Austrians were scrambling, trying to deploy. Some fumbled with weapons.
One hundred meters.
"Charge!"
Tonnerre surged into a gallop. The Austrian line rushed toward him—blue coats and pale faces and the glint of steel.
A carbine cracked. Something whistled past Charles's head with a high, thin whine.
Then they hit.
The impact jolted through his whole body. Tonnerre slammed into an Austrian horse, both animals staggering. Charles's saber came up and across—pure training—and connected with something that gave way with a wet sound that made his stomach turn. A man screamed.
The smoke came.
Gun-smoke from carbines and pistols, rolling over them thick and acrid. Charles couldn't see more than a horse-length through the choking fog. His eyes watered. His throat burned. He couldn't tell friend from enemy except by coat color when they appeared through the murk.
Sounds layered: men shouting in French and German, horses screaming, steel on steel, hoofbeats and curses and that wet impact of blades finding flesh.
Tonnerre moved beneath him, turning, responding to leg pressure Charles didn't remember giving. The horse was operating on instinct now, old battle reflexes from his previous rider.
Something hit Charles's shoulder—hard. Not a blade, but a glancing blow. He nearly lost his seat, caught himself by gripping with his knees.
His saber was moving—slash and recover, slash and recover. He felt resistance, heard screaming, kept moving because stopping meant dying.
The smoke thinned. Charles could see the road now, French dragoons scattered in individual combats. There—Corporal Rousseau, young face set with fierce concentration, trading blows with an older Austrian. There—Philippe, still mounted somehow, pressing an attack with that casual competence he brought to everything.
And there—an Austrian trooper on the ground, not moving, blood dark on trampled grass.
Charles's stomach clenched, but he forced his eyes away.
"Sir! Behind you!"
Charles turned. An Austrian dragoon was charging from his flank, saber raised. Charles brought his blade up—too slow—but Tonnerre sidled and the Austrian's swing missed by inches.
Charles cut back wildly. His saber caught the Austrian's arm and the man howled, wheeling away clutching his wound.
Charles sat there gasping, heart hammering. His right arm ached from gripping the saber. Tonnerre stood beneath him, sides heaving, head down.
Around him, the fighting was breaking up. The Austrians were withdrawing, spurring their horses back down the road. The French weren't pursuing—couldn't pursue. Their horses were spent.
Charles looked around, counting. Most of his men were still mounted. Corporal Rousseau had blood on his sleeve but was upright. Sergeant Duval seemed unhurt. Philippe was—where was Philippe?
There. Dismounted, holding his horse's reins while the animal stood on three legs. Philippe looked up, caught Charles's eye, shrugged.
"Casualties," Mercier called out, voice hoarse. "Call off."
The men responded one by one. One broken arm—a trooper named Ferrand thrown when his horse stumbled. Two men with cuts, neither serious. Three horses lame, including Philippe's.
And one man who didn't respond.
Charles dismounted on shaking legs. Several men had gathered around something—someone—on the ground. A trooper lying face-down. His shako was gone. Blood dark on the back of his coat.
"Who is it?" Charles asked, voice strange in his own ears.
"Private Moreau, sir." Sergeant Duval's tone was flat. "Took a cut to the back. Bled out before we could reach him."
Private André Moreau. Twenty-two. Married. Had a baby he'd never met. Charles had seen his letter just last week—the private showing it around proudly, a son named Jean-Claude.
The smell hit him—blood and voided bowels and churned earth. Charles's stomach lurched but he swallowed hard, forced it down.
"Sir." Mercier's hand on his shoulder. "We need to secure the area. The Austrians might regroup."
Charles nodded numbly. "Right. Pickets on the ridge. Duval, see to the wounded. Philippe—"
"My horse is lame but I'm fine." Philippe was walking over. "I can help with the others."
"Do that." Charles looked at Moreau's body, throat tight.
"I'll take care of him, sir," Mercier said quietly. "Check on the rest of the men."
Charles forced himself to walk the line, speaking to each trooper, checking wounds, trying to project confidence he didn't feel. The men seemed steady. Shaken, but functional.
When he reached Philippe: "Your horse?"
"Strained tendon. He'll need to be led back." Philippe looked down the road where the Austrians had fled. "They're not coming back."
"We drove them off," Charles said.
"We did." Philippe's tone was neutral. "Scattered them pretty well."
Charles walked back to where Tonnerre stood, head hanging low. He ran his hand down the horse's neck, feeling lather, feeling exhaustion.
If he'd rested them properly at the treeline. If he'd listened to Mercier's concerns. Would they have caught more of the Austrians? Would the enemy have escaped at all?
"Sir."
Mercier stood nearby. "We need to move. Get back across the bridge. The horses need water and rest."
"Yes." Charles straightened. "Form up the men."
"One more thing, sir." Mercier's voice was very quiet. "We'll need to bring Private Moreau. And his personal effects for his family."
Charles felt something cold settle in his chest. "I'll see to it."
He knelt beside Moreau's body, forcing himself to look. The private's pockets held a few coins, a worn letter, a wooden cross on a cord.
The letter was addressed to Moreau. Charles knew he shouldn't read it, but he needed to know.
My dearest André,
The baby came three days ago. A boy, healthy and strong. He has your eyes—everyone says so. I've named him Jean-Claude, after your father. He cries at night but settles when I hold him...
Charles carefully folded the letter and put it in his own pocket with the other effects. A son who would never know his father. A wife waiting for news that would break her heart.
He stood and walked away before his face could betray him.

They made it back across the bridge by late afternoon. The horses were in bad shape—several limping, all exhausted. The section moved slowly, stopping frequently.
Mercier organized everything with quiet efficiency. Lookouts stayed on the ridge during the withdrawal. Moreau's body was wrapped and secured. The wounded rode the steadiest animals.
When they reached the treeline on the near side—the position they should have held—Mercier called a halt.
"We'll rest here until the horses recover enough. Duval, set watches. The rest of you, see to your mounts."
Charles dismounted. Tonnerre stood with his head nearly to his knees. Charles loosened the girth, pulled the saddle off carefully, checked the horse's legs.
The left foreleg was warm. Not terrible, but swollen. Strained, probably. Charles had pushed too hard.
"Lieutenant."
Charles looked up. Major Beaumont stood a few paces away, must have ridden out when they didn't return on schedule.
"Sir." Charles straightened.
"Walk with me."
Charles followed Beaumont into the trees, out of earshot.
When they were alone, Beaumont stopped. "Report."
Charles did. The ride to the bridge. The decision to cross and take the far ridge. The engagement. One dead, three wounded, several horses lame. The Austrians scattered.
Beaumont was silent for a long moment. "You pushed your horses hard getting there."
"Yes, sir."
"Sergeant Mercier mentioned concerns about the pace. You continued anyway."
"I wanted to be in position to catch them in the open, sir."
"And you did. You drove them off." Beaumont's voice was measured, not quite approving but not condemning either. "But they escaped. Scattered, yes, but they'll regroup. You had tired horses that couldn't pursue, couldn't finish what you started."
Charles felt his face heat. "Yes, sir."
"You accomplished the mission, Lieutenant. The bridge is held. The Austrians are scattered and delayed. That counts for something." Beaumont paused. "But a more patient officer—one who'd rested his horses properly, who'd set up on the near side and let the enemy come to him—might have destroyed that detachment completely. Captured them, killed them, prevented any regrouping. As it stands, they'll be back in action within days."
Charles's throat was tight. "Yes, sir."
"Private Moreau is dead. Men die in combat—that's the nature of war, and you can't prevent every casualty. But with fresher horses, with a better position, maybe you'd have ended the fight faster. Maybe Private Moreau would still be writing letters to his wife about that new son of his. Or maybe not—we can't know. What we do know is that you had a chance to eliminate an enemy unit completely, and you gave up that advantage because you were impatient."
"I understand, sir."
"Do you?" Beaumont's expression was stern but not unkind. "You're not a bad officer, Leblanc. You think tactically, you're not afraid to act, your men followed you without hesitation. But tactics without logistics fails. Aggression without preparation costs opportunities. You pushed hard to get into position for a decisive strike, but gave up the ability to deliver it." He paused. "Your sergeants tried to tell you. Did you listen?"
"No, sir. Not enough."
"Then listen next time." Beaumont's tone softened slightly. "You'll write to Moreau's widow. You'll tell her he died serving France, that his sacrifice mattered. And you'll learn from this—that the unglamorous work of horse care and proper pacing isn't separate from winning battles. It's how you win battles." He turned to leave, then stopped. "Get your section home safely. And Lieutenant? This was your first real command. You'll make mistakes. The question is whether you learn from them."
He left Charles standing there alone.

They reached the forward post after dark. The exhausted section filed through in silence, Moreau's wrapped body drawing quiet looks.
Charles saw to the horses—or tried to, but Mercier and Duval had it handled. The wounded went to the surgeon. Moreau's body to the makeshift mortuary.
When everything was done, Charles finally made his way to the tent he shared with Philippe.
Philippe was already there, sitting on his bedroll. "Hell of a first command."
Charles sat heavily. He felt hollowed out.
"We drove them off," Philippe said quietly. "Scattered them pretty well. Beaumont seemed... not happy, but not furious either."
"We should have destroyed them completely," Charles said. "If I'd listened to Mercier about the horses. If we'd set up on the near side like we should have."
"Maybe. Or maybe they'd have seen us and never committed. Or maybe the fight goes differently and more of us get hurt." Philippe lay back. "Combat's unpredictable. You can't know every outcome."
"But I can listen to experienced NCOs when they give me good advice."
"Yes," Philippe agreed. "You can do that."
Charles nodded. He pulled off his boots, his jacket. The uniform still smelled like smoke and blood.
He should eat. Should wash. Should write that letter.
But instead he just lay down and stared at the tent canvas until exhaustion took him.

The next morning, Charles went looking for Sergeant Mercier.
He found him in the paddock, checking horses. Tonnerre was in a corner, left foreleg wrapped with fresh poultice showing beneath the bandage.
"How is he, Sergeant?"
"He'll mend. Strained tendon, nothing torn. Needs rest and easy walking for a week or two."
Charles stepped closer to Tonnerre. The bay nuzzled his pocket. The familiar gesture made his chest tight.
"Sergeant, about yesterday. The charge."
Mercier looked at him. "Sir?"
"I drew my saber at... when I gave the trot command. Ordered the men to draw at the same time."
"You did." Mercier's tone was neutral, waiting.
"Was that... should I have waited?"
Mercier considered this. "In a small action like yesterday? Doesn't matter much. But in a larger battle, you don't want to signal your commitment until the last possible moment." He moved to check another horse as he spoke. "Keep the enemy guessing. Make them think you might wheel away, might be a feint. Then when you're close enough that they can't react—that's when you draw and charge."
Charles nodded slowly. "So I showed my hand too early."
"Like I said, sir, in yesterday's fight it didn't matter. But it's good practice for when it does." Mercier glanced at him. "You asking because you're thinking about what you could have done better?"
"Yes, Sergeant."
"That's good. Shows you're learning." Mercier returned to his work. "Most important thing from yesterday wasn't the saber drill, though."
"The horses."
"The horses," Mercier agreed. "Everything else stems from that. Fresh horses mean you can pursue, can finish the fight decisively. Tired horses mean the enemy escapes."
Charles felt the weight of it. "I pushed too hard on the approach."
"You did." Mercier's voice was matter-of-fact, not judgmental. "I mentioned it. You kept the pace anyway."
"You were right. I should have listened."
Mercier was quiet for a moment. Then he set down his tools and turned to face Charles directly. Charles noticed the shift immediately—sergeants didn't usually look at officers quite this directly, didn't speak quite this frankly.
"Sir, what I'm about to say, I'd never say in front of the men. That's not how it works. Chain of command matters, discipline matters. But here, now, just us?" Mercier paused. "You need to hear this."
Charles straightened slightly. "Go ahead, Sergeant."
"You're learning, and that's good. You think fast, you're not afraid to act, you care about the men. But yesterday you made a mistake that cost us a complete victory. The Austrians scattered, yes, but they'll regroup. If we'd had fresh horses, we could have run them down, captured or killed the lot. Instead they escaped because our mounts were blown."
Charles felt heat in his face but forced himself to listen.
"You asked about the Italian campaign before. About how we managed those long marches." Mercier's voice was firm but not unkind. "It's because we understood that cavalry without horses is just infantry with spurs. You take care of the basics—food, rest, maintenance—or you lose your ability to fight effectively."
"I understand that now."
"Do you?" Mercier studied him. "Because understanding it here, after the fact, is different from remembering it in the moment when you're eager to get into position, when you think pushing a bit harder will give you an advantage."
Charles swallowed. "You're right. I knew you were concerned about the pace, and I dismissed it. I thought I knew better."
"And now?"
"Now I know I don't." Charles met the sergeant's eyes. "Next time, I'll listen."
Mercier nodded slowly. Something in his expression shifted—still serious, but perhaps satisfied. "Good. That's what I needed to hear." He picked up his tools again, and just like that, the unusual directness was gone. His tone returned to the normal sergeant-to-officer formality. "Now you've got a letter to write, sir. Best not to put it off."
Charles understood the shift. The moment of frank correction was over. They were back to their proper roles. But the lesson had been delivered, and Charles had heard it.
"Thank you, Sergeant."
Mercier nodded without looking up from his work, already moving to the next stall.

Charles sat at the camp desk with paper and ink. For a long time he just stared at the blank page.
Finally he began:
Madame Moreau,
It is my painful duty to inform you that your husband, Private André Moreau, was killed in action on October 8th, 1805, during an engagement with Austrian forces near Günzburg...
The words came slowly. How did you tell a woman her husband was dead? How did you explain that her son would grow up without a father?
Charles wrote that André had fought with courage. That he had been respected by his comrades. That he had served France well, that the mission succeeded, that his sacrifice had meaning.
It was all true. But it felt incomplete. The truth was more complicated—that a more patient officer might have achieved more, might have brought everyone home. But you couldn't put that in a letter to a widow.
When he finished, he folded it carefully and sealed it with wax. He sat there holding it, looking at the address.
Madame Jeanne Moreau
 Rue Saint-Martin
 Lyon
A woman waiting for news of her husband. A baby who would never know his father.
This was war. This was what he'd signed up for. The path to glory and honor and making the Leblanc name mean something.
It didn't feel glorious. It felt like responsibility, heavy and cold.
Eventually he stood and walked to the adjutant's tent to send the letter. It was his duty now. That's what being an officer meant.
Outside, the autumn sun was shining. The forward post continued its routine—men drilling, horses being exercised, life going on.
Private André Moreau was dead.
And Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc had learned his first real lesson about command: that competence required more than tactical thinking. It required patience, preparation, and listening to those who knew better.
The difference between a good outcome and a great one often came down to those unglamorous details.
He was beginning to understand that now.


CHAPTER 3
The winter settled over Bavaria like a wool blanket—heavy, damp, and inescapable. Charles stood in the converted monastery’s courtyard, breath steaming in the November cold, and watched his section prepare for morning drill. The stones underfoot were slick with frost, the sky the color of old iron.
Winter quarters near Munich were better than he’d expected. The officers had been billeted in requisitioned burgher houses in town—Charles shared rooms with Philippe in a neat stone house belonging to a cloth merchant who’d been paid handsomely for the privilege and told to be grateful about it. The enlisted men occupied the old monastery buildings: stone walls thick enough to keep out the worst of the cold, high-ceilinged halls that had once echoed with prayers now filled with the sounds of soldiers living in close quarters.
The horses had the best of it. The monastery’s stables were solid, well-ventilated, warm enough. Charles had inspected them personally the first day, running his hands along the stall partitions, checking the drainage, testing the hay for mold. Sergeant Mercier had watched him do it without comment, then nodded once when Charles was finished.
“They’ll do well here, sir.”
That had been three weeks ago. Now, late November, routine had settled in with the cold. Reveille at half past five, when the sky was still dark and frost rimed the windows. Stable call, feeding and mucking while the horses stamped and blew warm breath in the frigid air. Breakfast—hard bread soaked in wine that tasted of vinegar, sometimes cheese if the supply wagons had come through. Then drill, when the weather permitted and the ground wasn’t too frozen or too muddy.
Charles watched his men mount up, checking each one automatically. Corporal Rousseau—still only seventeen despite claiming eighteen when he’d enlisted—sat his horse well enough but favored his right stirrup slightly. Trooper Ferrand, arm healed from the break at the bridge, had his carbine slung wrong. Sergeant Duval was already correcting it with the patient efficiency Charles had come to rely on.
“Section ready, sir,” Duval reported.
“Walk them out. We’ll drill formations on the south field.”
They moved out in a column of twos, hoofbeats muffled by the frost-hard ground. Other sections were already drilling—Charles could see them in the distance, dark shapes against the pale grass. One section in particular caught his eye. Their movements were crisp, their formations parade-ground perfect, horses wheeling in near-unison.
“Pretty,” Mercier said beside him, watching the same section.
Charles glanced at the sergeant. “Very skilled.”
“He is.” Mercier’s tone suggested a “but” that never came. They reached their own field, and Charles began drilling his men through basic formations. But his attention kept drifting to that other section, to their mechanical precision, to how impressive they looked.
After an hour, Charles called a break to rest the horses. The other section—led by a lieutenant Charles didn’t know—was still drilling hard. Their officer called for a demonstration charge, and the horses surged forward in beautiful formation. But when they pulled up, Charles noticed their sides heaving, their movements sluggish.
“Watch,” Mercier said quietly.
The lieutenant called for another maneuver. The horses responded but slowly now, their enthusiasm dampened. One stumbled slightly. The lieutenant’s own mount tossed its head, fighting the bit.
“He’s working them too hard,” Charles said.
“He is.” Mercier was quiet for a moment, still watching. “Had a lieutenant like that in my first regiment. Always wanted to be first. First in the charge, first to report victory. Could drill his men prettier than any parade ground I’ve seen.”
Charles waited. Mercier told stories the way other men gave orders—deliberately, with purpose.
“Got his men to the battle fast,” Mercier continued. “So fast half the horses were blown before the fighting started. We were at Rivoli, ’97. Cold like this, actually. Ground frozen. He pushed them hard getting into position because he wanted to be the first to engage.”
“What happened?”
“We won the battle. He died.” Mercier’s tone was matter-of-fact. “Shot from his horse because the animal was too tired to maneuver when he needed it. Couldn’t turn, couldn’t dodge. Just stood there heaving while the Austrian sharpshooter took his time with the aim.”
Charles felt something cold settle in his stomach that had nothing to do with the November frost.
“His men survived, mostly,” Mercier added. “Walked home leading exhausted horses. He got his glory, though. Got it carved on a stone marker back in France. I’m sure his family was very proud.”
Across the field, the other section was attempting yet another formation change, the horses moving with visible reluctance now.
“Any fool can make horses move fast, sir,” Mercier said. “Any fool can drill pretty formations. Good officer gets his men there ready to fight and ready to leave after. Glory goes to the quick.” He paused. “Survival goes to the patient.”
They returned to their own drill. Charles worked his section through maneuvers steadily, carefully, never pushing too hard. When they finished an hour later, his horses were worked but not exhausted. Across the field, that other section was only just stopping, their mounts lathered despite the cold.
The days that followed fell into rhythm. Morning drill when weather permitted, stable work in the afternoons, equipment maintenance in the evenings. Charles found himself spending more time with the horses than in the officers’ mess, preferring Tonnerre’s straightforward company to the social complexities he still couldn’t navigate.
“You’re going to turn into a horse if you’re not careful,” Philippe said one evening, finding Charles in the stables yet again.
“Horses are simpler.”
“They are. But you’re still an officer.” Philippe leaned against the stall door, watching Charles check Tonnerre’s legs for the second time that day. “Speaking of which, there’s a ball next week. The Stadtmann family—wealthy burghers, own half the warehouses in Munich. All junior officers are expected to attend.”
Charles’s hands stilled on Tonnerre’s foreleg. “Expected?”
“By Beaumont. Yes.” Philippe’s grin was sympathetic. “It’s not optional, I’m afraid. Maintaining relations with the locals, showing the army’s civilized, all that.”
“I don’t dance.”
“Neither do I, really, but I manage to fake it well enough. Come on. One evening. Smile occasionally, drink some wine, try not to discuss horse logistics with anyone, then you can come back to your beloved stables.”
The week passed too quickly. Charles spent the intervening days dreading the ball with an intensity usually reserved for combat. The morning it arrived, he discovered that the lieutenant who’d been drilling his section so impressively had led a patrol into the countryside the day before and returned with two horses lame and the rest blown.
“Pushed them too hard,” Philippe mentioned at breakfast. “Tried to make good time, ended up walking half the section back. Beaumont was livid about the lame horses.”
Charles said nothing, but he thought about Mercier’s story, about pretty formations and exhausted mounts. About getting to the battle too tired to fight. About his own mistake at the bridge.
That evening, he stood in front of the mirror in their quarters, adjusting his dress uniform while Philippe hummed and fixed his own collar.
“You look like you’re going to an execution,” Philippe observed.
“I feel like I am.”
“It’s just a ball, Charles. A few hours of being polite. You’ve survived worse.”
The Stadtmann house blazed with light, every window glowing in the December darkness. Sleighs and carriages crowded the courtyard, liveried servants everywhere, officers in their finest uniforms mixing with Munich’s merchant class.
The ballroom was everything Charles hated—chandeliers, flowers despite the season, musicians playing something complicated and Viennese, dozens of people engaged in the elaborate social dance he’d never learned the steps to.
Philippe acquired wine with practiced ease and pressed a glass into Charles’s hand. “Drink this. Look pleasant. Survive.”
The next hour blurred into introductions and conversations Charles navigated like a man walking through fog. Philippe guided him through it, smoothly including him in conversations, rescuing him when he said the wrong thing or stood silent too long.
Then a new voice interrupted: “Leblanc, is it? From Bordeaux?”
Charles turned. The officer was perhaps twenty-five, captain’s rank, with the kind of face that suggested he’d never had an awkward moment in his life. His uniform fit perfectly, his smile was practiced, and something in his eyes suggested he was assessing Charles the way one might assess an opponent across a card table.
“Yes, sir. Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc.”
“Captain Jules Fontaine. Just transferred from the Third Regiment.” The handshake was firm, the gaze direct. “Getting to know everyone. Bordeaux—wine country. Your family in the trade?”
“Vineyard owners, sir. Small estate.”
“How lovely. I’m from Lyon myself—merchant family, ironworks.” Fontaine’s smile never wavered. “I heard about your action at Günzburg. The bridge crossing. Well done, that.”
“Thank you, sir.”
“Though I understand you lost a man. Private… Moreau, was it?”
The name landed like a punch. Charles felt his face stiffen. “Yes, sir.”
“Unfortunate.” Fontaine’s tone was sympathetic, but his eyes were watching Charles’s reaction with clinical interest. “Still, casualties are inevitable in war. Can’t let them weigh on you too much. The key is learning from these early engagements—understanding what works, what doesn’t. I’m sure you took valuable lessons from the experience.”
“I did, sir.”
“Good, good. We all make mistakes when we’re young.” Fontaine’s smile was encouraging now, almost paternal. “The important thing is not repeating them. Though of course, with more experience, you’ll find these things become second nature. The decisions that seem difficult now will—”
“How did you hear about Günzburg, sir?” Charles asked.
Fontaine blinked. “Pardon?”
“You said you just transferred from the Third Regiment. Günzburg was a minor action, not widely reported. How did you hear about it?”
Something flickered in Fontaine’s expression before the smooth smile returned. “Oh, you know how these things go. Officers talk. News travels. I make it a point to learn about the men I’ll be serving with. Their successes and their… learning experiences.”
Philippe materialized at Charles’s elbow. “Fontaine! There you are. We should let you circulate, Captain. I’m sure you have many more people to meet.”
“Indeed I do.” Fontaine nodded to Charles. “Leblanc. I’m sure we’ll work together soon. Always good to have reliable officers in the regiment. Even if they’re still learning.”
He moved away into the crowd, already engaging another group with that same effortless charm.
“What was that about?” Charles asked quietly.
Philippe watched Fontaine disappear into the crowd. “That was you accidentally making an enemy. Fontaine’s well-connected—family has money and influence. He’s good at his job, but he’s also good at making sure everyone knows he’s good at his job. And he doesn’t like competition.”
“I’m not competing with him.”
“You exist. That’s competition enough for men like Fontaine.” Philippe steered them toward a quieter corner. “Just be careful around him. He’s the type who remembers.”
The evening dragged on through more conversations Charles barely tracked. He spoke to merchants about requisitions, to a burgher’s wife about nothing at all, to Lieutenant Marchand—a pleasant senior lieutenant with a pregnant wife who asked polite questions about Bordeaux that Charles answered too formally.
“Not enjoying yourself?”
Charles turned. Major Beaumont stood beside him, holding what was probably not his first drink of the evening. The major’s eyes were slightly glassy, his formal uniform jacket unbuttoned at the collar.
“It’s a lovely ball, sir.”
“It’s tedious,” Beaumont said flatly. “But necessary. Keep the locals happy, show the army’s civilized.” He took a drink, his gaze drifting across the ballroom. “You look like you’d rather be anywhere else.”
“I’m fine, sir.”
“You’re a terrible liar, Leblanc.” There was no heat in it. “You prefer the stables.”
Charles hesitated, then decided on honesty. “Yes, sir.”
“Smart. Horses are simpler.” Beaumont’s gaze was still on the crowd—the dancers, the musicians, the elaborate social performance. “You’re odd, Leblanc. Awkward. The other officers don’t know what to make of you.”
Charles said nothing.
“But you’re competent,” Beaumont continued. “You care about doing things properly. That matters more than being popular.”
Before Charles could respond, Beaumont had moved away, heading toward where servants circulated with more wine.
Philippe found him twenty minutes later and they escaped into the December night, walking back through streets frosted with ice. The music faded behind them, replaced by the sound of their boots on frozen ground.
“That wasn’t so terrible, was it?” Philippe asked.
“It was terrible.”
“But you survived.” Philippe’s breath steamed in the air. “And you made an impression. The awkward genius from Bordeaux who prefers horses to people. They’ll remember you.”
When they reached their quarters, Philippe headed inside immediately. Charles stood in the street for a moment, looking toward the monastery buildings, the stable complex beside them.
“I know where you’re going,” Philippe called from the doorway, but he was smiling.
Charles walked to the stables alone. Tonnerre nickered softly when he arrived, and Charles spent a long time just standing there, checking the horse over, breathing in the familiar scents. The ball continued in the distance—music and laughter and the elaborate social dance he’d never quite learn.
But his horses were sound, his men were healthy, and tomorrow there would be drill.
The December days grew shorter and colder. Charles settled deeper into the routine, finding a strange comfort in its predictability. Morning drill when the weather allowed, afternoons in the stables, evenings that increasingly found him there too rather than in the mess with the other officers.
He noticed things, though. Noticed how Beaumont drank alone most evenings, sitting at the far end of the officers’ table, present but separate. Noticed how when younger officers told war stories—exaggerated, boastful—Beaumont’s expression stayed carefully blank. Sometimes he left mid-story, walking out without a word.
And he noticed Fontaine. The new captain had settled into the regiment with easy confidence, immediately popular, always at the center of conversations. Occasionally Charles would catch him watching, that same assessing look from the ball, as if trying to determine what kind of threat this awkward lieutenant might pose.
Charles tried to avoid him. It mostly worked.
The stables became his refuge. One afternoon in early January, Corporal Rousseau found him there, checking Tonnerre’s hooves for the third time that week.
“Can I help, sir?”
Charles glanced up. “You should be at drill.”
“Finished early. Thought I’d…” Rousseau gestured vaguely. “Learn something useful?”
Charles studied him. The young corporal was eager, always had been, but there was something else now—a genuine desire to understand the work, not just to impress.
“Check the gray in the next stall. Look for stones, cracks in the frog, any heat in the hoof wall.”
Rousseau took the hoof pick Charles offered and moved to the next stall. He picked up the gray’s hoof carefully, examining it.
“What am I looking for exactly?”
Charles came over. “Stones first—wedged around the shoe or the frog. See there? That dark spot? Work it out gently.”
They worked through the section’s horses together, Rousseau asking questions, Charles answering with a patience that surprised him. By the time they finished, two more young troopers had appeared, drawn by curiosity or boredom.
“Sir, could you show us that map reading thing?” one asked. “How to calculate march times by terrain?”
Charles blinked. “When was I talking about that?”
“Last week, sir. In the mess. You were explaining to Lieutenant Arnaud about elevation changes affecting movement.”
He didn’t remember that specifically. But he found himself nodding anyway. “All right. Let me get a map.”
It became routine without Charles quite noticing. Three times a week, sometimes more, young soldiers would find him after drill ended. They’d ask questions about horses, maps, equipment maintenance—the practical details that kept cavalry functioning. Charles found he could explain these things. Not smoothly, not with Philippe’s charm, but clearly. Directly.
“You’re good at this,” Mercier observed one afternoon in late January, watching Charles demonstrate proper saddle fit to Rousseau and three others.
“At what?”
“Teaching. Men listen to you.”
“They listen because I know what I’m talking about.”
“Plenty of officers know what they’re talking about. Men don’t listen to them.” Mercier’s tone was matter-of-fact. “They listen to you because you explain things like a soldier, not an officer. Make it practical. Make it matter.”
Charles wasn’t sure that was a compliment, but Mercier seemed to mean it as one.
The teaching helped him as much as it helped the young soldiers, he realized. Gave him something to focus on besides the social complexities of officer life that still baffled him. In the stables, showing Rousseau how to spot early signs of lameness or demonstrating to a young trooper why saddle fit mattered, Charles felt competent. Useful.
It was more than he felt at balls or in the officers’ mess.
February came with heavier snow. The drilling moved mostly indoors—saber work in converted warehouses, equipment maintenance in the monastery’s drafty halls. Charles spent more evenings in the stables, ostensibly checking on the horses but really just seeking the quiet comfort of their presence.
Philippe found him there one evening, sitting on a hay bale, watching Tonnerre eat.
“You know the men are starting to call you the Horse Lieutenant.”
Charles looked up. “That’s not a real rank.”
“No, but it’s what they call you. Said with respect, mostly.” Philippe sat down beside him. “You’ve changed since Strasbourg. More confident with them. Less worried about impressing the officers.”
“I was never going to impress the officers.”
“No,” Philippe agreed cheerfully. “But you’ve stopped trying, and that’s different. You’ve found what you’re good at.”
“Horses.”
“Leading. Teaching. Taking care of practical things.” Philippe was quiet for a moment. “It’s not what either of us expected officer life to be like, is it? All the academy training, all those lessons about tactics and strategy. Nobody mentioned that half of it would be making sure men don’t freeze, horses don’t go lame, equipment gets properly maintained.”
“You’re good at the other half, though. The social part.”
“I fake it better than you, certainly.” Philippe’s grin was self-deprecating. “But I notice things too. Notice how Fontaine watches you sometimes. Notice how the younger soldiers ask you for advice more than they ask their own section leaders. Notice how Mercier treats you almost like a peer these days.”
Charles said nothing, but something warm settled in his chest.
“You’re becoming a good officer, Charles. In your own odd way.”
The compliment sat awkwardly. Charles deflected: “I just check horses properly.”
“You do more than that. But if it makes you more comfortable to think it’s just about horses, go ahead.”
Philippe left him there in the quiet stable. Charles sat for a while longer, thinking about teaching and patience and the strange path that had led him from fumbling at the bridge to this—young soldiers asking his advice, Mercier’s respect, Philippe’s friendship.
It wasn’t glory. Wasn’t the kind of success he’d imagined when he’d left Bordeaux. But it was something solid. Real.
The next evening, Trooper Ferrand got drunk in a tavern and punched a local. The magistrate brought him back at midnight, bloody-faced and belligerent, and Charles found himself standing in the frozen courtyard trying to figure out what to do with a soldier who’d just assaulted a civilian.
“Confined to quarters,” he said sharply. “Three days. And you’ll work stable duty every evening for two weeks.”
Ferrand stumbled away muttering. Charles stood there alone, wondering if he’d just made it worse. Too harsh? Too lenient? He had no idea.
“Sir.” Duval had appeared from wherever sergeants materialized from at midnight. “Might want to reconsider that punishment.”
“It’s done.”
“It’s harsh, sir. Ferrand’s a good soldier mostly. Made a mistake, yes. But three days’ quarters plus two weeks’ extra duty? For a tavern fight?” Duval’s tone was careful. “Men will think it’s excessive.”
“He assaulted a civilian.”
“He punched a drunk who insulted him. Happens every night in every tavern in Munich.” Duval paused. “Question is—does the punishment teach him something or just make him resent you?”
Charles felt something twist in his chest. “I don’t know.”
“No, sir. That’s what you’re here to learn.” Duval’s expression was sympathetic. “Too light, other men think rules don’t matter. Too harsh, you lose their loyalty. You won’t always get it right.”
“How do I find the line?”
“Carefully, sir.”
Charles lay awake that night, staring at the ceiling while Philippe snored. He thought about Ferrand, about punishments and fairness, about lines he couldn’t find. The next morning, he sought Ferrand out in the stables.
“About last night. Confined to quarters is reduced to one day. The stable duty stands—but I’ll be working alongside you on some of those evenings. Teaching you proper hoof care, since you clearly have time to learn.”
Ferrand’s expression was unreadable. “Thank you, sir.”
It wasn’t warm. But it wasn’t resentful either.
Word spread through the section quietly that day. Leblanc had adjusted the punishment, admitted his own mistake, worked out something fairer. That evening, Charles found himself teaching hoof care to Ferrand and two other troopers who’d asked to learn, and Mercier watched from across the stable with something that might have been approval.
“Men notice fairness,” the sergeant said later. “Matters more than strictness.”
Charles filed the lesson away with all the others accumulating through the long winter: Patience. Observation. Practical care. Fairness over authority. The unglamorous work that kept men alive and loyal.
Leadership, he was learning, was messier than he’d expected. More complicated. Full of decisions that didn’t have clear answers.
But he was learning.
In early March, Charles sat at his desk one evening while Philippe attended yet another social dinner. He’d been avoiding this for weeks—the letter home he needed to write. Élise’s last letter had arrived a month ago, enthusiastic and slightly naive, asking about battles and glory. She’d mentioned that their father reread his letters at night when he thought no one was watching.
That detail had stayed with him, heavy and uncomfortable.
He stared at blank paper, trying to find something true to say that wouldn’t worry them. Finally, he wrote about winter quarters. About training. About the horses being in good condition. When he reached Moreau, the quill hovered before he wrote: I lost one man in October. Private Moreau. Writing to his widow was harder than the battle.
He didn’t write about the baby who’d never know his father. About the widow’s angry letter. About how that death still sat on his conscience months later.
He wrote about Mercier teaching him. About Philippe being a good friend. About learning through careful attention to detail and practical matters.
Those were the true things he could give them.
At the bottom: I think of home often. How was the harvest? Tell me about the vineyard.
He wanted to know about ordinary things. About life that continued whether or not he was there to see it.
He sealed the letter and set it aside. Outside, voices carried through the evening—officers returning from dinner, slightly drunk, singing off-key. Normal sounds. Garrison life continuing its rhythm.
Charles pulled on his coat and walked down to the stables. Tonnerre nickered softly, and Charles spent twenty minutes just standing there in the quiet darkness, one hand on the bay’s warm neck.
Tomorrow there would be drill. More training. More lessons.
Tonight there was just this: his horse, the smell of hay and leather, something solid in a world that increasingly confused him.
The rumors started mid-March—cousins in other regiments, supply convoys moving south, officers recalled from leave. Italy, everyone said. Had to be. Why else position them now?
By the time Philippe came back from an officers’ dinner with confirmation, the news surprised no one. Orders were coming. The regiment would march south as soon as roads were passable.
“A few weeks,” Philippe said, his eyes bright with excitement. “Maybe a month. Real battles, Charles. Real chance to distinguish ourselves.”
Charles thought about the bridge. About Moreau. About everything he’d learned this winter about patience and preparation and the cost of pushing too hard.
“Real chance to die, too.”
“That’s always true. But we’re better now than we were in October.” Philippe’s enthusiasm was infectious despite everything. “You’re better. The men respect you. Trust you.”
“They respect that I check horses properly.”
“They respect that you care whether they live or die.” Philippe clapped his shoulder. “You’ll do well. We both will.”
The official orders arrived three days later. Beaumont read them at morning formation, his face expressionless, grip tight on the paper. The regiment would begin preparations immediately. March south in two weeks.
When formation dismissed and officers began talking excitedly about the coming campaign, Charles watched Beaumont walk away alone toward his quarters. That evening in the mess, the younger officers celebrated—wine flowing, toasts to glory and victory. Fontaine held court at one table, already planning how they’d distinguish themselves.
Beaumont sat at the far end, present but separate, drinking slowly and methodically. A young lieutenant newly arrived from the academy told an enthusiastic story about what he imagined combat would be like. Beaumont’s expression never changed, but Charles, watching, saw something flicker in the major’s eyes. Not contempt. Something closer to pity.
The young lieutenant finished his story. Someone else started another. The celebration continued.
Beaumont stood quietly and left. No one seemed to notice except Charles.
Walking back to quarters later with Philippe, Charles asked: “How many campaigns has Beaumont been through?”
“Five? Six? Rivoli in ’97, Egypt, Marengo, some others.” Philippe glanced at him. “Why?”
“No reason.”
But Charles thought about it as he lay in bed. Six campaigns. Nine years of war. How many men had Beaumont commanded? How many had died? How many letters to widows and parents?
No wonder he drank. No wonder he kept separate from eager young officers who still thought war was glorious.
Beaumont knew what it cost.
In two weeks, Charles would learn more of it too.
The last evening before preparations began in earnest, Charles stood in the stables checking Tonnerre one final time. The bay was in perfect condition—the best shape since Strasbourg nine months ago.
Nine months. It felt like years. Charles had aged somehow, though the mirror showed the same face. But he was different now. Not better necessarily. Not more confident or charming or any of the things officers were supposed to be.
Just more competent. More careful. More aware of weight and cost.
Tonnerre leaned into his hand, eyes half-closed.
“Ready for Italy?”
The horse had no answer. But his ears were forward, his legs sound, his condition excellent.
Charles hoped he was ready too.
Footsteps behind him—Mercier appeared, carrying a bridle needing repair. The sergeant nodded and set to work at the bench near Tonnerre’s stall, working leather in the dim light.
They didn’t speak. The winter had built understanding between them—not quite friendship, rank prevented that, but something close. Respect. Trust.
After a while, Rousseau appeared too, then two other young troopers. They found tasks—grooming, checking equipment, ordinary stable work. But Charles suspected they were there for the same reason he was: needing to be around the horses, around each other, on this last quiet evening before everything changed.
“Sir?” Rousseau said eventually. “What do you think Italy will be like?”
Charles thought about lying. About glory and victory and making names known.
“Harder than what we’ve done so far,” he said instead. “Bigger battles. More danger. Less room for mistakes.”
“But we’re ready.” Not quite a question.
Charles looked at the young corporal—barely eighteen, eager and earnest, so desperate to prove himself. He thought about all the winter’s lessons: patience, preparation, the unglamorous work that kept men alive.
“We’re more ready than we were at Günzburg. We’ll have to be enough.”
Rousseau nodded, satisfied with that honesty.
They worked in comfortable silence until late bells rang. The young soldiers drifted away one by one. Mercier finished the bridle, nodded to Charles, departed.
Charles stood alone in the quiet stable, hand on Tonnerre’s neck, breathing familiar scents of hay and horse and leather.
Tomorrow they would begin preparations. In two weeks they would march. And then they would see what he’d learned. What he’d become over this long winter. Whether patience and careful preparation were enough when the smoke came again.
He wasn’t sure. But he knew more now than in October. Had Mercier’s lessons, Philippe’s friendship, men who trusted him to care whether they lived or died.
It would have to be enough.
Charles gave Tonnerre a final pat, checked the hay net one more time, and walked out into the March night. Above, the stars were sharp and cold, the same stars that would look down on Italy, on whatever battles waited there.
He was ready. Not eager anymore. But ready.
That was all he could be.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​

CHAPTER 4
They crossed the Alps in April, which was not the worst time to cross them and was not a good time either.
The road through the passes was narrow enough in places that the column moved single file, horses picking their way along ledges that dropped away to nothing on one side. Tonnerre kept his head down and his feet careful, which was all Charles could ask. The wind came off the snowfields and cut through the wool of his uniform as though it was not there.
The column stretched ahead and behind further than Charles could see, infantry and artillery mixed with cavalry, supply wagons lurching over stones that had been worn smooth by centuries of feet that had nothing to do with war. He had read about this route. Hannibal. Caesar. Napoleon himself, the first Italian campaign a decade ago. The road did not care who used it.
Corporal Rousseau rode beside him for a stretch that afternoon, looking at the peaks with the expression of a man who had grown up in Marseille and had not previously considered that the world contained this much empty space above the treeline.
"I did not know they were this big," Rousseau said.
"The books do not convey it well."
"No, sir." A pause. "Do you think we will find much fighting when we get down?"
Charles looked at the valley somewhere below the cloud layer, invisible from where they rode. "Yes," he said. "I expect we will."
Rousseau considered that. "Good," he said, with the conviction of someone who had not yet found out what he meant by it.
Charles said nothing.

* * *

The plain south of the passes was a different world. Flat, warm, the sky large in a way that made you aware of your own smallness. And the army.
Charles had thought he understood what the Grande Armee was. He had read the dispatches. He had been part of it for eight months. He had not understood it until he rode out of the mountains and saw the camp spread across ten kilometers of Po Valley, a city of canvas and smoke and horses, two hundred thousand men organized into something that moved and fed itself and killed with a precision that no individual man inside it could fully comprehend.
Major Beaumont found him standing at the edge of a rise looking at it.
"First time seeing the full thing?" Beaumont said.
"Yes, sir."
"It does not get smaller." Beaumont looked at it with the expression Charles had come to recognize: not hollow yet, but the distance there, the long familiarity that had taken the wonder out of it. "Get used to it. We march southeast tomorrow. We will be attached to Marshal Massena's corps for the duration."
"Yes, sir. What is the objective?"
"The Austrians have a defensive line along the Tagliamento. We are going to break it." Beaumont turned his horse. "Make sure your section is rested. We will cover forty kilometers tomorrow and fight the day after."
He rode back toward headquarters.
Charles stayed on the rise for another minute, looking at the camp. Somewhere in that mass of men were the engineers who would build the bridges, the artillery that would soften the Austrian line, the infantry who would go in first and take the casualties that cavalry did not have to take. And somewhere was the order that would tell Major Beaumont where to put his squadron, and Major Beaumont would tell Second Lieutenant Leblanc where to put his section, and Second Lieutenant Leblanc would put his section there.
The machine needed all of its parts.

* * *

Lieutenant Philippe found him cleaning his saber that evening, which he had already cleaned twice.
"You are going to wear the blade down," Philippe said, settling onto the ground beside him.
"It occupies the hands."
Philippe pulled out his own blade and inspected it without beginning to clean it. A habit of his, that: going through the motions of preparation without the purpose. "Beaumont briefed the officers this afternoon. Did you get the same?"
"Tagliamento crossing. We screen the artillery approach, then exploit any gap in their line."
"That is the one." Philippe set the blade across his knees. "How many of your men have been in a real battle? Not a skirmish. A battle."
Charles thought about it. "Sergeant Mercier. Maybe three others."
"Same for me." Philippe looked at the blade. "The rest will find out tomorrow what the difference is."
"Is there a way to prepare them?"
"Mercier says no. Says the only preparation that counts is knowing your job well enough that your hands do it without your head being involved." Philippe was quiet for a moment. "He is probably right."
From somewhere in the camp, a man was singing. Something from Provence, slow and minor, carrying in the still air. Charles did not know the song but recognized the register: the one people used when they were not performing for anyone.
"Philippe."
"Yes."
"Tomorrow. If something happens to me."
"Nothing is going to happen to you."
"If it does. Elise is in Bordeaux. The estate is outside the city, southeast, near the Garonne. My father's name is Henri."
Philippe was quiet for a moment. "I know."
"I wrote it down. It is in my left boot."
"Charles."
"In case."
"I know where Bordeaux is," Philippe said. "I have had your family's address memorized for four months. You wrote it down and put it in your boot last October, the night before we left for the first patrol." He adjusted his collar. "You do this every time."
Charles looked at him. "I do?"
"Every time. Boot, left side, folded small." Philippe stood, sheathed his blade. "Get some sleep. I will see you in the morning."
Charles sat with the cleaned saber in his lap for a while after Philippe left, listening to the singing he could no longer hear.

* * *

The Tagliamento was wider than the Gunzburg crossing by a factor of five, brown with snowmelt, the current fast enough to be audible from a hundred meters out. The Austrian line ran along the far bank: earthworks, gun emplacements, infantry in formed ranks behind the works. Charles studied it through the morning haze and felt the difference from Gunzburg the way you felt the difference between a candle and a fire.
They had ridden through the night to be in position before dawn. The horses were tired but not blown. Charles had kept the pace to a walk for the last three hours, ignoring the staff officer who had ridden alongside him twice with implied suggestions about urgency. Sergeant Mercier had caught his eye the first time and said nothing. That was enough.
Major Beaumont assembled the squadron officers at first light behind a stand of poplar trees that gave them cover from Austrian observation.
"Artillery goes in at seven," Beaumont said. He had a map but did not look at it; he knew the ground. "Infantry assault at the crossing point here, here, and here. Our job is to be ready to move through any gap that opens and hit their second line before it can consolidate. We do not force a crossing. We wait for the infantry to make one."
Captain Fontaine, commanding the other section, was looking at the Austrian line with the expression of a man calculating odds. "What if the infantry does not make a gap?"
"Then we hold our position and protect the artillery from any Austrian cavalry sortie." Beaumont folded the map. "Questions?"
There were none.
"Then get your men ready."

* * *

The French artillery opened at seven, as promised.
Charles had been near cannon fire before, the occasional shot at the Gunzburg engagement, but he had not been near this. Forty guns in a line, firing in sequence, each report overlapping the last so that the sound became continuous, a physical thing that got into your chest and stayed there. Tonnerre shifted and fussed for the first five minutes and then seemed to accept it as the new nature of the world, which was more than Charles managed.
Across the river the Austrian works disappeared into dust and smoke. He could see nothing of what the shells were doing. The artillery observers would know; Charles knew only the sound and the smoke and the vibration in the ground under Tonnerre's feet.
Sergeant Mercier came up beside him. "First time under artillery?"
"Outgoing. Is there a difference?"
"Not to your ears. Your nerves know, though. Outgoing you are doing something. Incoming you are just waiting."
"Is this going to be worse when it is incoming?"
Sergeant Mercier considered it. "Yes," he said.

* * *

The infantry went in at half past eight.
Charles watched from his position in the poplars as three columns moved toward the river crossings, engineers pushing pontoon sections ahead of them, musketry erupting from the Austrian works where the bombardment had not reached. Men went down. The columns kept moving. He had read about this in books, the mathematics of assault: enough men, enough momentum, absorbed losses until the objective was taken. Watching it was not like reading it.
He found himself counting, which he made himself stop.
The second crossing took the works at half past nine. A gap in the Austrian line, the infantry through it and pushing into the second position, the Austrians pulling back to consolidate. A staff officer came riding hard along the line.
"Major Beaumont. Gap at the center crossing. Move now."
Beaumont turned to his officers. "Forward. Keep your dressing. Do not get ahead of the infantry screen."
Charles looked back at his section. Twelve men, all mounted, all watching him. Sergeant Duval steady at the back. Corporal Rousseau at the front, young face tight with something that was not quite fear and not quite excitement but lived between them.
"Forward," Charles said. "Column of twos. Sergeant Mercier, with me."
They moved out of the trees and into the open ground and the sound changed immediately: not just artillery now but the broken noise of a battle that had lost its shape, musketry from three directions, shouting in French and German and something that might have been Italian, smoke drifting in layers over the churned ground between the river and the Austrian second line.
The crossing was a pontoon bridge wide enough for two horses abreast. The planking shook under the weight of the section moving across it. Charles kept his eyes on the far bank and his hands quiet on the reins and let Tonnerre pick his own footing on the moving surface.
On the far bank: the smell of it. Powder and blood and the particular sour smell of ground that had been turned over by shellfire, something agricultural and wrong at the same time. Dead men in Austrian blue along the works. A horse down, trying to rise, not succeeding. Charles kept moving.

* * *

The gap in the Austrian second line was a quarter kilometer wide, a break between two infantry battalions that had pulled back at different speeds and had not yet closed. Through it Charles could see open ground and then a road and then the confusion of an army beginning to realize it was losing.
Major Beaumont pointed. "Through the gap. Hit the road junction. Disrupt whatever they are trying to form up."
"Yes, sir."
Charles looked at the gap, at the ground between here and there, at the Austrian infantry on both flanks of it, close enough to be a problem if they had time to organize. They did not yet have time.
"Section. Column of twos. We move fast through the gap, spread to line on the far side, hit the road junction. Sergeant Mercier, left flank when we clear. Sergeant Duval, right flank. Rousseau, stay with me." He looked at his men. "Do not stop for anything until we are through."
He did not wait for acknowledgment. He put Tonnerre at the gap and rode.
The flanks of the gap blurred past. Austrian infantry close on both sides, close enough to see their faces, too surprised and too disorganized to do more than a few scattered shots that went wide. The section poured through behind him, the sound of their hooves a different note on the harder ground on the far side.
"Line! Left and right!"
The section spread as trained, Sergeant Mercier peeling left, Sergeant Duval pulling right, the line forming in motion. Ahead: the road junction, a crossroads with a stone marker, half a battalion of Austrian infantry trying to form column for movement and not yet formed.
"Sabers. Forward at the trot."
They did not charge. Charles had thought about this on the way across the bridge. Against formed infantry a cavalry charge was butchery; against infantry caught in movement it was disruption. He did not want to destroy this battalion, which would cost him men he could not afford. He wanted to scatter it, keep it from being useful for the next hour while the French infantry consolidated the crossing.
The section came in at a controlled trot, sabers out, a line of horses moving with deliberate weight. The Austrian column, half-formed, broke. Not in panic, not all of them, but the shape of it dissolved, men pulling away from the road, officers shouting orders that could not find their targets anymore.
Three volleys of musketry came from the disorganized mass, ragged and high. One man at the end of Charles's line went down, horse shot. The rider was up immediately, on foot, pulling back toward the crossing.
Charles wheeled the section right and moved parallel to the road, keeping the Austrians disrupted, keeping them from reforming. Not pressing. Not charging. Presence, the threat of it, the suggestion that any attempt to consolidate would bring the cavalry back.
After twenty minutes Major Beaumont sent a rider: pull back, infantry has the junction.
Charles disengaged and rode the section back toward the crossing.

* * *

The man who had gone down was Private Lacroix. His horse was dead at the road junction, shot through the neck. Lacroix himself had walked back across the pontoon bridge and was sitting on the bank when Charles found him, left arm held at an angle that was wrong.
"Broken?" Charles asked.
"Something is, sir." Lacroix's face was gray under the campaign tan. "Horse went down sideways. I went with it."
"Surgeon is at the rear. Sergeant Duval will take you."
"Yes, sir."
Charles crouched beside him. Lacroix was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been in the section since the garrison at Munich. He could name every horse in the section by habit and had a younger brother who wrote him letters about wanting to enlist.
"You rode well today," Charles said.
Lacroix looked at him. "Did we do what we were meant to do, sir?"
"Yes."
"Good." He shifted his arm and his face went tighter. "My horse. Is he"
"He did not suffer," Charles said, which was approximately true. A neck shot at the speed of a musket ball was fast.
Lacroix nodded and looked at the river.
Sergeant Duval appeared. "Ready when you are, Lacroix."
Charles watched them go and then turned to look at the far bank, where the battle was still audible but changed in character, the sharp exchange of the crossing replaced by the steadier sound of infantry working through a position that was already taken. The Austrian second line had broken. Somewhere further along the road there would be cavalry pursuit, other units, men he did not know. The battle was larger than what he could see. It was always larger than what he could see.
Sergeant Mercier materialized beside him.
"Well done, sir," Mercier said. "The decision not to charge the column. That was right."
"They were not formed. A charge would have cost more than it gained."
"Yes." Mercier watched the far bank. "Most young officers see cavalry and think charge. You thought about what you actually needed to accomplish."
Charles did not say anything.
"I am not praising you to praise you," Sergeant Mercier said. "I am saying it because I want you to understand what you did and why it worked, so you can do it again. There will be a next time."
"I know."
"Good."

* * *

The casualty count for the day's crossing came in at evening mess: four hundred and twelve French dead, over a thousand wounded. The Austrians had lost more, which was the point of an assault crossing, but four hundred and twelve was not a number that sat easily.
Charles sat with Lieutenant Philippe and ate without tasting what he was eating.
"Four hundred," Philippe said.
"And twelve."
"Right." Philippe pushed food around his tin plate. "Lacroix?"
"Broken arm, maybe collarbone. Surgeon says he will mend."
"Lucky."
"Yes." Charles thought of the man whose horse had gone down on the far side of the gap, Austrian infantry, had not seen what happened to him. One of their men. "Do you know what happened to Virel? The one who went down on their side."
Philippe was quiet for a moment. "Captured. Beaumont got word this afternoon. He is at an Austrian field hospital."
"Alive?"
"Wounded, but yes."
Charles absorbed that. Wounded and captured was not good and was much better than the alternative.
"Four hundred and twelve," he said again.
"We are a very small piece of a very large thing," Philippe said. "I keep having to remind myself of that."
"Does it help?"
"Not really." Philippe set his plate down. "But it is true."
The camp was quiet for its size. The usual sounds, horses and men and equipment, but at a lower register than the previous evenings, as though the day had pressed something out of the noise and left only the functional sounds behind.

* * *

Charles could not sleep. He walked the horse lines in the dark, checking animals by habit, the same inspection sequence Sergeant Mercier had taught him. He found Major Beaumont doing the same thing at the far end of the line, alone, a lamp in one hand.
Beaumont looked up. "Leblanc."
"Sir. I did not mean to intrude."
"You are not intruding." Beaumont ran a hand down the flank of a gray mare and moved to the next horse. "Could not sleep either?"
"No, sir."
"It is always the first real one that does it. After Marengo I walked the lines all night." He said it without self-consciousness, the way you mentioned weather. "After a while you learn to sleep anyway. The body insists."
"How long does that take?"
Major Beaumont was quiet for a moment. "Different for everyone." He moved to the next horse. "Your section performed well today. The decision at the road junction was sound."
"Sergeant Mercier said the same."
"Sergeant Mercier is right." Beaumont held the lamp up, looked at a hind leg, set it down. "You did not try to destroy them. You disrupted them. That is a harder thing to learn than courage."
Charles waited.
"Courage most men have enough of when they are young," Beaumont said. "Judgment is rarer. It comes from asking the right question before you act." He moved along the line. "Today you asked what you actually needed to accomplish. Most lieutenants ask how to get the most glory from the situation. They are not the same question."
"You saw the action?"
"I was watching all three sections. That is my job." Beaumont reached the end of the horse line and turned back. "The four hundred and twelve. You are thinking about that number."
"Yes, sir."
"Good. Think about it. Remember it. When the numbers get comfortable is when you should worry." He walked past Charles toward the officers' tents. "Get some sleep, Lieutenant. We march at dawn."
Charles stood in the dark with the horses for a while after Beaumont left.
The major had said when the numbers get comfortable. Not if.

* * *

He wrote a letter home the next morning before the march, sitting on his bedroll with the portable writing desk Elise had given him balanced across his knees.
Dear Father and Elise,
We have crossed the Tagliamento. I cannot say more about the engagement but the crossing was successful and the section performed well. I am unhurt. Lacroix has a broken arm and will recover. Virel is a prisoner but alive.
He stopped.
He wanted to write about the four hundred and twelve. He wanted to write about Beaumont in the dark with the lamp and what he had said about the numbers getting comfortable. He wanted to write about Rousseau's face at the gap, the tight set of it, the way the boy had come through the fire and come out the other side and was still recognizably the same person he had been going in, which was not nothing.
He did not know how to write any of it in a way that would make sense to people who had not been there. He did not know if it made sense to him yet.
He wrote:
The army is larger than I understood before I was inside it. I think I am beginning to understand my small part of it.
Your son and brother,
Charles
He sealed it and addressed it to Bordeaux and put it with the other mail and went to find Tonnerre.

CHAPTER 5
The Italian campaign did not end at the Tagliamento. It kept going, the way campaigns did, the army moving southeast through the summer heat, one engagement becoming the next with barely enough time between them to bury the dead and rest the horses.
Charles kept a record in the back of his field journal, not a diary, just dates and locations and the names of men lost or wounded. By August the list was two pages long. He was not sure why he kept it. He told himself it was practical, that the names mattered for the official record. He did not examine the other reasons closely.
The work of it was what he had not expected. Not the fighting, which was terrible and also contained within itself a clarity that ordinary life did not have. The work between the fighting: the requisition forms, the horse inspections, the discipline cases, the endless correspondence with supply officers about rations and forage, the mapping of roads that turned out to be tracks, the tracking of men who were sick or lame or drunk or simply not where they were supposed to be. He had thought of cavalry as motion and violence. Mostly it was administration.
Sergeant Duval handled most of the administration, had handled it before Charles arrived and would handle it after, but Charles was learning the structure of it, the way the paperwork connected to the reality it described, the way a ration count on paper became hungry men or fed ones depending on whether the numbers were right. He found he was good at it. This did not surprise him. He had grown up tracking the estate accounts, harvest yields, the cost of cooperage against the price of wine in a good year and a bad one.
What surprised him was that being good at administration felt like being good at something small. At eighteen he had imagined himself in the smoke, blade out, leading charges that would be remembered. At nineteen he spent Tuesday afternoons arguing with supply sergeants about whether three kilos of oats per horse was adequate given the distances being covered. It was adequate. He was right. The horses were better for it. Nobody would remember it.

* * *

There was a second engagement in July, a rearguard action outside a small town whose name Charles did not bother to learn because it was not on any map he had access to. The Austrians were pulling back and leaving a cavalry screen to buy time, and Major Beaumont's squadron was sent to push through it. The infantry battle for the town itself was already underway on the right flank when they rode up; Charles could hear it before he could see it, the rolling exchange of musketry, then the heavier percussion of a cannon working at close range, then a sound under all of it that he had no category for yet, a collective human sound, many men together, neither shouting nor screaming but something between.
Charles's section took the left flank. The Austrian screen was a hundred men to his fifteen, which was not as bad as it sounded because they were trying to delay, not fight, and a screen that stands and fights loses its purpose. He recognized this before he gave the order, felt the shape of the situation the way you felt weather coming, and put the section at a diagonal that threatened their line of retreat rather than their line of resistance. To his right the infantry fight continued, smoke thickening over the town roofline, the musketry now in ragged bursts rather than volleys, which meant it had moved into the streets.
The Austrian screen broke without a charge. The cavalry peeled away and rode, and Charles let them go because he did not have the numbers to pursue safely and because letting them go meant no one on his side got hurt. He brought the section to a halt on a low rise and watched the Austrians diminish into the distance, and became aware, now that there was nothing immediate requiring his attention, of what the morning had produced in the field to his right.
The town was two hundred meters away. The infantry had taken it or were taking it; the musketry had slackened to occasional single shots, which usually meant one side had given way. The road running from the town toward his position had a wagon overturned across it, and near the wagon a man in French blue sitting with his back against a wheel and both hands pressed to his abdomen, looking at his hands. Beside him another man lay face-up in the road with no one attending him.
Further along the road two soldiers were walking away from the town, one leaning on the other, moving with the careful deliberate gait of men whose bodies had become suddenly uncertain. Behind them a sergeant directed three men carrying a door they had taken off its hinges, using it as a stretcher. Charles looked at what was on the stretcher long enough to understand what he was looking at, then looked back at his section.
Corporal Rousseau was watching the road. The eagerness that had been on his face at the start of the campaign was present now in a different form, compressed into something quieter and more careful, the expression of a man absorbing information he had not previously had.
The section had come through the morning without a casualty. This was correct. Charles had made the right decisions and they had worked and no one was hurt. Forty meters to his right the infantry had made different decisions, or had not been able to make any decisions because the ground gave them no choices, and the man by the wagon wheel was still looking at his hands.
Sergeant Mercier rode up beside him. Neither of them looked at the road.
"They did not want to fight. So we did not make them."
"No. But a less patient officer would have charged anyway, to finish what was already finished." A pause. "The infantry to our right had no such choice."
"No," Charles said. "They did not."
Sergeant Mercier looked at him for a moment. "The men on that stretcher did their job correctly too. It cost them anyway." He said it without judgment, the way he said most things. "That is also what war is."
They rode back to the squadron. The road took them past the wagon. Charles did not intend to look and looked anyway: the man who had been sitting against the wheel was gone, moved by someone, and there was a dark stain on the road where he had been. They reached the squadron and Charles reported to Major Beaumont: screen broken, no casualties in his section. Beaumont nodded and made a note. That evening he assigned Charles the reconnaissance duties for the following week's advance. Charles understood it to be a form of communication, and felt the shape of what was being communicated, and was not certain it was entirely a good thing.

* * *

Corporal Rousseau had changed over the summer. Not in the ways Charles had expected, not harder or quieter or more careful. The eagerness was still there but it had found a different object. At the start of the campaign Rousseau had wanted to fight. By August he wanted to be good at fighting, which was not the same thing and was considerably more useful.
He started asking different questions. Not when will there be action but how did you know the screen would break, not what is the plan but why that plan and not another. He began taking the younger troopers through the same afternoon training Charles had started in Munich, the horse inspections, the equipment checks, the habit of looking at ground before riding over it. He did not wait to be asked. He simply started doing it.
"You are going to make him a sergeant early," Lieutenant Philippe said one evening, watching Rousseau put a trooper through a saddle inspection for the third time.
"He is not ready yet."
"No. But he will be." Philippe adjusted his collar. "He is like you were in Munich, except warmer."
"I was not cold."
"Charles. You absolutely were." Philippe said it without heat, fondly. "You were correct about everything and communicating that fact to the men by the manner in which you existed in their vicinity."
Charles thought about that. "And now?"
"Now you are correct about most things and have learned to let people discover that themselves rather than announcing it." Philippe paused. "It is an improvement. Rousseau is already there. He has a gift for making people feel capable."
"That is a useful gift."
"More useful than yours or mine, in some respects." Philippe looked at the evening sky. "He is going to be a very good soldier if he lives long enough."
The qualifier sat in the air between them. Neither of them addressed it.

* * *

The promotion came in September, six weeks before the end of the Italian campaign.
Major Beaumont called him into the headquarters tent on a Tuesday morning. Charles stood at attention while Beaumont read the relevant portion of the orders in the flat tone of a man for whom paperwork was paperwork regardless of content.
"By order of the Emperor, Second Lieutenant Charles Leblanc is hereby promoted to the rank of Lieutenant, with all privileges and responsibilities attendant thereto. Effective the fifteenth of September, year of our Lord eighteen hundred and six."
Beaumont set the paper down. "Congratulations, Lieutenant."
"Thank you, sir."
A pause. Beaumont picked up the next paper on his desk, which was a requisition form. "That will be all."
Charles walked out of the headquarters tent and stood in the September heat for a moment.
Lieutenant. He had been working toward it since Munich, since his father's five-year plan, since the garrison, since the first fumbled months of learning what cavalry actually meant. He had imagined this moment often, usually in the terms of what it would mean for the Leblanc name, the way his father would read the letter, Elise's response.
What he felt was mainly: three years in. Two more to Captain. He was on schedule.
He went to find Sergeant Mercier to tell him the section would need to sew a new rank insignia onto the spare uniform.

* * *

That evening Lieutenant Philippe produced a bottle of something from his saddlebag that was approximately wine and poured two cups.
"To Lieutenant Leblanc," Philippe said. "Who achieved this distinction by being relentlessly correct about horses and supply chains."
"You make it sound like an insult."
"It is a toast. Drink."
They drank. The wine was not good. The campaign had used up all the good wine weeks ago.
"How does it feel?" Philippe asked.
"The same."
"It always does at first." Philippe held his cup and looked at it. "I made Lieutenant in April. I thought I would feel different. More like an officer."
"Were you not already an officer?"
"In rank. I mean in here." Philippe touched his chest in a way that was only slightly theatrical. "I thought the promotion would settle something. Confirm I was in the right place."
"Did it?"
"No." Philippe drank. "I think the confirmation does not come from the rank. It comes from something else. The men. The way they look at you when it matters." He was quiet for a moment. "Your section follows you, Charles. Not because of your rank. They started following you before you had a rank that warranted it."
Charles did not know what to say to that.
"That is what I mean," Philippe said. "You are already what the rank says you are. The paper is just the army catching up with the fact."
He poured more wine. It did not improve with a second cup.

* * *

The campaign ended in October with the army occupying a line of small Italian towns that the maps called victories and that the towns themselves experienced as a complicated inconvenience. Charles's section was billeted in a village outside Udine whose name he could pronounce by the third day and not before.
He sat one evening on the low wall outside the house they had requisitioned and watched the village doing what villages did in the evening: a woman drawing water from the well, two old men talking at a doorway in a language he did not speak, a dog moving between the houses on some private business. The section was inside, sleeping or gambling or writing letters, the sounds of it muffled through the stone walls.
He had the field journal open, the list in the back. He read through it. Tagliamento: Lacroix broken arm, Virel captured recovered. The rearguard at the unnamed town: no casualties in his section. He had written it that way because that was the accurate entry for his section. The accurate entry for the infantry engagement happening simultaneously would have been written by someone else in a different journal and would have looked different.
He had done this. Not alone, not without Sergeant Mercier and Sergeant Duval and Lieutenant Philippe and the training and the luck that you could not control. But he had done his part correctly. He had read the ground and made the decisions and kept his men alive and accomplished the missions, and the record showed it in the absence of names on his list.
He was good at this.
The thought arrived without ceremony, the way true things often did. Not pride exactly, not satisfaction. A recognition. He had been trying to be good at this for two years and it turned out he was, and the feeling was not what he had expected it to be when he had imagined it at eighteen on a carriage road in Bordeaux.
He had imagined glory. What he had was competence, and competence felt like a different thing entirely. Quieter. More certain. Less like winning and more like understanding a language well enough to stop translating it.
He closed the journal and watched the woman at the well finish drawing water and carry it inside. The old men at the doorway were still talking. The dog had disappeared.
He was good at this. He thought about Moreau's widow, about Lacroix's broken arm, about the four hundred and twelve at the Tagliamento, about Virel in an Austrian field hospital for six weeks. About Beaumont saying when the numbers get comfortable.
Being good at this was not uncomplicated.

* * *

He wrote home about the promotion.
The letter took him two evenings. The first evening he wrote the formal version, the one that reported the promotion and the campaign and the successful conclusion of the Italian operations. He sealed it and then opened it again and wrote a second draft.
The second draft said:
Dear Father and Elise,
I am promoted to Lieutenant. This is the plan working as it should. Two more years to Captain, perhaps less if the campaigns continue to go as this one has. I am on schedule to come home.
I am good at this, Father. I do not know if that is what you expected when you sent me. I am not sure it is what I expected. But I am good at it, which feels important to report, though I am not certain how to explain what it costs.
The section is in good condition. Lacroix's arm healed well. The horses are sound. We are in a village outside Udine that has a well in the square and two old men who talk outside a doorway every evening and have done so, I imagine, for forty years, and who will be doing so again when we are gone.
Tell Elise I am sorry I missed whatever she has done this year. I expect she has done things. Tell her to write and tell me.
Your son,
Charles
He sealed the second draft and addressed it and left the first draft in the candle flame.

* * *

Sergeant Mercier found him at the horses the morning before they broke camp for the march north.
"Sir. A moment."
"Yes, Sergeant."
Mercier ran a hand down Tonnerre's neck, not checking anything, just the habitual contact. "You have done well this campaign. The section has done well."
"Thank you, Sergeant."
"I am not saying it to be pleasant." Mercier's tone was the one that meant he was about to say something he had been thinking about for some time. "I am saying it because I want to ask you something."
Charles waited.
"When this is done. When you have made Captain. What then?"
Charles looked at him. "Home. The estate. That was always the plan."
"I know it was the plan." Mercier was looking at Tonnerre. "I have seen other officers with plans. Good officers. Men who knew what they were doing and why. And I have seen them promoted and given more men and more responsibility, and the plan gets pushed forward. One more campaign. One more season. The army does not give men up easily when they are useful."
"I am aware of that."
"Are you?" Mercier looked at him now, directly. "You are useful, sir. You are more useful than you were six months ago, which means you will be given more to do. That is how it works."
Charles said nothing.
"I am not telling you to leave. I am not telling you to stay. I am an NCO and it is not my place to tell a lieutenant anything about his career." Mercier straightened. "I am telling you to keep the plan in your head. Keep it specific. Captain, then home. Not Captain and then one more thing and then home."
"Yes, Sergeant."
Mercier nodded once and went back to his work.
Charles stood with Tonnerre for a while, the horse warm against his hand in the October cold.
The plan was still clear. He was still on schedule. He was Lieutenant Leblanc, two years from Captain, and after Captain, home.
He told himself this several times, which was not something he had needed to do before.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 6

The cold in Prussia was a different animal than the cold in the Alps.

In the mountains, cold was vertical — it came off stone faces and glacial wind and the altitude made it clean, if brutal. You understood it. In Prussia, the cold was flat and gray and everywhere, rising from the mud and the dead fields in equal measure, settling into the folds of your coat and the gaps in your boots. By the third week of November, Charles had stopped noticing individual moments of discomfort and begun simply existing inside a general state of it.

They were somewhere east of the Oder, pushing toward Küstrin, following a road that had been churned by three armies and a hundred supply wagons into something barely navigable. The Grande Armée was in pursuit now, mopping up Prussian remnants after Jena — not the clean work of an advancing campaign but the slow, irregular work of grinding down a broken force that didn't know yet it was finished. The Prussians were fighting in patches. A rear guard here, a cavalry screen there. Stubborn holdouts at road junctions that made no sense to hold.

This morning, the squadron had been detached from the main column to secure a junction two kilometers west of a supply depot the army needed.

The briefing had taken ten minutes. Colonel Varennes — a staff officer who had been attached to them from Paris, five days ago, with new orders and a general's manner — had unrolled his map on an ammunition box and explained the situation in the tone of a man explaining something simple to people he suspected of being slow. The junction was held by Prussian cavalry and a half-battery of four light guns. The approach was four hundred meters of open ground — a harvested beet field, flat and completely without cover. The guns would have clean sight lines for the entire distance.

Beaumont had stood across the ammunition box from Varennes, looking at the map. "The eastern track," Beaumont said, not raising his voice. "If we take the horses along the drainage ditch—"

"The eastern track is soft ground," Varennes said. "We'd lose the element of speed."

"We'd lose half the first wave to the battery before they reach the guns."

Varennes folded the map. "The order stands."

* * *

Charles found Philippe that night at the picket line.

It was late, well past the time a man had any business being out of his bedroll, and the temperature had dropped to the point where the mud on the road was beginning to crust around the edges. Philippe was at the far end of the line, checking the knots on Renard's lead rope — checking them with the focused attention of someone who'd already checked them twice.

"You're not worried about the knots," Charles said.

"The knots are excellent." Philippe didn't look up. "I'm enjoying the company."

Charles stopped beside him. The horses stood close together in the dark, breath rising in small clouds. Down the line, a sentry moved past, boots crunching on the frosted grass, and then was gone.

"The staff position," Philippe said, after a moment. "Beaumont told me he offered it. In October."

"He did."

"You didn't tell me."

Charles said nothing.

"I heard it from Duval, Charles. Duval." Philippe finally looked at him. "You know what Duval told me? He said you turned it down in about thirty seconds. Like there was nothing to think about."

"There wasn't."

"There was a great deal to think about." Philippe pulled his collar straight, the automatic gesture he'd been making since Charles first met him. "A staff position means another three years of service, minimum."

"Yes."

"So you chose this instead."

"Five years," Charles said. "Make Captain and go home. A staff appointment changes the equation."

Philippe looked at him for a long moment, something behind his eyes that wasn't quite the easy amusement he usually wore. He looked tired. He had looked tired for a week, which was unremarkable in itself — they were all tired — but there was a quality to it now, a quietness.

"Élise wrote me," Philippe said.

Charles didn't answer immediately. "I know she writes you."

"She asked me to look after you." Philippe's mouth curved slightly. "I wrote back and told her you didn't need it. That you were the most capable man in the squadron and possibly the most infuriating." He paused. "She wrote back and said competence wasn't care."

The horses shifted. Somewhere in the dark, a horse stamped once and went still.

"She's right, of course," Philippe said. "She usually is, your sister." He adjusted his collar again. "You don't tell people things, Charles. You talk to no one about you. You know that?"

"I know."

"It's not a complaint. It's just — I've watched you for a year. You'll talk tactics until midnight. You'll sit with a man when he's frightened and you'll find the exactly right thing to do. But you talk to no one about yourself." Philippe's voice was mild, not pressing. "I've been trying to figure out if you know it and don't mind, or if you genuinely don't notice."

"I know."

"Which is it?"

Charles thought about that honestly. "Both," he said. "Depending on the day."

Philippe laughed, quietly, a real one. He gave Renard one last pat on the neck and stepped back. "Go home when you can, Charles. That's all I mean. Don't let the plan get longer."

"Five years," Charles said. "Captain and home. Nothing longer."

Philippe nodded. He was already turning back toward the bivouac, collar up against the cold, hands stuffed in his coat. "Good night," he said, without looking back.

Charles stayed at the picket line a little longer. Tonnerre was six horses down, and he found him in the dark by feel — the particular shape of the gelding's nose, the warmth of his breath. He stood there a moment, not thinking about anything specific.

Then he went back to his bedroll, lay down with his boots on, and put his hand on the left one. The paper was there. He had put it in before dark, his family's address in Bordeaux in Élise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card. He'd done it before every engagement since Günzburg. He wasn't sure why, exactly. The address hadn't changed. Élise would still know where to send the letter regardless.

His hand rested on the boot. He fell asleep like that.

* * *

Morning came gray and cold, which was how all mornings came now.

Mercier had the horses ready before Charles reached the line. The sergeant moved through the inspection sequence without looking up: left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Then hooves, each one lifted and examined. Then the spine, both hands running from withers to croup. He did it while Charles stood beside him, and he did not hurry.

"Philippe's section goes first wave," Charles said. "We're in support. Second wave, seventy meters back."

Mercier set down a hoof, moved to the next horse. "Don't stop in the middle."

"I wasn't planning to."

"Men slow down when they see the first volley hit. They lose their nerve for a moment — not deserting, just — they slow." Mercier's hands moved along a foreleg, checking for heat. "If you feel the section slow, you push through them. Don't stop in the middle of that field."

"Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier straightened, patted the horse's shoulder, moved to the next one.

* * *

Philippe was adjusting his collar when Charles found him before the line.

"It's manageable," Philippe said, with the careful tone of someone building a belief while they say it. Testing the weight of the words. "The guns are light. By the time we're at a hundred meters, they won't have time to reload."

"They'll have time for two volleys," Charles said.

"Probably two, yes."

They stood beside their horses. Charles wanted to say something. He could feel the shape of it — some version of last night's conversation, something that continued it — but the men were nearby and the moment wasn't right, and anyway he didn't know exactly what he wanted to say.

"My section goes first," Philippe said. Not asking. Just confirming. "You come through when we reach the guns."

"Yes."

Philippe nodded. He looked at Charles for a moment — not the tired look from last night, something steadier — and then he reached up and adjusted his collar one more time, a reflex.

"Brief your men," he said. "Don't let them think too long."

He turned and walked back to his section.

Charles watched him go for a second. Then he went to find his own.

* * *

He found Mercier a moment later, checking the girth on his horse for the third time.

"Two volleys," Charles said. "Maybe three if they're fast. Philippe's section takes the battery. We push through, deal with the flanks."

Mercier checked the girth. Said nothing.

"Varennes won't move the approach. Beaumont tried."

Mercier released the stirrup. "Don't stop in the middle."

Charles said, "Yes, Sergeant."

Mercier swung up.

* * *

Philippe's section moved at ten past nine, when the light was flat and even and gave no shadows.

Charles held his section in column, seventy meters back. From here, he could see the junction — a low stone wall and a farmhouse beyond it, the four gun carriages arranged in a rough line, the Prussian cavalry visible as dark shapes behind them, formed in two groups on the flanks. The beet field between here and there was frozen in ridges from the cart tracks and scattered with the black stumps of harvested plants.

Philippe's section went at the trot, then the canter. Eleven men in an extended line, sabers not yet drawn. They had made it perhaps a hundred and fifty meters when the guns fired.

The sound was flat in the cold air, not the rolling crash of the summer battles but something more compressed, efficient. Two horses went down in the first volley — one immediately, one struggling for a moment and then still. The section kept moving, closing the gap. Charles felt Tonnerre shift beneath him, a muscle-tightening that wasn't panic, just awareness, and he put a hand briefly on the gelding's neck.

The second volley came at a hundred and thirty meters. One man. The section barely paused. They were close enough now that Charles could hear them — hooves on frozen ground, a shout from somewhere in Philippe's line — and then they were at the guns, and the surface of it became immediate and particular and left no room for watching.

Charles pressed his section forward.

Through the gap in the stone wall. The guns already taken. Prussian artillerymen running or on the ground. He swept left where one of the Prussian cavalry groups was reforming, caught them before they had any order to them, drove them back in two hard exchanges that he would not remember clearly afterward. The second group melted north, into the bare poplar trees at the edge of the field.

Then it was over.

Charles pulled Tonnerre up near the farmhouse wall. His hands were shaking slightly, the fine trembling that came in the aftermath and lasted maybe two minutes before it stopped. He breathed. He counted his section: Rousseau, Duval, Marchetti, the two Girard cousins, the others — all of them there, three men with cuts, one with an arm he was holding odd, none of them down. He looked for Philippe.

He did not see him.

* * *

One of Philippe's men was kneeling beside a gun carriage, looking up when Charles arrived.

"Corporal Bonnard," Charles said. "Where is he?"

Bonnard was already on his feet, stepping back to give Charles room, and that was how Charles knew before he saw him — the specific way Bonnard moved, the expression of someone who has realized they need an officer.

Philippe was on the ground behind the gun carriage, back against the wheel. He was conscious. He had both hands pressed to his chest, high left side, and there was blood on them and on the front of his coat. His face was pale but his eyes were clear, tracking Charles as Charles knelt beside him.

"The junction," Philippe said.

"Taken. Both flanking groups dispersed."

"Good." He breathed carefully, the breath shallow and deliberate. "The guns?"

"Ours."

"Good," he said again.

Charles put his hand over Philippe's hands and applied pressure. The wound was high and left and there was too much blood for a surface wound. He looked up at Bonnard. "Surgeon. Now."

Bonnard was already moving.

Charles kept his eyes on Philippe. "Where does it hurt?"

"It is where it is." A pause. A careful breath. "I can't feel my left hand."

"The surgeon's coming."

Philippe nodded, eyes closing briefly, then opening again. He looked at Charles with the slightly unfocused quality of a man navigating pain. "You're bleeding," he said.

Charles hadn't noticed. There was a cut on his forearm — shallow, cloth already sticking to it, not serious. "It's nothing."

"Good."

They stayed like that for a moment, Charles's hand over Philippe's, the gun carriage at Philippe's back. The cold was very clear. From somewhere across the junction, men were shouting about horses. The ordinary noise of aftermath.

Charles looked at Philippe's collar. It was turned up wrong on one side, askew from the fall or the press of the engagement. He reached with his free hand and straightened it.

The gesture was ridiculous. The collar made no difference. Philippe watched it happen with an expression that was careful and grateful in a way that had nothing to do with the collar.

"Captain and home," Philippe said. "Still the plan?"

"Still the plan."

"Good." He shifted slightly, winced. "My section will need someone. After."

"Your section's fine," Charles said. "They came through."

"They'll need someone to look at them the way you do." Philippe's eyes were tracking carefully, fighting to stay on Charles. "Like they're capable. You do that. You don't notice it."

"Don't use up your breath."

"I'm using it correctly." But he stopped talking.

The surgeon arrived in six minutes. Charles counted, afterward, in the journal.

* * *

There were two lucid stretches in the two hours that followed.

The first was brief — Philippe surfacing from the surgeon's work to say something about Renard, that someone should make sure Renard was checked properly, that the gelding had been limping slightly this morning. Charles said he would see to it. Philippe believed him and settled.

The second came later, when the light was already changing. He surfaced clearly, enough that he looked at Charles with real focus.

"Élise wrote me," he said. Not remembering that he'd said it last night, or perhaps knowing and saying it again. "She asked me to look after you. I told her you didn't need it."

"You told me."

"She was right, though." A breath, slow and even. "You don't need looking after. But you could let people try." He paused. "Go home before it gets comfortable, Charles. The longer you wait, the better you'll be at it, and the harder it will be to leave." His voice was quiet, tired, not pressing. Just saying the true thing. "I know how you think. You'll start calculating who depends on you. You'll find you're useful. It's easy to stay for that."

Charles didn't answer.

"Go home when you can." He reached up toward his collar, a reflex, but his hand made it only as far as his chest before it stopped. He couldn't quite reach it.

Charles reached over and straightened it.

"Thank you," Philippe said. Something in his face settled, the last small tension leaving it.

He went quiet after that, in and out, the breathing slow and occasional. At some point in the late afternoon the breathing changed — not a moment, not a sound, just a quality of the silence that was different from the silences before it. The surgeon shook his head. He said something to Charles. Charles heard the words and understood them and filed them somewhere for later.

Outside, the junction was their junction now, and men were moving around it, doing the ordinary work.

* * *

He found Mercier at the horse line.

The sergeant was going through the inspection sequence, methodical, on a horse that had come through without injury and needed no inspection. Left foreleg, right foreleg, left hind, right hind. Hooves. Spine. He worked through it in full, and when it was done he started it again, more slowly.

"Philippe is dead," Charles said.

Mercier's hands stilled on the horse's flank. He stood that way for a moment, looking at nothing in particular. Then he continued the inspection, the spine, both hands moving steadily.

"I know," he said.

He finished the sequence. He straightened. He looked at Charles with the specific quality of attention Mercier reserved for things that mattered, not long, not dramatic, just clear and direct.

"The pressure is starting," Mercier said.

"I know."

"You don't, yet." He moved to the next horse, started at the left foreleg. "You did well today. The men know that. Philippe's section knows that. Varennes knows it, which he won't say, but Beaumont does and he will." He pressed two fingers against the foreleg, checking. "Men will start looking at you differently. They'll hand you problems because you solve them. They'll ask you to take on the next thing because the last thing went well. And you'll be able to do it." He paused. "That's the dangerous part."

Charles said nothing.

"The plan was specific," Mercier said. "Captain. Then home. Not Captain and the next engagement after that, not Captain and we need you through the winter, not Captain and — it does not finish itself, Lieutenant. It is never finished. There will always be one more problem in front of you that you can solve and they cannot. That is not a reason to stay."

"I know."

"You will feel it differently in six months." Mercier moved to the right foreleg. "I'm telling you now while you still believe me."

He kept going down the horse, unhurried. He did not look at Charles again.

Charles stayed a moment longer, watching the sequence, and then he turned and went to find his journal.

* * *

The page at the back was two pages now, names in the small careful print he used for entries he wanted to keep. He found the bottom of the second page, found the space below the last name, and wrote Philippe's.

*Arnaud, Philippe. Lieutenant. November 1806.*

He looked at it for a moment. Then he closed the journal.

Philippe's section was quartered in the farmhouse, twelve men using the main room and the attached barn, and Charles found them at dusk by the state of them — not disorganized, but quiet in the specific way of men who have lost someone and don't yet know the new shape of things.

Bonnard looked up when Charles came through the door. Bonnard was twenty-three, from Lyon, had been Philippe's corporal for four months, a steady man who checked everything twice and didn't make noise about it. He looked at Charles with the expression of someone who has run out of the thing he was relying on and is deciding, in real time, whether the next available thing will hold weight.

Charles said: "Horses watered and fed?"

"Yes, sir."

"Bonnard — your horses are watered but three of them are short on forage. I checked on my way in." He hadn't — he was guessing from the supply situation, from what he knew of what had come in that afternoon. "Get with Rousseau and go to the depot before they close it. They'll tell you they don't have it. Tell them you need a signature from Major Beaumont authorizing priority issue, and if they want to argue it, I'll be along in an hour."

Bonnard was already on his feet. Something in him had straightened slightly.

"The rest of you," Charles said, looking around the room. "Tonight is tonight. Tomorrow morning I want a head count on equipment — carbines, sabers, saddlery — anything that needs attention before we move. Write it down. If you don't write it down, it doesn't exist." He paused. "You came through the junction. That was well done."

He didn't make it more than that.

He met with Duval about the casualty report. He walked the perimeter once, looking at the horses. He had the argument with the supply depot — not Bonnard's forage argument, which Rousseau handled efficiently, but a separate argument about canteens and the fact that the resupply schedule was two days off and someone at the depot had decided that wasn't his problem. Charles made it his problem, calmly, specifically, with dates and regulations and the promise of Beaumont's signature, and resolved it in twenty minutes.

The depot sergeant watched him leave with the expression of a man who had expected to win that argument.

* * *

At the picket line, late, Tonnerre was working through inadequate hay with the focused patience he brought to most things. Charles stood beside him in the dark. The junction was quiet now, the farmhouse lights low, the sentries moving in their regular rotation.

He was still on schedule.

The thought arrived without ceremony, the way his mind worked when it was calculating rather than feeling. Captain in — call it three more years if the campaigns continued to go this way. Home before thirty. The plan was specific. The plan was intact.

*Go home before it gets comfortable.*

Philippe had said it last night and again this afternoon. The words had a different weight now, the second hearing.

*When the numbers get comfortable*, Beaumont had said, four months ago. *That's when you should worry.*

*It is never finished*, Mercier had said tonight. Not bitterly. Just as a fact.

Charles thought about Bonnard's face in the farmhouse — that moment of deciding whether the next available thing would hold weight. How quickly Bonnard had come to his feet. How the section had organized itself around two sentences about forage.

He had noticed it. Filed it. Moved on.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck. The gelding kept eating.

Philippe's name was on the list now. Two pages. The list had been one column when he started it in September. Now it ran into the margins.

He had not written to Élise yet. He would have to write to her, and he would have to tell her about Philippe, and he did not know yet how to do that. Philippe had said *competence wasn't care*. Élise had said it first, to Philippe, and Philippe had carried it to Charles, and Charles had understood it intellectually and immediately and had not known what to do with it.

The cold was very clear. Somewhere north of them, past the bare poplars and the frozen fields, there were more Prussians, more junctions, more approaches with four hundred meters of open ground.

He was still on schedule. Five years, make Captain, go home. The plan was specific.

But something in the calculation had changed. Not the numbers — the numbers were the same. Something in how the numbers felt. He turned it over and couldn't find the edge of it.

Charles stood at the picket line for a long time.

He did not sleep.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 7

The assignment came on a Tuesday morning, nine days after the junction at Küstrin, and Charles received it the way he received most things he didn't want: without visible reaction, which was the only reaction that helped.

Three days escorting a supply convoy on the eastern road. Twelve wagons, a contractor's outfit running flour and dried meat and medicine from the depot at Frankfurt to the forward supply point near Posen. The convoy had its own outriders but not enough of them. Two men, which was not adequate for the roads east of the Oder in November of 1806, where Prussian irregulars were still operating in small groups and the difference between a guarded convoy and an unguarded one was the difference between the army eating next week or not.

Beaumont gave him six men and three days and the name of the convoy's point of contact: a Vieillard, contractor, Frankfurt address.

"It's not what you want," Beaumont said. He wasn't apologizing. He was acknowledging a fact.

"It's what's needed," Charles said.

Beaumont looked at him for a moment. "Yes," he said. "Get it done."

* * *

Mercier did not say anything when Charles told him. He was working through the morning inspection, left foreleg, right foreleg, and he continued through it without changing his pace.

"Six men," Charles said. "I'll take Rousseau, Marchetti, the Girard cousins, Ferrand, and Bonnard."

Mercier set down a hoof. "Bonnard."

"He needs something to do. Three days without purpose is worse for him than three days on the road."

Mercier moved to the next horse. "Reasonable."

"I wasn't asking your opinion."

"No, sir. You were telling me your reasoning. That's different." He pressed two fingers against a foreleg, checking for heat. "You do that when you've made a decision you're not certain about."

Charles said nothing.

"Bonnard is a good choice," Mercier said, after a moment, in the tone of a man closing a subject.

* * *

The convoy was staging on the eastern edge of the town, and Charles found it at first light the next morning by the sound of it: the creak of wagon wheels, the particular complaint of draft horses in cold weather, men's voices carrying across the frozen ground.

Twelve wagons was what Beaumont had said. There were fourteen. The two additional ones were smaller, a different type, their canvas covers secured differently from the contractor's main outfit. Charles noted this and filed it.

He was looking for Vieillard when he saw the problem.

The lead wagon had stopped. Not stopped waiting. Stopped stuck, the left rear wheel sunk to the axle in a patch of ground that had looked solid from the road but wasn't, a thin crust of frost over mud that had not frozen through. The driver was still on the box, uncertain. Two men stood beside the wheel looking at it with the expression of men who have decided the problem belongs to someone else.

A third person was already on the ground behind the wagon. Crouched, looking at the wheel and the mud. Not standing back from it. Close, close enough that the mud was on their boots and the hem of their coat.

Young. A woman. She straightened, looked at the two men standing beside the wheel, and said something Charles couldn't hear at this distance. The tone carried: not shouting, not asking. Telling. The two men looked at each other and then began moving.

Charles stopped walking.

She had seen the ground wrong from the road, same as the driver. But she'd assessed what needed doing faster than anyone else near her, which meant she'd already moved past the mistake to the solution before most people had finished registering the problem. That was something. Charles noted it the way he noted things: filed it, moved on.

He waited until the wagon was clear. The men had gotten a plank under the wheel and she'd directed the driver to ease forward. Then he approached.

"Vieillard," he said.

She was brushing mud off her coat. She looked up. She was perhaps eighteen, dark-haired, with the specific quality of attention of someone who is always in the middle of three things at once and has learned to assess new information quickly to determine whether it requires any of those three things to change.

"Vieillard is my father," she said. "I'm Margot Vieillard. You're our escort."

"Second Lieutenant Leblanc." He looked at the wheel. "The road is soft for another fifty meters. You'll want the planks out before the next wagon hits that stretch."

She looked at him. A pause. Not rude, not long. The pause of someone finishing a count before responding.

"I know," she said. "I've already sent someone for them." She held out her hand. "It's good to meet you, Lieutenant."

He shook it. Her grip was direct, her glove worn at the thumb. "And you."

She had already turned back to the wagon by the time he finished the sentence.

* * *

They made fourteen kilometers the first day, which was good for the road conditions and the size of the outfit. Charles rode the length of the column twice in the morning, assessed the weak points. The third wagon's harness needed attention, two of the draft horses were laboring on the softer ground. and brought his concerns to Rousseau, who handled them without needing further direction. By midday the column was moving steadily.

He found himself watching Margot Vieillard without intending to.

Not watching the way Philippe would have watched. Philippe had had an easy appreciation for a pretty woman that required no particular justification. This was different. She was managing the convoy the way Charles managed a section: continuously, with a particular alertness that looked like calm from the outside. She moved up and down the column. She checked the loads, checked the animals, checked the drivers. She knew the name of every man in her outfit, which was eleven people, and she addressed them by name and with specificity, not general direction.

He couldn't hear most of what she said. He heard the results of it.

At the midday halt she was tallying something in a small ledger, standing at the side of the road, the ledger open against her forearm. Her lips moved slightly when she counted. She finished, looked up, and he realized he had been watching her for perhaps thirty seconds without any obvious pretense.

"Lieutenant," she said. It was not unfriendly.

"The third wagon's harness," he said. "The right trace is fraying. It should hold today but I'd look at it tonight."

She looked at him. "I saw it this morning. My man Lefebvre is handling it at the halt."

"Good."

He had no further reason to be standing there. He turned his horse and rode back to the head of the column, and he could feel, without looking, the specific quality of her attention following him for a moment before she returned to her ledger.

* * *

That evening they made camp in the lee of a farmhouse whose occupants had either fled or been evacuated, the windows shuttered, the yard stripped of everything useful. Margot's men had the routine down: wagons in a square, animals inside, fire at the center. Efficient. Not military-efficient, which was a specific kind of efficiency built around command and compliance, but a different kind: the efficiency of people who'd worked together long enough to know what each other would do.

Charles posted his men and walked the perimeter once, then found himself with the particular problem of the evening halt on escort duty: nothing specific to do. His section at the bivouac had always had something. Here, the waiting was the work.

He was checking Tonnerre's near foreleg when he heard footsteps on the frozen ground and looked up.

Margot Vieillard was carrying two tin cups. She held one out.

"Coffee," she said. "Or what we're calling coffee."

He took it. "Thank you."

She stood beside him, her own cup in both hands. The fire at the center of the wagon square threw light across the yard. One of her drivers was singing something, low, a working song of some kind.

"You've been watching my operation all day," she said. Not accusing. Noting.

"Your operation is worth watching. It runs well."

She looked at him over the rim of her cup. "That's either a compliment or an inspection."

Charles thought about that. "Both," he said. Then, because it was accurate: "More compliment than inspection. You handled the wheel problem faster than anyone I've watched handle a stuck wagon."

"I've had practice."

"How long have you been running the convoy?"

She was quiet for a moment. "Since May. My father was ill in the spring. He recovered, but the contracts didn't wait, so I went ahead with the run." She paused. "He recovered fully. He decided I could keep managing the runs."

"How old are you?"

She looked at him. Not offended. Something else. The look of someone accustomed to the question who has not yet decided how to feel about it.

"Eighteen," she said. "How old are you?"

"Twenty."

"And you're a lieutenant."

"Yes."

"With six men and an escort assignment."

He heard it. Not mockery, just precision. "Yes."

She looked at the fire. "My father has been contracting supply runs for the army for eleven years. He started when I was seven. I grew up on these roads." She said it plainly, without apology or pride, the way Mercier talked about things he'd learned, as simple fact. "I know the eastern road from Frankfurt to Posen in three seasons. I don't know it in deep winter, not east of the Oder, but I'll learn it."

"When does deep winter start?"

"Six weeks. Maybe four."

Charles did the arithmetic. The army would push toward Warsaw before then. The supply lines would extend further. The convoys would run in worse conditions than this.

"The irregulars will be more active when the temperature drops," he said. "They'll need the supplies themselves. Two outriders isn't sufficient."

"I know. I've been requesting three since August." She looked at him. "The army allocates what it allocates."

He recognized the observation. He'd made the same one about different problems. He didn't say so.

She finished her coffee and held out her hand for his cup, which he gave her. "Good night, Lieutenant," she said. "We're moving at first light."

"I know."

She looked at him for a moment, that pause again, like she was finishing a count. Then she turned and walked back toward the wagon square, her boots crunching on the frost.

* * *

The second day was harder. The road worsened east of the second creek crossing, the drainage ditch on the south side having overflowed and frozen in ridges across the road surface that the wagon wheels caught badly. They lost an hour mid-morning to a wheel replacement on the seventh wagon, the old wheel having cracked on a frozen rut, and another half-hour in the afternoon to a section of road so soft they had to unload two wagons, move them through light, and reload on the far side.

Charles worked the problem both times. He didn't make himself the center of it. Rousseau handled the wheel replacement, the Girard cousins handled the unloading. But he assessed, directed, kept moving. At the unloading point he worked alongside Margot's men for the better part of an hour, moving sacks of flour from the wagon bed to the roadside and back, because there were not enough hands and standing around watching was not a useful contribution.

When it was done he was cold and his back ached and there was flour on his coat.

Margot was checking the reloaded wagon. She glanced at his coat.

"You didn't have to do that," she said.

"Faster with another pair of hands."

"Yes." She checked the securing rope on the wagon cover. "Your men follow you well. The younger one. Bonnard. He works hard."

"He's having a difficult week."

She looked at him. A question she didn't ask.

"A friend of mine died nine days ago," Charles said. "Bonnard was in his section. He's looking for something to put his hands on." He paused. He hadn't meant to say that. "The work helps."

She held his gaze. "I'm sorry about your friend."

"Thank you."

She went back to the wagon cover, found the rope correctly tensioned, moved on. He watched her go and thought, with the odd clarity that cold and physical work sometimes produced, that she was the first person who had said that simply, without adding anything to it. Everyone else had surrounded it with other words, with what came next, with duty and schedule and the shape of the days ahead. She had just said it, and moved on, which was somehow exactly right.

* * *

The afternoon brought a thin snow, not heavy but persistent, and the road became worse. They made camp two kilometers short of where Charles had planned, at a crossroads with a stone shrine and enough flat ground for the wagons. Not ideal. Adequate.

He was posting the watch rotation when Margot appeared at his elbow.

"The road south at this crossroads," she said. "It runs eight kilometers to a village called Hreben. There's an inn there. Dry shelter, a proper stable for the animals."

"Eight kilometers south is eight kilometers off the route."

"It's also warm and dry, which the horses need more than we need to be sixteen kilometers along by tomorrow morning instead of fourteen." She said it matter-of-factly. "The animals are working in conditions they weren't bred for. They need to be out of the cold tonight."

Charles looked at the horses. He'd been thinking about the route. He hadn't been thinking about the horses.

"I'll look at the south road," he said.

He rode it himself, in the dark, with Rousseau. It was frozen solid. Better footing than the main road had been all day. The village was real, the inn was real, the stable was large enough for the draft horses. They were back at the crossroads in forty minutes.

"We go south," he told Margot.

"I know," she said. "I sent Lefebvre ahead an hour ago to arrange it."

He looked at her.

"You would decide yes," she said, without apology. "So I sent Lefebvre while you were deciding."

"You were certain."

"I've made this run eight times. I know when the animals need shelter and when they can camp." She held his gaze. "If I'd been wrong, I'd have recalled Lefebvre."

He thought about this. She hadn't waited for his authority because she'd already made the assessment and the outcome was clear to her and the delay of waiting for a lieutenant to confirm what she already knew would have cost an hour she didn't want to spend.

He knew that logic. He used it himself, occasionally, when Beaumont was available but the decision was obvious and the delay would cost something. The difference was that she was eighteen and running a supply convoy and he was the one with the commission.

"Next time," he said, "tell me before you send the man."

She nodded. Not an apology. An acknowledgment. The distinction was clear in the way she did it.

"Agreed," she said.

* * *

The inn at Hreben was run by a Prussian couple who had made a pragmatic peace with the French occupation and now fed soldiers with the same flat efficiency with which they'd fed travelers before the war. The stable was warm and dry and smelled of hay and old timber, and Charles made sure the horses were settled before he went inside.

The inn's main room had a long table and a fireplace and Margot's eleven men and his own six filling most of the available space. He found a seat at the end of the table, ate what was put in front of him, and listened to the room.

Bonnard was talking. This was new. Bonnard had been quiet for nine days, since the junction, since Philippe. He was talking to one of Margot's drivers, something about horses, and the driver was answering with the enthusiasm of a man who would rather talk about horses than most things. Bonnard's face had a quality Charles hadn't seen in it for a week. Not happy. Not that. But present.

Margot was sitting three places down, eating, reading from a small book she'd propped against her cup. Charles watched her for a moment. He was doing it again. The watching. He was aware of it now.

"What are you reading?" he said.

She looked up. A beat. "A history of the Hanseatic League," she said.

"Why?"

"Because the trade routes they established are still the routes we use. Understanding why they made the choices they made helps me understand the roads." She looked at him. "Why does that surprise you?"

"It doesn't," he said, which was only partially true.

She closed the book against her palm. "What do you read?"

"Tactical histories. Cavalry engagements, mostly. De Brack. Lasalle's methods. Older material when I can find it: Marlborough's campaigns, the Dutch wars."

"So you study the same thing I study. How to move things from one place to another most effectively."

He thought about that. "What I study is how to destroy things most effectively."

"Is it different?"

He picked up his cup. "Yes," he said. "Entirely."

She looked at him for a long moment, not challenging, thinking. "My convoy moves food and medicine. Without it the army stops. Without the army stopping things, the supply lines don't exist. Without the supply lines, there's no convoy." She tilted her head slightly. "We're part of the same machine."

"Different parts," Charles said.

"Of course. But when your part breaks, people die fighting. When my part breaks, people die not fighting." She said it without drama, the same flat accuracy with which she said everything. "I used to think supply work was the lesser work. The background. But eleven runs have convinced me otherwise."

He looked at her. "What changed your mind?"

"The bridge at Glogau," she said. "Last August. The army needed to cross before the Prussians destroyed it. The crossing worked. But the reason the army could hold the crossing point for the three days it took, the reason those men ate and kept their ammunition, was two convoys that reached them on time." She paused. "I was the second convoy. Six hours to spare."

She said it the way Mercier talked about things he'd done: not modestly, not immodestly. As fact. Charles filed it.

"Six hours is a narrow margin," he said.

"The road was worse than expected. A ford had shifted." She shrugged, one shoulder. "You plan for the road you know and adjust for the road you find."

He recognized that too. He had said essentially the same thing to Mercier about a different kind of road. He didn't say so.

"The bridge at Glogau," he said. "I know the action. Two squadrons of the 5th Hussars held that crossing point."

"Yes." She looked at him. "Were you there?"

"No. I read the report."

She nodded. Picked up her book. "Then we've both studied it."

* * *

The third day was clear, the snow having stopped overnight and left the road hard and clean and better footing than anything they'd had since Frankfurt. They made good time, better than Charles had projected, and reached the forward supply point at Posen two hours before dark.

The handoff was efficient. Margot's men had done it before, the supply point officers had a routine for receiving the convoy, and the whole process took ninety minutes from arrival to the wagons stripped and logged and signed over. Charles watched it the way he'd watched the rest of the convoy's operations: with the specific attention of someone cataloguing what worked and why.

Margot was at the depot office with the supply point's administrator, going through the manifest, when Charles found her. She finished the last page, received the receipt, folded it into her coat. She looked up.

"We're done," she said.

"You're returning to Frankfurt?"

"Tomorrow morning. The road is better going west than it was coming east." She paused. "Your section is at the garrison north of Posen?"

"Yes."

She held out her hand. "Thank you for the escort, Lieutenant. Your men were useful."

He shook it. Her grip was the same as the first morning. Direct. The worn thumb of the glove. He had noticed that already. He noticed it again.

"The two additional wagons," he said. "The smaller ones at the back of the column. They weren't on the manifest."

She looked at him.

"I noticed them the first morning," he said. "I didn't press it. They came through with the convoy without incident, which is the outcome that mattered. But I noticed."

She held his gaze. A pause. Not the counting pause, something else, something that might have been consideration.

"Medical supplies," she said. "A surgeon at a forward post south of Posen requisitioned them privately. The army's allocation process takes six weeks. He needed them in ten days." She did not break eye contact. "The paperwork follows the supplies, Lieutenant. It doesn't always lead them."

Charles thought about the Küstrin depot in November, the supply sergeant who'd decided the resupply schedule wasn't his problem until Charles had made it his problem with dates and regulations. He thought about what Mercier had told him about the difference between the army working correctly and the army working.

"The paperwork follows the supplies," he said.

"Yes."

He let it go. It was the right call. Not because the paperwork didn't matter, but because a surgeon's requisition moving faster than the official channel was the machine self-correcting around a broken part, and that was usually the right outcome.

"Safe road back," he said.

"And you." She looked at him a moment longer. The pause again, finishing something. "You watched my operation for three days as if you were trying to figure out what category it belonged to."

He didn't answer immediately.

"Did you decide?" she said.

"Not yet," he said.

She smiled. It was brief and genuine and he hadn't seen it before. She smiled less than Philippe had, which meant it meant more when she did. "Come find me if you do," she said. "I'll be on the Frankfurt road most of the winter."

Then she turned and walked back toward her wagons, and he watched her go, and he did not have a useful thought about what had just happened.

* * *

He rode back to the garrison north of Posen with his six men in the late afternoon, the sun dropping behind the bare poplars on the western horizon. Bonnard rode beside him for a stretch without speaking, which was a new thing. Bonnard had been quiet since the junction but the quality of the quiet had changed somewhere in the last three days. Less submerged. More simply quiet.

"The convoy woman," Bonnard said, after a while. Not a question.

"What about her."

Bonnard was quiet for a moment, looking at the road. "She reminded me of you. The way she ran things."

Charles said nothing.

"Like she was always three steps ahead, not cold about it." Bonnard considered the road. "Like she cared about getting it right because the wrong answer cost something real, not because she wanted to be seen getting it right."

Charles looked at the horizon. He didn't say anything.

"Anyway," Bonnard said, and fell back.

* * *

That night Charles wrote in his journal. Not the lists. The journal proper, which he used rarely, the running record he'd been keeping since Günzburg. He wrote about the three days on the eastern road in the practical way he wrote about most things: the conditions, the distances, the decisions. The wheel at the first morning. The unloading at the frozen rut. The south road to Hreben.

He wrote: *convoy well-managed. Vieillard, M. Eighteen, contractor's outfit, Frankfurt. Eight runs. Knows the road. Assessed correctly at every decision point. The additional wagons: medical supplies to forward post, paperwork to follow. Right call.*

He looked at what he'd written.

He wrote: *she said we study the same thing. How to move things from one place to another. I said no, I study how to destroy things. Both true.*

He closed the journal.

He thought about what she'd said at the inn: *when my part breaks, people die not fighting.* The same weight on both sides, counted differently. He'd spent a year learning to think about the weight on his side. He hadn't thought about the accounting on hers.

The plan was still the plan. Captain and home. Five years from the start, which was now three and a half years from now, minus whatever the winter brought, minus whatever came east of Posen when the army moved on Warsaw. The numbers were the same.

Something in the calculation was moving again. Not the numbers. The thing underneath them.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck. The gelding was stalled for the night, close enough to touch, and the horse turned his head without lifting it, the slow acknowledgment of a familiar hand.

He thought about the worn thumb of her glove. He thought about *come find me if you do.* He thought about the way she had said *I'm sorry about your friend* and then moved on, which had been exactly right.

He did not write any of this down.

He lay on his bedroll with his boots on, hand on the left one, the paper still there. Bordeaux, Élise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card.

Three and a half years.

He closed his eyes.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 8

December came in hard and stayed.

The army was east of Warsaw by the second week of the month, the Grande Armee spread across a hundred kilometers of Polish countryside that did not want it there and was not equipped to say so. The roads were not roads in any French sense of the word. They were mud that had frozen into ridges and then thawed and then frozen again, rutted by the passage of two armies moving in opposite directions, and the horses picked their way across them with the careful indignation of animals who had not agreed to this.

The Prussians were still in the field. Not in strength, not anymore, but in pieces, the remnants of an army trying to reform behind the Vistula while Cossack units from the east moved in the opposite direction, arriving to support an alliance that was still being negotiated by people who did not have to ride in the weather. Between the Prussian rear guards and the Russian advance elements there was a zone of ambiguity that was not safe to be in without attention.

Major Beaumont called his officers together on a gray Tuesday morning outside a farmhouse whose owner had left three weeks ago and not returned.

"The squadron takes the eastern screening line from here to here." He pointed on the map. "Sixty kilometers of front, which is too much for our numbers and is what we have. I am splitting us into three operational sections. Fontaine, you have the north. Leblanc, you have the center. I will take the south."

Captain Fontaine looked at the map with the expression of a man calculating whether his portion of it was better or worse than the other portions. Fontaine was two years senior to Charles, Lyons family, had purchased his rank before the wars made rank something you earned, and had since spent considerable effort demonstrating that he was not merely purchased. He was good enough to know that Charles had not purchased anything.

"The center section is the longest stretch," Fontaine said. It was not clear whether he was objecting or noting.

"Yes," Beaumont said. "It is also the most likely approach route for Cossack patrols, which is why I am giving it to Leblanc."

Fontaine looked at Charles with an expression Charles had learned to read as being filed away for later.

"Questions?" Beaumont said.

There were none.

"Move out at first light. Report by rider twice daily. Any contact with Russian or Prussian forces, fall back and report. We are screening, not engaging. Clear?"

"Yes, sir," both officers said.

Beaumont folded the map. "Fontaine, a moment. Leblanc, you are dismissed."

Charles walked out of the farmhouse into the cold. He had twelve kilometers to cover before dark to reach the center section's start point, and he had eighteen men now instead of twelve, because Bonnard's section had been formally absorbed into his command the week before. Not a merger on paper, not yet. In practice, he had thirty men looking at him for direction when they were all in the same place, which was most of the time now.

He found Sergeant Mercier and Bonnard outside with the horses.

"We are the center section," Charles said. "Sixty kilometers east to west, we cover the middle twenty. We move at first light. I want the men rested and the horses checked tonight."

"Yes, sir," Bonnard said. He had filled out slightly in the weeks since the junction, the look of a man finding the new shape of things. He ran his section the way he'd watched Philippe run it, which was to say: the way Philippe had learned it from Charles, which meant there was an odd recursive quality to watching Bonnard work that Charles had noted and not examined closely.

Mercier said nothing. He was looking at the horse line with the expression of a man who had opinions about twelve additional horses that had been added to his responsibility without anyone asking.

"Mercier," Charles said.

"Sir."

"The additional horses."

"Seven of them need new shoes before they go another fifty kilometers on these roads. Two of them should not be on these roads regardless of their shoes."

"Which two?"

Mercier pointed without speaking. Charles looked at both horses, assessed, agreed.

"Pull them from the line. Put them with the supply train." He thought about it. "The shoeing, we do tonight or we lose tomorrow."

"I know a farrier in the village three kilometers west. He was still there this morning."

"Then go find him."

Mercier was already moving.

* * *

The center screening section in the second week of December was not dramatic work. It was cold work, which was worse in many respects because discomfort was more wearing than danger. They rode the line in rotating pairs, four hours on and eight off, the ground white and silent and offering nothing except the occasional track in the snow that might have been a Cossack patrol or might have been deer. Charles sent riders to identify twice and got deer both times, which meant either the Cossacks were not yet operating in his sector or they were operating too carefully to leave tracks he could read.

He wrote in his field journal every evening. Not the lists in the back, the working notes in the front: road conditions, track sightings, the state of the horses, the men who were showing early signs of cold sickness. He had seven men with various degrees of it by the second week, none of them serious, all of them functional. The farmhouse they were using as a base had a fireplace large enough to heat the main room to something approximating warmth, and he rotated men through it in shifts so everyone got time off the horses.

Bonnard ran his section's rotation without being told how. This was the right outcome and Charles was aware of it without dwelling on it.

He was at the horse line one evening, late, running Tonnerre through the inspection by feel in the dark, when he heard hooves on the frozen road. He had a hand on his pistol before he recognized the sound as a single horse, trotting, which was courier pace rather than pursuit pace.

The rider was from Beaumont. The message was short: a convoy moving east on the main road, supply run to the forward depot, needed screening cover for the final fifteen kilometers. Could Charles provide two riders for tomorrow morning?

He read it twice.

He provided two riders. He did not think about who might be running the convoy. He went back to Tonnerre.

* * *

The convoy was not Margot Vieillard's.

He was not sure why he had thought it might be. The roads east of Warsaw in December were full of supply convoys; she had said she ran the Frankfurt to Posen route, which was further west. He was further east. The geometry did not work.

The convoy that came through in the morning was run by an older man, Galland, a contractor's agent from Paris who had been doing this work since before Charles was born and regarded the war as a feature of the landscape, like weather, to be managed rather than considered. He thanked the two riders, moved his convoy through the screening zone in four hours, and continued east without particular comment.

Charles was watching the last wagon clear the sector boundary when Rousseau appeared beside him.

"Not who you expected?" Rousseau said.

Charles looked at him.

"You went to see the convoy yourself," Rousseau said. "You do not usually check a routine screening detail personally."

"The road conditions were uncertain."

"Yes, sir." Rousseau's tone was exactly neutral, which was itself a form of comment.

Charles went back to the farmhouse and wrote in his field journal: *convoy, Galland. 14 wagons flour, medicine. No contact. Road conditions adequate.*

He did not write anything else.

* * *

The message from Major Beaumont came on the eighteenth of December, delivered by a staff rider who arrived at midday and ate Sergeant Mercier's rations without being invited.

*Report to my position tomorrow. Bring Bonnard.*

Charles read it twice, then handed it to Mercier, who read it once and handed it back.

"Any particular reason to bring Bonnard?" Mercier said.

"Not one that is written in the message."

"No." Mercier looked at the road east, where the courier had come from. "He is going to give you something."

"He is going to give us something," Charles said.

"He is going to give you something," Mercier said again, with the deliberate repetition that meant he was not going to argue about it further and did not need to.

* * *

Major Beaumont's position was a commandeered manor house twelve kilometers southeast, large enough to serve as a makeshift headquarters for the squadron and two attached artillery units. Charles arrived with Bonnard at mid-morning and found Beaumont at the map table with a senior staff officer he did not recognize.

"Leblanc. Good." Beaumont looked at Bonnard. "Corporal Bonnard."

"Yes, sir."

"As of today you are promoted to Sergeant. Confirmed." He handed Bonnard a folded document without ceremony. "You will formally command the second section under Lieutenant Leblanc's oversight. Any questions?"

Bonnard looked at the document. "No, sir."

"Good. Wait outside."

Bonnard went. Charles stood at the map table and waited.

Beaumont looked at the staff officer, who excused himself. They were alone.

"Sit down," Beaumont said.

Charles sat. This was unusual. Beaumont did not typically invite sitting.

"The squadron is going to be restructured in January," Beaumont said. "The corps is getting reinforcements from Spain and a new cavalry formation is being created from the existing units. This squadron will be the core of a new regiment. Three times the men, three times the complexity." He was looking at the map, not at Charles. "I need officers who can run sections without supervision. Not check in with supervision. Actually run them."

Charles waited.

"You have been doing that since November," Beaumont said. "Since before November, in fact. You have been running Philippe's section operationally since August, before it was made official." He picked up a pen and set it down. "I am giving you two sections formally, which you already have informally. In the spring restructure there will be a formal recommendation for promotion. Captain, if the review goes as I expect."

Charles heard it: Captain. The next step in the plan. The right outcome.

"Thank you, sir," he said.

"Do not thank me. It is the correct allocation of a useful resource." Beaumont looked at him now. "You know what comes with it."

"More responsibility."

"More permanence." Beaumont's voice was level, stating a fact. "A captaincy in this army, in these conditions, in the spring of 1807, is not a short commission. It is not a rank you hold for a year and then take your leave. The army will need you in that role and will keep you in it." He paused. "I want to make sure you understand that before the recommendation goes forward."

The fire in the hearth behind Beaumont made a sound. The manor house had been abandoned in some haste; there were still family portraits on the walls, a woman in eighteenth-century dress looking out of a gold frame at the map table with an expression of mild displeasure.

"I understand," Charles said.

Beaumont looked at him for a moment longer. "Your plan," he said, "was five years."

"Yes, sir."

"How many years have you been in now?"

Charles did not need to calculate. "Two and a half."

"Two and a half." Beaumont picked up the pen again. "The recommendation goes forward in January. Think between now and then about what you are agreeing to. That is all."

He went back to the map.

Charles stood, recognized the dismissal, and walked out of the manor house into the December cold.

Bonnard was standing with the horses. He had the promotion document folded into his coat pocket and the look of a man who has received something he was not certain he had earned.

"Congratulations, Sergeant," Charles said.

Bonnard straightened slightly. "Thank you, sir." He paused. "And you, sir? What did he want?"

"He wanted to discuss the spring restructure."

Bonnard nodded. He was too good a sergeant to ask follow-up questions about things that were not his business.

They rode back to the screening position. The cold was very clear. The road was frozen solid, which was at least consistent.

Charles thought about Beaumont's question. *How many years have you been in now?*

Two and a half. Two and a half years from October 1805 put him in April of 1808 at the five-year mark. If the captaincy came in the spring and the army's need for him in that role extended the timeline, the five-year mark became a reference point rather than a finish line.

*Not Captain and one more thing and then home*, Mercier had said, in October, on the road back from the Italian campaign.

He had remembered it. He was still remembering it. The memory was doing less work than it used to.

* * *

He wrote to Elise on Christmas Eve, sitting in the farmhouse with the fire low and most of the men asleep.

He had been composing the letter in his head for six weeks, since the junction at Küstrin, since Philippe. Every time he started it the words arranged themselves into something formal and procedural, the shape of an official report, and he would set the pen down and go back to whatever needed doing.

Tonight he wrote it without stopping.

*Dear Elise,*

*Philippe is dead. I should have written this six weeks ago and could not find the words and am not certain I have found them now but the not-writing is worse than the writing.*

*He died on the seventeenth of November at a road junction east of Küstrin. The wound was in the chest. The surgeon was there in six minutes. I counted it afterward, in the journal. He was conscious until the late afternoon. He spoke twice during that time. One time he asked me about his horse, Renard, whether someone would check him properly. I told him I would see to it. He believed me.*

*The second time he said: go home before it gets comfortable. He had said it the night before, at the picket line, and he said it again. I thought it was the wound and the fever and the surgery making him repeat himself. I am less sure of that now.*

*He said you wrote to him. He told me what you wrote: that competence wasn't care. He was right that you were right. I am not certain what to do with it.*

*I am promoted to Lieutenant. I have two sections of men now. Major Beaumont is recommending me for Captain in the spring. The plan is on schedule.*

*That last sentence is true and also somehow beside the point, which is a thing that has not happened before. I do not know what to do with that either. I am telling you because you will understand it better than I do.*

*Your brother,*
*Charles*

He sealed it before he could read it again. He put it with the outgoing mail.

Then he sat with the fire for a while, not thinking about anything particular. Outside, the snow had started again, quiet and even, covering the tracks in the road.

He had said *the plan is on schedule* and it was true. It had been true every time he had said it since Munich, since the Alps, since the Tagliamento, since Philippe. It would still be true in the morning.

He was not sure why it felt different than it used to. He turned that over for a while and could not find the edge of it, and eventually he went to his bedroll and put his hand on his boot and lay in the dark, listening to the snow.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 9

January was the coldest month Charles had yet experienced, which was saying something given the November at Küstrin and the December on the screening line. The cold in Poland in January was geological. It came from the ground and the sky simultaneously and the space between them offered no relief.

The army was in winter quarters, the push east suspended until conditions allowed movement. The suspension was necessary and was also a specific kind of difficult, because an army in winter quarters was an army with time, and time produced problems of a different type than combat: boredom, illness, discipline, the slow erosion of a man's sense of purpose when there was no immediate task in front of him.

Charles ran training. He did it every morning, two hours before the midday meal, with both sections together. Horse inspections, the inspection sequence Mercier had drilled into him in Munich, then weapons maintenance, then mounted movement practice in whatever open ground was not frozen to an impassable depth. It was not enough to fill the days. It kept the men functional.

Sergeant Bonnard ran the administrative work. He had taken to it with the focused attention of a man who understood that administrative competence was a different kind of skill from fighting competence and was determined to acquire it. He checked things twice, which he had always done; he now also understood why he was checking them and what the results connected to, which was the part that mattered.

Sergeant Mercier watched all of this with the expression of a man keeping his opinions to himself.

"Say it," Charles told him one afternoon, after the training had ended and the men were dispersing.

"I wasn't saying anything, sir."

"You were having thoughts."

Mercier considered this. "I was thinking that you have built something here that functions without you. Both sections. The systems run. The men know what to do. Bonnard knows what he is doing." He paused. "I was thinking about what that means."

"It means we are prepared for the spring campaign."

"It means," Mercier said carefully, "that Major Beaumont's case for your promotion is well-supported by the evidence. And that the army will notice that. And that the army finding useful things tends to want to keep them." He went back to his horse.

Charles watched him go.

The army finding useful things tends to want to keep them.

He had been in the army for two years and four months. He had seventeen months left on the five-year plan, which meant the plan was more than half over and the finish line was closer than the start. Captain, then home. The plan was specific.

He noticed that the plan felt more like a sentence than a destination. He noticed that and did not examine it.

* * *

The town was called Lowicz, which nobody in the French army could pronounce correctly, and it had three things of note: a Benedictine abbey that the army was using as a hospital, a market square large enough to drill a section in, and a supply depot that received convoys twice a week from the west.

He did not think about the convoys. He noticed the depot schedule in the course of understanding the logistics of his quarter of the town and filed the information without attaching anything to it.

On the sixth of January he was crossing the market square on his way from the depot offices to the billet when he heard a voice giving direction to a wagon driver. He knew the tone before he heard the words: not shouting, not asking. The specific register of someone who has already assessed the situation and is distributing the results of that assessment to the people who need to act on it.

He stopped.

Margot Vieillard was standing at the back of a wagon on the north side of the square, a manifest in one hand, directing a driver who was trying to position his vehicle against a warehouse door. She had her coat collar up against the cold and her breath was visible and she was checking the manifest against what was coming out of the wagon with the automatic accuracy of someone who knows what number she is expecting and verifies it without looking like she is verifying it.

She looked up when he was still thirty meters away. He had not made a particular sound. She simply looked up, which was the thing she did.

"Lieutenant Leblanc," she said. Not surprised. Filing him.

"Vieillard." He stopped a reasonable distance from the wagon, not wanting to be in the way of the unloading. "I didn't know your run came this far east."

"It didn't, until November. The route extended." She looked back at the manifest, checked something, made a mark. "You're quartered here?"

"My sections, yes. East quarter, near the abbey."

"How long?"

"Until the weather breaks. March, probably. Maybe February if we're fortunate."

She nodded. She handed the manifest to a man beside her who had the look of her second in charge, said something to him in a low voice, and walked toward Charles. She moved across the cobblestones with the particular efficiency of someone who did not waste steps: the same quality she'd had on the convoy road, no gesture that wasn't purposeful.

"I have about an hour before the next wagon needs checking," she said. "Is there somewhere warm in this town that serves something approximating food?"

"There is an inn on the south side. The food is what it is."

"Good enough," she said. "Buy me something hot to drink and I'll tell you why the eastern route is worse than anything you've ridden this year."

* * *

The inn was not crowded at midday. They took a table near the fire and a woman brought two cups of something that was not quite coffee and not quite tea and was very hot, which was the relevant quality.

Margot pulled off her gloves, the right one first and then the left, and wrapped both hands around the cup. Charles noted the worn thumb of the left glove, the same one, which meant she had not replaced them in the two months since the Posen run. This told him something about how she spent money and where her priorities sat, which was information he had not intended to acquire and had acquired anyway.

"The eastern route," he said.

"Four convoys since November," she said. "Frankfurt to Lowicz, which is a hundred and sixty kilometers longer than Frankfurt to Posen. The roads east of the Vistula are worse than anything I have dealt with. I lost a wagon at a river crossing in December, the ford was marked as reliable and was not. The load was flour. We recovered about a third of it."

"Were you hurt?"

She looked at him. "No. My man Lefebvre wrenched his shoulder getting the wagon out."

"Good. That he wasn't more seriously hurt."

"Yes." She drank. "The escort situation is better than it was. I have three riders now instead of two."

He remembered telling her two wasn't sufficient. She had said she'd been requesting three since August. He did not say either thing.

"The depot here," he said. "Who do you deal with?"

"A Lieutenant Arnaud." She paused. "Different Arnaud. He is thorough but slow. The paperwork takes twice what it should."

"I know him. I can speak to him about the turnaround time."

She looked at him over her cup. "You don't have to."

"The depot efficiency affects my sections' supply schedule. It's a reasonable thing for me to address."

She held his gaze for a moment. There was something in it that was not quite amusement and not quite exasperation.

"You always do that," she said.

"Do what."

"Find a practical reason for the thing you were going to do anyway." She set down her cup. "You were going to offer to speak to him because it would help me. You found the tactical justification afterward."

Charles thought about this. It was accurate. He was not sure how she had identified it when he himself had not.

"Possibly," he said.

"You did it on the convoy too. You worked the unloading with my men because there weren't enough hands and it was faster. That was true. But you also just did it because the work was there and you were there."

He did not say anything.

"I'm not criticizing it," she said. "I do the same thing. I always find the practical reason." She looked at the fire. "My father says it's because we are both uncomfortable with the idea of doing something for no reason except that we want to."

"Your father knows me."

"He knows the type." She picked up her cup again. "He's been working with military officers for eleven years. He says most of them fall into two categories: the ones who need to be the reason for everything, and the ones who find reasons for things that need doing." She looked at him. "You are the second kind. So am I."

Charles considered that. "And which kind is more useful?"

"The second. Obviously." She said it plainly. "The first kind get men killed being the center of things. The second kind get things done."

The fire made a sound. Outside, someone was crossing the square with a cart, the wheels loud on the frozen cobblestones.

"How is the man from the Posen run?" she said. "Bonnard."

He looked at her. She remembered Bonnard's name, two months later, from a passing conversation in the snow.

"He is promoted to Sergeant," Charles said. "He runs his section well."

"Good." She nodded, genuinely. "He was looking for something solid to stand on. I could see it."

"Yes."

"Did you know that when you brought him on the escort?"

"I suspected it. I thought the work would help."

"It did," she said. "Or you did. Or both." She finished her coffee. "My father's other observation is that the second type of officer, the ones who get things done, are the ones the army has the hardest time releasing. Because they are too useful."

Mercier had said the same thing, in different words, in October on the road from Udine, and again in December outside the farmhouse on the screening line. It was beginning to feel like the thing everyone around him was saying, in rotation, as though they had agreed on it separately and were delivering the same message in different containers.

"I know," he said.

She looked at him. "Do you?"

"Beaumont has recommended me for Captain in the spring." He had not intended to say it. He had not told anyone except Mercier, who had extracted it through observation rather than being told. He said it now without understanding quite why.

She was quiet for a moment. "And after Captain?"

"The plan was five years and then home." He heard the past tense as it came out. He had not used the past tense before. "The plan is five years."

She looked at him steadily. "Is it still the plan?"

Charles picked up his cup. It was empty. He set it back down.

"Yes," he said. It was the honest answer. The plan had not changed. The plan was intact and he was on schedule. He believed this.

"You don't sound certain," she said.

"I am certain about the plan. I am less certain that the plan is sufficient."

She was quiet.

"A plan," he said, slowly, "is a set of numbers. This many years. That rank. Then home. The numbers are the same as they were when I started. But something in the territory around the numbers is different than I expected it to be."

He had not said this to anyone. He was not sure he had said it to himself clearly until this moment.

Margot was looking at the fire. "I had a plan," she said. "When my father was ill in May and I took the first run. The plan was: do this once, come home, let him recover, go back to normal." She paused. "By the second run, normal had shifted. After the fourth run, I wasn't sure what I was going back to."

"You kept going."

"I kept going because the work was real and I was good at it and the men needed the supplies." She looked at him. "The plan was fine. It just turned out the work was a different thing than I thought it was going to be."

He understood that. He had understood the same thing in an Italian village in October, watching a woman draw water from a well. Being good at this is not uncomplicated.

"The plan still matters," he said.

"Yes," she said. "It does." She pulled her gloves back on, right hand first. "But you should probably be honest with yourself about what you are deciding when you decide to accept the promotion."

He looked at her.

"You are not just accepting a rank," she said. "You are accepting what comes with it. And from what you have described, Major Beaumont has been clear about what comes with it." She stood and picked up her manifest from the table. "That is not a criticism. It might be the right decision. I don't know your family or your home or what you are going back to." She looked at him steadily. "But decide it. Don't let it be decided for you by the accumulation of small yeses."

She pulled her collar up. "Thank you for the coffee. I'll be here through Thursday."

She went out into the cold and he watched the door close behind her, and he sat with the empty cups for a while, not thinking about anything in particular and thinking about several things at once.

* * *

He did not sleep well that night.

Not because of anything dramatic. The billet was warm enough, the men were quiet, the horses were settled. He lay on his bedroll with his hand on his boot and the paper there, Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting, and he thought about what Margot had said.

*Don't let it be decided for you by the accumulation of small yeses.*

He had said yes when Beaumont gave him the second section. He had said yes in December when Beaumont asked if he understood what the captaincy would mean. He had said yes to the escort duty in November, yes to the screening line in December, yes to the extended training schedule in January.

Each yes was the correct answer in the moment it was asked. Each yes was what the situation required of the person who understood it clearly. He was the person who understood it clearly. So he had said yes.

The accumulation of those yeses had produced something he had not quite looked at directly until a woman at an inn in Lowicz had named it for him.

He lay with the paper under his hand and thought about his father's instruction: *five years. Make Captain and come home.* He thought about Elise's letter to Philippe: *competence isn't care.* He thought about Mercier, twice: *the army doesn't release useful things.* He thought about Philippe himself, at the picket line in November, adjusting his collar: *go home before it gets comfortable.*

It was not yet comfortable. He was not sure it was ever going to be comfortable, which might have been the point. He was not sure if the absence of comfort was a warning or a justification.

He did not know the answer to this. He turned it over for a long time and arrived back where he started, which was not resolution but at least a clearer view of the question.

He fell asleep at some point. In the morning he woke up and checked his sections and ran training and was good at it, which was the one thing that had not changed.

* * *

He wrote to Beaumont that evening.

The letter was short.

*Major Beaumont: I accept the recommendation for promotion, with the understanding you have described regarding the nature of the commitment. I am asking that the recommendation note specifically that my intention is to complete my service at the five-year mark and request discharge accordingly. I recognize this may not be honored. I am asking that it be noted.*

He read it twice. He sent it.

Then he went to check the horses, because the horses needed checking and it was something concrete to do with his hands while the rest of it settled.

Mercier was at the far end of the line, doing the same thing.

"I sent the acceptance," Charles said.

Mercier nodded. He moved to the next horse. Left foreleg. "Did you put conditions on it?"

"I asked for the five-year mark to be noted in the recommendation."

"That is not conditions," Mercier said. "That is a request."

"I know."

Mercier moved to the right foreleg. He said nothing else.

Charles stayed at the line for a while, hand on Tonnerre's neck, the gelding's breath visible in the cold. The abbey bells rang eight. The town was quiet. Somewhere to the east, past the frozen fields and the Vistula and the things that lived in the space between the armies, the spring was still two months away.

He was still on the plan.

He was on the plan.

CHAPTER 10

The battle of Eylau began in snow and ended in snow and in between was something Charles could not find adequate categories for, even afterward, even with the journal open and the pen in his hand.

He had been at the Tagliamento. He had been at the bridge at Günzburg. He had ridden through the gap at the Austrian second line with his saber out and his section behind him and come through it intact, and he had thought, afterward, that he understood what large-scale combat was.

He had not understood.

Eylau was the eighth of February, 1807, a Thursday, which seemed an irrelevant detail and was somehow the one he kept returning to. The army had been maneuvering for a week in temperatures that dropped at night to the point where sentries had to be relieved every hour to prevent frostbite. The Russian army was at Eylau. The French army went to meet it. The mathematics were approximately equal, which was not a situation Napoleon preferred and which produced, in Charles's experience, the worst kind of battle: the kind where the outcome required everyone to pay the full price.

The night before, Major Beaumont assembled the officers in a barn that was warmer than the open ground by perhaps four degrees.

"Tomorrow will be difficult," Beaumont said. He said it the way he said most things: as a fact, without apology. "The ground is frozen, which means the artillery will be effective in ways it is not in softer conditions. The Russian line is well-positioned and they are not short of guns. Our job is to be available where we are needed and to respond quickly when the order comes." He looked at each officer in turn. "Keep your men tight. Do not disperse for any reason until you hear from me. Questions?"

There were none.

"Get some sleep," Beaumont said. "Both of you."

Charles went back to his section's position and sat against a wagon wheel with his coat pulled tight and looked at the sky, which was clear and very cold and full of stars. He could see his breath. He could hear, from somewhere north of them, the sound of the Russian army settling in for the night, which sounded remarkably similar to the sound of the French army doing the same.

He thought about writing a letter. He thought about Tonnerre's shoe, which Mercier had checked twice that evening. He thought about the paper in his boot, Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting.

He thought about Margot Vieillard saying *decide it, don't let it be decided for you*, three weeks ago in Lowicz, and about Beaumont's response to his letter, which had been a single line: *noted. Recommendation forwarded.*

He did not think about the morning.

At some point the cold woke him and he realized he had been asleep, which had not been his intention but was probably his body's correct decision. He found Mercier awake at the fire, which had been reduced to coals.

"Go back to sleep," Mercier said.

"I was asleep."

"Then go back."

He did not go back. He sat with Mercier at the coals until first light, neither of them speaking. This was not uncomfortable. It was the particular silence of two people who have been in enough bad situations together to not need to fill the space before a new one.

* * *

The battle started with artillery.

It always started with artillery. But Eylau's artillery was a different order of magnitude than anything Charles had been near, and the opening exchanges, both sides simultaneously, produced a sound he felt in his sternum and could not locate directionally. It came from everywhere. The ground carried it. The air carried it. Tonnerre stood with his ears flat and his muscles rigid under Charles's hand, and Charles kept his voice low and his hand steady and thought about nothing except the horse.

The French infantry went forward in the snow at half past seven.

Charles watched it from his position on the left flank, which gave him a sight line across the main engagement without being in it. The infantry moved in columns through snow that was ankle-deep in the good places and knee-deep in the drifted sections, and the Russian guns opened at four hundred meters and the columns kept moving, and he could see the gaps appearing in the French lines but could not, at this distance, see the individual men who had been in those gaps a moment before.

He counted his section. He counted again.

At eight-thirty the French left flank collapsed.

He heard it before he saw it: the change in the musketry, a shift in direction and density, and then through the snow and the smoke he could see the French infantry pulling back, not retreating in order but coming apart, the shape of the formation dissolving into individual figures moving in all directions that were not toward the Russian line.

A staff officer arrived at a hard gallop. "Major Beaumont. Murat's order. All available cavalry to the center. Now."

Charles looked at Beaumont.

"You heard it," Beaumont said. "Forward."

* * *

The charge at Eylau was not Charles's charge. It was Murat's charge, eighty squadrons, ten thousand horsemen riding into the center of the Russian line to buy time for the French infantry to reform. Charles was one section in the left flank of a mass of cavalry that was itself a geological event, the ground shaking with the weight of it, the sound of hooves on frozen earth a continuous percussion that you felt in your teeth.

He kept the section together by sound and presence rather than sight. The snow reduced visibility to perhaps thirty meters. He could see Rousseau on his left and Mercier on his right and beyond them shapes that were his men or were other cavalry he could not distinguish. He kept Tonnerre at the pace the formation required and kept his voice in the register that his section knew.

The Russian line was somewhere ahead. The snow told him nothing. Then the musketry came and the snow told him everything.

He did not remember the next minutes clearly. This had happened before, at the Tagliamento gap, the way combat at close quarters stripped itself of sequence and left only discrete images: Tonnerre jumping something that was a man or a piece of equipment, the face of a Russian soldier in gray coat with an expression of absolute focus that was exactly the same as Charles's own expression, the sound of a saber hitting something, his hand still connected to it, pulling back. Rousseau beside him, then not beside him, then beside him again. Mercier somewhere to the right, his voice carrying the tone that meant he was directing men and did not need assistance.

Then the charge was through the line and the formation was re-forming on the far side and Charles was counting and the count came up short.

He counted twice. He counted three times.

He was short two men. Then a figure emerged from the snow from the direction of the Russian line, one of the Girard cousins, walking, leading his horse, the horse limping. One. Still short one.

"Ferrand," Mercier said, beside him. Not a question.

"Yes." He could see Mercier's face clearly enough to know that Mercier had already counted and had arrived at the same number.

They rejoined the mass of cavalry pulling back and reforming for a second pass. Charles looked for Ferrand in the shapes moving through the snow and could not find him. He looked at where the Russian line had been, now reforming itself, the gaps the cavalry had punched through it already closing. He looked at the snow between here and there.

Ferrand was twenty-two. He was from Auxerre. He had a habit of checking his carbine three times before mounting up, which Charles had noted on the first day in Munich as either diligence or anxiety and had eventually concluded was both.

He was somewhere in the snow between the lines and Charles could not go back for him.

The order came to reform for a second pass. Charles put the section in order and moved.

* * *

The battle lasted until dark.

The casualty count, when it came, was twelve hundred French dead at Eylau, closer to three thousand counting wounded. The Russians had lost more. Nobody in the French army seemed to find this ratio particularly satisfying. The army held the field, which was what winning meant, and the Russians withdrew, which was what losing meant, and in the morning the field at Eylau was a landscape that Charles could not look at steadily.

He wrote in the journal that night. Not the lists. He started the list in the back and then could not continue. He closed the journal, sat with it in his lap, opened it again.

He wrote Ferrand's name. *Joffre, Ferrand. Cavalry. February 1807.*

He looked at the name for a while.

He had lost men before. Moreau at the bridge, which had been his mistake and had stayed with him. The men at the Tagliamento who were not in his section but whose faces he had seen. He had lost men in ways that felt connected to decisions, the wrong choice at the wrong moment, the gap between what he had prepared for and what the ground presented.

Ferrand had died because a mass of cavalry had ridden through a Russian line at Eylau on a Thursday in February and Ferrand had been in the wrong place in it. Charles could not identify the decision that would have changed the outcome. He had kept the section together, he had maintained formation, he had done what the situation required of the person who understood it clearly.

Ferrand was twenty-two and from Auxerre and checked his carbine three times before mounting up.

Major Beaumont found him at the horse line. He did not say anything for a moment. He stood beside Charles and looked at the horses.

"Ferrand," Beaumont said.

"Yes, sir."

"I know." A pause. "The remaining Girard cousin."

"His horse will recover. He is unhurt."

"Good." Beaumont was quiet. Then: "You held the section together in the second pass. Mercier told me. The visibility was down to nothing and you held them."

Charles said nothing.

"I am not saying it to comfort you," Beaumont said. "I am saying it so you know it was noted." He turned slightly toward Charles. "The casualty is not a consequence of your decisions today."

"I know that, sir."

"Knowing it and feeling it are different processes."

"Yes."

Beaumont looked at the horses for another moment. "The list in the back of your journal," he said.

Charles looked at him. He had not told Beaumont about the list.

"Mercier mentioned it," Beaumont said, without apology. "He said you had kept it since Günzburg." A pause. "Some officers do not write anything down about the men. Some write official records only. Some keep their own counts." He looked at the dark. "I keep mine in my head. I have kept them in my head for twelve years. I do not know if any method is better than another. I know that the counting is the right thing to do."

He walked back toward headquarters.

Charles stayed with Tonnerre until the horse had settled, then went back to his bedroll and lay down. He did not put his hand on his boot. He lay on his back and looked at the ceiling of the tent and thought about Ferrand checking his carbine three times, which was a thing he would not do again.

He thought about the five-year plan.

He thought about it in the specific, mathematical way he had always thought about it: October 1805 to October 1810, five years, make Captain in the spring, go home in the fall of 1810 with the commission completed and the debt to his father honored. The numbers were unchanged. The plan was unchanged. He had said yes to the promotion and it had been noted and it would proceed and in the fall of 1810 he would go home.

He thought: and after Eylau. And after Ferrand. And after Philippe.

The plan would not change because of those things. He was not going to let it change because of those things. He was going to complete the plan and go home and build something on the Bordeaux estate that was not about destroying things, which was the thing he had been good at for two years and four months now and would be good at for seventeen more months and then he was going to stop.

That was the plan.

He said it to himself several times, which was a thing he had noticed he needed to do more often now than he had at the beginning. At the beginning the plan had been a fact. Now it was a decision he remade each time, which was a different thing. Not worse, he told himself. Different. A decision you kept making was a decision you kept meaning.

He closed his eyes.

Somewhere outside, the other Girard cousin, the one who had come back, was talking to someone. Charles could hear the voice without the words: low, steady, the register of someone telling a story that needed to be told out loud.

He did not go to sleep for a long time. When he did, it was without meaning to, the same way he had fallen asleep before the battle, the body deciding what the mind could not.

In the morning he woke up and the plan was still the plan and Ferrand was still dead, and both of those things were true simultaneously, and he put on his boots and went to check the horses, because the horses needed checking and it was February and the spring was coming whether anyone was ready for it or not.

CHAPTER 11

The promotion orders arrived on the fourteenth of March, 1807, by courier from corps headquarters, in a sealed envelope that Sergeant Mercier brought to Charles without comment and stood beside while Charles read it.

*By order of the Emperor, Lieutenant Charles Leblanc is hereby promoted to the rank of Captain, effective the first of March, year of our Lord eighteen hundred and seven...*

He read it to the end. He folded it and set it on the table.

"Well," Mercier said.

"Yes."

Mercier picked up the envelope, looked at the outside of it, set it back down. A rare gesture from him: the thing he did when he was finding words. "Your father's instruction."

"Five years. Make Captain and go home." Charles looked at the orders. "I am two and a half years in."

"Yes."

"The plan says Captain and then home. It does not say Captain by a specific date."

"No," Mercier said. "It does not."

They were quiet for a moment.

"I need to reorganize the company," Charles said. "The restructure means I have additional men coming from Fontaine's old section. I will need a third sergeant."

"Rousseau," Mercier said, without hesitation.

"Yes. I was going to ask your opinion."

"That is my opinion." Mercier picked up his hat. "Congratulations, Captain."

He went out.

Charles sat with the orders for a while. Captain. The thing he had been working toward since Munich, since the garrison, since his father had said five years and Charles had said yes. The thing Beaumont had recommended in January and the army had confirmed in March. The next step in the sequence.

He thought about his father reading the letter that was already on its way. He thought about Elise, who would read it too and would understand things his father would not, the same way she always understood things, at a distance and exactly correctly.

He thought about the second half of the instruction. *Make Captain and come home.*

He had made Captain.

He put the orders in his coat pocket and went to find Rousseau.

* * *

The spring campaign began in earnest in May, the army moving northeast through country that had been fought over twice in the past year and was beginning to show it. The villages were quieter than they should have been. The farms were worked by old men and children. The roads had the particular quality of roads that had carried too much weight: rutted and patched and rutted again, the patches no longer distinguishing themselves from the damage around them.

Charles's company moved with the corps, screening ahead and on the left flank, the work that cavalry did when it was not fighting: watching and reporting and staying between the army and whatever was coming from the other direction. The Russians had withdrawn after Eylau but had not dispersed. They were somewhere northeast, reforming, and the question the spring campaign was designed to answer was whether they were going to fight again or negotiate.

He had sixty-three men now, organized into three sections under Mercier, Bonnard, and Rousseau. He rode the length of the company twice daily, inspecting, adjusting, noting what needed attention. The additional men from Fontaine's section had taken four weeks to integrate cleanly and were now part of the working unit without the seams showing. This was faster than he had expected and was a product of Bonnard's work more than his own.

He told Bonnard this directly, which was the kind of thing Philippe would have done and Charles was still learning to do.

Bonnard looked at the ground when he heard it, which was the response of a man who had not yet learned to receive this kind of thing easily. "Thank you, sir."

"Don't thank me. It's an observation."

"Yes, sir." A pause. "Rousseau helped. He has a way with the new men."

"I know. That's why he's a sergeant."

Bonnard looked up. He was twenty-four now and had a quality that men twice his age took years to develop: the ability to be completely present to the person in front of him. Philippe had had it. Bonnard had grown into it.

"Sir," Bonnard said. "Are you going home? When the war is done?"

Charles looked at him. "Yes."

"I thought so." He said it without judgment. "You have always been going. Even when you are here you are going."

Charles did not know what to do with that, so he said nothing.

"I don't mean it as a criticism," Bonnard said. "It's just a thing I noticed."

He went back to his men. Charles stayed where he was for a moment, looking at the road ahead, which curved north through a stand of bare birch trees toward whatever the spring was going to produce.

* * *

He found Margot Vieillard at a supply depot outside Allenstein on the third of May.

He was not looking for her. He was at the depot for a requisition argument about oats, which was a more complicated argument than it should have been because the depot officer was the same lieutenant who had slowed her paperwork in Lowicz, who apparently slowed everyone's paperwork everywhere as a matter of principle. Charles resolved it with the same tool he always used: specific dates, specific regulations, and the implication of Beaumont's name.

She was coming out of the depot office when he came in. They both stopped.

"Captain," she said. The new rank, noting it.

"You're this far east," he said.

"The route extended again." She looked at him with the particular attention he had learned to recognize, the kind that missed nothing. "You look different."

"The same."

"No." She did not say it critically. "Eylau."

He looked at her. He had not told her about Eylau. "You heard about it."

"Everyone heard about it." She studied him a moment longer, then: "Are you done here? There is a place in the town with adequate coffee."

"I have a requisition."

"I know. I heard you in there. You'll win it in ten minutes." She was already walking. "Find me when you're done."

He won it in twelve minutes. He found her at a table near the window of a narrow inn, her gloves off, the left one with the worn thumb, both hands around a cup. She had her ledger open beside her but she was not reading it. She was watching the market square.

He sat down. A woman brought him coffee without being asked, which meant Margot had ordered it before he arrived, which meant she had been certain he would come.

"You're always certain," he said.

She looked at him. "About some things."

"What things?"

"That you were going to resolve the requisition and come find me." She picked up her cup. "That Eylau was bad. That you are not entirely the same person who came through Posen in November."

He thought about the list in the back of his journal. Two and a half pages now. He thought about Ferrand, twenty-two, from Auxerre.

"I am the same person," he said. "The same plan."

"I know." She looked at the square. "That's not what I meant."

He waited.

"You came in with a different weight," she said. "Not heavier. More settled. Like you stopped arguing with something."

He considered that. He had stopped arguing with something, though he would not have named it that way. After Eylau he had remade the plan deliberately, consciously, as a decision rather than a fact, and in making it consciously he had stopped the internal argument about whether it was still valid. The plan was valid. He was going home. He had stopped debating it with himself.

"The war will end this summer," he said. "Probably. The Russians can't sustain another Eylau and neither can we."

"And then?"

"And then I put in for discharge and go to Bordeaux."

She was quiet for a moment, looking at the square. "When you say Bordeaux," she said, "what do you see?"

He thought about it honestly. "My father's study. The view from the upper fields over the Garonne in the evening. Elise at the table reading." He paused. "The work of building something. The estate. I know what it needs. I have known for two years what it needs and have not been there to do it."

"Building," she said.

"Yes."

She smiled, brief and genuine, the same one he had seen at Posen, the one she gave rarely enough that it meant something. "I've been thinking about that," she said. "After."

"What do you think about it?"

"I'm going to keep running convoys for a while. Maybe two more years. Get the eastern routes stable enough to hand to someone else and then do something else." She picked up her cup. "I don't know what yet. Something that stays in one place."

"That would be different for you."

"Very." She did not sound troubled by it. She said it the way she said most things: as information, something to be assessed and planned for. "My father says I will be bored in a week."

"Will you?"

"Probably not." She looked at him. "I know how to build things. I built this operation. I could build something else."

He looked at her, sitting across the table with her coffee and her ledger and the worn thumb of her left glove, and thought about the build versus destroy conversation at the inn in Hreben, six months ago. She had said: when my part breaks, people die not fighting. She had known then what she was building and why it mattered, and she still knew, and she was already thinking about what came next.

"You'll be good at it," he said.

"Yes." She said it simply, without false modesty. "So will you."

They sat for a while. The market square outside was doing what market squares did in May: the sounds of commerce and animals and ordinary life that had continued through two years of occupation and would continue after the occupation ended. Outside the window a woman was arguing with a vendor about the price of something. The argument was in Polish and Charles could not follow it but the shape of it was universal.

"I have to get back to the depot," Margot said, eventually. She pulled on her gloves, right first. "My next run is Thursday. I'll be through Allenstein again in three weeks."

"I may not be here in three weeks."

"I know." She stood. "I'll look for you anyway."

"I'll look for you," he said.

She nodded once and walked out into the square, and he sat with the coffee and watched her cross to the depot, and thought about the fact that she had said *building* the same way he had said it, with the same specific weight, and had not been surprised by it.

* * *

He wrote home that evening.

*Dear Father,*

*The promotion is confirmed. I am Captain as of the first of March. The plan is on schedule.*

*I want to tell you what I mean when I say that. I do not mean that I am moving automatically toward something I decided before I understood what I was deciding. I mean that I have been here long enough to understand what I am and what I am not, and what I came here to do and what I have done, and the plan remains the right plan. Not because it was always right. Because I have chosen it again, having seen the alternatives.*

*The war will likely end this summer. When it does, I will request discharge. I will come home. I will tell you the rest of it in person, which is the right way to tell it.*

*Your son,*
*Charles*

He sealed it. He sat for a moment, and then wrote a second letter.

*Dear Elise,*

*I am Captain.*

*I know you have already calculated what that means for the timeline, so I will confirm it: I am coming home. Not immediately. When the war ends. But I am coming.*

*There are things I need to tell you that letters are not adequate for. I will tell them to you in person. I expect you already know most of them, because you usually do.*

*Your brother,*
*Charles*

He sealed that one too and put both in the outgoing mail, and went to check the horses.

CHAPTER 12

Friedland was the fourteenth of June, a Saturday, and the summer had finally arrived in the way that summer in the east arrived: suddenly, completely, as though it had been waiting just past a door someone had left closed.

The army was at Friedland because the Russian general Bennigsen had made a mistake. He had crossed the Alle River with the bulk of his force and posted them with the river at their backs and the town behind them and only two bridges for retreat. Napoleon had seen this and moved immediately, ordering everything toward Friedland, and by eight in the morning the French were arriving in strength and the Russians were discovering that the position they had chosen for a defensive engagement had become a trap.

Charles's company came up with the rest of the cavalry reserve at midmorning. He could hear the battle before he saw it: artillery from both sides, the steady exchange of a well-developed engagement, and underneath it the musketry that meant the infantry was in contact across the whole line. He brought the company to its assigned position and waited, which was the thing cavalry did in most battles: waited until the infantry created a situation that cavalry could exploit.

Waiting before a battle was different now than it had been in the Alps, before the Tagliamento, before Eylau. At the Tagliamento he had felt the anticipation as a physical pressure, something in the chest and the hands. Before Eylau it had been something colder, a clear-eyed calculation. Now it was something else again, something he did not have a precise name for. Not calm exactly. A kind of settled attention, the quality of a man who has done this enough times to know what to do with himself while he waits.

He rode the length of the company once. He checked the horses by eye. He came back to the front.

Sergeant Mercier was beside him, which was where Mercier was always found in these moments, not through instruction but through the long habit of being where he was needed without being asked.

"Bennigsen has given us the battle," Charles said.

"Yes, sir."

"It will be decisive."

"Yes, sir."

"The men are ready."

"Yes, sir." Mercier looked at the smoke rising on the French left. "Private Guillot's horse is favoring the near foreleg. I told him to stay in the rear position."

"Good."

"And Corporal Arnaud has been vomiting since dawn. Nerves. He is recovered and functional."

"Keep an eye on him."

"Yes, sir."

They stood in the June heat and watched the battle develop.

At half past five in the afternoon the order came: cavalry to the left flank, exploit the gap at the river bend.

Charles looked at his company. Sixty-three men, horses rested, sabers ready. He knew every name. He could have given you the home village of every man in the front rank and the condition of every horse in the rear. He had been doing this for two years and he was good at it.

He drew his saber.

"Company. Column of fours. Forward."

* * *

The gap at the river bend was real and it was wide and the Russians had not been able to close it because they were trying to cross two bridges simultaneously and the bridges could not handle what was being asked of them. Charles brought the company through the gap and hit the Russian left flank while it was trying to move, not fight, and drove it back against the river in three hard exchanges that he would not remember cleanly afterward.

He remembered specific things. The way the ground sloped toward the water and how Tonnerre's hooves changed cadence on the slope, adjusting without being told. A Russian officer on a gray horse who turned and charged rather than ran, which was the correct decision and did not save him. The sound of Mercier's voice somewhere to the right, steady and carrying, the voice Charles had heard in every engagement since Munich and had learned to use as a navigation point.

He remembered the moment the Russian line broke. Not a dramatic moment. A threshold reached: enough men had moved away from the fight that the men still in it had no choice but to move too, and the movement became general, and the fight was over.

He pulled the company back from the river's edge, counted, counted again.

All sixty-three. He counted a third time.

All sixty-three.

He stood in his stirrups, looked at the length of his company, at the faces he knew. Mercier, expressionless. Bonnard, checking his men with the systematic attention of a man who had learned the inspection sequence until it was reflex. Rousseau, his face flushed, already talking to the trooper beside him, the energy of the engagement still moving through him. The Girard cousin who had survived Eylau and had a new wariness in his eyes that would not leave.

All sixty-three.

He put his hand on Tonnerre's neck.

The gelding's neck was wet with sweat and his breathing was fast and even, recovering. The horse had been with him since before the Alps, since Munich, since the garrison. He had carried Charles through the Tagliamento gap and the screen outside Küstrin and the charge at Eylau and this, and he was still here, and his ears were already coming up from their flat-back position, the battle awareness fading, the horse returning to himself.

To the north the French left was pressing the Russian line into the river. To the south the artillery was still working. The battle was not over for everyone in it. For Charles's company, for this hour, it was over.

He looked at the river. The water was moving fast, green, the summer sun on it making it look clean from this distance. He thought about Ferrand, who had not come back from the snow at Eylau. He thought about Philippe, who had bled out against a gun carriage wheel in November. He thought about Moreau at the bridge, the first name on the list, the wife and the infant son, the letter he had written under Beaumont's instruction.

All sixty-three. Today.

He gave thanks for it in the simple way he gave thanks for things: a moment of recognition, nothing performative, just the acknowledgment of a fact that mattered. Then he reformed the company and went to find Beaumont.

* * *

The armistice came through three days later.

It was not entirely a surprise. The battle had been decisive; Bennigsen had lost a third of his force at the river and the Tsar had signaled through channels before the smoke cleared that he was prepared to discuss terms. Napoleon moved fast, as he always moved when he had leverage, and within a week the shape of the negotiations was clear.

The war was ending.

Charles sat with this information in the particular way he sat with large things: turning it over, examining it from different angles, looking for what it required of him. The practical requirements were immediate: he had a company to demobilize to whatever degree the peace terms required, men to account for, horses to assess, equipment to inventory. He started on those the morning after the armistice and worked through them methodically for two days.

On the third day he allowed himself to acknowledge what the armistice meant in terms that were not practical.

The war was ending. He was Captain. He had twenty-six months left on a five-year plan that had produced the captaincy on schedule. The next step was home.

He had been moving toward this for two years. He had made it specific, kept it in view, remade it as a conscious choice every time the weight of the work made it easier to simply continue. He had said yes to dozens of things and no to nothing, and the plan had held not because it was easy but because he had held it.

He walked to the horse line at evening. Tonnerre was at his usual place, working through a bucket of oats with focused attention.

"It's done," Charles said.

The horse did not look up.

"The battle. The campaign." He put his hand on the gelding's neck. "We are going home."

The horse kept eating, which was the correct response to news of any significance.

Charles stayed at the line for a while, hand on the horse, the summer evening cooling slowly around him. The camp noise was different in the days after an armistice: lighter, a different register, the sound of men who had been carrying something and are beginning to put it down. It would take weeks to put down completely. He knew that. He had done this after Italy and the weight had come off slowly, in stages, like a coat that is too familiar to be removed all at once.

But the direction was set. He was going home.

He went to find paper and write to his father, and then to find Mercier, and then to get some sleep, because tomorrow there was still work to do and there would be work to do until the day he left, and doing the work correctly until the last day was the only way to do it.

CHAPTER 13

The Treaty of Tilsit was signed on the seventh of July, 1807, on a raft in the middle of the Niemen River, which struck Charles as an unnecessarily theatrical venue and which he was not present for. He was present for its effects, which arrived in the form of orders, redeployment schedules, and the particular kind of administrative chaos that follows a major war's end: units dissolving, units reforming, men going home and men being reassigned and men falling through the gaps between the two categories.

His company was assigned to garrison duty in a town called Tilsit, which was the same name as the treaty and which seemed like a coincidence that the army had not noticed or didn't care about. The garrison assignment was indefinite, which was the army's way of saying they had not decided what to do with this particular set of useful men and were not prepared to release them until they had.

Major Beaumont delivered this information at a meeting of the squadron officers in the first week of July. He was direct about it, as he was direct about most things.

"The garrison assignment is indefinite but I expect it to run six months, possibly through the spring. The peace terms require a French presence in the region while the political reorganization proceeds. We are a useful presence."

Charles looked at the map. Tilsit to Bordeaux was roughly fifteen hundred kilometers.

"Captain Leblanc," Beaumont said.

"Sir."

"I know what you're thinking."

"Yes, sir."

"I am not in a position to accelerate your discharge. The garrison assignment is a corps decision."

"I understand."

"What I can do," Beaumont said, "is ensure that when the discharge request goes forward, it goes with my full recommendation. Which it will." He looked at Charles. "Six months. Possibly less."

"Possibly more," Charles said.

Beaumont did not deny it. "Possibly more. That is the honest answer."

Charles looked at the map again. He had been looking at maps for two and a half years and knew how to read what they said and what they did not say. What this map said was: the army had him and was not finished with him.

He knew this. He had known it since December, since Beaumont's conversation in the manor house, since Mercier's warning in October of the year before. He had said yes to the promotion with his eyes open and the conditions noted and the conditions had not been honored, which was entirely predictable and which he had predicted and which did not help.

He was six months from home. Possibly more.

He went back to work.

* * *

Tilsit was a reasonable town that had been through a difficult two years and was attempting to return to the version of itself that had existed before the war arrived. The market ran on Tuesdays and Fridays. There was a Lutheran church and a Catholic one and they had apparently coexisted without incident for long enough that the coexistence was unremarkable. The river was wide and flat and in the summer evenings it reflected the sky in a way that made it look deeper than it was.

Charles quartered his company in a section of the town's eastern district, in buildings that had been used for that purpose before and had the particular quality of spaces that have housed many different groups of men and carry the impression of all of them. He organized the garrison routine the way he organized everything: systematically, with clear responsibilities and predictable structure, because men without clear responsibilities in a garrison situation found their own activities and those activities were rarely beneficial.

He wrote home twice a week. His father's replies were formal and warm in the way his father was warm, the warmth present and not easily named. His father had read between the lines of the letters about the promotion and had written: *I am proud of you, Charles. The work you have described sounds like the work of a good officer. I look forward to hearing the rest of it.* His father did not ask when he was coming. He understood enough about armies to know that question had no useful answer.

Elise wrote more. She always had. Her letters had the quality of conversations rather than dispatches: she followed threads from previous letters, asked questions about things Charles had mentioned in passing, noticed what he had not said. She had written about Philippe: *I thought he was extraordinary, from the way you never talked about him. You don't talk about the things that matter to you directly, you talk around them, and the shape of what you leave out tells me more than what you include. The shape of Philippe in your letters was very large.* She had written about the promotion: *Captain. Father walked around the estate saying it to himself for two days. I heard him in the library at ten at night saying it to no one.* She had written: *Come home, Charles. Not soon necessarily. Just come.*

He wrote to her about Margot Vieillard, which surprised him when he found himself doing it.

*There is a woman I have met several times this year, a contractor running supply convoys on the eastern routes. Her name is Margot Vieillard. She is eighteen, not yet nineteen, and she manages a fourteen-wagon operation with the competence of someone who has been doing it for forty years. I am telling you this because you will ask about it eventually and I would rather tell you directly than have you extract it from the shape of what I leave out.*

*I do not know what to tell you about it beyond the facts. We have spoken honestly about things that are difficult to speak honestly about. She will be on these roads for another year or two and then she will do something else. I will be in Bordeaux in six months, possibly more. I am not certain of anything further than that. You may draw whatever conclusions you like.*

He sealed it, looked at the envelope, and wrote Elise's name on it, and thought about what she would make of the phrase *I am not certain of anything further than that*, which was either an evasion or the most honest thing he had written in a year and he was not certain which.

* * *

He found Mercier at the stable on a Thursday evening in late July, which was where Mercier could always be found when there was no immediate work pressing and sometimes when there was.

He told Mercier about the garrison assignment. The indefinite timeline. Beaumont's six months.

Mercier listened. He was cleaning a bridle with the particular patience of a man who had cleaned a thousand bridles. He did not look up.

"You are going to wait," Mercier said.

"I don't have another option."

"You could resign your commission."

Charles looked at him. "I signed the promotion. I accepted the terms."

"Yes." Mercier set down the bridle. He looked at Charles now, directly, with the quality of attention he reserved for things that mattered. "I am not suggesting you resign. I am noting that you believe you have no options in order to point out that you do have options and are choosing not to use them. That is different from not having options."

Charles absorbed that.

"You are choosing to honor the commitment," Mercier said. "That is the right choice. But calling it the only choice is how men end up resentful of things they freely chose." He picked up the bridle again. "Own it, sir. You chose it."

"I chose it."

"Yes." Mercier ran the bridle through his hands, checking the stitching. "And in six months, or however long it is, you will choose to leave. And that will also be a choice you make, not a thing that happens to you."

Charles said nothing for a moment. Outside the stable the summer evening was doing what summer evenings did in Tilsit: the light lingering longer than it had any right to, the air still, the river carrying some sound from the town on the far bank.

"Mercier," he said.

"Sir."

"When I go. Will you come?"

Mercier went still for a moment, the bridle in his hands. It was the only time Charles had seen him go completely still outside of the second before combat.

"Come," Mercier said.

"To Bordeaux. The estate needs work. Real work, not military work. My father is older. I could use someone who knows horses and knows how to get things done." He paused. "It's not an order. It's a question."

Mercier was quiet for a long time. He set the bridle down on the stable rail, precisely, as though its placement mattered.

"I have been in the army for seventeen years," he said.

"I know."

"I don't know what I am outside it."

"No," Charles said. "Neither do I. But I expect we'll find out." A pause. "There is no obligation. I am asking."

Mercier looked at the bridle for a moment. Then: "I will think about it."

"That's all I'm asking."

Mercier picked up the bridle and went back to the stitching. Charles stayed in the stable for a while, with Tonnerre and the smell of hay and the familiar sounds of horses settling for the evening, and thought that whatever came next, the question had been worth asking.

* * *

Margot Vieillard came through Tilsit on the ninth of August, on a run from Frankfurt that had been extended twice and was now covering a route that would have seemed impossible six months ago when the fighting was still active. She arrived with fifteen wagons and a calm efficiency that had the depot officers sorted and signed within two hours of arrival.

He found her at the depot. She looked at him the way she always looked at him: completing the assessment before speaking.

"Captain," she said.

"Vieillard."

"How long are you in Tilsit?"

"Six months. Possibly more."

She absorbed this without visible reaction. "And then Bordeaux."

"And then Bordeaux."

She nodded. She pulled her gloves off, right first, left second, the worn thumb. "Walk with me. I want to see the river while there is still light."

* * *

The river was wide and calm and the evening light was doing what it had been doing all summer, lingering beyond what was reasonable. They walked along the bank without hurrying. Her eleven men were at the depot; she had an hour before she needed to be back.

"I'm done with the eastern routes after this run," she said. "I'll do the Frankfurt to Posen run through the winter and then I'm going to stop."

"Stop the convoys?"

"Stop everything. For a while." She looked at the water. "My father is in good health. The operation runs without him now, or runs with less of him. I have been thinking about what I said to you in Lowicz, in January."

"Don't let it be decided by the accumulation of small yeses."

"Yes." She walked for a moment. "I have been saying yes to the next run for two years. Each yes was the correct answer. Each run was needed and I was the person who could do it well and the work mattered." She paused. "That is not a reason to stop. But it is also not, by itself, a reason to continue."

He recognized the logic. He had been running the same calculation for eighteen months.

"What will you do?" he said.

"I don't know yet. Something in one place." She glanced at him. "My father has been talking about expanding the operation, a warehouse in Frankfurt, routes to the south. Something I could manage from one location rather than from the road." She was quiet for a moment. "Or something else entirely. I haven't decided."

"You'll be good at whatever it is."

"Yes." She said it simply. "So will you. The estate, the vineyard. Whatever you build there."

They stopped at a place where the bank was clear, the river visible down a long straight stretch. The far bank was Prussia, technically, or whatever Prussia was becoming under the treaty reorganization. The map was being redrawn again.

"You knew I was going before I told you," he said.

She looked at him. "Yes."

"How long?"

She considered it. "Since Posen. You were always going. I told you that. Even when you were here you were going."

"Bonnard said the same thing."

"Then Bonnard is perceptive." She looked at the river. "I am not saying it as a criticism. I am saying it because it is one of the things about you that is true, and it is not a flaw."

He looked at her. "What are the others?"

She looked back at him. The evening light was on her face, the specific quality of late summer light in the east, warm and horizontal and making everything it touched look more itself than usual.

"You notice what needs doing and you do it," she said. "Without needing to be seen doing it. You hold things for people without telling them you are holding things. You find the practical reason for what you were going to do anyway, which is a defense against something, I think, though I don't know against what."

He thought about Elise writing: *competence isn't care.*

"My sister says something similar," he said.

"Your sister sounds like someone worth getting home to."

He looked at the river. "Yes."

They stood for a moment, the water moving past them toward the Baltic, the sound of it filling the space.

"I looked for you in Allenstein," she said. "In June. After Friedland."

"I wasn't there."

"I know. The depot told me your company had moved." She was quiet. "I wanted to know you were all right."

He looked at her.

"I did not know you until November," she said. "I have seen you five times in eight months. That is not enough to know someone fully." She said it carefully, the way she said careful things: plainly, without performing the care. "But I know enough to want to know the rest. And I know that the rest is in Bordeaux, not here."

"Margot."

"Don't apologize for it," she said. "It is not something to apologize for."

"I wasn't going to apologize." He turned toward her. "I was going to say that I thought about you at Eylau. Not during. After. When I was counting the men and they were all there." He paused. "I thought: she would understand this. The specific relief of this particular number."

She looked at him for a long moment.

"I would," she said.

The evening bell rang from the church in the town, the sound carrying clear across the river. She looked at the water a moment longer and then turned back toward the depot.

"Come find me before you leave for Bordeaux," she said. "If the road takes you through Frankfurt."

"The road goes through Frankfurt."

"Then come find me."

He watched her walk back along the river bank, the same unhurried efficiency, the same quality of motion that wasted nothing. At the bend of the path she did not look back, which was entirely in character, and he stood at the river for a while after she was gone, watching the water, thinking about the distance from here to Bordeaux and what it meant that he already knew the road.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 14

The discharge request went to Beaumont on the first of January, 1808.

Charles had written it twice. The first draft was formal and brief, the language of official correspondence, a request for discharge at the completion of the garrison assignment with reference to the five-year service plan. He read it and wrote a second draft.

The second draft said the same things. He sent the second draft.

Beaumont called him in the next morning.

The meeting was in Beaumont's office, which had been a merchant's counting room before the garrison and still had the ledger shelves on the north wall, empty now, the wood worn smooth at the edges by years of men reaching for account books. Beaumont was at the desk. He had the discharge request in front of him.

"Sit down," Beaumont said.

Charles sat.

Beaumont looked at the request for a moment, not reading it, the way a man looks at something he has already read and is now considering. Then he looked at Charles.

"I want to keep you," he said.

Charles waited.

"The regiment is restructuring in the spring. The corps needs experienced company commanders. I have three positions and I am short on officers who can fill them without supervision." Beaumont set the request down. "I want you in one of those positions. You know that."

"Yes, sir."

"I want you to reconsider the discharge."

Charles looked at him. He had expected this. He had thought about it for three months, since August, since the river in Tilsit, since the moment when he had acknowledged to himself clearly that he was choosing to leave and not being driven out. He had thought about what he would say when Beaumont made the argument, and he had found the words in December, on a quiet night, and he had them now.

"Sir," he said. "I am the best officer you have for those positions. That is not arrogance, it is your own assessment and I have heard you make it."

"Yes."

"That is exactly why I should go." He said it calmly. "If I am that useful here, I am being kept by my usefulness rather than by choice. And a man kept by his usefulness rather than by choice is a man who has let the decision be made for him." He paused. "I told you in January of last year that I wanted the five-year mark noted. You noted it. I am at two years and nine months. I am asking for the remainder to be counted against the garrison assignment, which ends in March. The request stands."

Beaumont looked at him. A long look, the kind that assessed and did not give away the result.

"Who told you that?" he said. "About being kept by usefulness."

"Several people," Charles said. "At different times. I was slow to hear it."

Beaumont was quiet for a moment. He looked at the request on the desk.

"The recommendation will be complete," he said. "Full endorsement. I'll see to it." He picked up his pen. "March, then."

"March. Yes, sir."

Beaumont signed the relevant document. He did not say anything else, and Charles recognized that the silence was a form of respect, the acknowledgment that the argument had been made and heard and settled and did not require further comment.

He stood and came to attention.

"It has been," Beaumont said, not looking up from his desk, "a privilege to command you, Captain Leblanc."

"Sir." Charles held the attention for a moment. "The privilege was mine."

He walked out of the counting room into the January cold.

* * *

He told Mercier that evening.

"March," Charles said.

Mercier was at the horse line, which was where Mercier was when Charles needed to find him. He said nothing for a moment, his hands on a horse's flank.

"I thought about your question," Mercier said.

"I know."

"Bordeaux."

"Yes."

Mercier moved to the next horse. Left foreleg, right foreleg. The inspection sequence he had done ten thousand times, in mud and snow and summer heat, in Germany and Italy and Poland. He worked through it without varying his pace.

"I have a sister in Lyon," he said. "I haven't seen her in six years."

Charles waited.

"I would need to stop in Lyon on the way." He moved to the hind legs. "If the road goes through Lyon."

"The road goes through Lyon."

Mercier straightened. He looked at Charles with the direct, unperformed attention that Charles had learned over two and a half years to read as the fullest version of what Mercier had to give.

"Then yes," he said. "I'll come."

* * *

He told Bonnard and Rousseau together.

They were in the company office, such as it was: a converted storeroom with a table and three chairs and a shelf of company records that Bonnard kept with the meticulous accuracy of a man who understood that the records were a form of respect for the men they described.

"March," Charles said.

Bonnard nodded. He had known. Charles could see it in the lack of surprise: Bonnard had been anticipating this conversation since August, had organized his thinking around it.

Rousseau looked at the wall for a moment, then at Charles. He was twenty-two now, a sergeant, with a sergeant's steadiness that was beginning to overlay the eager young corporal of Munich. There was still something light in him, something that the war had compressed but not extinguished.

"Who takes command?" Rousseau said.

"Bonnard, pending Beaumont's confirmation. I have already recommended him."

Bonnard looked at the table. The expression of a man who has just been handed something he was not certain he had earned and was not going to argue about it.

"The company is solid," Charles said. "Both of you built that. What you do with it after March is yours." He looked at both of them. "You don't need me to tell you how to run it. You haven't needed me for six months."

"That's not entirely true," Rousseau said.

"It's mostly true. And the rest will fill in quickly."

He said the things that needed to be said about the transition, the paperwork, the handover schedule. He said them efficiently, because efficiency was how he did things, and because the practical details gave structure to a conversation that would otherwise have had too much feeling in it for any of them to manage cleanly.

When he was done, Rousseau stood and held out his hand. "It was a good company, sir."

Charles shook it. "It was."

Bonnard stood. He held out his hand and Charles shook it, and they looked at each other for a moment. Bonnard was twenty-four and from Lyon and had joined the cavalry because his father had been cavalry and had come through the junction at Küstrin and the winter in Poland and Eylau and Friedland and garrison duty in Tilsit, and had become in the process someone Charles would trust with anything he had.

"Philippe would be proud of you," Charles said.

Bonnard looked at the floor for a moment. Then he looked up.

"I know," he said. "I think about that."

Charles left them to it.

* * *

He spent the last two weeks in order. There was a great deal of administrative work in the handover of a company, and he did all of it correctly, because doing things correctly until the last day was the only way he knew how to do things. He signed the transfer documents and the inventory records and the formal recommendation for Bonnard's promotion and the letters of service for the men who would be discharged along with him.

There were eleven of them, men whose service terms ended near his own, and he wrote a letter of service for each one that was specific to that man: his skills, his particular contributions, what a future employer or a family or a village should know about what this person had done and who he was. It took three evenings. He did not think of it as extra work.

On the last night he sat with the field journal.

He opened it to the back. The list was three pages now, dense in the margins, the names written in the small careful print he had developed for entries he wanted to keep. He read through it from the beginning. Moreau, at the bridge, October 1805. The names from the Tagliamento. The July rearguard action, no casualties in his section, but he had written the names of the infantry dead he had seen on the road, because they had been real and they had been there and the record should know they existed. Ferrand. Philippe.

Three pages. Two and a half years.

He did not try to make it mean something specific. It meant what it was: people who had been in the world and were no longer in it. He had kept the count because Beaumont had said the counting was the right thing to do, and Beaumont was right, and Charles would keep the count for the rest of his life, which was what keeping it meant.

He closed the journal.

He put his hand on his boot. The paper was there: Bordeaux, Elise's handwriting, folded to the size of a playing card. He had put a new one in when the old one became too worn to read, and he had done this twice, and the address had not changed, and now he was going there.

He took the paper out of his boot and held it.

Tomorrow he would leave Tilsit with Mercier and eleven other men, moving west on the road toward Frankfurt and then Lyon and then south and west to Bordeaux, a distance of fifteen hundred kilometers that he would cover in stages, taking his time, because there was no longer any urgency about the direction.

He thought about Margot, and Frankfurt, and the fact that the road went through Frankfurt.

He thought about Elise, who would already know he was coming because she always knew, and who would have things to say that would take a long time to say and which he wanted to hear.

He thought about his father saying *Captain* to himself in the library at ten at night, to no one, the way people said true things when they were alone.

He put the paper in his coat pocket. Not the boot. He was not going into combat tomorrow.

He lay on the bedroll.

He closed his eyes.

In the morning, he was going home.

# The Smoke — Manuscript

CHAPTER 15

Frankfurt was three weeks from Tilsit, moving at the pace of men and horses that were not in a hurry.

He had not been in a hurry for the first time since Munich. The sensation was unfamiliar enough that he noticed it in the first day, the absence of urgency in his body, the way the road looked different when you were not moving toward something that required speed. He noted it and did not try to recreate the urgency. He let the road be the road.

Mercier rode beside him most of the day. He talked less than Charles had expected, given that the constraint of military hierarchy was loosening as each kilometer passed. He had always been a man of few words. He was apparently a man of few words outside the army too, which was not a disappointment, merely information. They rode and watched the country and stopped when the horses needed water and ate when they were hungry and made camp before dark.

At night Mercier sat with the others, the eleven discharged men who were spreading gradually in the direction of their various homes, dropping off one or two at a time as the road reached their regions. He did not seek them out and did not avoid them. He was present in the way he had always been present: fully, without performing it.

Charles watched this and thought about what Mercier had said in the stable in Tilsit: *I don't know what I am outside it.* He was finding out on the road, as was Charles, and what they were finding out so far was that they were approximately themselves, which was either a relief or simply a fact.

* * *

Frankfurt appeared on the eleventh day, the city's outline rising above the Main River in the March morning light. Charles rode to the river and looked at it and then turned his horse toward the address he had carried in his coat pocket for three months, since August, since Margot had said *come find me before you leave for Bordeaux.*

The Vieillard depot was on the south side of the river, a substantial building with wagon access and a yard large enough for a full convoy to stage. Charles arrived at midmorning and told the man at the gate his name.

He waited in the yard.

She came out of the depot office in work clothes, ledger in hand, and saw him and stopped. A moment, the familiar pause, the assessment completing itself.

"You came," she said.

"I said I would."

She looked at him for a moment. He was in plain clothes now, the first time she had seen him without the uniform, and he watched her register this, file it.

"Come inside," she said. "I'll be done in an hour."

He waited in the depot office, which smelled of paper and wood and the particular dry smell of grain sacks. He sat in a chair near the window and watched the yard and listened to the sounds of an operation working. Men moving, wagon wheels, the voices of her drivers calling to each other in the shorthand of people who have worked together long enough to not need full sentences. The same sounds as any working operation: organized, purposeful, the sounds of things being built.

She was done in forty minutes.

They walked to the river. It was a clear March day, cold but not biting, the kind of cold that felt like the end of winter rather than the middle of it. The Main was running fast with snowmelt.

"Lyon first," she said. He had written her the route. "Then Bordeaux."

"Yes. Mercier wants to see his sister."

"Good." She looked at the river. "And then the estate."

"The estate."

She was quiet for a moment. Then: "Tell me what it looks like. The view you described in the letter. From the upper fields."

He thought about it honestly, trying to see it rather than describe it.

"The Garonne is in the valley," he said. "In the evening light it moves slowly enough that it looks still, but it isn't. The color changes depending on the sky. The vineyards are on the slopes above it, the rows running uphill, and in August when the vines are full the green of them is very particular, a specific shade I have not seen anywhere else." He paused. "I have been away for two and a half years. I don't know what condition it's in."

"You're afraid it won't be what you remember."

"I'm not afraid. I'm uncertain. Those are different."

She glanced at him. "Yes, they are." She looked back at the water. "It will be different. Things always are. But the fields and the river will be there."

"Yes."

"And Elise."

"And Elise."

She smiled, the brief genuine one, the one he had been watching for since Posen. "You talk about her the way you talk about things you trust completely. There aren't many of those."

"No."

"She sounds like someone who will know what to do with you when you get home."

"She usually does."

They stood at the river for a while. Downstream, a barge was working against the current, slow and deliberate, loaded with something he could not identify from this distance.

"I am going to Posen in the summer," she said. "And then I am going to think about what comes next."

"Will you write?"

She looked at him. The pause. "If you do."

"I'll write from Lyon," he said.

"Then I'll write back." She turned from the river. "Come for dinner tonight. My father wants to meet you."

"You told him about me."

"I told him I had met a cavalry officer who understood supply logistics. He was curious." She was already walking. "He will ask you difficult questions about the eastern roads. I suggest you know the answers."

"I know the answers."

"I know you do." She did not look back. "Come at seven."

* * *

He met her father at seven. Henri Vieillard was a man of sixty who had been doing the work of the roads for thirty years and could read a route the way Charles could read ground: quickly, accurately, with the attention of someone who understood that the difference between the right road and the wrong one was consequential. He asked difficult questions about the eastern routes. Charles knew the answers. The old man listened to them with the careful attention of someone evaluating a source.

At the end of the evening Henri Vieillard shook Charles's hand and said: "You are welcome in Frankfurt, Captain."

Charles left at ten and walked back to the inn where Mercier and the others were quartered. The night was clear, the March cold precise, the stars visible over the city's roofline. He could see his breath.

He thought about the view from the upper fields above the Garonne. He thought about Elise at the table. He thought about Margot at seven tomorrow morning, when he was leaving, at the depot gate.

He thought: I am going home.

The thought had a different quality now than it had for two years. It had been a plan, then a decision, then a choice remade daily in the weight of the work. Now it was a direction. A thing he was moving toward rather than holding in front of him.

In the morning he said goodbye to Margot at the depot gate, brief and direct, the way they both said things. She held out her hand and he shook it, the worn left glove, the same one, and she looked at him with the assessment completing itself one more time.

"Bordeaux," she said.

"Frankfurt," he said. "Eventually."

She held his gaze for a moment. Then she turned back to the depot.

He rode south with Mercier.

* * *

Lyon was ten days further, through country that was becoming more familiar the further south they went. The light changed below Lyon, became the light of the south, horizontal and warm even in March, the kind of light that made the stone walls of the farmhouses glow. Mercier spent three days with his sister, who was older than him and had a family and a house full of children who treated Mercier's silence with the casual acceptance of people who had always known him.

Charles waited in Lyon and wrote a letter to Margot from Frankfurt, as he had said he would, and wrote one to Elise that said only: *I am in Lyon. I am two weeks from home. I will be there before the end of the month.*

He sent both and rode south.

* * *

He came home on a Thursday, which seemed again like an irrelevant detail and was the one he kept.

The road from Lyon south and then west took him through country that became more his with every kilometer, the Massif on the right giving way to the flatlands and then the river and then the first vineyards he recognized, the rows familiar, the specific angle of the morning light on the stone walls of the estate boundary.

He stopped at the gate.

The gate was the same gate it had been when he left. Stone posts, iron hinges, the latch his father had repaired himself in the summer of 1803 and had never been fully satisfied with. Beyond it the drive went up through the vineyards toward the house, the house he had grown up in, the view from the upper fields above the Garonne in the evening.

He sat on Tonnerre for a moment.

Mercier waited behind him without speaking.

Charles opened the gate and rode through.

* * *

Elise was on the front steps when he came up the drive. She had always had the ability to know when something was arriving, which their father attributed to the dogs and Charles had never believed. She was standing on the steps with her coat on and her hair not yet done for the day, as though she had come out in a hurry, and she watched him ride up the drive with an expression that he could not fully read at this distance and could read completely when he was close enough.

He dismounted.

She walked down the steps and stood in front of him. She was twenty-two now, two years older than when he had left, and there was something in her face that had not been there before, a quality he recognized as the same thing that had arrived in his own face, the residue of having lived through a stretch of time that required something real.

She looked at him for a long moment without speaking.

"You're thinner," she said.

"You're taller."

"I'm not taller. You're thinner." She hit his arm once, not hard, the gesture of someone who has been waiting to do it for a long time. Then she held onto his arm and did not let go.

"I got your letters," she said.

"I know."

"All of them."

"I know."

She looked at him. "There is a great deal I want to ask you."

"I know that too."

"And you're going to actually answer."

He thought about Margot at the depot gate, about Mercier at the stable in Tilsit, about Philippe at the picket line in November adjusting his collar. About Beaumont saying: *knowing it and feeling it are different processes.* About all the things he had not said in letters and had carried instead in the list and the journal and the paper in his boot.

"Yes," he said. "I'm going to answer."

She looked at him steadily. She had always looked at things steadily; it was the quality that had made her right about him since they were children.

"Good," she said. "Come inside. Father is in the library. He has been in the library all morning pretending to read."

He handed Tonnerre's reins to Mercier, who took them without a word and led the horse around toward the stable, already looking at the building with the professional attention of a man assessing what needs to be done. Charles watched him go for a moment.

Then he followed Elise up the steps and through the door, into the house that smelled of books and bread and the specific morning light that came through the eastern windows in March at this latitude, and had always smelled this way, and was exactly itself.

His father was standing in the library doorway, which meant he had heard the horse on the drive. He was older, which Charles had expected, and the same, which Charles had hoped, and he looked at his son with an expression that had no performance in it at all.

"Charles," he said.

"Father."

They stood for a moment. His father moved first, crossing the room, and put his hand on Charles's shoulder, and Charles stood with his father's hand on his shoulder and the morning light through the windows and Elise at his side, and did not say anything, because there was nothing to say that the moment did not already contain.

* * *

He went to the upper fields that evening.

He went alone, after dinner, after the conversation that had gone on for three hours and had covered some of what needed to be covered and had left most of it for later, which was the right amount for the first evening. He walked up through the vineyards in the last of the light, the rows of vines on either side of him in the not-yet-green of early spring, the bare wood of them sharp against the evening sky.

The view from the top was the view he had held in his memory for two years. The Garonne in the valley, moving slowly in the evening light, the color of it exactly as he had remembered, the specific quality he had tried to describe to Margot standing at the Main in Frankfurt. It was the same. It was also different from the memory of it, because the memory had been carrying the weight of everything he was moving toward and the thing itself did not have to carry that weight.

He stood in the last of the light and looked at the river.

He thought about the list. Three pages, dense in the margins, in his coat pocket in the journal. He would keep the list. He would keep it for the rest of his life, because keeping it was the right thing to do, and because the names in it were real and had been real and the record should know they existed.

He thought about Margot, on the road east toward Posen, doing the work she was good at until she decided what came next. He thought about Mercier in the stable below, already assessing what needed to be done with the deliberate attention of a man who had found that he was approximately himself outside the army and was making his peace with that.

He thought about the plan. Five years, make Captain and go home. He had made Captain in two and a half years and had come home in three, which was not the plan exactly but was close enough that his father's instruction had been honored in the way his father had meant it: do what is required, develop what you have, come back and build something with it.

He had not built anything yet. That was what came next.

He looked at the fields in the evening light, the bare vines waiting for spring, the river moving in the valley below, the whole of the Bordeaux evening settling into its own quiet. He thought about what he knew now that he had not known when he left: what it cost to be good at destroying things, and what it meant to be ready to stop.

He thought about starting tomorrow.

The light went, the way light in the south went in March, not gradually but all at once, and he walked back down through the vineyards toward the house, where the windows were lit and Elise was visible through the glass at the table she always sat at, and his father's lamp was on in the library, and Mercier's light was in the stable, and the door was unlocked.

He went inside.
