# The Scattered Worlds: Transit

THE SCATTERED WORLDS

Book Two

TRANSIT


## Chapter 1: The Sound of It

Maria Webb | Meridian, Forward Section — Bridge

March 14–April 6, 2042 | Mission Elapsed Days 1–23

The sound was the first thing she had not expected.

She had expected the weight — the specific quality of artificial gravity in a rotating ring, which she had logged hundreds of hours in at Luna Base and which was close to Earth-standard but carried a subtle Coriolis drag if you moved quickly in the direction of rotation. She had expected the smell — recycled air running through the same scrubbers it would run through for the next forty-one years, carrying the faint mineral quality of systems that had been cycling for months of pre-launch testing. She had expected the light — LEDs calibrated to a full-spectrum cycle, the ship on a twenty-four-hour day that matched nothing outside its hull but that the crew's circadian rhythms required.

The sound she had not expected.

Meridian was not quiet. She had understood this intellectually — 4,312 people in a sealed environment, plus the environmental systems and the drive assembly and the agricultural rings and the manufacturing bays and the medical wing — but the intellectual understanding was different from the actual experience of standing on the bridge at 0300 on Mission Elapsed Day 3 and hearing the ship as a continuous, layered presence. The ring rotation hum. The HVAC at its particular frequency. The drive's deep-background vibration, felt more than heard, in the bones of the floor. The distant sound of voices from the corridor outside the bridge, which should have been empty at 0300 but wasn't, because the ship operated on three watches and the graveyard watch had its own population and its own rhythms.

She stood at the forward viewport and listened to the sound of 4,312 people existing around her, and understood for the first time what she had taken command of.


---

The departure had gone cleanly.

She had run the burn sequence the way she ran every burn sequence — completely, with her full attention on the specific task in front of her and the peripheral awareness she had developed in twenty-three years of flight tracking everything else. Cole had called every element. Meridian's systems had responded correctly. The departure had been, from a technical standpoint, nominal.

What had not been nominal was what came after: the first hour of post-burn coast, when the ship was on trajectory and there was nothing operationally required of her and she was standing at the commander's station on the bridge with the viewport showing the stars ahead and the growing distance behind, and understanding that she was not going to land this ship. She was going to hand it to someone else, in forty-one years, and that person was going to land it. She was going to be on it when that happened and she was going to be eighty-seven years old.

She had known this. She had known it for years. Knowing it and experiencing the first hour of the trajectory that made it real were different things, and the difference had a quality she did not have a word for.

She had stood with it for forty minutes and then called Cole to take the watch and gone to check the engineering deck.


---

The first week was operational. She had expected this and designed her schedule around it: the transition from launch configuration to cruise configuration required hands-on coordination that the automated systems could manage but that she wanted to run personally, because the first week of cruise was when you found out what the ship actually did rather than what the specifications said it would do. There were always gaps between those two things. You found them by flying.

The gaps in Meridian's first week were minor and all of them expected by her engineering team. The Ring Two atmospheric mixing showed a slight gradient in the southwest section that was within spec but that the environmental systems automatically compensated for on a tighter cycle than the model predicted. The agricultural ring's irrigation sensors were producing calibration drift in a pattern that Kofi's team had flagged as predictable given the launch vibration profile. The forward communications array had a positioning actuator that was running at ninety-four percent efficiency rather than the certified ninety-eight, which Hollis identified and corrected in three hours.

She documented all of them. She was going to be documenting the gap between specification and performance for forty-one years, and the first week's documentation would be the baseline everything else was measured against.

By the end of the first week the ship was in stable cruise configuration. She ran the cruise-phase status check with her senior team and the numbers were good.

"How does she feel," Cole asked after the check, which was not a technical question.

She thought about how to answer honestly. "Like something that knows what it's for," she said.

Cole nodded. He had the expression he had when she said something that landed correctly — not agreement, exactly, but recognition. He had been her first officer through the entire Phoenix mission and the four months of pre-launch operations and this first week, and in all that time he had never stopped being the person who understood what she meant when she wasn't being precise.

"The crew," he said.

"Tell me."

"The first cohort settled faster than I expected. The orientation held. People found their rhythms in the first week, which is earlier than the behavioral team projected." He paused. "The grief is present. It's not destabilizing, but it's present. Elena is running the early-transit support sessions and attendance is higher than projected."

"Higher is good."

"Higher is good," he agreed. "She says people are talking about the things they need to talk about. That's what the sessions are for."

Maria looked at the navigation display. They were eleven days and roughly 400 million kilometers from Luna Base. Luna Base was already invisible to the naked eye, which had not been true seven days ago. The distance was accumulating in a way she was tracking without deciding to track it, the way you tracked things that mattered.


---

She had a standing meeting with Elena on Tuesdays.

They had established it in the second week, not because it was on any operations schedule but because Elena had come to her on Day 9 and said: I need a read on you every week. Not a formal brief — I have enough formal briefs. A conversation. Twenty minutes.

Maria had agreed without asking why. She had known Elena for sixteen years. When Elena said she needed something, the operational response was to provide it.

The first Tuesday meeting was on Day 12. They sat in Maria's day office, which was small and functional and had a viewport that showed a quadrant of stars that had not changed perceptibly since launch and would not change perceptibly for years.

"How are you sleeping," Elena said.

"Consistently. Six hours, sometimes seven."

"Dreams?"

"Some. Nothing that interrupts the sleep."

"What kind of some."

Maria considered. "Boston. The impact zone. I haven't been there since 2025 but I still have a specific picture of it." She paused. "Departure. The burn."

"Are the departure dreams distressing?"

"No. They're accurate." She looked at Elena. "I dreamed the burn sequence correctly. All the numbers were right."

Elena had the expression she had when something confirmed rather than surprised her. "That's a consolidation pattern. Your mind is filing the departure as something that happened and is resolved rather than something ongoing. That's healthy."

"I know."

"You've been sleeping well for twenty-three years of high-stress flight operations. I'm not worried about you." Elena looked at the viewport. "I'm asking because the data is useful. The commander's sleep quality is one of my calibration points."

"Calibration for what."

"For understanding what the ship's stress level actually is, as opposed to what the official reporting says it is. You're very good at presenting nominal and being nominal. Some people are very good at presenting nominal and not being nominal at all." She was quiet for a moment. "I need to know which is which."

"I'm nominal," Maria said.

"I know. That's useful data." Elena stood up. "Same time next week."


---

On Mission Elapsed Day 18 they passed the point at which Earth was no longer visible to the naked eye from the observation deck.

ARIA announced it the way ARIA announced navigational milestones: a brief, factual statement in the ship's daily operations log. The announcement said: Sol is no longer visible to the naked eye from the ship's observation platforms at standard illumination settings. It would become visible again only with telescope assistance, and would remain at sub-naked-eye brightness for the remainder of the transit.

Maria was not on the observation deck when the milestone passed. She was in a meeting with the agricultural team about the Ring Three irrigation calibration data. She noted the ARIA announcement in her peripheral reading and returned her attention to the calibration discussion.

She went to the observation deck at 2200 that night, after the watch change, when the deck was mostly empty. A few people were there — she recognized Hartwell from the geology team, who was standing at the aft viewport with the telescope interface showing Sol as a bright point indistinguishable from the nearby field stars, and a pair of younger crew members she knew by face but not name who were sitting against the far wall, not looking at anything in particular.

She stood at the aft viewport for a while. Sol was there in the telescope view, a G-type star among the stars, surrounded by its neighbors in no particular configuration of significance. The sun. The solar system. Everything that had happened in the solar system for the past seventeen years, and before that for her entire life, and before that for all of human history.

She stayed for eleven minutes. Then she went back to her cabin and read the engineering status report and went to sleep.


---

On Mission Elapsed Day 23 she held the first all-hands.

Not a briefing — the ship had weekly briefings by cohort, and the information channels were functioning, and the crew had access to the operational status reports at the level of detail their role required. This was something different: the first time she had addressed the full complement of 4,312 people simultaneously since departure.

She had written and rewritten what she was going to say for five days. The final version was two minutes and twenty seconds long. She had timed it.

The all-hands came over the ship's audio system at 0900 ship time, when all three watches had overlap.

She said: We are twenty-three days out. All systems are nominal. The crew is adjusting well. The work we are doing right now — settling into the rhythms of the ship, establishing the routines that will carry us for the next four decades, learning how to live well together — is not preparation. It is the mission. The mission is not what happens when we arrive. The mission is what we build between here and there, and what we carry when we get there.

She said: I am going to be honest with you throughout this transit. When things are good, I will say so. When things are difficult, I will say so. You will not receive a more optimistic account from me than the situation warrants, and you will not receive a bleaker one. I owe you the accurate picture.

She said: We left Earth twenty-three days ago. October is coming. I know you are thinking about it. So am I. We will mark it together, and we will carry it together, and then we will keep doing the work. That is what we came here to do.

She closed the channel and sat in her chair on the bridge for a moment.

Then she opened the engineering status report.

The sound of the ship continued around her — the ring hum, the drive vibration, the distant voices — and she worked inside it, the way she had been working inside the sounds of ships for twenty-three years, and the way she was going to keep working inside this particular sound for the rest of her life.

It was enough.

More than enough.


## Chapter 2: What Grows

Adeyemi | Meridian, Agricultural Ring Three

April 3–19, 2042 | Mission Elapsed Days 20–36

The tomatoes were behind schedule.

Not significantly — four days, within the tolerance range the planting calendar allowed — but Adeyemi had been tracking the Ring Three yield projections against the mission parameters for three years before launch and he knew the difference between within tolerance and nominal, and these were within tolerance.

He stood at the end of Row 14 with his tablet and looked at the growth staging data and thought about what he was going to tell the weekly review.

The answer was: the tomatoes are four days behind and here is why and here is the adjusted projection and here is what we are watching. He had been giving this kind of report for two years at Luna Base and it was the right report to give: specific, honest, forward-looking. You told people what was happening, what you knew about why, and what you were going to do about it. You did not soften the four days or explain it away. You said four days and let the four days be what they were.

Still, standing in Row 14 with the curved wall of Ring Three overhead and the irrigation mist cycling and the smell that was the best smell on the ship — soil and water and the specific green-alive smell of plants doing what plants did — he found he was not distressed about the four days. He was curious about them.

The launch vibration profile had been within spec. The soil amendment ratios had been certified. The atmospheric composition in Ring Three was holding to within two percent of the target parameters. The light cycle was running correctly. Four days was not a system failure. Four days was the difference between what the model predicted and what the seeds actually did, which was a gap that existed because seeds were not models.

He made a note in his tablet: initiate secondary germination trial, Row 14 south half, amended calcium carbonate ratio plus three percent. Then he walked the rest of the row.


---

Ring Three was the largest of the ship's three rotating rings, and it was the one that smelled like a place.

Adeyemi had worked in controlled growing environments for fourteen years — first in Lagos, where the university's agricultural research station had been his introduction to the specific pleasure of feeding systems, and then in Houston as part of Elena's crew selection program evaluation team, which had led, through a chain of conversations and decisions he had not entirely planned, to his position on the Ring Three growing team. He had worked in greenhouses in three countries and on two continents and in the Luna Base agricultural module, and he understood what it meant for a growing space to feel like a place versus feel like a system.

Ring Three felt like a place. He had not been certain it would.

The curvature helped — you could not see the far end of the ring from any position inside it, which meant the space felt larger than it was, the way a forest feels larger than a field of the same area because you cannot see its edge. The twelve-hour light cycle was calibrated to match a latitude of about thirty degrees north, which was not arbitrary: the growing team had selected it because the majority of the crops in Ring Three were varieties optimized for that band, and the light simulation was accurate enough that the plants responded to it as if it were real. There were 847 crop varieties growing in Ring Three. He had checked this number four times before launch and had memorized it not because he needed to but because it was a number that felt important to carry.

He walked the ring for two hours every morning. Not inspection — the automated monitoring systems handled inspection, and the growing team's daily tasks were structured around the data those systems produced. Walking was something else. Walking was the version of paying attention that didn't have a data output.


---

He had met Mikael in the second week.

Ring Three had its own population — the growing team of twenty-two people, plus the roughly forty additional crew members who held food systems support roles and rotated through Ring Three on a regular basis. Mikael was one of the rotation crew: an agricultural systems engineer from Ring Two's production coordination team who came to Ring Three twice a week for cross-ring systems checks.

He had appeared on Day 9 at the south entry with a tablet and a toolbag and had looked at the ring the way people looked at it when they came for the first time since launch — with the expression of someone recalibrating their expectations upward.

"It's bigger than I remembered," Mikael said.

"You've been here before?"

"During the construction tours. Two years ago. But it was empty then." He looked at the rows, the trellises, the overhead irrigation network. "It's different with things growing."

"Everything is different with things growing," Adeyemi said.

Mikael had looked at him with the expression of someone who had just decided whether they were going to like a person, and had decided yes. He had the quality of his father — Adeyemi had not known Dmitri, had only heard him described by Elena during orientation, but the quality was recognizable: steady, direct, genuinely curious about the right things.

They had talked for twenty minutes about the Ring Two and Ring Three interdependencies — the atmospheric exchange, the water reclamation handoffs, the thermal load sharing that kept both rings within their operating ranges. It was the kind of conversation Adeyemi liked: specific, technical, with both people knowing their piece of the system well enough to understand how the pieces connected.

He had given Mikael the standing invitation to come to Ring Three on off-rotation days if he wanted to. Mikael had come four times in the first month.


---

The weekly growing review was on Fridays at 0900.

The Ring Three team met in the section office at the ring's midpoint, which was the only enclosed room in Ring Three and which smelled, inevitably, of soil. Adeyemi ran the review. The format was consistent: yield status by section, anomaly flags from the automated monitoring, water and atmosphere consumption versus projection, and an open floor for anything the team wanted to raise.

The Day 27 review had three anomaly flags: the tomato germination delay, a humidity variance in the northeast section that the atmospheric team had already identified and was correcting, and a single plant in the herb beds — a basil variety — that was showing signs of something the monitoring system had categorized as stress response but that Adeyemi's senior horticulturist, a woman named Osagie, had classified as most likely insufficient drainage.

"Most likely," he said.

"Eighty-three percent confident," Osagie said. She had a precise relationship with her own certainty — she did not round up or down. Eighty-three was eighty-three. "I want another forty-eight hours of data before I amend the drainage profile."

"Forty-eight hours," he said. "Flag it if it changes before then."

They went through the rest of the review. The numbers were good. The tomato delay was noted, the amended germination trial logged, the adjusted projection included in the weekly report to the ship's food systems coordinator. He sent the report at 1000.

After the review, while the team dispersed to their daily tasks, Adeyemi stayed in the section office and looked at the yield projections for the first full growing cycle. The ship's food system was designed with layered redundancy — Ring Three was one of three food production sources, alongside the protein synthesis bays and the preserved stores that were the emergency backup. Ring Three alone could not feed 4,312 people. Ring Three plus the protein synthesis bays and efficient rationing could. Ring Three running at full capacity was the condition under which the rationing became comfortable rather than managed.

He wanted Ring Three running at full capacity.

Four days behind on tomatoes was not going to stop that. He knew this. He was still going to fix the four days.


---

On Day 33 he went to one of Elena's early-transit support sessions.

He had attended two during the orientation period on Luna Base, which had been the mandatory sessions, and had not attended any of the optional ones. He was not avoiding them — he simply had not found, in the first month aboard, that he needed what the sessions offered. He had his work, which was good work, and the work kept him inside what was real and manageable. That was enough.

He went on Day 33 because the woman who managed the Row 8 legume beds — a quiet, precise person named Osei who had been on the growing team since the pool closed and who Adeyemi had come to rely on for the detailed monitoring work that required the most patience — had not come to the Day 31 or Day 32 morning walkthroughs. She had completed her tasks, her data entries were correct, she had not reported any physical or personal issue. She had simply not been present in the ring at the times she was usually present.

He was not her manager in any formal sense that extended to her attendance at morning walkthroughs. She was a full-team member with autonomy over her schedule within the task completion requirements. He had noticed the absence and had noted it and had not said anything, which he thought was correct.

He went to the session because he wanted to know what was available, in case it became relevant to mention to her.

The session was in the Ring Two common area — fifteen people, including himself and two others from the growing team he recognized. Elena ran it. The format was simple: no agenda, no required participation, a room where people could say what they were carrying if they wanted to, and could be in the presence of other people who were also carrying things if they didn't.

He did not speak. He sat near the back and listened.

The things people said were specific. A man who had left his mother in Nairobi and who was calculating, every day, what she would be doing at the time he was thinking of her, and finding the calculation both comforting and unbearable. A young woman who had been in the ship's engineering team since the pool closed and who found, now that the ship was real and flying, that the reality of it was different from the planning in a way she could not explain. A man from the navigation team who was sleeping badly and who had not told the medical team because he didn't want it on his record, and who said this in the session because the session record was protected, and Elena confirmed this without being asked.

After the session, walking back to Ring Three, Adeyemi thought about Osei and her absence from the morning walkthroughs and the specific quality of what the people in the session had been carrying, and he understood better what to watch for.

He went to Row 8 the next morning and spent twenty minutes there, not with any particular task, just present. Osei arrived at her usual time. They worked in adjacent rows. After a while she said: "The legume nitrogen readings are normalizing."

"I saw that," he said. "Good trend."

They worked without speaking for another hour. The ring was quiet around them, the irrigation cycling, the light at its morning setting.

He did not ask her anything. He was present. She was present. The plants were growing.

On Day 36, she was back at the morning walkthrough.


## Chapter 3: The Archive Begins

Strand | Meridian, Archive Office, Ring Two

May 2–28, 2042 | Mission Elapsed Days 49–75

The first question she had to answer was what it was for.

Not what it was — that was defined in her role designation, which read: Ship Historian / Governance Archivist, responsible for maintaining the official record of Meridian's transit and community life, including governance decisions, significant events, and oral histories as collected. The role had been designed by the governance working group in 2040 and had been ratified by the full crew pool during the constitutional review. There was a job description. There was a mandate.

What the job description did not answer was the prior question: what was a record for, when the people it was recording had no one to send it to?

On Earth, a record was for accountability. You documented what governments did so that citizens could evaluate them. You documented what happened so that courts could adjudicate. You documented corporate decisions and medical decisions and building decisions so that consequences could be traced back to causes. The record existed in a web of institutions and processes that gave it meaning — gave it teeth, as Thomas Rourke had said in the message exchange she had initiated in 2045, when she was three years into the archive and still working out what she was building.

On Meridian there were no courts in the external sense. No regulatory bodies. No press. No institutions outside the ship. The record would be read by the people it was about, and by their children, and eventually by whoever received the data package Meridian would transmit back to the solar system at specified intervals — messages that would arrive years after they were sent, addressed to a civilization that might not exist in any form Meridian's founding generation would recognize.

She had written about this problem in the first week. She had written about it badly, in the way you wrote about a problem you did not yet understand. She had saved the draft and set it aside and come back to it on Day 49 and written about it better, and what she had written was: the record is not for accountability. It is for continuity. It is the thread that connects what was to what will be. Its function is not to enable judgment. Its function is to prevent forgetting.

She had not been satisfied with this either. But it was closer.


---

Her office was a converted equipment room on Ring Two's inner circumference — ten square meters, a desk, a chair, two storage panels that held the archive's physical backup drives, a viewport that showed the ring's interior curvature rather than space because the archive office was on the wrong side for a space-facing viewport. She had not asked for a different office. The interior viewport was fine. She could see Ring Two's common area from it, which meant she could see people moving through their days, which was more useful than stars.

She had been given an assistant — a position her role designation authorized but that she had not filled during the orientation period because she had not yet known what she needed assistance with. In April she had asked for a candidate and had been given three names by the governance team. She had interviewed all three and had selected a man named Reeves, who was thirty-one and had a background in computational linguistics and data architecture, and who had a quality she valued: he asked the second-order question. Not what is the answer, but what kind of answer is this.

Reeves had set up the archive's technical infrastructure in the first two weeks: the filing taxonomy, the search protocols, the automated ingestion pipelines that pulled governance meeting minutes and official records into the archive without requiring manual entry. The technical work was his. The interpretive work was hers.

"What are we actually keeping," he had asked on his first day.

"Everything," she said, and then, seeing his expression: "I know that's not an answer. I mean: we're keeping more than the official record. The governance minutes and the decisions and the technical logs are going in automatically. What I'm doing on top of that is what you'd call oral history — conversations, interviews, context that doesn't show up in official documents."

"Who do you interview?"

"Everyone who will talk to me. Starting with the people who are least likely to be in the official record."

He had thought about this. "You're filling the gaps."

"I'm finding out where the gaps are."


---

Her first interview was Maria.

She had requested it in the third week, which she thought was the right timing — early enough that the launch was still immediate, late enough that Maria had settled into the ship's rhythms and was not in the compressed operational mode of the first two weeks. Maria had agreed to an hour on Day 52.

She brought her recording device and a set of questions she had been writing and rewriting for two weeks. She sat in Maria's day office, which was small and functional and smelled of coffee, and she started with the question she always started with: Tell me what you noticed that you didn't expect.

Maria had looked at her with the expression of someone who was going to answer precisely.

"The sound," Maria said. "I expected the gravity, the smell, the light. I didn't expect how much the ship sounds like itself."

Strand wrote this down. "What does it sound like?"

"Like 4,312 people existing." A pause. "The ring hum. The drive. The ventilation. The voices you hear when you shouldn't be able to hear voices, because the corridor is supposed to be empty but isn't, because the ship is never empty." Maria looked at the viewport, which was currently showing a quadrant of stars. "I've been flying ships for twenty-three years. I know what ship ambient sounds like. This is different because the people are here permanently. On a mission, the ambient changes when the mission changes. On Meridian the ambient is stable. It's going to be this sound for forty-one years. I notice it more than I expected to."

"Is that a good difference or a different difference," Strand said.

Maria considered. "Different," she said finally. "Good would imply I'd evaluated it. I haven't evaluated it yet. I've noticed it."

Strand wrote: she notices before she evaluates. This is either caution or precision. Probably both.

She asked Maria about the departure. About the first week. About what command meant on Meridian versus what it had meant on Phoenix, which was a question Maria paused on for long enough that Strand did not fill the silence.

"Phoenix had a mission profile," Maria said finally. "A defined sequence of tasks with defined success criteria. I knew what done looked like. On Meridian, I'm not sure I'll ever be done. The work I'm doing right now doesn't have a completion state. It has a continuation state." She looked at Strand. "I'm adjusting to that."

"Is adjusting difficult?"

"Yes," Maria said, without elaboration, in the way she said things that were simply true.

The hour ran to ninety minutes. At the end of it, walking back to the archive office, Strand looked at her notes and understood that she had gotten something important from the conversation but that she was not yet sure what it was. This was what good interviews felt like — the significance arrived later, when the notes had been sitting long enough to show their shape.

She wrote the Maria Webb entry that evening. It was fourteen pages. She marked it as the first formal oral history entry in the archive.


---

Her second interview was Elena, on Day 58.

Elena was different from Maria in every way that an interview subject could be different. Where Maria answered the question precisely and stopped, Elena answered the question and the two questions beneath it and then asked Strand what she had actually been trying to find out. Strand found this illuminating and slightly unnerving.

"You're doing something more than documentation," Elena said, about forty minutes in. She had the expression she had when she was framing something she had already understood. "The archive as you're describing it is also a community psychological resource. You're creating a shared memory for a group that will otherwise have fragmentary and individual memories of the same events."

Strand looked at her. "I hadn't framed it that way."

"You've been doing it that way regardless. The oral history component specifically — you're not just recording individuals' accounts. You're creating the raw material for a collective narrative." Elena looked at the viewport, at the Ring Two common area beyond it. "That's important. More important than you may be giving yourself credit for."

"Or more important than I should be doing alone," Strand said.

Elena looked at her with the expression that meant: yes, and you've just identified the next question. "Do you need more resources?"

"I need to understand what I'm building before I can answer that."

"Fair," Elena said. "Keep going. Tell me when you know."

Strand wrote the Elena Marsh entry that night. It was twenty-two pages. She noted in the margin: she sees what the archive is before I do. Use this. The archive should be what she described. Make it that.


---

By Day 75, the archive contained forty-one formal oral history entries, the complete governance meeting record from Days 1–75, the engineering and agricultural log summaries, the medical team's anonymized population health summary, and the first of what she planned to be monthly narrative essays: her own account of what the ship felt like, month by month, as seen from the particular vantage point of someone whose job was to watch.

The first essay was four thousand words. It was not about events. It was about texture: what people talked about in the Ring Two common area at 1800 on weekday evenings, when the dinner rush had subsided and the small clusters that were forming — the specific configurations of people who had found each other in the first two months and were becoming something — sat at the tables or stood at the viewport and talked. What the conversations sounded like. What they were usually about and what they were really about, which was the same thing they were always really about: the question of what they had left and the question of what they were going toward, and the daily work of living in the space between those two things.

She sent the essay to Elena.

Elena sent back three words: This is it.

Strand saved the message in the archive, in the personal correspondence file, with a note: first external confirmation that the project is what I think it is. Day 75. Keep going.

She kept going.


## Chapter 4: October

Dr. Elena Marsh | Meridian, Behavioral Systems Office, Ring Two

October 9–14, 2042 | Mission Elapsed Days 209–214

She had been preparing for this since April.

Not the date itself — she had been preparing for the date since before launch, since the crew orientation on Luna Base when she had stood in front of fourteen different cohorts and talked about what the October date would mean and how the community was going to mark it together. The preparation for the date was documented, structured, distributed. She had a plan.

What she had been preparing for since April was the gap between the plan and what the community was actually going to need. Those were not the same thing, and she had been doing this work long enough to know that the gap was always present and that the quality of the preparation was measured not by how well the plan held but by how quickly and accurately you could respond when it didn't.

She had been running the early-transit support sessions since Day 6. By October she had run forty-one sessions, had seen approximately 900 individual crew members at least once, and had a working picture of the community's emotional landscape that was more granular than any intake survey or population health report. She knew who was struggling and in what specific way. She knew who was performing fine and was fine. She knew who was performing fine and was not fine, which was the category that required the most attention.

She had also, in the sessions and in the casual conversations that happened throughout the ring, been taking the temperature on October. Not asking about it directly — asking about it directly was the wrong approach, because it elevated the anxiety rather than surfacing it. She had been listening for how people talked around it, which told her more than direct questioning would have.

What she had heard, by the time October arrived, was a community in a specific state: present with what was coming, not overwhelmed by it, waiting for someone to give them a form for it. The waiting had a quality she recognized from grief work — the specific tension of people who have prepared themselves as well as they can and who are now carrying the preparation alongside the knowledge that preparation is never fully adequate.

She had the form. She had been designing it since April. She had not been certain it was right until she ran it past Kim in September, and Kim had said: yes. That's right. That's what they need.


---

The October community sessions ran over six days, October 9 through 14.

Not because the date was October 14 — it was, the asteroid's nominal impact window centered on that date, which was why they had chosen it as the date when the mission had still been in the planning phase and when October 14th had existed only as a probability in a calculation. But the six-day window was deliberate: she did not want the marking to be a single point of intensity that people built toward and then crashed after. She wanted it to be a sustained space, a period of time during which the community held what it was holding collectively rather than individually.

The format for each session was the same: ninety minutes, open floor, no agenda beyond the acknowledgment that October was here and that being here for it together was the thing they were doing. She had trained the twelve facilitators in September, running three practice sessions with the facilitator team until she was confident they understood the difference between a therapeutic session and a community holding session. The therapeutic work was individual. This was community.

She ran the first session herself, on October 9, in the Ring Two main common area. Three hundred and twelve people came. The room was full.

She had planned opening remarks of four minutes. She gave them and stopped. Then she waited.

What happened in the first session was the thing she had hoped for and had not been certain of: people talked. Not every person talked. Perhaps sixty people said something in the ninety minutes, which in a room of three hundred was a small fraction, but the room was not the sixty people who talked. The room was all three hundred, sitting with the sixty voices saying the things that were also true for the others, and the collective quality of that — the not-being-alone-with-it quality — was the thing that mattered.

A man near the center said his daughter was in Jakarta and he had not been able to reach her via the relay for three weeks. He said this without drama, as a statement of fact that was also the heaviest thing he was carrying. The room held it.

A young woman who had been on the ship's engineering team since the pool closed said she was afraid of the wrong thing — not afraid of the asteroid hitting, but afraid of not being afraid enough, of being here in this ring doing this work while something enormous was happening at a distance she could no longer calculate in terms that meant anything. The room held this too.

An older man whose name Elena knew — Warren, whose wife had been on Meridian's crew since January 2042, whose wife called him every week and was 214 days into the transit — said his wife had recorded a message for him before the communication delay made real-time conversation impossible, and that the message was thirty minutes long, and that he had watched it eleven times, and that the thirty minutes contained everything she knew how to say and he knew how to hear, and that it was enough and not enough simultaneously. The room was very quiet after this.

She did not direct these contributions. She listened to them. She made note of specific people to follow up with individually. She watched the room's body language — the degree to which people were leaning in or pulling back, which told her whether the session was holding the weight or buckling under it.

It held.

After the ninety minutes she offered to stay for anyone who wanted to remain, and forty people stayed, and she stayed with them for another hour, which took her past her scheduled next task and which she had built buffer for.

Walking back to the behavioral systems office, she took the long route through Ring Three, past the growing rows where the evening light cycle was setting the tomatoes and legumes into their low-red late-day illumination. She walked slowly. She had done this particular kind of work for thirty years, and she still needed the transition space — the time between the session's weight and the next thing.


---

October 14th fell on a Wednesday.

She had a session at 0900 and a session at 1900, which bracketed the day without claiming it entirely. In between was the day, which the ship was going to live through in whatever way it lived through it.

The 0900 session was smaller — 148 people, the more private response to the date. These were, generally, the people for whom October 14th was not an abstraction. People with family in the impact probability zone. People who had been working on the deflection mission data and knew the trajectory calculations in the specific way that made the date concrete rather than symbolic.

She ran the session. She said what needed to be said at the beginning and then let the room say what needed to be said.

A woman named Carver, who had been in the trajectory analysis group before joining the ship, said: I ran the last calculation before we left Luna Base. The number was thirty-three percent. I have been carrying that number for 214 days. Today it is either the right number or the wrong number. I will never know which.

Elena looked at her. She said: that is an extremely important thing to say, and I want the room to hear it. You did your work precisely and honestly, and the answer to whether it was enough will never come back to you in a form you can use. That is the specific weight of your particular contribution to this mission. It is not a failure. It is the shape of what you did.

Carver looked at her for a moment and then looked at the table and then nodded very slightly.


---

The 1900 session was the largest of the six-day window. 641 people. Every ring on the ship had a session running simultaneously, coordinated to begin at the same time — 1900 ship time, which was synchronized across all watches for the occasion. It was the closest thing to a community-wide gathering that the ship's physical layout could produce.

Kim ran the Ring One session. Adeyemi, who had been attending sessions with increasing regularity since September and whom Elena had asked to serve as a community anchor in Ring Three, sat at the front of the Ring Three session and did not facilitate — just sat there, present, available, the way he was available in the growing ring.

Elena stood at the front of the Ring Two main common area and looked at 641 people and felt the weight of what they had all come to do together.

She said: Today is October 14th. We are 214 days from Earth. We do not know what has happened there. We will not know for months. This is the specific condition of our position in space — we live in the past of the place we left, receiving its signals after the fact, unable to send anything that arrives in time to matter.

She said: We are also here. We are alive and the ship is functioning and the work is continuing and we are, in the most literal sense, the thing we were sent to be. Not a backup plan. Not a contingency. The answer. This is what it looks like to be the answer, and it is not triumphant and it is not clean and it is not what any of us imagined when we agreed to be part of it. It is just this: a room full of people, on a day that matters, not alone.

She paused.

She said: I don't know what has happened. I know that we are here. I know that we are together. I know that the people we left behind, regardless of what October brought them, would want to know that we are here, that the ship is flying, that the work is continuing. Tell them, in whatever way you tell the people you carry with you. Tell them we are here.

The room was quiet.

It was the right kind of quiet — not the quiet of people who had run out of things to say but the quiet of people who were saying something without words. She stayed in it for thirty seconds, which was longer than thirty seconds usually felt.

Then she opened the floor.


---

Afterward, when the session was over and the room was clearing, Kim found her near the door.

"How are you," Kim said.

"Ask me tomorrow," Elena said.

Kim looked at her with the expression she had when she was deciding whether to push. She did not push. "Tomorrow," she said.

Elena walked to her cabin. She sat in the chair by her desk, the same chair she had been sitting in since launch, and she looked at the wall and she thought about Sofia.

Sofia was in Houston. Or had been in Houston as of the last relay, six weeks ago, which given the current communication delay meant Sofia had sent that message ten weeks ago, which meant she had sent it twelve days after the impact window opened. The message had said: I'm here. We are managing. The city is intact. I am thinking of you constantly. I love you.

She read the message again. I'm here. We are managing.

She did not know what managing meant. She did not know what the city's being intact meant in the context of whatever had happened on October 14th. She did not know if this message was the last one that was coming or if there were more behind it in the relay queue, slowed by the growing communication lag.

She closed the tablet.

She had done everything today that she had come to this ship to do. She had held the room and she had said the right things and she had been the person the community needed her to be. She had earned the right to sit in her cabin and not know things.

She sat for a while.

Then she got up and prepared tomorrow's session notes, because the sessions were not finished and the community still needed what they needed, and she was still the person who could provide it.

This was the work. It had always been the work.

She prepared the notes and went to sleep.


## Chapter 5: Dispatch One — Boston

Thomas Rourke | Jamaica Plain, Boston

October 15–November 3, 2042

He was in the monitoring office when it happened.

The impact was in the North Atlantic, approximately 400 kilometers southwest of the Azores. The object entered the atmosphere at a shallow angle — shallower than the 2025 Boston impactor, which had hit steep and fast and local. This one came in at about 18 degrees, which stretched the energy release across a longer atmospheric path and produced a sequence of events rather than a single detonation.

The sequence: a fireball visible across Western Europe, North Africa, and the eastern seaboard of the United States. An atmospheric shockwave that broke windows in Lisbon and knocked people off their feet in the Canaries. A marine impact that generated a water column two kilometers high at the strike point and a wave train that had thirty minutes to cross the Atlantic before it reached the Azores, an hour and a half before it reached the Portuguese coast, two and a half hours before it reached the Carolina beaches.

Thomas was in the monitoring office at 11:22 AM EST when the first alerts came through. He was looking at the implementation data from the third federal consultation cycle, a set of spreadsheets that Watts had sent over the previous afternoon, and the alerts arrived on his secondary screen: first the USGS seismic network, then the tsunami warning system, then the FAA ground stop order, then the emergency alert on his phone.

He read them in order.

Then he stood up and went to the window and looked at the Jamaica Plain street outside, which looked exactly as it always looked in mid-October — the trees in their second week of turn, a dog walker crossing at the corner, someone's music from an upstairs window — and he thought: the world is going to look like this for a little while longer and then it is going to look different.


---

The wave hit the Carolina coast at 1:51 PM EST. The surge height at Hatteras was eleven meters. The evacuation orders had been issued with two hours of warning, which was not enough warning for the permanent residents and was enough warning for most of the evacuation infrastructure to function.

The wave hit Nova Scotia at 2:18 PM. Height at Halifax harbor: six meters. The downtown core was inundated.

Boston was mostly protected by geometry — the wave energy had partially dissipated and refracted around Cape Cod, and the harbor's orientation reduced the effective surge height to approximately three meters at the waterfront. The seawall held. The neighborhoods below the seawall did not all hold.

Thomas drove toward South Boston at 2:00 PM, before the wave arrived, because the community center where Watts had been running the Thursday sessions was three blocks from the water and he wanted to know if Watts had gotten everyone out.

Watts had. He had been running evacuation drills in the waterfront communities since January 2041, after the tsunami warning system had been upgraded and the community monitoring office had updated its emergency protocols. The drills had been criticized at the time — the impact probability was 33%, people said, not a certainty, why are we drilling for a scenario that is more likely not to happen. Watts had run the drills anyway.

Everyone got out.

Thomas stood with Watts on the high ground above the flooded block at 2:45 PM and they watched the water covering the street where the community center was and neither of them said anything for a long time.


---

He wrote the first dispatch to Meridian on November 3rd.

Not because that was when he had processed what happened — he had not processed it by November 3rd and was not sure he would process it in any conventional sense, because the processing of large things happened in layers and over years and he was three weeks in. He wrote the dispatch because that was the right time: far enough from the immediate that he could be accurate, close enough that the specificity was still present.

He had the ship's address from Ben Oakes, who had called on October 16th in a call that lasted forty minutes and that was one of the most important conversations Thomas had ever had. Ben had given him the dispatch address — the relay protocol that the agency had established for crew family members to send messages to Meridian — and had said: the delay is currently about six weeks. By next year it will be longer. Send what you want to send.

Thomas sent:

October 14th happened. Impact location was North Atlantic, approximately 400 kilometers southwest of the Azores. The object was approximately 200 meters at its longest axis, entering at a shallow angle. The energy release produced an atmospheric event visible from six continents and a marine impact that generated a wave train across the Atlantic.

The Boston waterfront absorbed approximately three meters of surge. The seawall held. Watts's evacuation protocols worked. Everyone in the Thursday session cohort is accounted for. The monitoring office is intact.

The losses are significant. The Azores took a direct wave sequence. The Portuguese coast, Morocco, and the British coast from Cornwall to Wales sustained damage ranging from severe to catastrophic. The eastern seaboard of the United States sustained damage from Maine to Florida, heaviest in the Mid-Atlantic. Estimated deaths so far: between 800,000 and 1.2 million. The range reflects incomplete data from the Portuguese coast and from the island chains in the wave path.

I want you to have the accurate numbers. Not the broadcast numbers, which are optimistic, and not the worst estimates, which are not yet validated. The range I've given you is what the agency data says as of November 3rd.

I am still here. The work continues. The federal consultation framework implementation is ongoing. The third review cycle was interrupted by the October events and has been rescheduled.

I am sending this knowing it will take six weeks to reach you, and that by then you will have been living with the not-knowing for that long, and that the numbers I've sent will be less uncertain than they are now and that I cannot send updated numbers with this message because it only travels one way and at one speed.

I will send more when there is more to send.

Thomas


---

He sent the dispatch at 11:48 PM on November 3rd, sitting in the monitoring office with the October darkness outside and the implementation spreadsheets still open on his secondary screen.

He sat for a while after sending it. He thought about the ship — about Maria Webb on the bridge and about Dmitri's son Mikael in the agricultural ring and about all 4,312 people who were approximately 1.3 billion kilometers from Earth right now and who were going to receive his message in six weeks and who were going to learn what he had just told them.

He thought about what it would be like to be that far away and to get that message.

He could not fully imagine it. He could imagine the distance, approximately — the numbers existed — but the experience of being that distance and knowing that what had happened had happened and not being able to do anything about it was outside what his imagination could produce.

He hoped they were all right. He knew that was an inadequate thing to hope for and he hoped it anyway.

He closed the implementation spreadsheets. He would look at them in the morning.

Outside, Boston was doing what Boston had always done: absorbing what came and continuing. The water had receded from the flooded blocks. The seawall was intact. The monitoring office was intact. The Thursday sessions were rescheduled for next week.

The work continued.

He went home.


## Chapter 6: Cruise

Maria Webb | Meridian, Bridge and Forward Section

January 2043 | Mission Elapsed Days 293–324

Five months.

The ship had a rhythm now. She had been waiting for it — not impatiently, because she had learned years ago that operational rhythms could not be forced, only observed until they stabilized — and it had arrived somewhere around month three and had been consistent since. The graveyard watch was quieter than the day watch in ways that were not just about population. The Ring Three morning walkthrough produced a particular sound that carried into the common corridor. The senior engineering briefing on Mondays had a different texture than the Tuesday navigation check because the engineering team ran long when things were interesting and the navigation check ran precise regardless.

She knew these rhythms the way she knew ship sounds: not by analyzing them but by having been present for long enough that their patterns had registered without requiring her active attention.

She was also, five months in, beginning to understand which aspects of command on Meridian were not going to be like anything she had commanded before.


---

The council met on the first and third Wednesdays.

The governance framework called for a nine-member elected council with a rotating chair, monthly community meetings that were open to all crew, and a set of standing committees covering infrastructure, social systems, science, and conflict resolution. The council was not Maria's council. It had been elected by the crew in October, during the governance activation phase, and it operated with its own mandate and its own decision-making process.

Okafor had been elected council president by a margin that reflected, she thought, the community's good instincts: he was a systems engineer who had spent twenty years working on infrastructure projects that required holding complex interdependencies in mind simultaneously. He was not a politician. He was someone who understood how the pieces connected, which was a different and more useful thing for a ship.

Her relationship with the council was clear in the governance framework: she had operational authority over the ship's systems, safety, and navigation. The council had authority over resource allocation, community governance, and the social and cultural life of the crew. The two domains were theoretically distinct and practically adjacent at almost every significant decision point.

She had expected this. She had studied the framework for two years. She had even helped design some of the language in the operational authority section. Knowing the framework and living inside it were, as she was discovering, different things.

The first significant overlap had come in November, when the council had voted to allocate additional Ring Two common area space to a community event that the infrastructure committee had proposed — a weekly gathering that would use a section of the common area for approximately four hours on Friday evenings. The infrastructure committee had correctly determined that the allocation was within their authority. Maria had noted, in the engineering review, that the proposed layout would interfere with a ventilation maintenance cycle that ran on Friday evenings.

This was not a safety issue. It was a scheduling conflict.

She had brought it to Okafor directly, which was the correct channel, and Okafor had reconvened the infrastructure committee, and the committee had adjusted the event layout to accommodate the maintenance cycle. The process had worked. It had also taken four days and three separate meetings to resolve a scheduling conflict that she would have resolved in an afternoon on any previous ship.

She had said nothing about this to anyone except Cole, who had said: that's going to be every time, at least for the first year.

"Better or worse over time?" she had asked.

"Better," he said. "As long as neither side treats the other domain as subordinate." A pause. "As long as you don't treat the governance domain as subordinate."

She had looked at him. He had the expression he had when he was saying something she needed to hear that she was not going to like hearing.

"I know," she said.

"You know intellectually," he said. "The operational habit is different from the intellectual knowledge."

This was accurate and she had filed it and was working on it.


---

The message from Thomas arrived on January 14th.

The communication delay from Earth to Meridian's current position was approximately six and a half weeks. Thomas had sent the dispatch on November 3rd; it arrived January 14th. She read it in her day office, alone, at 0600 before the morning watch briefing.

She read the numbers twice. Between 800,000 and 1.2 million. The range reflects incomplete data.

She sat with the report for twenty minutes. She thought about the North Atlantic, which she had flown over hundreds of times in twenty-three years of commercial and military flight, and about the Azores, which she had landed at once on a routing diversion, and about the Portuguese coast, which she had never visited but which existed in her mind as a specific geography.

She thought about the thirty-three percent probability. The deflection had worked and had not worked enough, and the gap between worked and worked enough had cost between 800,000 and 1.2 million lives and the destruction of an indeterminate length of coastline on multiple continents.

She thought about the Phoenix mission. She had deployed three impactors within solution tolerance. The momentum transfer had been within the achievable range. The physics had produced an outcome at the lower end of what was achievable, not outside it. She had done what she had gone to do.

She had known this was a possible outcome. She had known it while she was deploying the impactors. Knowing it then and reading Thomas's message now were not the same.

She forwarded the message to Elena with a note: I'm forwarding this to you for community communication planning. Please advise on timing and format. I'll defer to your judgment on both.

Then she went to the morning watch briefing.


---

Elena came to her at 1400.

She had a plan, which Maria had expected. Elena's plans were always specific and always correct in their essentials, with room for adjustment in the margins.

"I want to tell the community in the next forty-eight hours," Elena said. "Not because there's urgency — the information is six weeks old and stable — but because there's no benefit to holding it. People know the impact happened. They've been living with the not-knowing for three months. The specific information, even though it's heavy, will be less destabilizing than the continued not-knowing."

"Agreed."

"I want to announce the information in a community session format rather than through the operations channel. The operations channel is for operational information. This is community information."

"Agreed."

"I'm going to recommend we run the announcement session tomorrow evening and have support sessions running in parallel in each ring. The support sessions should be available to anyone who needs them immediately after the announcement."

"How many facilitators do you need?"

"I have enough. I've been preparing the facilitator team for this since October." She looked at Maria. "I need you to be present at the main session. Not to speak — I'll speak. But present."

Maria looked at her. "You want the community to see me receive this information in the room with them."

"Yes. You're the commander. Your presence at a community session that carries this kind of weight tells the community something about what this ship is. It tells them the commander doesn't insulate herself from what they're carrying."

Maria nodded. "I'll be there."

"Thank you."

"Elena." She paused. "The message has a personal component. Thomas mentions that Sofia sent a message. That it arrived before the impact window. That she said the city was intact and she was managing."

Elena's expression did not change in any way that Maria could read. "I know," she said. "I have been receiving relayed messages from Sofia since launch. The last one I have is dated October 26th, which means it was sent twelve days after the impact." She paused. "It says she is all right. It says the city is mostly intact. It says she loves me."

"That's good," Maria said.

"Yes," Elena said. "It is."

She left. Maria sat in her day office and looked at the navigation display and thought about the specific weight of running toward one thing and away from another simultaneously, and about the fact that this was not a choice she had made but a geometry she was inside, and that the only available response to a geometry you could not change was to do the work correctly.

She opened the operations log.

The work was the work.

## Chapter 8: Before the Message

Adeyemi | Meridian, Agricultural Ring Three

May 2043 | Mission Elapsed Days 413–443

Everyone knew the message was coming.

Not the specific contents — those were Elena's responsibility and she was going to tell the community when she was ready to tell them and not before, which was correct and which Adeyemi understood and which did not make the waiting easier for the people in Ring Three who understood that the waiting was for information about October.

He had known this moment was coming since before launch. He had thought about it during the orientation sessions — the calculation was not difficult: impact window October 2042, communication delay from Earth's position to Meridian's position in the months after launch approximately six to eight weeks, therefore the earliest possible confirmation would arrive in December 2042 at the absolute optimistic end and more likely in the January-through-June 2043 window depending on when the confirmation was sent and what it said.

He had done this calculation in 2041 and had put it away and had not revisited it, because there was nothing productive to do with it between then and now. You could not prepare for news that had not arrived. You could only be ready, which was different from prepared.

He thought he was ready.

The gardens in May were the best they had been since launch.

"The ship doesn't have a perspective."

"The readings are good," Adeyemi agreed.

He did not comment on the attendance. He ran the walkthrough the way he always ran it: start at Row 1, move through the sections methodically, note what was growing and what was struggling and what was within tolerance and what wasn't.

People walked with him. They were quiet, mostly. Some of them had questions about specific rows; he answered them. Most of them just walked.

This was the thing he had learned since launch, the thing Elena had told him was what he was doing without quite knowing it: the ring was a resource. Not because of the plants, though the plants helped, but because of what the ring was — a place where something was growing, slowly and patiently, regardless of what was happening elsewhere. You could come here and walk the rows and the rows would be doing the same thing they were doing yesterday, which was: growing. The continuity of it was not comfort exactly. It was something quieter than comfort. It was evidence.

Evidence that the work continued. That the systems were holding. That the thing they had come to do — build something at the other end, carry the seeds of what comes next — was happening in this ring, row by row, every single day.

He walked the ring with twenty-six people and said almost nothing and arrived at Row 14 and checked the tomato germination status and wrote down the reading and moved to Row 15.

Outside the ring, somewhere in the ship's communication queue, a message was waiting.

The plants were growing.

He wrote down the Row 15 reading.


## Chapter 9: The Message

Dr. Elena Marsh | Meridian, Communications Office / Behavioral Systems Office

June 4, 2043 | Mission Elapsed Day 447

It arrived at 03:12 ship time.

The communications system logged it automatically and flagged it according to the protocol she had established with the communications team in January: all confirmed Earth-origin messages to be routed first to the behavioral systems office for review before distribution. The flag triggered a notification to her personal device.

She had been awake for two hours when the notification arrived. Not because she had known it was coming at 03:12 — she hadn't, the relay schedule was not precise enough for that — but because the kind of awareness she had been carrying since October did not sleep reliably.

She read the message in her office.


---

The message was from the agency's public information relay, a consolidated transmission that included confirmation of the impact, preliminary casualty figures, infrastructure status, and program continuation updates. It had been compiled on May 9th — about three and a half weeks before it arrived — and represented the agency's first official comprehensive summary to Meridian following the October events.

The numbers in the message were close to what Thomas's dispatch had said. Between 1.4 and 1.7 million confirmed or presumed dead. The Azores, the Portuguese coast, Morocco, the British west coast, the eastern seaboard. The infrastructure degraded but functional. The Framework governments intact. The program continuing.

She read it twice. She noted the specific numbers. She sat with them.

Then she opened the personal message queue.

There were thirty-one personal messages from Earth in the overnight relay. She did not read them — they were addressed to individual crew members and were private. She noted the count for the morning briefing and moved on.

Then she opened her own relay.

Sofia's message was dated October 26th. It had arrived in a previous relay that she had logged and set aside, waiting for the official agency confirmation to arrive before she processed the personal messages. The October 26th message said: I am here. The city is intact, mostly. The seawall held. We were lucky. We are managing. I think about you every day. I love you.

There were two messages after October 26th. One was dated November 19th. One was dated January 3rd.

She read the November 19th message.

Sofia was in Houston. Houston was inland — the wave track had not reached it. The November message said: Things are difficult here in the ways you would expect. The infrastructure disruption from the eastern seaboard has affected supply chains and there are shortages of things I did not previously think of as supply-chain dependent. I am keeping a list of things I did not previously think of as supply-chain dependent. The list is interesting and also occasionally infuriating. I am well. I am teaching. The students are very present with what has happened, which makes the teaching both harder and more worth doing.

She read the January 3rd message.

Sofia said: It is the new year. I have been marking your transit time on the calendar, which I know is slightly absurd but which also gives me something to do with the distance. By my calculation you are approximately 2.4 billion kilometers from Earth, which is close enough to the truth that I am going to say it is the truth. That is a very large distance and I am not going to pretend it is not. I am also not going to pretend that the distance changes what it changes — what I feel about the fact of you being out there, doing what you went out there to do. The distance is the distance. You are still you. I am still me. The calendar keeps moving.

She read it again. Then she put the tablet down on her desk and sat for a while in the early-morning quiet of the behavioral systems office.


---

At 06:00 she called Maria.

"It's here," she said.

"I know. The communications log flagged it at 03:12." Maria's voice was the voice she had at 06:00 — alert, not yet fully in the operational register, the border between sleep and the day. "Numbers?"

"Consistent with Thomas's dispatch. One point four to one point seven million confirmed or presumed dead. Infrastructure degraded, functional. The program is continuing. Second ship on track for 2044 launch."

A pause. "All right. What do you need from me?"

"I'm running the announcement session tonight at 1900. Parallel support sessions in each ring simultaneously. I need you at the main session in Ring Two — not to speak, just present."

"I'll be there."

"I also need you to know that I have personal messages from Sofia dating through January. She's well. Houston was not in the wave path."

A longer pause. "Elena."

"I know," she said. "Thank you for asking."


---

She spent the day preparing.

The preparation was mostly internal — the session format was already designed, the facilitator team had been briefed and re-briefed since January. What she was preparing was herself: running through the specific sequence of what she was going to say and how she was going to say it, the way she would have prepared for any high-stakes presentation, except that this was not a presentation. It was a community receiving difficult news, and her job was to hold the container while they received it.

She walked Ring Three in the morning, which she had been doing periodically since Adeyemi had told her, in October, that it helped people to be in the ring before difficult community events. She had not asked him to say this. He had said it the way he said things that were true: specifically, without prompting.

The ring was good. The growing was good. She walked two sections and let the smell of it — the particular alive smell that she had come to associate with the ship working the way it should — settle something in her that the morning's preparation had made tight.

She thought about Sofia's message. The calendar keeps moving.

She thought about the 1.4 to 1.7 million. The specific number on the specific day, which was also the specific number that was the result of everything she and Maria and Sarah and Elena and a thousand other people had built over seventeen years. The program had produced one outcome. The deflection had produced another. Both were real. The work had been the work and now the work was something different.


---

At 1900 the Ring Two main common area held 628 people.

She stood at the front of the room and looked at them — the faces she knew from sixteen months of sessions and the faces she knew from assessment and the faces she knew from the daily texture of the ship — and she said:

"We have received confirmation from the agency relay. The impact occurred on October 14th, as projected. The object entered the North Atlantic approximately 400 kilometers southwest of the Azores. The wave train affected the Azores, the Portuguese coast, the British west coast from Cornwall north, Morocco, and the eastern seaboard of the United States from Maine to Georgia."

She paused.

"The confirmed and presumed dead are between 1.4 and 1.7 million people. This number will not be fully resolved for years. It represents the loss of specific people — people who had names and families and histories, people who had colleagues and neighbors, people who were connected to people in this room."

She paused again. The room was completely still.

"The infrastructure damage is significant and not civilizational. The Framework governments are intact. The program is continuing. Luna Base is operational. The second ship is on schedule for launch in 2044."

She looked at the room.

"I want to tell you what I know about what this means for the people in this room, which is the part I can speak to directly. Some of you have family in the affected regions. I have been working with the communications team since January to process the personal message relay, and there are personal messages in the queue for crew members with family in or near the affected areas. Kim and the communications team have been working through the personal relay backlog since March and the messages are being distributed today."

A ripple in the room — not sound, but the physical adjustment of hundreds of people who had been holding a specific tension and who felt it shift.

"For crew members whose family is in the affected regions and who have not yet received a personal message: I want you to know that the absence of a personal message does not confirm the worst. The relay queue is complex, the backlog is real, and messages are arriving out of chronological order. Please speak with Kim or any member of the behavioral support team before drawing conclusions from the absence of a message."

She stopped. The room was very quiet.

"We received this news today," she said. "We are going to keep receiving it as more information comes through the relay over the coming weeks. We are going to sit with it together, and we are going to carry it together, and we are going to keep doing the work. Because the work is the answer. It was always the answer. And the fact that the answer was needed — the specific, terrible fact that it was needed — is not a failure of the answer. It is the reason the answer exists."

She opened the floor.


---

The session ran for two hours and twelve minutes.

Afterward, in the quiet of the emptying room, Maria came to her. They stood together for a moment — not speaking, not needing to.

"Good," Maria said finally, which was not a word she used loosely.

"Yes," Elena said.

She went back to her office. She read Sofia's January message one more time. The calendar keeps moving.

Yes, she thought. It does.

She wrote tomorrow's session notes and went to sleep.


## Chapter 10: After

Maria Webb | Meridian, Bridge / Forward Section

June–July 2043 | Mission Elapsed Days 447–488

She sat in the 1900 session without speaking for two hours and twelve minutes, which was the longest she had sat anywhere without a task in front of her since before the Phoenix mission.

Elena had asked her to be present, not to speak, and she had honored this exactly. She had chosen a seat in the second row, visible from the front, not in the back where she might have seemed to be observing from outside. She sat and she listened and she was the commander of the ship in a community session, and these two things were in her simultaneously and she let them both be there.

The things people said were specific and irreversible. A man who said his mother had been in Lisbon and he had not received a personal message. A woman who said she had known the probability was 33% and had chosen to come anyway and that choosing had felt completely different before October than it felt now. A young member of the navigation team who said almost nothing and then said: I keep thinking about the people who evacuated correctly and the wave hit them anyway. The room had been very quiet after this.

She had listened to all of it without directing any of it, which was what the session required and which was also, she discovered, the hardest kind of presence she had ever had to maintain.

Afterward she had said one word to Elena and gone back to the bridge.


---

She worked through the numbers herself.

Not because Thomas's dispatch or the agency message were inaccurate — she had no reason to doubt either — but because working through numbers was the thing she did when she needed to understand something at the level of precision that made it real rather than abstract. She took the impact data from the agency relay and the Thomas dispatch and the three subsequent relays that had arrived since the first official message, and she built a picture.

The picture was: a 180-meter object at shallow entry angle over the North Atlantic, kinetic energy equivalent to approximately 200 megatons. A marine impact producing a wave train. The affected coastline measured in thousands of kilometers. The affected population measured in tens of millions. The dead measured in the 1.4-to-1.7-million range that was the current best estimate, and which would narrow over years as the missing-persons accounting became possible in the damaged regions.

She compared the number to what she knew about the Phoenix mission. The three impactors had transferred momentum within Solution Two tolerance. The trajectory deviation had been real. The deviation had moved the probability from 56% to 33%, which was a reduction of 23 percentage points, which meant the deflection had prevented the worst-case scenarios — objects larger than 180 meters, steeper entry angles, different impact locations — that sat in the upper portions of the probability distribution. The 180-meter shallow-entry Atlantic impact was not the worst case. It was a case. The deflection had moved the probability downward and the specific outcome had come from the remaining probability.

This was what the numbers said.

She sat with the numbers for a long time. Then she filed the analysis in her command log — not for anyone to read, but because having it in the log made it a fact she had processed rather than a weight she was carrying without examining.

She did not revisit the question of whether she could have done more on the Phoenix mission. She had been through that question in January when Thomas's dispatch arrived, and she had been through it since, and the answer was the same each time: she had deployed the impactors within solution tolerance. The physics had produced the outcome the physics produced. The Phoenix mission had been correctly executed.

She had made her peace with this. Mostly. In the way that peace was available.


---

The council met on the first Wednesday after the announcement.

Okafor brought an agenda item she had not expected: a proposal for a formal community memorial, to be designed by a committee drawn from the crew, for the dead of October 14th. Not a one-time event — a permanent fixture, something that would be part of the ship's physical space.

She had not thought about this. She had been thinking about the operational response to the news and the community's psychological adjustment and the communications protocols for the ongoing relay. She had not been thinking about monuments.

Okafor presented the proposal clearly and without pressure: the council had discussed it in a closed session and wanted to bring it to the floor as a community decision, with design input from all cohorts, with a timeline of six months for design and construction. The initial concept was a space in the Ring Two common area — a small alcove adjacent to the main gathering area, with a surface for recording names, and a light installation that would cycle through a daily pattern connected to Earth's time zones.

She listened to the presentation and said: I support this. What do you need from my office?

Okafor looked at her with the expression he had when he had expected more friction. "Space allocation confirmation and materials authorization," he said.

"You'll have both by Friday."

Cole, walking with her to the bridge after the meeting, said: "That was efficient."

"It was a good proposal."

"You didn't ask any questions."

"I didn't have any questions." She stopped at the bridge entrance. "The memorial is governance work. He presented it correctly. I responded correctly. That's what the framework is supposed to feel like when it's working."

Cole looked at her. "It's been feeling like that more often."

"I know," she said. "I'm working on it."


---

In July, forty-six days after the announcement, she was on the observation deck at 2200 when Hartwell came in.

Hartwell was the geology team lead — the person who had been studying the pre-launch planetary survey data and who had been preparing the Proxima arrival protocols for three years. They had worked together during the Phoenix mission; Hartwell had been on the science team at JPL then, one of the people who had given her forty hours of asteroid dynamics tutorials. She was thirty-two now and had been on Meridian since boarding began, and she had the quality that Maria associated with good field scientists: she was more interested in what the instruments said than in what she expected them to say.

They stood at the aft viewport. The solar system was not visible — it had not been visible without telescopic assistance since Day 18 — and the forward viewport showed the direction they were going, which showed stars in the density pattern of the galactic plane, no single star distinguishable as the destination.

"Can you show me where we're going," Hartwell said.

Maria pointed. "Proxima Centauri. Magnitude eleven from Earth, not visible to the naked eye from there. From here — approximately two-thirds of the distance — we're not yet close enough for it to be visually distinct from the field."

"But it's there."

"It's there."

Hartwell looked at the viewport. "I've been thinking about the arrival protocols. After the announcement, after everything in June." She paused. "I've been thinking that what we're going to find there matters differently than I thought about it before October."

Maria looked at her. "How so."

"Before October, the arrival was the end of the transit. The completion. We get there, we do the survey, we establish the settlement, mission accomplished." She was still looking at the stars. "Now I keep thinking that the arrival is when we find out if there's somewhere to go. Whether the survey data holds. Whether the terminator ring is actually habitable. Whether—" She stopped.

"Whether there's something worth building," Maria said.

"Yes."

Maria looked at the viewport with her. The stars were where they always were — the galactic field, the destination hidden somewhere in it, forty years ahead.

"There is something worth building," she said. "We haven't surveyed it yet, and the data is strong, and the survey will tell us what it actually is. But yes. There is something worth building."

Hartwell was quiet for a moment. "How do you know?"

"Because we're going to build it," Maria said. "That's not circular. The value of the thing is partly in the building." She paused. "The arrival is not the end. The arrival is when the real work starts."

Hartwell nodded slowly. She had the expression she had when she was incorporating something into a framework rather than just hearing it.

They stood at the viewport for a while. The stars were where they were. The ship was carrying them toward one of those stars at eleven percent of light speed, which was fast enough and not fast enough, which was the condition they would be in for thirty-six more years.

It was enough.

Maria went back to the bridge. There was an engineering review at 0800 and she had not read the preliminary report yet.

She read it.
