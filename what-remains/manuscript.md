# What Remains

A Novel


## Chapter 1

He called from Portland at nine-fifteen, as promised.

She was in the equipment cage when her phone went, doing the kind of inventory work that didn't require much thought, which was why she was doing it. The cage smelled like rubber and dry nylon and the particular mineral smell of gear that had been in smoke. She knew the smell so well it didn't register as smell anymore. It registered as home.

She set down the harness she was examining and answered.

"Portland," she said.

"Just pulled off the freeway." His voice was good. Warm, unhurried, the same register he'd used all summer in her briefing room and her corridor and the field. She'd been hearing it for five months. She had not yet found the frequency at which it stopped having any effect on her. "Hotel's near the airport. I have an early flight."

"Okay."

"How's the base?"

She looked through the cage wire at the training slope, gold in the evening light, empty. The crew was gone. The season was over. The particular silence of a place that had been running at operational capacity for five months and was now, suddenly, not.

"Quiet," she said.

"Remy."

"It's quiet. That's accurate."

She heard him exhale. Not impatience. Something warmer than that.

"I know," he said. "I know it is."

She didn't say anything. She turned the harness over in her hands, checking the stitching the way she'd been checking it when he called, pulling at the webbing to feel for the slight looseness that meant wear rather than damage.

"November," he said.

"November."

"I'll have an address for you."

"Okay."

He was quiet for a moment. She could hear the ambient sounds of wherever he was, traffic, a door, the particular acoustic quality of a parking lot.

"Are you all right?" he said.

She thought about the question the way she thought about all questions, with the portion of her attention it deserved rather than the deflecting fraction she'd been allocating to it for most of her adult life. She had made a decision to stop doing that. The decision was three days old. She was still learning what it required in practice.

"I will be," she said. Which was not the same as yes and was more honest than yes and he would hear the difference because he heard those differences.

"Okay," he said. Nothing else. Just okay, and then: "Get some sleep."

"You too."

She hung up and stood in the equipment cage for a moment with the harness in her hands and the empty base around her and the October dark settling into the edges of the sky.

She had four months.

She knew what she did with four months. The same thing she'd done for twelve previous off-seasons: maintenance work, the equipment, the reports, the planning documents for the following year. The physical rhythm of keeping the base ready for the season it didn't know yet it was going to have. She was good at it. She'd been good at it long enough that the structure of it had come to feel like a choice rather than a reflex.

This year it was going to feel different, and she knew it, because the structure was the same but the thing she'd been using the structure to avoid was different. Usually she was avoiding the possibility of feeling something. This year what she was avoiding was the full weight of having felt it.

That was new territory. She'd file that and come back to it.

She finished the harness check. She moved on to the next one.


---

The base had thirty-one pieces of major equipment that required end-of-season inspection before they went into storage. She had documented this process for eleven seasons at Redmond and she could do it in her sleep, which was useful because the first week of October felt, in certain ways, like a kind of sleep. Not the dangerous kind. The restorative kind. The kind that happened after something requiring sustained intensity when the body needed to stop.

She ran through the checklist. Reserve parachutes logged and sent to the loft for repack. Jump harnesses catalogued by condition, worn items pulled for replacement. Helmets. Goggles. Saws. Bladder bags. The Pulaskis and McLeods ranked in the tool room by condition, the handles she'd replaced herself over the course of the summer tagged for her own records.

She worked in the mornings and some of the afternoons and let the evenings be what they were, which was mostly quiet and mostly hers.

He called when he said he would, most nights, from New York. The calls were short. Neither of them had developed, yet, the specific vocabulary of people in the habit of talking at a distance, the particular fluency that came from practice. They were still learning what the distance required. She was better at physical terrain than emotional terrain, but she'd been saying that about herself for fifteen years and she was starting to recognize it as a description that had stopped being accurate somewhere in the last five months without her updating the record.

She ran the fence line in the mornings. The same route she'd run all summer, out past the training slope and along the eastern perimeter and back along the north, the circuit she knew the way she knew her own hands. The October mornings were cold now, genuinely cold, the frost still on the grass at first light, the eastern ridge sharp against a sky she always checked out of habit for smoke and found clean.

She passed the spot at the fence line where she'd ended it with Reid in July. She passed it every morning and catalogued what she felt about passing it, which was not guilt and not exactly grief and not relief either. Something more like the particular quality of terrain you've already crossed. You note it. You don't cross it again.

She kept running.


---

Torres called on a Thursday, ten days in.

"How are you doing," Torres said. Not a question, exactly. Torres had a way of delivering the question as if she'd already gathered the information and was giving Remy the opportunity to confirm or correct.

"Working through the equipment inspection," Remy said. "Sixty percent through the major items."

"That's not what I asked."

"I know."

Torres was quiet for a moment. In the background, Remy could hear what sounded like a coffee shop, the ambient noise of other people's conversations.

"I watched him pack the truck," Torres said. "Monday morning. He was done in forty minutes. He's efficient."

"He is."

"He didn't look at the base when he drove out. He looked at you."

Remy didn't say anything.

"I'm not making a thing of it," Torres said. "I just. I noticed. That's what I do."

"I know what you do."

"Are you coming to the end-of-season thing next week? Vasquez is organizing it. There's a dinner. Nothing serious."

She thought about the end-of-season dinner, the crew ritual she'd attended for eleven years, the particular quality of an evening with people who had spent the summer being professional at each other and were now, briefly, permitted to be slightly more human than that.

"Yes," she said. "Where?"

Torres told her. She wrote it down.

"Reid called when he got to Portland," Torres said. Not quite a question.

"Yes."

"Good." A pause. "That's good, Remy."

She could feel Torres not saying anything else about it, the deliberate restraint, which was one of the things she valued about Torres. She knew where the line was and she stayed on the right side of it.

"See you Thursday," Remy said.

"Thursday," Torres said, and hung up.


---

She went to Bend the second week of October.

Her mother was expecting her. This was not unusual. What was unusual was that she drove down without a specific reason, without the practical errand that had usually been her cover story for visits, a thing needing repair or a document needing a signature, some operational necessity she could hand her mother to explain why she was there.

She drove down because she wanted to see her mother's face. That was the whole reason. She was still getting used to the idea that wanting something could be sufficient justification for going after it.

Her mother opened the door before she got to the porch.

"You look tired," Elena said.

"I'm fine."

"I know you're fine. I said you look tired." She held the door open. "Come in. I made soup."

The kitchen was the kitchen it had always been, the same table, the same window above the sink looking out at the backyard where the aspen had gone gold. The photograph was on the sideboard where it had always been, Thomas Reyes in his jump gear, seventeen years old, grinning at whoever was behind the camera. She looked at it when she came in and kept looking.

Her hands. The shape of them, the way they rested at the wrists. She had her father's hands. She had known this for a long time, had been aware of it in an intellectual sense, but something in October had changed the quality of that awareness. It was a different kind of knowing now.

"Sit," her mother said. "The soup needs five minutes."

She sat.

"How's the base?"

"Quiet. Equipment inspection. The usual."

Her mother moved around the kitchen with the efficiency of someone who has done this particular set of tasks so many times the movement is automatic, leaving the attention free. Elena had always been this way. Competent in the background. Watching without appearing to watch.

"He drove to Portland Monday," Remy said.

Her mother kept stirring the soup. "I know. You told me."

"He's in New York now. The edit."

"How long does that take?"

"Most of the winter, he thinks. He has three months of footage."

Her mother set two bowls on the table and sat down across from her. She looked at Remy the way she'd been looking at her for thirty-some years, with the particular patience of someone who has decided to wait for the actual question and not accept the proxy questions offered in its place.

"I'm going in November," Remy said. "New York."

Her mother's expression didn't change. "Good."

"I've been twice. For training conferences. I didn't find it comfortable."

"No, you wouldn't." A pause. "You'll find it interesting."

Remy looked at her mother. She thought about what it was to be seen clearly, in this kitchen, by this woman, and not to have to construct a version of events that required less explanation.

"I told him what you said," she said. "About doing it again."

Her mother looked at her steadily. "Did it help?"

She thought about the fence line and the October morning and everything she'd understood standing at it. The decision that had taken fifteen years to make and three seconds to say.

"Yes," she said.

Her mother nodded once, as if this confirmed something she'd already known, and lifted her spoon.

They ate the soup. Outside the aspen moved in the wind, the gold of them, the particular October light that was different from the September light in a way she had always been able to identify without being able to name. Her father's hands rested in her lap, her hands, both things at once.

She stayed until after dark and drove back north on the empty highway with the Cascades invisible to the west and the high desert stretching flat and dark to the east and the October stars very clear above all of it.

She thought about November.

She thought about a grid of streets she'd have to look up and an address she'd been promised and what it meant to be in someone's world on their terms, in the space they'd built, with the life around them that was their actual life.

She had been trying to prepare for it. She recognized this as a reflex and not a plan. You could not prepare for terrain you hadn't walked. You could only go and read it when you got there.


---

The last week of October, Danny came back.

She hadn't expected him until spring and she looked up from the tool room doorway to find him crossing the parking lot with a bag over one shoulder and his particular loose-jointed walk that she'd been watching for seven seasons.

"What are you doing here," she said.

"Visiting." He stopped in front of her. He looked good. The summer had been hard on his knee and he was walking evenly, which was information. "Thought I'd help with the end-of-season cleanup."

"I'm almost done."

"I know. I'll help with the rest." He looked at the base, the empty lot, the tool room behind her. "It's strange without the crew."

"It's the off-season. It's always like this."

"Not always." He looked at her. "How are you doing?"

She thought about how many people had asked her that in the last month and what it meant that she'd begun, slowly, answering it honestly instead of deflecting.

"Better," she said. "Actually better."

He nodded. Something in his face settled into something she recognized as relief, the particular relief of someone who has been waiting a long time to hear a specific thing and has finally heard it.

"Good," he said. Then: "You told me something important. At the end of the season. I know it wasn't easy."

"No."

"I've been thinking about it."

She waited.

"I don't blame you for it," he said. "I want to make sure you know that. The drainage, the position, the seconds. I know what happened. I also know why you've been carrying it and I." He paused. "I'm glad you put it down."

She looked at him steadily. The morning was cold, the sky above the parking lot the thin blue of high autumn, and somewhere beyond the eastern ridge a raven was making its sound at intervals, regular and indifferent.

"Thank you," she said.

"Also," Danny said, "Reid Matthews."

"Yes."

"I've been processing that."

"Okay."

"I think it's good." He said it carefully, the way he said things that mattered to him, with a specificity that kept them from being larger than they needed to be. "I think he's the kind of person who does the work. And you need someone who does the work."

She thought about a journalist who had put down a camera in the shale and caught a man who was going down. She thought about five months of patience that had never made her feel responsible for it.

"He does the work," she said.

"Okay then." Danny picked up his bag. "Show me what's left. I'll start on the McLeods."

She showed him what was left. They worked through the afternoon in the quiet companionship of two people who had spent seven seasons developing a specific shorthand, and by four o'clock the tool room was finished and the light was going gold and long across the training slope.

She made coffee in the mess kitchen and they sat at the table and looked out at the slope the way they'd looked out at it for seven years, and the quality of the looking was different now in a way she couldn't precisely name but could feel, some weight gone out of it that had been there so long she'd mistaken it for the view.

"November," Danny said.

"Yes."

He wrapped his hands around his mug. "New York's loud."

"I know."

"You'll be fine."

"I know." She looked at the slope. The gold of it, the last grass of the season, already past the point of carrying fire. "I know I'll be fine."

She meant it. That was what was different. She meant it not as reassurance but as accurate assessment, the way she assessed terrain, without the portion of her that had always been tracking the exits.

She drank her coffee. The training slope caught the last of the light and held it briefly before the October evening took it.

She went back to the base and finished the last of the work. She filed the end-of-season reports. She checked the equipment storage one more time and locked the cage.

She was ready for November, or as ready as she was going to be. She had the address. She had a flight.

The direction had changed. She was still getting used to walking in it.


## Chapter 2

He sent the address in the first week of November, a street in Brooklyn she had to look up on her phone to find on the map, and the map showed her a grid she could read in the abstract but could not yet feel the weight of.

She flew into JFK on a Tuesday morning and took a car service across the bridge because she had heard that cabs were variable and she had no patience for variable. The city arrived incrementally through the window. The bridge first, then the water below it, gray-brown and enormous, and then the streets and the density of them and the particular quality of New York traffic that was less like driving and more like water finding its level.

She found the building. A brownstone on a residential block, four stories, the front steps worn concave in the middle from years of use. She stood on the sidewalk for a moment with her bag over her shoulder and looked at it.

She read it the way she read terrain. What the surface told you about the structure underneath. This block had been here for a long time. The trees were old, the roots pushing up the sidewalk in places, and the brownstones across the street had the settled look of buildings that had stopped trying to be anything other than what they were. It was not beautiful in the way the Oregon landscape was beautiful. It was interesting. She had expected not to like it and found she was willing to revise.

She rang the bell.

He opened the door and she stood in the entrance with her bag and looked at him and there was a moment, one specific second, where the accumulated distance of six weeks collapsed into the fact of him being here, in front of her, unhurried, the specific lines of his face and the particular quality of his steadiness, and she understood that she had been managing the distance more actively than she'd admitted to herself.

"You found it," he said.

"You sent a good address."

He smiled. Not the careful smile he'd deployed all summer in her domain, the one that gave her room. This was easier than that. Unguarded. She catalogued it as new information.

He held the door and she came in.


---

The apartment was on the second floor, one flight up, and it was smaller than she expected and denser than she expected and more organized than she expected. A working space that had made certain concessions to being a living space rather than the other way around. Two monitors on a desk that occupied most of one wall. Shelves of notebooks, spines out, dates written on the edges in a handwriting she recognized from the cache log. Equipment cases stacked in the corner near the door, the same cases she'd watched him load into the truck in October, now in their proper geography.

A kitchen that was genuinely small but had a good coffeepot and a window that looked out at the building next door, which was close enough that she could see its brickwork clearly. A bookshelf that had run out of shelf and moved to a stack on the floor. A bed through a half-open door.

She stood in the middle of it and read it.

"It's not much square footage," he said.

"No." She looked at the monitors, the notebooks. The way the desk was positioned at the window to catch the light. The coffeepot, which was the good kind. "It works for what you need it to do."

"I've been here six years."

She picked up the nearest notebook from the shelf. His handwriting on the spine, a date from four years ago. She set it back.

"The editing suite is on the other side of the bridge," he said. "Rented space. Most of the technical work happens there."

"What's here?"

"The thinking." He looked at the desk. "I draft there. Notes, structures. Things I'm trying to work out before the footage tells me if I'm right."

She looked at his desk and thought about her own process, the way she read terrain before a jump, the mental map built from everything she knew and what the map couldn't account for. Different domain, same structure.

"Show me the footage," she said.

He looked at her. "Now?"

"You've been editing for six weeks. Show me where you are."


---

The footage was three months of her life rendered into something that already had a shape. She could feel the shape before she understood all of it. The way he had organized it, the sequences he was working with, the cuts he was considering. He sat beside her at the desk and walked her through the assembly edit on the monitor and she watched.

She watched herself.

Not the version she designed to be seen. Not the deliberate public face, the competent foreman, the Remy who ran briefings. The version the camera had caught when the camera was running and she had either forgotten it was there or stopped being careful because there was something more immediate to be careful about. She read a fire line in one sequence and her face was entirely absorbed, the calculation visible in the set of her jaw and the angle of her attention, and she looked at it the way she looked at footage she'd reviewed after training exercises, the same objective attention.

"That's the second week," Reid said.

"I know." She recognized the terrain.

"This one's from the Whitman fire." He advanced the timeline. Her hands on a map, the one from the briefing room, moving the way she always moved them. He had been filming from the doorway. She hadn't known.

She watched her hands.

"Is it all right?" he said.

She thought about the question. She thought about what it meant to watch yourself rendered accurately, not performed, not curated, just yourself doing the work you do, caught by someone who had been paying close enough attention to know the difference between what she showed and what was actually there.

"It's accurate," she said. Which was the most honest answer.

"That's what I was trying for."

She looked at the frozen image on the screen, her hands on the map, the particular tilt of her head that she'd never once seen from the outside. She thought about the cache log footage, the drive she'd given him and then taken back. The four seconds. The fact of what he'd chosen not to include.

"The section about my father," she said.

He didn't hesitate. "I can show you."

He found it in the timeline. She watched. Thomas Reyes, public record, rendered carefully, the facts of his life and death handled without flourish and without evasion. Three minutes. A still photograph from a regional newspaper, the date of the accident, the outcome in plain language. And then a cut to terrain, the Ochocos from the air, the ridgelines and drainages she had been reading her whole life, and nothing more.

He had not editoralized. He had not explained. He had given the audience the facts and trusted them to feel what there was to feel.

"You didn't try to connect it," she said.

"No."

"To me. To why I do this."

"No." He looked at the screen. "Anyone watching will connect it. You don't need to be the one who says it."

She thought about what that had cost in craft terms, the restraint of not explaining, the discipline of trusting the audience to carry the weight. She thought about how well she understood that particular discipline.

"It's good," she said. Meaning it fully, the way she meant things that were simply true.

He looked at her. The monitor light in the dim room, the November afternoon outside the window going to gray. His face in that light, the particular way he looked when he'd been working, slightly drawn, entirely present.

"Are you okay?" he said.

"Yes." She looked at the frozen image on the screen. Her father's face, younger than she'd ever known him, grinning at whoever was behind the camera. The same grin she'd seen in the photograph on her mother's sideboard. "I'm okay."

She was. Mostly. Something about seeing it made it real in a way the abstract had not been. She had known the section existed. She had not known what it would feel like to watch it, to see her father's life handled with that specific care by someone who understood what it meant to her without her having to say it.

She was okay. The okay had some weight in it. That was allowed.


---

He made dinner in the small kitchen while she sat on the counter, which was not a place she would normally sit, but the kitchen was too small for two people to occupy comfortably at the same level and the counter gave her a place that was out of his way. He cooked the way he did most things, without unnecessary motion, and the kitchen smelled like garlic and something she identified as thyme.

"There's a restaurant on DeKalb," he said. "We can go tomorrow."

"This is fine."

He looked at her over his shoulder. "You sure? I know the kitchen situation."

"I've cooked in worse. On deployment."

"You have lower standards than I do for kitchens."

"I have accurate standards. A kitchen that makes hot food is a kitchen."

He made a sound that was almost a laugh. She filed it. New information. He was less careful here, in his own space, the particular relaxation of a person on their own ground. Not careless. Just not performing the patience she'd been watching him perform all summer in her world. She was glad to see it. She had wondered what he was like when he wasn't being careful.

He was like this. Easier. Warmer in the way of someone who didn't need to be precise about the warmth because there was no strategic reason for it.

"There's a park," he said. "Prospect Park. It's not far. I'll show you tomorrow if you want."

"Okay."

"The grid is mostly comprehensible. Brooklyn's easier than Manhattan."

"I can read a grid."

"I know you can." He set a bowl in front of her. Pasta, which she had not been expecting, with a sauce that smelled better than it had any right to given the size of the kitchen and the time he'd taken. "I just wasn't sure if you'd want to navigate or whether you'd want to know the neighborhood first."

She looked at him. He was still standing, his own bowl on the counter beside her, and he was close enough that she could see the details she'd been memorizing at a distance for six weeks. The lines of his face. The way he held himself when he wasn't in the field.

"Reid," she said.

"Yes."

"I've been here for four hours."

"Yes."

"I know you know how to read rooms." She held his eyes. "You don't have to be careful with me."

He looked at her for a moment. Something in his expression shifted, settled.

"Good," he said, and he set his bowl down and kissed her.

It was different from the base. Warmer, less charged, the way he'd been all evening, easier in his own space. She had her hands full of pasta bowl and she set it down and he was close and his hands were at her waist and the November city was outside the window and she thought for one second, clearly: this is what I said yes to. This specific thing. The ordinary weight of it.

Then she stopped thinking and let it be what it was.


---

Later, in the dark of his room with the city sounds at the window, she lay still and listened to the sound of a city that never fully quieted. Traffic. A siren at a distance. The particular quality of a building containing other people's lives, sounds she couldn't identify, someone's television two floors up.

She was used to the sound of the base at night. The absolute dark of it, the wind in the pines, the occasional machinery sound from the airfield. This was different in every sensory detail. The mattress, the smells, the sounds, the weight and warmth of him asleep beside her.

She was not uncomfortable. She catalogued this as something worth noting.

She was in someone else's world on someone else's terms and she was not looking for the exit. That was new. That was specifically and deliberately new and she intended to stay in it.

She looked at the ceiling of his room and listened to the city and thought about the October ridge line, the way she'd watched it find itself in the morning light. She thought about her father's hands, her hands, both names at once.

She was Remy. She was Maren. She was lying in a Brooklyn apartment in November and she was, for the first time in a long time, not counting the ways she could leave.

The city made its sounds. She let them be what they were.

She slept.


## Chapter 3

She woke before him, which she'd known she would.

She lay still for a moment and listened to him breathe, the specific rhythm of deep sleep, and looked at the ceiling and thought: this is the morning after. She had not spent a morning-after in someone else's space in longer than she'd thought about carefully, long enough that the protocol felt uncertain in a way that irritated her. She knew how to navigate most things. She would figure this out the same way she figured out other terrain she hadn't walked: by walking it.

She got up quietly and found her clothes in the dim light and went out to the main room.

The apartment in the morning was different from the apartment in the evening. The light through the kitchen window was gray and flat, November light, the angle of it low against the building next door. She could hear the street two floors down, a delivery truck idling, someone on a phone. The city was already going.

She found the coffeepot and the coffee and made it the way he'd made it the night before, measuring carefully. Then she stood at the kitchen window with the mug and looked at the brick wall of the building next door and thought.

She thought about the footage. About her father's face in the monitor's light, handled carefully, made into something honest without her being asked whether she wanted it made into something. He had not asked her about the section. He had told her what he was doing and done it. That was the agreement they'd made. She had given him the season. He had made something of the season that was his to make.

She had agreed to that.

What she hadn't agreed to, hadn't anticipated, was what it would feel like to watch it. To see the three minutes he'd made of her father's life and understand that those three minutes would be what strangers knew. The facts of Thomas Reyes, rendered carefully, without interpretation. It was good work. It was honest work. She had told him it was good and she had meant it.

She was also still working out what it cost her. Both things were true.

She heard him get up. The sound of his footsteps on the wood floor, the bathroom, and then he came out to the kitchen and stopped in the doorway when he saw her.

"Coffee's made," she said.

"I can see that." He looked at her for a moment, reading her the way she read things. He was good at it. "Are you okay?"

"Yes."

He came into the kitchen and poured a mug and stood on the other side of the small space. They were close enough to touch and he wasn't touching her, which she read as intentional. Giving her room.

"The section about your father," he said.

"Yes."

"I should have warned you. Before we watched it. I thought you'd want to see it directly but I should have told you what was in it first."

"It's not about being warned." She looked at her mug. "It's good, Reid. I told you it was good and I meant that. I'm still." She paused. "I'm still working out what it costs to watch it. That's not a criticism."

"I know."

"I knew this was part of it." She looked at him. "I gave you the season. I knew what I was giving you."

"You gave me more than the season."

The room was quiet. Outside, the delivery truck had moved on and the street below was making its ordinary morning sounds.

"I know," she said. "That's what I'm working out."

He looked at her steadily. He didn't try to fix it or minimize it or turn it into a thing she was handling wrong.

"Okay," he said.

That was all. Just okay. And then: "There's a place two blocks over that does eggs. You should eat before we go to the park."

She looked at him. The warmth of him in the small kitchen, the same warmth she'd watched him carry all summer, only easier here, unguarded. She thought about what it meant that his response to her working through something difficult was to feed her. The practicality of it. The specific, un-dramatic care.

"Okay," she said.

They went to get eggs.


---

The park was larger than she'd expected. She had expected something urban and compressed, a city park, grass and paths and benches. She got something that had actual topography, actual trees, the November canopy gone bare but still constituting something, still making a shape against the gray sky.

She walked beside him and read the terrain the way she always read terrain. The hill in the middle, the circulation of paths around it, the way the grade changed. The weight of people, families and dogs and a man playing a guitar near the fountain who was good enough that she slowed to listen.

"The lake's there," Reid said. "When it's warmer you can see the birds."

"What kind?"

"Herons, mostly. Egrets when it's really warm."

She nodded. She was looking at a stand of trees at the far end of the path, deciduous, the kind she couldn't identify precisely from this distance. In Oregon she knew the trees. Here she would have to learn them.

"I don't know the trees here," she said.

"I can tell you." He looked at the stand she was looking at. "Those are London planes. The ones with the peeling bark."

"What's the peeling for?"

"Air quality. The bark peels to shed pollutants. Urban adaptation."

She looked at the bark, the way it came off in plates, revealing the pale underskin. A tree that had developed its own mechanism for surviving bad air. She thought about that with interest.

"Show me the rest," she said.

He showed her the rest. He knew the park the way she knew the base perimeter, by repetition and attention, and he named things as they passed them without making it a lecture. The boathouse, the bandshell, the particular bend in the path where on clear days you could see the Manhattan skyline through a gap in the trees.

She could see the skyline from here, the towers of it, the particular compressed density of a city that had had to build vertically because it had run out of horizontal. She read it.

"You navigate it," she said.

"What?"

"The grid. You said Brooklyn's easier than Manhattan." She looked at the skyline. "But you navigate all of it."

"I've been here twelve years. You learn it."

She liked it.

That was new also. She had been good at her life for a long time. Liking it was different from being good at it. Liking it required knowing what you had.

She knew what she had.

## Chapter 23

The season's last significant fire came in the first week of September.

The Trout Creek complex, started by a dry lightning storm on a Tuesday, ran fast on the Wednesday with a southwest wind that the models had not given sufficient weight to. By Thursday morning it was twelve thousand acres and she was at the incident command post at four in the morning with a cup of coffee and the weather briefing and a fire behavior analyst who was telling her what she already knew: the wind was the variable.

She built the plan around the wind.

The jump was in a drainage she'd worked twice before, a configuration she knew: steep east slope, mixed conifer and sage on the lower tier, a natural barrier at the top of the drainage that gave her anchor options. She had Vasquez and Torres and Chávez on this one. She had left Danny off the list, the knee a precaution she'd built in without discussion, and Danny had accepted this with the particular grace of someone who trusted the person making the call.

She was in the air by eight.

She read the terrain from the plane. The fire's behavior, the smoke column, the way the wind was moving in the drainage. She filed what she saw and built the sequence of calls she expected to make and left room for what she couldn't anticipate because there was always something she couldn't anticipate.

They jumped.


---

The wind shifted at noon, which she'd been watching for, and she keyed her radio and called the adjustment two minutes before it would have mattered. They were in the black, standing on ground that had already burned, safe.

She stood in the black and looked at the fire making its run to where they'd been and she felt the specific quality of the correct call, the one that had held.

Not because she was right. Because she'd read it honestly and called what she read and it had been right. The rightness was a function of honest reading, not of certainty. She'd never been certain. She'd been honest.

She thought, standing in the black with the smoke column reorganizing above her and Vasquez already moving to the next task, about what it meant to read things honestly.

She had been reading herself honestly for two years. Not perfectly. Honestly. Paying the cost of what she saw rather than looking away.

The terrain was the terrain. The fire was the fire. She'd read it right today and she'd be back tomorrow to read the next piece of it.

"Good call, Remy." Torres, coming up on her left.

She didn't answer. She never did. The call had been the call.

She moved to the next task.


---

By Saturday morning the perimeter was holding and the resources from John Day had relieved the overnight rotation and she stood at the incident command post and looked at the topographic map and the containment lines and understood that the fire had been contained.

Not finished. Fires were never finished until the rain came. But contained, inside lines that held, managed into something that was no longer running.

She called the air resources and released them. She wrote the preliminary status report. She briefed the liaison from the Forest Service.

She came back to the base on Saturday afternoon.

Reid was in the common room. He looked up when she came in and he read her face the way he'd been reading it for two seasons, with the particular quality of someone who had learned what the different kinds of tired meant.

"Good?" he said.

"Good," she said. "Contained. Clean containment."

He stood up. He came to her and she let herself be held, the way she always did now, immediately, without the period of recalibration she used to need.

"Okay?" he said.

"Yes." She was. She was okay and the fire was contained and the season had three weeks left in it and she was standing in the common room being held by a person she had said the actual thing to in July.

"The wind shift," she said.

"I saw the status reports."

"I read it two minutes early."

"I know." He pressed a kiss to her hair. "I know."

She thought about the Ochoco jump where she'd read it wrong. She thought about reading it right today. The same person, the same process, honestly applied. Sometimes right and sometimes wrong and always paying for what she got.

That was the job. That was the whole job.

She pulled back and looked at him.

"I'm going to shower and sleep," she said. "And then I want to eat something good."

"I found a place in Redmond," he said. "Italian. It's better than it should be given the location."

"Italian," she said.

"Tonight?"

"Tonight."

She went to shower. She thought about the fire and the wind shift and the clean containment and the things she'd said in July and the people she'd trusted and the trust that had held.

All of it was still there when she got out of the shower. The base, the common room, the man she'd said the actual thing to, the season almost over.

She got dressed. She went to find him.

They went to the Italian place in Redmond that was better than it should have been given the location, and she ate more than she'd eaten in four days, and the September evening was cool through the restaurant window, and she thought: this is the season. This is what the season is.

Hard and good and both at once. The fire and the containment. The risk and the coming home.

She had chosen it. She was still choosing it. Every season.

She would choose it again.


## Chapter 24

## Chapter 25

## Chapter 26
